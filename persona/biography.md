## Timeline

- **1972 — Born in Saigon, Vietnam.** 📰 Year and place consistent across independent bios. The exact date "January 13" is aggregator-only — treat as low confidence. Vietnamese surname Đỗ; "Christopher Do" in formal records. Corroboration: on the 2024 Travis podcast he states he is **51 years old** at recording (Feb 2024), consistent with a 1972 birth year. 🗣️ ([[2026-07-14-research-biography-dossier]], [[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])
- **1975 (April 30) — Family flees the Fall of Saigon** when Chris is three; his father had served in the South Vietnamese military. He says he has **no memory of his home country**. The family is resettled via a **Catholic church-based sponsorship program**, landing first in **Kansas City, Missouri**; their sponsor was an older widowed white woman he refers to affectionately as "Aunt Barney." Other relatives were sponsored to Arkansas and to San Jose. 🗣️ (video corroborates the dossier's refugee/Kansas-City account first-hand) ([[2026-07-14-research-biography-dossier]], [[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])
- **~1977 — Moves to San Jose, California** to join extended family (more of his father's siblings were there); he frames the San Jose choice as largely logistical/"random." Grows up in a striving suburban middle-class household that moved every few years to better neighborhoods chasing the "American dream"; as a first-gen immigrant kid he always **identified the family as poor**, then was shocked to learn from school paperwork that their income didn't match that self-image. 🗣️ ([[2026-07-14-research-biography-dossier]], [[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])
- **Family structure & the two-poles origin (self-reported; name-free).** Middle child of **three brothers**: an **older brother** (4 years older), highly logical, **master's from Stanford**, computer-science/software engineer in Silicon Valley; a **younger brother** (1 year younger), free-form **artist who never went to college**. Chris positions himself literally in the middle, with a BFA in art & design. His **father is an engineer** (father's side: technical, stable Silicon Valley engineers); his **mother is a self-taught artist** (oils and pastels) who worked at **IBM as a designer/drafter on chip / chipboard design**, drawing by hand at high magnification; mother's side are artists (photographers, painters) — emotional, creative, financially unstable, "many divorces." Chris frames his identity as the fusion of these two poles. He credits his own path not directly to his mother but to an early "obsession with emblems and logos." 🗣️ (video corroborates and greatly enriches the dossier's father-engineer / mother-artist contrast) ([[2026-07-14-research-biography-dossier]], [[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])
- **Childhood in San Jose — schooling, a near-drowning rescue, and an older-brother surrogate father (self-reported; name-free).** 🗣️ In the 2022 Carlos Segura interview Chris adds specifics to the San Jose childhood: the family briefly lived at his **grandmother's house in Kansas City, Missouri** before his parents settled them in San Jose; he attended **John Steinbeck Middle School**, and still names two formative teachers who showed small kindnesses to an outsider immigrant kid — **Mr. Ted Thompson** (social studies) and **Mrs. Karen New York** (English/lit). Around **age ~12** (pre-junior-high), after heavy rain, his **younger brother** (1 year younger) nearly drowned in a deep pool of **Coyote Creek**; Chris — by his own account not a swimmer — went in, pulled him out, and performed **CPR** until he revived. His **older brother** (4 years older) functioned as a **surrogate father** in America: he spotted Chris's talent (praising his metal-shop sculptures), got him his **first credit card**, found him his **first freelance client**, and flew him to a **San Diego animation festival** as a teen. - **Late 1970s–1980s — Latchkey childhood & the outcast experience (self-reported; from the 2019 Debbie Millman interview).** 🗣️ Both parents worked "all the time" to provide a future, so on weekdays they were absent and Chris and his brothers were **latchkey kids** who "ran around like animals" with near-total freedom — he cites walking a creek alone for hours and exploring caves at **age 11** ("things that could have gotten us killed… I'd never allow my son to do the things we were allowed to do"). His parents held **high expectations "as a result of their sacrifice."** His father's parenting was **"laissez-faire, hands-off"** — he would **"lecture me for hours to help me think about things"** rather than use "the belt," uncommon (Chris says) for his father's culture and generation — corroborating the report-card-forgery / inner-critic notes above. Growing up in a **"predominantly Caucasian neighborhood,"** he felt like an outcast "without a tribe," endured **ridicule, racial slurs, and physical fist-fights,** and struggled with **identity and insecurity for a long time** ("everybody from this insecure place is looking just to belong"). ([[2019-04-10-yt-wHhcuix-ir8]]) He again cites **growing up bullied** in a 2019-04-02 on-stage interview — framing it as why he "got fired up and fired back" on social media to defend a guest photographer whose character was being publicly questioned. 🗣️ ([[2019-04-02-yt-aDLmEezhnG0]]) A 2020 confessional deepens the same formation: as a kid all he wanted was to be **accepted** — "not even loved or respected, just accepted" — and he **deliberately made himself invisible** so he wouldn't be noticed and picked on at school; the years of "pulling it all in and repressing and hiding" **cost him his voice and his sense of who he was** ("I didn't know who I was, I just tried to be somebody I wasn't"), which he says he only recovered later through radical **self-acceptance** ("the best thing that's ever happened to me… was the acceptance that I'm just gonna have to be me"). 🗣️ ([[2020-01-11-yt-d7V7eJn3-oc]])
- **~1979 (age ~7) — Earliest creative signals (self-reported; name-free).** 🗣️ In a 2020 passion/purpose monologue Chris traces the clues to his creative calling back to early childhood. Yet he long **denied the path** — "I denied being a creative because I thought it meant a lifetime of suffering, pain, poverty," wanting a piece of the American dream instead — waving off a commercial-art teacher and a family friend who both spotted his talent. - **~1996 (first year in business) — The maker→CEO transition (why he stopped doing hands-on design).** 🗣️ [self-reported] In his **first year in business** he had "a very fortunate problem": more people gave him work than he could physically do in a day. His response was to **bring in friends to execute while he managed and art-directed**. He discovered a leverage skill — he could take work from people whose process was less refined, **add ~10% of effort, and make it "twice as good"** — multiplying their talent and scaling the services he could offer. Eventually he could **hire people better than himself**, which created a new job: **keeping revenue flowing** to keep the team "fed with work and money" so they'd stay and elevate the work. He frames this as the arc from a **"maker mindset" to an "entrepreneurial mindset"** (nothing wrong with either — just different paths) and says that over his life he "learned to love making a difference more than making things." ([[2018-07-04-yt-uVhWhMv6sO4]])
- **Early Blind — the payroll-tax failure (self-reported cautionary tale).** 🗣️ The **first time he had 10 employees**, Chris didn't know he **owed payroll taxes** on them: he wrote payroll checks straight from a checkbook and spent the withheld tax money on **computers and "culture things,"** then got a blunt end-of-quarter email from his accountant saying he was **~15% in the hole** and at risk of running afoul of the state. He cites it as a lived example of how not knowing business fundamentals (especially finance/taxes) sinks entrepreneurs — the origin material behind his *Create & Orchestrate* framework (below). - **~2000s — Scaling Blind via "I do / We do / You do."** Chris says his "modus operandi from the jump" was to get others to do what he did; he leveled up raw hires designer → art director → creative director using a three-stage teaching model, freeing himself from day-to-day delivery. - **Late 1990s → ~2016 — Blind's scale, in self-reported numbers.** During the **late-'90s internet boom** Blind was, in his telling, **"one of six companies in the world"** doing motion work at that level; demand so outstripped supply that in **1999** (pre-dot-com-crash) his producer would simply "double the budget" when they had no capacity and clients still approved — the moment he learned he could turn work down. - **2007 → ~2015 — Blind's near-collapse and reinvention (motion-design shop → branding consultancy).** 🗣️ [self-reported] **2007 was Blind's "Zenith"** — "almost $7 million" in revenue for a relatively small West Coast firm — just before the economy crashed.
