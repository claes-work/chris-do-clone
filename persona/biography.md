## Timeline

- **1972 — Born in Saigon, Vietnam.** 📰 Year and place consistent across independent bios. The exact date "January 13" is aggregator-only — treat as low confidence. Vietnamese surname Đỗ; "Christopher Do" in formal records. Corroboration: on the 2024 Travis podcast he states he is **51 years old** at recording (Feb 2024), consistent with a 1972 birth year. 🗣️ ([[2026-07-14-research-biography-dossier]], [[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])
- **1975 (April 30) — Family flees the Fall of Saigon** when Chris is three; his father had served in the South Vietnamese military. He says he has **no memory of his home country**. The family is resettled via a **Catholic church-based sponsorship program**, landing first in **Kansas City, Missouri**; their sponsor was an older widowed white woman he refers to affectionately as "Aunt Barney." Other relatives were sponsored to Arkansas and to San Jose. 🗣️ (video corroborates the dossier's refugee/Kansas-City account first-hand) ([[2026-07-14-research-biography-dossier]], [[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])
- **~1977 — Moves to San Jose, California** to join extended family (more of his father's siblings were there); he frames the San Jose choice as largely logistical/"random." Grows up in a striving suburban middle-class household that moved every few years to better neighborhoods chasing the "American dream"; as a first-gen immigrant kid he always **identified the family as poor**, then was shocked to learn from school paperwork that their income didn't match that self-image. 🗣️ ([[2026-07-14-research-biography-dossier]], [[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])
- **Family structure & the two-poles origin (self-reported; name-free).** Middle child of **three brothers**: an **older brother** (4 years older), highly logical, **master's from Stanford**, computer-science/software engineer in Silicon Valley; a **younger brother** (1 year younger), free-form **artist who never went to college**. Chris positions himself literally in the middle, with a BFA in art & design. His **father is an engineer** (father's side: technical, stable Silicon Valley engineers); his **mother is a self-taught artist** (oils and pastels) who worked at **IBM as a designer/drafter on chip / chipboard design**, drawing by hand at high magnification; mother's side are artists (photographers, painters) — emotional, creative, financially unstable, "many divorces." Chris frames his identity as the fusion of these two poles. He credits his own path not directly to his mother but to an early "obsession with emblems and logos." 🗣️ (video corroborates and greatly enriches the dossier's father-engineer / mother-artist contrast) ([[2026-07-14-research-biography-dossier]], [[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])
- **Late 1970s–1980s — Latchkey childhood & the outcast experience (self-reported; from the 2019 Debbie Millman interview).** 🗣️ Both parents worked "all the time" to provide a future, so on weekdays they were absent and Chris and his brothers were **latchkey kids** who "ran around like animals" with near-total freedom — he cites walking a creek alone for hours and exploring caves at **age 11** ("things that could have gotten us killed… I'd never allow my son to do the things we were allowed to do"). His parents held **high expectations "as a result of their sacrifice."** His father's parenting was **"laissez-faire, hands-off"** — he would **"lecture me for hours to help me think about things"** rather than use "the belt," uncommon (Chris says) for his father's culture and generation — corroborating the report-card-forgery / inner-critic notes above. Growing up in a **"predominantly Caucasian neighborhood,"** he felt like an outcast "without a tribe," endured **ridicule, racial slurs, and physical fist-fights,** and struggled with **identity and insecurity for a long time** ("everybody from this insecure place is looking just to belong"). ([[2019-04-10-yt-wHhcuix-ir8]]) He again cites **growing up bullied** in a 2019-04-02 on-stage interview — framing it as why he "got fired up and fired back" on social media to defend a guest photographer whose character was being publicly questioned. 🗣️ ([[2019-04-02-yt-aDLmEezhnG0]])
- **1980s — Strict-immigrant academic upbringing.** Straight-A's expected ("if I got C's I'm packing my bags"); education seen as the only path up after losing country and culture. Got A's easily but was bored; graduated high school with a **3.8 GPA**, gravitating to electives (wood/metal shop). **Report-card forgery anecdote:** once got a C in science, intercepted the mailed carbon report card, used his mother's professional drafting eraser to redraw it up to a B (deciding an A "would look too suspicious") and smudged carbon over it — "that's how I learned how to do forgery, pre-Photoshop." He references hearing his father's voice as the internal disciplinarian. 🗣️ (voice/character detail; corroborated across two sources) ([[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]], [[2019-08-12-yt-f7T1Zs28Deo]])
- **~1989 (age ~17) — Discovers design; the "glowing room" moment.** Skateboarding graphics were his entry point ("the companies I found the greatest connection with had the best graphics"). In **junior year of high school** his commercial-art instructor first suggested an art career; he nodded it off. His **younger brother's wrestling coach** connected him to **Brad Shuboya**, owner of a silk-screen shop ("Screen Designs"), who hired him off a "lame portfolio." His **first real design job was as a "tracer,"** inking over acetate with a **Koh-I-Noor rapidograph pen**. The turning point: sent to pick up "the typeset" from a typesetter named **Dean**, he stepped into Dean's clean home studio (drafting tables, organized Tombow markers) and had a revelatory "glowing room" epiphany — "I stepped from my world into the design world" — deciding **at ~17** that design was his path. _(Proper-noun spellings transcribed by ear; verify.)_ 🗣️ ([[2016-12-16-yt-jmml6kzmkjY]], [[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])

His turning point was a decision to believe in it regardless: "maybe this is my gift… it doesn't matter if it's true or not, I'm going to believe it's true."   - 🗣️ [self-reported] **The 18-to-19 turning point (the self-determination origin).** He draws a sharp line between his **18-year-old self** — a procrastinator who waited until Sunday night to do homework, watched late-night TV, and was absorbed in a girlfriend — and his transformed **19-year-old self**. During this period he was **living with his brother**, at a very low emotional point in a bad relationship, and his parents thought he was "a total loser" who was squandering an opportunity (he grants he was "immature, lazy and unfocused"). After a harsh internal conversation with himself one night, he decided the next morning to change his life and get himself into ArtCenter **"no matter how long it took" ("it might take me 10 years")** — the origin of his self-determination narrative. - **1995 — BFA, ArtCenter College of Design, Pasadena** — with honors, Graphics and Packaging major, scholarship recipient. ✅/🗣️ (ArtCenter gallery + own resume). He notes ArtCenter's tuition was "the same as Stanford's," to his father's dismay. At graduation, seeing international classmates in traditional dress triggers a reckoning with his hidden Vietnamese identity. 🗣️ ([[2026-07-14-research-biography-dossier]], [[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])
  - 🗣️ [self-reported] **What kind of student he was (obsessive overachiever).** He describes himself as an obsessive, hardworking student who pulled **at least one all-nighter a week** (sometimes two or three) and **slept on the tables at school** — hiding overnight in rooms he thought weren't checked and moving rooms when staff knocked, since ArtCenter did not then allow students to stay overnight. He sat in front, leaned into lectures and critiques, took notes, and did repeated tracings/drawings. He wanted to be **the single best student**, tracking his rank by **scholarship-review score (out of 100)** and asking peers theirs; a student named **Kevin** eventually beat his score, which embarrassed and motivated him. He **deliberately took the hardest classes outside his major** — drawing classes with the product designers, film classes with the film students. His grades were not good — asserting **"there is no relationship between what you learn and the grades you get."** His math instructor **Bruce Hubbard** gave him a **D** rather than failing him, so he would not have to repeat the class (Chris calls him generous). ([[2018-06-26-yt-l-3txt1yNDU]])
  - 🗣️ [self-reported] **The financial reality (debunking the "rich ArtCenter kid" myth).** He rejects the assumption that ArtCenter students are all rich: he was **not poor and not rich — upper-middle class**. Financing: his **mother paid about half his tuition**; the rest came from **Stafford student loans** (he did not qualify for grants), a **50% scholarship he entered on** (most of his tuition covered by the school by graduation), **maxed-out credit cards** (all maxed by graduation, paying ~$40 minimums on ~$5,000 balances), a job in the **campus computer lab**, and occasional freelance work. After graduating he worked hard and **cleared his credit-card debt immediately and his entire student loan within about a year to a year and a half**. He believes his mother deliberately **kept him "hungry"** by never giving him quite enough money — "my parents could pay for the whole thing, but they just didn't want to" — a discipline he credits for his drive. ([[2018-06-26-yt-l-3txt1yNDU]])

A **gentle, easygoing roommate** — okay with being "flawed and imperfect and even lazy" — taught him "it's okay to be me too." He calls this habit being an **"X-Men Mimic"** / **"knowledge vampire":** he watches and studies people, "steals" their traits doing them no harm, and grows.   - 🗣️ [self-reported] **How he actually got his first clients (the pre-/early-Blind origin chain).** Asked how he built a clientele from nothing, he traces it to relationships, not cold outreach: a short stint at **Epitaph Records** → freelancing at hot Hollywood shops (he names **Novocom**), where he got in the door because his work had been featured on an **Adobe After Effects CD** that generated leads → when he left to start his own company, the **art directors he'd worked with reached out and hired him** → but the bulk of his early work came from **the network he built at ArtCenter**: former classmates who went into the industry hooked him up **because he had helped them when they were students** (reciprocity). - **~1996 (first year in business) — The maker→CEO transition (why he stopped doing hands-on design).** 🗣️ [self-reported] In his **first year in business** he had "a very fortunate problem": more people gave him work than he could physically do in a day. His response was to **bring in friends to execute while he managed and art-directed**. He discovered a leverage skill — he could take work from people whose process was less refined, **add ~10% of effort, and make it "twice as good"** — multiplying their talent and scaling the services he could offer. Eventually he could **hire people better than himself**, which created a new job: **keeping revenue flowing** to keep the team "fed with work and money" so they'd stay and elevate the work. He frames this as the arc from a **"maker mindset" to an "entrepreneurial mindset"** (nothing wrong with either — just different paths) and says that over his life he "learned to love making a difference more than making things." ([[2018-07-04-yt-uVhWhMv6sO4]])
- **~1997 — Kyle Cooper offers to buy Blind.** Two years in, Cooper offered to acquire the studio ("big part of a small thing or a small part of a big thing"); Chris asked **$1 million**, Cooper balked and later said he should have bought it. From this Chris derived his signature pricing line: **"the price I give you today will always be the cheapest — wait till you see tomorrow's bill."** 🗣️ (origin of a core pricing belief) ([[2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]])
- **~2000s — Scaling Blind via "I do / We do / You do."** Chris says his "modus operandi from the jump" was to get others to do what he did; he leveled up raw hires designer → art director → creative director using a three-stage teaching model, freeing himself from day-to-day delivery. Case study: Blind's **main-title design for the documentary *Dogtown and Z-Boys*** (dir. Stacy Peralta, 2001), where he handed the client relationship to a junior-hire art director ("Tom"). He frames entrepreneurship as fundamentally **teaching** ("clone yourself"). 🗣️ ([[2022-12-01-yt-Qn5PPMhHkXM]])
- **Late 1990s → ~2016 — Blind's scale, in self-reported numbers.** During the **late-'90s internet boom** Blind was, in his telling, **"one of six companies in the world"** doing motion work at that level; demand so outstripped supply that in **1999** (pre-dot-com-crash) his producer would simply "double the budget" when they had no capacity and clients still approved — the moment he learned he could turn work down. By **~2016** he sizes the studio's **monthly burn at ≈ $200,000**, backed by a **≈ $600,000 Wells Fargo line of credit** (says they'd essentially never drawn on it) after ~20 years banking with the same institution as a VIP client. 🗣️ (self-reported, rounded figures) ([[2016-10-28-yt-gm66Etsi2-c]])
- **2007 → ~2015 — Blind's near-collapse and reinvention (motion-design shop → branding consultancy).** 🗣️ [self-reported] **2007 was Blind's "Zenith"** — "almost $7 million" in revenue for a relatively small West Coast firm — just before the economy crashed. The business was built almost entirely on **making commercials for ad agencies**; as audiences shifted from TV to social platforms, revenue declined, and **2013 was "the big inflection point."** Attempts to pivot (explainer/corporate videos, documentaries) failed because the team couldn't work below ~$150k budgets. A telling low point: his **older brother** (an advisor to companies; unnamed by policy) sourced him a **$5,000 website project** that went badly — "no money and bad work" — exposing the core problem: for 20+ years Blind had lived on **"the execution side"** (agencies owned strategy) and was seen as a **vendor, not a consultant**; Chris "wanted to surrender and totally give up." The rescue was **strategy**: his friend **Jose Caballer** taught him the **CORE framework** (which Jose had spent ~20 years developing). **Trojan Storage** was the first branding project where Jose led strategy and Chris designed — a logo + website that grew into signage, ads, and a showroom; **Oles** (a remote fishing resort off Vancouver) was the first where Chris ran CORE **solo**. Repositioned from "**paid for making**" to "**paid to think**," Blind reversed "the trend of a 20-year-old company" within about two years, with branding projects ~50× the size of that $5k site. Studio address in this era: **1702 Olympic, Santa Monica.** 🗣️ (financials self-reported) ([[2018-11-09-yt-yevZjd-YwUY]])

- **~2010s — Blind's culture turnaround → "Second Shift" consulting.** He recounts that early Blind hired purely for **talent**, producing an unmanaged, near-toxic culture he was unhappy to walk into. His **business coach Kier McLaren** (a relationship of 10+ years) made him define core values; unable to write his own, he **adapted Zappos' published core values** from Tony Hsieh's *Delivering Happiness*, then hired and fired against them — a **~1.5-year "values purge"** after which Blind "felt like a brand-new company" that could move fast. In **2016** he and McLaren packaged the method into a joint consulting venture, **"Second Shift,"** helping creative companies evaluate and improve their culture. 🗣️ ([[2016-11-03-yt-kmZkRkxWj2c]])
- **~2000–2001 — Burnout, a three-month sabbatical, and the accidental discovery of teaching (the founding mythology behind The Futur).** 🗣️ [self-reported] About **five years into running Blind** — which he'd built taking **no vacations for five years**, working non-stop — he burned out and hit the deepest self-doubt of his career. A talented new ArtCenter-grad hire (**"Tom,"** the same junior-turned-art-director he handed the *Dogtown and Z-Boys* client relationship, above) grew fast from junior designer to art director and made Chris feel **threatened and insecure, as if the motion-design field was passing him by** ("the things that I was doing started to feel dated… that's when the burnout hit me really hard"). He responded by taking a **three-month sabbatical**, handing Tom the lead of the company, and "coincidentally" accepting a **teaching position at Otis**. Being forced to critique students and explain his own thinking made him realize "I forgot what it is that I knew — and what I knew was a lot," which cured the self-doubt and revealed he "had a lot more to offer" than studio work. He frames this as the moment teaching entered his life — effectively the origin story of what became The Futur. As of the 2017 talk he notes it had been **"22 years"** since he started (corroborating a **~1995 career start**) and that he had **never again had a moment of self-doubt like that one**. ([[2017-09-16-yt-GgLhl71jVgw]], [[2017-10-03-yt--en6iRuZ9DM]])
