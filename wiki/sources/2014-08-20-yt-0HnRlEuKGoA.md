# How to Ask for the Sale | Chris Do & Jose Caballer Roleplay a Sale — Part 2

★ **L3-candidate** — earliest-era (Aug 2014, "The Skool" / pre-rebrand of The Futur)
instance of the two-host **role-play-a-sale** format that later became a signature
Futur teaching device, and it already voices several canonical Chris Do sales
frameworks (get to yes/no fast, budget-qualify early, "the ask = advance to the next
step," scaffolding questions toward the ask, win-without-pitching). Worth promoting to
topics/persona in a later synthesis pass as a probable origin point. See notes.

## Summary

Skool-era (Aug 2014) episode, **Part 2** of a role-play sales training. **Chris Do**
and **Jose Caballer** improvise a networking-mixer encounter and then debrief it. In the
role-play, **Chris plays "John," a tough, businessy client** — founder of "Ameate Tech,"
a ~10-year-old company making high-end video cards ($2k–$20k) for post-production and
streaming, growing ~200%/yr but now flattening and wanting to expand toward the
home/"YouTuber" market. **Jose plays the passionate designer/salesperson** who approaches
John, learns his business through questions, and tries to secure a follow-up. After the
scene, both hosts compare their real-world approaches to *asking for the sale*.

The through-line: the "ask" is not asking for money or a signed deal — it is asking to
**advance to the next step** (a card, a meeting, a presentation). The hosts genuinely
disagree in emphasis: one leans transactional (qualify hard, get a fast yes/no), the
other leans relationship/context-first; the episode presents both as valid styles.

## Key claims

All dated 2014-08-20. Paraphrased unless quoted below.

- **The "ask" means advancing to the next step, not closing.** You're not asking for the
  money or the deal; you're asking to get to the next contact/meeting. It usually takes
  one to three "dates" before a deal can close. — attribution: host framing the lesson,
  likely **Chris** (attribution: uncertain)
- **Get to a fast yes or no.** The worst answer is "maybe," because it drains your time;
  qualify hard to reach yes/no quickly. — **Chris**
- **Qualify budget early, and decline gracefully.** Chris will ask a caller for the
  budget up front; if it's far too low (his example: "$400"), he thanks them for saying
  so, explains it can't work given how his company is structured, and offers referrals
  rather than waste anyone's time. — **Chris**
- **Scaffold questions toward the ultimate ask.** Build a stair-step series of questions
  that establishes the problem and leads to the ask — e.g. "Can I do a presentation for
  you?" / "Is there a project coming up I could look at?" — **Chris**
- **Talk about the problem, not the product/service.** Don't be the person offering a
  vacuum to everyone you meet; discuss the client's problem and insight first, and only
  pitch if you can genuinely solve it. Accept that not everyone wants what you provide,
  and be okay with that. — host (attribution: uncertain; shared point)
- **Be able to state who you are in ~two sentences.** Establish credentials concisely
  (a "two-sentence log line"); don't ramble about yourself or freeze up. — both hosts
  (each demos their own version)
- **Chris's direct-to-client credibility comes from tenure.** Chris frames himself as
  having done direct-to-client "from the beginning," with a deck/portfolio that gives him
  a high probability of walking away with a project once he gets face time to show work.
  — **Chris**
- **Leads come from the people around you; give value to the community.** "Tell me who
  you are and I'll tell you who your friends are" — get involved, volunteer, and let more
  powerful people see your work, or you won't get bigger leads. — attribution: uncertain
  (the direct-to-client tenure tell reads as **Chris**)
- **Be a well-rounded person for sales.** Have many hobbies and interests so you have
  multiple "entry points" with people; talking only about design (e.g. favorite
  Helvetica) only connects you with other designers who can't hire you. Chris's version:
  MMA, volleyball, skateboarding, video games, comics. Jose's version: read Harvard
  Business Review / Business Week to learn common business problems. — **Chris** (hobbies)
  vs **Jose** (read HBR)
- **Win-without-pitching / plant seeds.** The stated goal is to do such good work planting
  seeds and goodwill that you rarely have to "close" or pitch — but getting there takes
  hard work and sacrifice first. Chris cites closing his agency and spending ~2 years at
  financial cost "planting the seeds for the school" before being able to harvest it.
  — "don't want to pitch / plant seeds" = **Jose** (attribution: uncertain);
  "I closed my agency … for the school" = **Chris**
- **Business people know the rules of engagement.** They understand a meeting/lunch means
  you'll ask something; if you beat around the bush they walk away. Designers often don't
  realize this. — host (attribution: uncertain)
- **Objection to pure transactionality.** The fast-qualify approach is fine when a lead
  already comes with context (they already know something / are already in a
  conversation); the objection is qualifying "without context." Clients ultimately came
  for *alignment* and shared *passion/point of view*, not merely "growth." — **Jose**
  (context)

Roleplay/context (Chris's client character "John," not persona data): Ameate Tech makes
$2k–$20k high-end video/streaming cards; ~10 yrs old, bootstrapped from a garage, ~200%
YoY growth now flattening; sells to post-production/videographers/event pros; wants to
broaden toward home broadcasters/YouTubers without alienating loyal pro customers; reaches
pros via sponsored influencers, product reviews, trade shows; not experts at making video
themselves. — Chris (in character; **context, not persona**)

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris = persona voice. Jose = context.

**Chris Do** (get to yes/no fast):
> "Here's what I've learned. The worst answer that you can get is maybe because it sucks
> all of your time … So I try to get to the yes or no really fast."

**Chris Do** (qualify budget early):
> "Before we go too deep into this conversation, I just want to get an idea of what budget
> we're talking about here. … 'Oh, I have $400.' I said, 'Great. I really appreciate you
> telling me that up front. Given the nature of my company and how we have to structure our
> bills, there's no way I can do this project for you. So I don't want to waste your time …
> I have to make some recommendations for your referral, but this is not a job I can take
> on at this point in time.'"

**Chris Do** (scaffolding to the ask):
> "Build a series of questions that leads you to the ultimate ask. So the ask is, can I do
> a presentation for you? … is there a project that's coming up that I could take a look at?"

**Chris Do** (concise credential pitch):
> "We work with Fortune 500 companies and Madison Avenue agencies, but we're also expanding
> our business to work with clients just like you."

**Chris Do** (approachable framing — the Tesla/dentist encounter):
> "We make videos for clients. Sometimes they're like testimonials and sometimes they
> explain what people do and we do it for all kinds of clients. … I want to make it kind of
> approachable and then scale it up."

**Chris Do** (be well-rounded):
> "I'm really interested in mixed martial arts. I love volleyball. I grew up skateboarding.
> I like video games and comics. The list goes on and on. … So I have lots of entry points."

**Chris Do** (sacrifice behind the school):
> "I closed my agency and I spent two years … at a big financial cost to myself just
> setting the stage and planting the seeds for the school. And that took a lot of sacrifice
> and a lot of work and a lot of noise making. But now we can harvest it."

**Jose Caballer** (objection to the transactional model — *context*):
> "I have a big problem with it in general because it comes from an industry and from a
> model that … has been fairly transactional."

**Jose Caballer** (context-first — *context*):
> "I have a problem if it's without context. Like, meaning if there's not already an
> established … 'we're already here to talk.' Somebody's already talking to me. So the
> context is they know something."
