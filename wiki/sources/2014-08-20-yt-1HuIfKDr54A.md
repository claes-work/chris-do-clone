# How To Ask for the Sale — Part 1 (How To Close Deals With Design Clients)

## Summary

Earliest Futur/Skool-era episode (Aug 2014), co-hosted by **Chris Do** ("the business of
design") and **Jose Caballer** ("the design of business," founder of The Skool). Both speak
heavily. The episode is the third in a mini-series (prior two: confidence, and active
listening) and covers "the ask" — asking for the sale/meeting/job. The two present two
contrasting sales philosophies and close with a discovery-call **role-play** (Chris plays the
selling designer, Jose plays the CEO of an eco-friendly bottle company).

Because it is co-hosted with role-play, attribution matters: only **Chris-Do-attributed**
statements are persona voice data; Jose Caballer's are context. Where the exchange is
interwoven and the speaker is not clearly resolvable, claims are marked `attribution: uncertain`.

The two positions:
- **Chris Do** — pragmatic/direct: qualify hard, get to yes/no fast, and *scaffold* toward
  the ask within one conversation so it never feels abrupt. Broadcast/advertising background
  (leads historically via reps), now learning direct selling.
- **Jose Caballer** — "karmic equity"/long-tail: deliver value (webinars, speaking,
  volunteering, free work) and let opportunities come inbound. This is Jose's material and is
  recorded here only as context.

★ **L3-candidate** — P2 long-tail, but this is early foundational sales material and appears to
be an origin point of Chris Do's later Futur qualifying/closing frameworks (the "do I like you /
can I do the work / can you afford it" client-qualification triad, and the scaffolded soft-ask).
Worth promoting to L3 during a synthesis pass to date-stamp the framework's origin — with the
caveat that much of the episode is Jose's content and must be filtered out.

## Key claims

All dated 2014-08-20. Paraphrased unless quoted below.

**Chris Do (persona data):**
- Before pursuing a sale, qualify the prospect on three things: (1) Do I like the person / are
  they good energy? (2) Is there work here that I can actually do (in my area of expertise)?
  (3) Can they afford my services — is the budget big enough? (lines 244–289)
- The affordability filter is non-negotiable because of agency overhead: designers, producers,
  and coordinators all have to be supported, so very small budgets (his example: $500) can't
  work. (277–286)
- Asking for the sale abruptly is "high friction" — it puts the person on the spot, produces an
  inauthentic yes, and alienates them (his analogy: bluntly demanding a barbecue invite). Instead
  you must *build toward* the ask within one conversation. (216–244, 512–514)
- Have a clear goal for every meeting. (recapped at 385–388)
- Get to a yes or no fast so you can move on; your time is valuable (family, company to run) —
  don't drift through meeting after meeting without an outcome. (116–120, 473–479)
- Practice conversational rapport constantly with strangers — servers, an old lady, people in
  the elevator, on an airplane — with no intent to close, purely to learn about them. This makes
  the real ask non-awkward because it's not the first time you're doing it. (338–362)
- Fear of judgment is what stops most (introverted) designers from talking to people; recommends
  Dale Carnegie's *How to Win Friends and Influence People*; people are as afraid of you as you
  are of them. (329–383)
- His own context: advertising/broadcast world, leads traditionally came through reps; a market
  transition is forcing him to sell directly to smaller ($10–30M) companies; he learned his
  current sales method from a business adviser (~10 years) and has been using it ~4.5 months with
  good results. (194–216)
- Do research before the meeting; know the context going in. (during role-play debrief, 622–627)

**Attribution uncertain (Chris/Jose interwoven in the financial-qualifying exchange, 290–328):**
- Financial qualification is hard; ask it softly — "Do you have a budget? Do you mind sharing
  it?" or "What have you spent on something like this in the past?"; if they hedge, "Give me an
  average, I won't hold you to it"; bracket it: "Is it between $50,000 or $500,000?" A half-
  million project usually signals a corporate buyer (VP/director). (306–322)
- When selling direct, build a profile of the prospect (e.g., from their LinkedIn). (323–328)
  *(This block answers "how do you do it" and reads as primarily Jose, but contains a direct
  compliment to the other speaker and first-person "what I usually ask," so the speaker isn't
  cleanly resolvable — treat as uncertain, not persona voice.)*

**Jose Caballer (context only — NOT persona data):**
- Alternative philosophy: build "karmic equity" by delivering value outward (webinars, speaking,
  volunteering, free work) so clients come to you inbound; framing quantitative timeframes on it
  is a mistake. (389–465, 509)
- Example: volunteered ~$100,000 of services to Opportunity Green (logo + speaking + board seat)
  and leveraged it into a ~$500,000 network engagement. (452–465)
- Goal of his recent speaking/educating was to build The Skool and validation, not to sell
  directly. (166–188)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do (persona voice):**

> "Can you afford my services? Because I meet a lot of people who want to hire us and they have
> $500... but for me that's not [enough] and I can't work like that because we have a big system
> overhead here — designers, producers, coordinators, all these kinds of people that we have to
> support." (277–286)

> "You got to get to yes or no fast so you can move on... you got to go for the ask and you got
> to scaffold towards [it]." (473–479)

> "I try to engage people randomly — in the street, anywhere, at a restaurant, in the elevator —
> and just have a conversation with them, because I want to build rapport... I'm not trying to
> close anybody at that point, I just want to learn." (338–362)

> "People are just as afraid of you as you are of them. The reason why you don't talk to people
> is simply because you're afraid of being judged, you're afraid of being thought less than." (373–378)

**Chris Do — role-play (the scaffolded soft-ask; Chris playing the selling designer):**

> "I would love to be able to present some work to you, if this is a relationship that you think
> might be of benefit to your company... because I think we can really help you with your
> branding, your strategic positioning, as well as some of your marketing needs." (585–594)

**Jose Caballer (context — NOT persona voice):**

> "If you build an awesome brand, you want people knocking at the door." (attribution: uncertain — spoken in the inbound-vs-outbound exchange, 146–150)

> "You need to build karmic equity... you can do that simultaneously." (509–511)
