# Social Media, Why Linkedin, Twitter & Instagram Matter to Designers

## Summary

Early live-stream episode from the "The Skool" era (the precursor brand to The Futur),
co-hosted by **Chris Do** ("the business of design") and **Jose Caballer** ("the design
of business"). The episode is a loose, live-audience conversation about why and how
designers should use social media, filmed at Blind with a small in-studio group of
designers (Vincent, Dominique, Orlando, Nick) and producer Nicole.

This is **early (2014) social-media advice** and should be read as an **early position**
that predates The Futur's later, more refined content-strategy doctrine. Some ideas here
(own your name, share your process, play to your strengths, provide value, be
consistent) foreshadow later Chris Do positions, but the platform specifics (Twitter as
the central network, Vine, Tumblr, Instagram "owns your images" debate) are dated to
2014.

**Attribution note:** Chris Do and Jose Caballer speak roughly equally and talk over
each other frequently. Only **Chris Do**'s statements are persona data; **Jose Caballer**
is context (a different person). Several exchanges are conversational banter where
attribution of a specific claim is ambiguous — those are flagged
`attribution: uncertain`.

## Key claims

All dated **2014-09-17**. All paraphrased unless quoted in the next section. Treat all as
**EARLY (2014) positions** that may have evolved in his later content.

**Chris Do:**
- His own entry into heavy social-media use came from an **organic follower-growth
  competition** with colleagues at the office — no mass-following, no buying followers,
  no "douchy" tactics; the process taught him why and how to do social media. (early position)
- **The future belongs to those who can gather an audience around a common interest.** The
  old TV model (produce a show, a station airs it, sell advertising to a demographic) is
  being replaced by a model where the individual is content creator, producer, channel,
  network, and advertiser all in one. Whoever can captivate and control an audience holds
  power. (early position — foreshadows his later personal-brand/audience doctrine)
- Once a show/audience grows to a certain point, advertisers and sponsors follow — an
  audience is inherently valuable to others. (early position)
- **"Nobody's watching you" / "nobody cares"** — people are paralyzed by fear of making a
  mistake, but until you have an audience nobody notices; just put it out, and you can
  delete a bad post. (early position; foreshadows later "just start / volume" advice)
- **Your work is worthless unless someone sees it** and you aggregate an audience around
  it; holding work back to keep it "valuable" is misguided. He points to the show itself
  being given away free as copyrighted content to build audience/value. (early position)
- On the **copyright/ownership fear** (Instagram/Facebook owning your images): the best
  photographers, writers, comedians, artists all use these platforms and see the obvious
  value exchange of growing an audience; refusing on ownership grounds is "ignorance." He
  argues platforms have never meaningfully exploited users' images commercially because
  doing so would trigger a mass boycott/exodus. (early position — specific to 2014
  platform terms)
- **Own your own name online.** If you search his name you find everything he owns; owning
  your name matters, especially with a common name. (early position; persists in later work)
- He researches/looks at a candidate's social media before hiring — cites an example of a
  design-school candidate whose feed was all PBR (beer) and parties, reading that as "not
  paying attention." Be careful what you post because it tells your life story. (early position)
- **Best practice — tailor content per platform**; do not take one piece of content and
  blast it identically across all channels (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, LinkedIn are
  different audiences); that burns people out and they go numb. (early position)
- **LinkedIn**: great for professional/career-advice content and audience; don't post
  what-you-ate photos or be overly self-promotional there or "you'll get burned" — provide
  lots of value. (early position)
- **Share the right times of day**: morning before work (~7–9am) for inspirational content,
  afternoon (~3–4pm) as people leave work, prime time 7–9pm; Sunday mornings/afternoons
  are strong for articles. Think about when you consume content — that's when others do too. (early position)
- **Play to your strengths / use the platform tailored to you** (photographer, visual
  storyteller, comedian, witty/sardonic). Graphic designers are especially suited to
  visual social media because they're good at graphics and typography. (early position)
- **Make it visual**: he used to post plain typographic quotes that "disappeared in the
  feed" as just words; converting them into images (via an app called "Poster," with a
  ph) produced a dramatic increase in shares and likes because we live in a visual space. (early position)
- **Be consistent / it's a marathon, not a sprint** — rhythm matters. Experiment, find
  what works, and let audience response define your brand voice and who you attract. (early position)
- **Share your process** — give people a glimpse into how you work and come up with ideas;
  people enjoy that and get invested. (early position; persists strongly in later work)
- Twitter best practice: **post a good photo of yourself** — people want to know you're a
  real person, not a robot. (early position)
- Don't over-post the same thing (e.g., sunset after sunset from one user "hogging my
  feed") — capture the moment and move on. (early position)

**Jose Caballer (CONTEXT — not persona data):**
- His social-media motivation is **personal brand and narrative** — what value/story
  you're telling; control your own narrative because if you don't, someone else will.
- Motivation is also **efficiency / life-hacking**: rather than answer the same question
  for a small audience, put the answer out to the world once; encourages people to ask
  questions online.
- "**The more you share, the more you get back.**"
- Believes in the 21st century **who you are and what you do are one thing** — rejects a
  separate work Persona vs. personal self; combines personal and professional in his own
  channels.
- Raises "we live in public" as a real historical shift (references a Netflix documentary).
- Points out platform business models are **data** (e.g., selling firehose/"pipeline"-type
  data access and sentiment analysis to corporations).

**In-studio audience (CONTEXT):**
- A designer (Dominique) frames posting small ideas as **focus-group / market testing** —
  put out small ideas cheaply, see which resonate, before investing heavily. Chris and Jose
  affirm this (compared to Kickstarter-style market validation).
- Viewer "David Wang" (chat): "Can you afford NOT to master social media if you want/need
  to work another five-plus years?" — framed by the hosts as a fear-based sales angle.
