# Overcoming Client Objections in Sales

★ L3-candidate — This episode is an early (Skool-era, 2014) origin/seed of several
objection-handling moves Chris Do repeats across his later Futur content: pivoting from
price to value, the opportunity-cost/"what did it cost you" reframe, the car/luxury
analogy for asymmetrical spending logic, breaking a project into a paid Discovery phase,
and "tell the truth / be naked" credibility. Worth promoting if the synthesis pass
covers his objection-handling framework.

## Summary

Skool-era episode (the show was branded "the School," pre-"The Futur") co-hosted by
**Jose Caballer** ("I talk about the design of business") and **Chris Do** ("I talk
about the business of design"). The stated topic is handling sales objections, and the
audience-voted #1 objection is **price** ("it's too expensive").

The bulk of the episode is a **live role-play**: Jose plays a marketing director whose
CEO is a fiscally tight numbers-person; Chris plays the design-firm owner trying to close
a video project quoted at ~$100,000 against a prior $10,000 vendor. They explicitly call
out when they are "role playing." They move through three sub-topics: (1) the value/price
objection, (2) credibility/credentials, and (3) turning a "no" into a project.

## Key claims

_All dated 2014-09-17. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Attributed per speaker._

**Chris Do (persona):**
- Price feels large only in the abstract; shift the conversation off the dollar amount and
  onto the client's business objectives ("let's put the money aside for a second... what
  do you hope the video to do?"). Diffuse the money question immediately.
- Use the "any car gets you A to B" analogy: probe what car the client and CEO drive
  (a BMW M5, a Porsche 911) to expose the **asymmetrical logic** — people happily pay a
  premium for experience in cars, homes, restaurants, but get "sticker shock syndrome"
  on their own marketing.
- Reframe the technical/aesthetic comparison: a business buyer can barely see the quality
  difference designers see, so **you will lose the aesthetic fight every time** — sell the
  video as a means to a business objective, not an aesthetic object.
- Quantify **opportunity cost**: the prior cheap video ran 3-4 months late; tie that delay
  to the client's revenue math (targeting +$2.4M/yr = ~$200k/mo of new business) to show a
  3-month slip potentially cost ~$600k — far more than the fee.
- Handle a hard barrier by **pivoting, not confronting**: "every time I hit a barrier I
  just pivot, I don't want to hit it head on." Don't argue that a lowball budget is an
  insult; table it and dig for the real problem.
- Be willing to walk away: recounts a client who set a $1,500-$3,500 ceiling; Chris knew
  it wasn't business worth taking. "Sometimes you have to be okay with that."
- Position yourself as an advisor to the CEO — "almost like a spiritual advisor to the CEO
  when it comes to building their business"; at a company this size you should get to the
  CEO's table.
- Credibility without infrastructure: if you lack a fancy office, go to them, look sharp,
  wear better clothes ("don't go in a $10 Gap t-shirt... wear a $50 one"). Real content +
  confidence lets you move past the trappings.
- People pay a premium for **assurance/insurance** — you're less likely to screw it up than
  "the kid working out of his basement."
- Tactical credibility moves: **tell the truth** ("we're small, we've never done this
  before, but we're hungry and we'll do everything needed"); nimbleness and passion as
  selling points; give your cell number "call me 24/7"; provide **three references** (past
  clients, email + phone) and pre-warn them; put it all in a tight proposal even for small
  jobs. (Chris notes he makes his proposals "super fancy.")
- Confidence is decisive: if you walk in unsure of who you are, you've already lost the bid.
  "Be totally naked and tell the truth" — admit what you don't know; clients extrapolate a
  person who fronts knowledge into future problems.
- Be yourself, unvarnished — set the expectation that the work will be messy; like-minded
  clients thrive in that. Don't sell a super-polished persona you aren't.
- Turn "no" into diagnosis: when a no is soft ("not right now"), surface the real blockers
  (boss/approver above them, budget discomfort, unclear deliverables, too busy, a tempting
  competitor offer) and address them.
- **Break the project into a paid Discovery phase** to de-risk: instead of $100k up front,
  do a ~$20k (two payments of $10k) two-day Discovery/strategy engagement so the client and
  their boss can see the value and manage risk. Segment the project to get enrolled.
- Claims a real analogous engagement: walked in with **every stakeholder objection pre-
  answered** in a prepared presentation and closed a **$150,000** job; then managed
  delivery with live feedback inside sprint cycles.
- Believes "you get what you pay for."
- Whole approach summarized as "mental Jiu-Jitsu": bring the person onto your team, listen,
  stay confident, solve the problem rather than staying rigid.

**Jose Caballer (context / co-host):**
- Reports price was the #1 tweeted objection; frames his own growth over ~a year working
  with Chris around confidence and "getting to the point."
- Credibility via team: when he launched "the group," he positioned it as a collective of
  top UX/consulting talent and **brought the actual team to the pitch** (e.g., for an Art
  Center College of Design pitch) despite having no office (worked from a loft).
- Advocates **assembling an "All-Star team"** to nullify a competitor's specific advantage
  (e.g., recruit a famous coder to neutralize a rival's known dev strength); reach experts
  directly ("just be very clear about the ask"), the "impenetrable wall" is a perception.
- In role-play, voices the fiscally-tight-CEO framing and the "we better get serious
  service" objection; also plays the referred-but-unconvinced client.

**Shared / uncertain attribution (rapid role-swap):**
- The specific numeric revenue math in the role-play (revenue ~$2.5-3M, +$2.4M target,
  ~$200k/mo, $600k opportunity cost) is co-constructed live; the driving logic is Chris's
  but individual numbers are traded between both. `attribution: uncertain` on exact figures.
- "Switch price to value" ($100k is a lot, but $100k for a vintage Gullwing Mercedes is
