# How to Get Clients - Lead Generation & Close Sales

## Summary

Early Skool-era episode (Sep 2014), co-hosted by **Chris Do** and **Jose Caballer**.
The format is a loose two-person conversation answering audience questions (Luis Moreno,
Archie) about the full sales flow: lead generation → closing → delivery → follow-up.

The two hosts split the roles by their real strengths, and this framing matters for
attribution. **Jose Caballer** positions himself as the front-end / "warming leads"
person and owns the **lead-generation** framework (referrals, nonprofits, blogging,
speaking at conferences). **Chris Do** — described in the video as having run his studio
(Blind) for ~18 years with sales reps who did lead gen for him, so he mostly "just
closed" — owns the **closing, contracts/pricing, delivery, and follow-up** material.

Only Chris-attributed statements are persona data for the clone. Jose's lead-gen
framework and his "core strategy / The Group" onboarding material are **context only**.
The transcript is loosely structured with a running weed/joke bit and several garbled
passages, so a few lines are marked `attribution: uncertain`.

## Key claims

_All dated 2014-09-17. Paraphrased unless in the verbatim section. Speaker attributed._

**Chris Do (persona data):**
- Chris uses a simple contract based on **deliverables** as a **fixed bid**, not hourly,
  because he prefers **value-based pricing**. (Chris)
- Chris will charge two clients **different prices for the same work** because the value
  is the same but each company's ability to pay differs; a multi-billion-dollar company
  pays more (partly for the assurance the work won't be botched), a small mom-and-pop
  under ~$5M revenue pays less. (Chris)
- Chris frames this pricing-by-company-size as ethical, citing that the Graphic Artists
  Guild's pricing/ethical standards guide uses **revenue-relative pricing brackets**. (Chris)
- Closing is about identifying the client's **pain points / stumbling blocks** — wrong
  timing, price too high, worry about deliverables — then figuring out how to move it
  along; sometimes you just leave it alone because they're not ready to buy. (Chris)
- Direct-client lead cycles (smaller companies, no rep involved) can take **6–9 months**
  to close; Chris calendars follow-ups so as not to become annoying. (Chris)
- On confidence as the core of closing: the moment a client "smells" doubt, they're out;
  Chris says he's **never had doubt** because of how he was raised — "unconditional faith
  in the ability for the universe to provide." (Chris)
- "The customer is always right" is false and a road to disaster — clients came to you
  for your **expertise**; over-prescriptive client direction should be pushed back on. (Chris)
- Chris's rule for handling a client who insists on a cheaper/wrong approach: don't say
  "no," instead lay out the consequence plus alternatives (do it in CG for X, or pay to
  rent the right equipment). Illustrated with a **car-commercial** shoot where the client
  wouldn't pay for a techno crane, the shot came out unusable, and Chris's team had to
  rebuild the car in CG and re-track/stabilize the shot. (Chris)
- Chris's delivery philosophy: **move the friction/conflict up front** into the contract
  and negotiation so the rest of the engagement is just executing against it, aiming for
  a **frictionless** client experience. (Chris)
- Follow-up after a project: send a **personal handwritten note** and a small, tailored
  token of thanks (learned from paying attention to the client's tastes). Keep gifts
  small because many corporate clients have **anti-bribery policies** (can't exceed ~$20);
  Chris likes ~$15 gift baskets, and cited gifting a wasabi-loving client wasabi peas from
  an Asian market. Startups without such policies can get fancier gifts. (Chris)
- Testimonials/referrals: Chris's executive producers get complimentary client emails;
  at some point he asks permission to use them as testimonials. (Chris)

**Jose Caballer (context only):**
- ~95% of sales should come from **referrals**; the other ~5% (for growth / winning the
  clients you want) requires proactively hunting — a friend, Aaron Ross, calls these
  "Spears." (Jose)
- You must explicitly **ask existing clients for referrals** — people don't think to
  refer you unprompted. A soft script: "we're expanding and can take on a few more
  clients; if you know anyone who could use our services I'd be happy for a referral." (Jose)
- Jose's three lead-gen moves: (1) get involved in your community / a **nonprofit board**;
  (2) **blog** about your industry and its problems (not about how awesome you are) — he
  cites Hootsuite auto-reposting; (3) prove expertise by **speaking at conferences** —
  submit topics, expect rejections, take speaking classes / Toastmasters. (Jose)
- Recommends the book **"Guerrilla Marketing for Consultants"** as a lead-gen workbook. (Jose)
- Investing in a **documented delivery process / blueprint** builds client confidence;
  freestyling delivery leads to resentment, ghosting the client, and damaged reputation. (Jose)
- Onboarding at "The Group" was a 1–2 day **"core strategy" kickoff** run like a mini
  strategic retreat to set the tone/bond with the client. (Jose)
- "It's always your fault, never the client's — you took the job." (attribution: uncertain — back-and-forth, likely Jose)
- "The sales process doesn't end at the contract, it begins at the contract" — sales
  continues through delivery. (attribution: uncertain — likely Jose as the front-line seller)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do (persona voice):**

> "I like to do value based pricing, and so I'm usually figuring out the client's comfort range in terms of budget and what market value is. So I could possibly be doing the same amount of work for two different clients and charge them two very different prices, even though to me the value is the same." (Chris)

> "That doesn't mean that the customer is always right, because they're not... it came to you for your expertise, and sometimes you kind of have to look at what the objectives are." (Chris)

> "You don't need to say no. You can say, if we do it your way and it goes wrong — and we believe it will — there are two other options here: we'll do it in CG, it'll cost this much, or you can just pay up front and rent the right kind of equipment so we can do it the right way." (Chris)

> "What we do is we move the friction, the conflict, up front. We work it out in the contracts, in the negotiation process, so they know what we're getting into, and the rest of it is just we're executing against that." (Chris)

> "I've never had doubt. I've always had faith that whatever I set to do I will do... that's how I was brought up — complete unconditional faith in the ability for the universe to provide." (Chris)

> "A personal note goes a long way — the good old-fashioned analog. It's not about the size and the scale or how much money you spend, but just to genuinely thank them." (Chris)

**Jose Caballer (context):**

> "95% of your sales should come from referrals... the other 5% in growing your business or getting the clients that you want requires you to go out there and find those clients and hunt them. A friend, Aaron Ross, calls it Spears." (Jose)

> "Blog about what you do — not about your growth and how awesome you are — about your industry and the problems." (Jose)
