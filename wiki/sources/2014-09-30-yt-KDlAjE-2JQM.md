# Confidence, Culture, Gender Roles around Money

## Summary

Early **Skool era** livestream ("The Skool Live") co-hosted by **Chris Do** ("the business of design") and **Jose Caballer** ("the design of business"), with **guest Rocio Villalobos** (founder of Vidro Media, transitioning to a new "conscious business" agency, Shift Co-Created). The three discuss money through the lenses of confidence, culture, and gender roles. The framing is loose and improvised: Rocio drives a "male vs. female energy" thesis about money, Jose contributes a personal-development / "money as emotion" angle, and Chris contributes value-based, confidence-rooted views drawn from his own upbringing. The back half is an improvised negotiation role-play in which each co-host plays a designer talking price with a client.

## Key claims

_All dated 2014-09-30. Paraphrased unless quoted. Attributed per speaker._

**Chris Do (persona data):**
- Chris frames money as a means to an end, not a goal in itself — he is motivated not by money but by what it allows: reaching a point where he can spend his leisure time (and make leisure his professional time) with his wife and two young sons, as fast as possible. (Chris)
- Chris's background is that of a refugee: his family lost everything — country, language, culture — "in a night," and had to rebuild in America. (Chris)
- In his (Asian immigrant) upbringing the focus was not money per se but **education as empowerment** — "your ticket out," something nobody can take from you. (Chris)
- Chris's father modeled extreme work ethic: went from busboy (couldn't speak English, so he cleared tables) to chief engineer at a semiconductor / computer company within ~20 years; worked from 5am, never took vacations or sick days. (Chris)
- His father instilled "work hard now, work a lot less later" — compress the intense work into youth, then enjoy the rest, rather than pacing it out. Chris attributes his intensity and singular focus to this. (Chris)
- Talking about money is, for Chris, "a reflection of how I feel about myself"; because he feels confident in the value he provides, he refuses to undervalue himself. (Chris)
- When bidding, Chris's internal method is to ask for the maximum he can request "that this person won't laugh at me for," anchored on the value he believes he provides. (Chris)
- Early in freelancing he told clients "pay me whatever you think is fair," never worried about the money, trusting that once inside he'd prove himself and the money conversation would resolve naturally. His paradox: "the more I ran away from money, the more money came after me." (Chris)
- He once took roughly a 70% pay cut to pursue the right opportunity over a higher first offer, because the decision was not about money. (Chris)
- On self-worth (advice given to his wife): you must love yourself more than anyone else does; relying on someone else to control your happiness (external orientation) puts too much on the other person. (Chris)
- Chris cites **Oprah** as a formative example on money: she never set out to be a billionaire but to help a lot of people, and the money followed the value/help. (Chris)
- Chris's closing takeaway (note the deliberate contrast with Jose): don't use money to validate *who you are* or your value; use money to validate your *idea* — whether the concept is marketable. If you're pursuing a passion and don't make money, it isn't sustainable — "otherwise it's a hobby." So he argues to *focus* on the money, but as a test of the idea, not of self-worth. (Chris)
- In the negotiation role-play, Chris (playing the businessperson/agency) demonstrates: fishing for the client's budget by quoting a wide range (e.g. "$50k–$150k") and reading the client's reaction, "taking the temperature" on price before building an estimate; reframing a cheap "reskin" request toward strategy and a paid discovery phase; and "time is money" / do-it-once framing. (Chris)

**Jose Caballer (context):**
- Jose grew up lower-middle-class Latino, immigrated at ~11–12; money was scarce, and he saw it as something other people had that he wanted. (Jose)
- To build long-term success and bigger companies, he argues you must strip the *emotional* aspect out of money; emotions are heavily attached to it (e.g. inherited attitudes from parents/culture). (Jose)
- Money is "just a resource," like anything else — you're not taking it away from anybody. (Jose)
- He describes personal-development work — a "clearing," talking to your 13-year-old self to reprogram early money perceptions (Chris frames the same idea as "talking to your inner child" / a therapist's approach). (Jose)
- Advocates a "North Star" (defined goal / what keeps you true) and having a business coach; wishes he'd hired one ten years earlier. (Jose)
- Jose's closing takeaway (contrasting Chris): money "doesn't actually exist, it's just paper"; focus on delivering value and the money comes — you know you've delivered value because people are willing to pay for it. (Jose)

**Rocio Villalobos (guest, context):**
- Runs Vidro Media, transitioning to Shift Co-Created, a "conscious business" serving conscious brands; co-produced a women's conference ("Together") on collaboration and conscious business. (Rocio)
- Central thesis: money attitudes split along **"male vs. female energy,"** not literally man vs. woman — artists tend toward the "female" (heart, fairness, empathy, passion), which can undercut confidence and pricing. (Rocio)
- "Female-energy" challenges around money: perfectionism, feeling "not good enough," undervaluing oneself, undercharging, and guilt about charging. "Male-energy" traits: ego-driven, competitive, "conqueror." (Rocio)
- Her background (explicitly *not* Chris's): grew up in Latin America, Peruvian father, American mother; lost her father at age five; her mother, a teacher, became the breadwinner, modeling both "male and female energy." (Rocio)
- Remedy for low money-confidence: self-development — identify your genuine talents/value and believe in them while working on weaknesses; the learning itself builds confidence. Advice to women designers: know your strengths, work on weak spots, have more open conversations with colleagues, find a mentor or coach, and "get out of your own way." (Rocio)
