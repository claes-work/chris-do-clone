## Summary

"The Skool"-era live show (Chris Do + co-host **Jose Caballer**) interviewing guest
**Mark Fidelman** — CEO of Rainforest, a Forbes.com columnist, and author of the book
*Socialized!* ("how the most successful businesses harness the power of social").
Titled "How to Use Social Media to Grow your Business w/ Mark Fidelman." The episode
also announces a show rebrand: from **"The Skool Live"** to **"The Process"** ("that's
what we focus on here... you got to trust the process"), still on the Skool Network.
Audience questions come from **Nicole** (freelancer angle) and **Boise/Boy** (video
question).

**Attribution note:** Mark Fidelman is a social-media/marketing guest — **his tactics
are CONTEXT, not persona**. Only **Chris Do**-attributed statements are persona
material; Jose and Mark are not. The caption transcript has **no speaker labels** and
is garbled in places, so several exchanges are marked uncertain. These are **early
(2015) views on social media** and may be dated (platforms, tactics, and the freelance
marketplaces named have since changed).

**Who leads:** Mark supplies the substantive framework (the bulk of the marketing
content). Jose drives interview structure and framing. Chris contributes designer-lens
reactions and, in the back half, a substantial monologue on "sharing your process" that
is the strongest persona material in the episode.

## Key claims

Guest — Mark Fidelman (CONTEXT, not persona):

- (2015-02-11, Fidelman) The takeaway of *Socialized!*: if your customers are on social
  media, become a "social media powerhouse" — a publishing/media powerhouse — by
  leveraging content creation and influencers, not just posting on channels.
- (2015-02-11, Fidelman) [EARLY-2015 VIEW] Social's power is that channels which used to
  cost money (newspapers, magazines, TV, radio) are now free and can reach a very niche,
  targeted audience — "there's never been a more affordable way of reaching those
  customers."
- (2015-02-11, Fidelman) Because it's free, you compete with millions; you rise above
  the noise by being the best storyteller and best designer — "become the best
  Storyteller and best designer and you'll rise above the noise."
- (2015-02-11, Fidelman) Use a "voice of the customer" exercise (~20 questions) to learn
  what customers want, then produce educational or entertaining content tied (indirectly)
  to your product; build email capture into every campaign for remarketing.
- (2015-02-11, Fidelman) To grow a YouTube channel: first have a good product; then reach
  out to influential channels in your space for cross-promotion ("probably the fastest
  way to grow"); then ask subscribers what content they want.
- (2015-02-11, Fidelman) The biggest obstacle to becoming a social-media company is
  culture: executives were rewarded for hoarding information (power), and culture change
  must be top-down — bottom-up efforts rarely work without an executive sponsor.
- (2015-02-11, Fidelman) [EARLY-2015 VIEW] Most effective content formats (for him):
  ebooks (15–20 pp., educational, capture email) and infographics (design-forward, more
  digestible than an article). Notes his own list was "almost two years old" and would
  update it.
- (2015-02-11, Fidelman) Video is "the future" — every company will run its own studio
  and become its own media publisher — but quality video is too hard for most companies
  to produce well right now.
- (2015-02-11, Fidelman) [EARLY-2015 VIEW] For personal branding, Gary Vaynerchuk is the
  ideal; there are "four types of personal brands" (from his Udemy course); you need a
  purpose plus consistency, and the goal is to educate or entertain your followers.
- (2015-02-11, Fidelman) [EARLY-2015 VIEW] Freelancer advice: use marketplaces (oDesk,
  Elance, freelancer.com) for leads; build a portfolio site highlighting notable/campaign
  work; build reputation where marketers are — Twitter and Pinterest.

Host — Chris Do (persona):

- (2015-02-11, Chris) His Art Center graduation speech in 1996 argued digital would beat
  print — and "I think we're now a little bit past that." (Self-reported biographical
  detail: attended Art Center [College of Design], graduated ~1996.)
- (2015-02-11, Chris) Creative-culture obstacle to self-promotion: creatives don't share
  work in progress because they chase perfection, and even students hide portfolios for
  fear of being judged — "your idea of what you can do is always greater than what you're
  capable of doing physically," so you have to let that go.
- (2015-02-11, Chris) "Sharing your process" is a high-ROI move: it enrolls your audience
  and future clients by showing how you work, so buyers know what they're getting rather
  than seeing only a finished product they can't reverse-engineer.
- (2015-02-11, Chris) A designer's audience should be the highest-possible influencer in
  their client base — go all the way up to creative directors and CEOs, especially at
  design-savvy startups now hiring designers internally rather than only through agencies.
- (2015-02-11, Chris) Final thoughts / his own social-media takeaways: be transparent and
  comfortable being seen as imperfect; enroll people in your process; provide value by
  entertaining or educating; video is the future; and always do a value exchange — ask
  for an email so you can have a real conversation with genuinely connected people.

Host — Jose Caballer (NOT persona):
