# Identity & Web Design — Trojan Storage Branding Case Study — @thefutur (2015-03-24)

★ L3-candidate — adds a distinct end-to-end client-identity *process* (user profiling via
CORE, "mind walking" empathy map, six-category brand definition, stylescapes review,
fun↔functional sweet-spot, and the "straight line" project-management model) not yet
captured in [[wiki/topics/branding/branding]].

"The Skool" era episode of *The Process* co-hosted by **Chris Do** ("Christo") and
**Jose Caballer**. Chris walks through a real client engagement from his design studio
[[wiki/entities/blind]] — the identity redesign and website rebuild for **Trojan Storage**,
a self-storage company founded by USC business grads. Only Chris-Do-attributed statements
are persona training data; Jose Caballer is a context co-host (he ran the CORE framework
session and wrote much of the copy); unclear speakers are flagged.

## Summary

Answering three recurring viewer questions — (1) how do you determine a project's design
style, (2) how do you determine voice/messaging, (3) how do you manage the client and
team — Chris narrates the Trojan Storage identity + web project end to end. He frames the
whole thing as "turning on the light": rather than designing on intuition in the dark, he
runs a facilitated live session with the client using Jose's **CORE framework** to define
the users first. He profiles three customer types by demographics and psychographics, does
a **"mind walking"** empathy exercise (what the customer thinks/hears/does/feels/says/sees),
and surfaces non-obvious insights (over 90% of Trojan's clientele is female, many single
moms in life transition, lower-middle-class, moving-as-stress). Those insights drive
requirements: safe/secure, kid-friendly, simple and intuitive.

He then defines the brand across six categories (borrowed from Marty Neumeier), writes a
positioning statement *with* the client in the room, runs a competitive audit (Public
Storage, Extra Space), and evolves the logo — a Trojan-crimson padlock — into an abstract
solid structure that doubles as a lock. He introduces process concepts: **scaffolding**
(small steps toward the goal), the **fit check** (validating direction early so the client
is never surprised), **stylescapes** (typography-driven mood boards presented one at a time
for consensus feedback), and a **fun↔functional spectrum** used to find the client's "sweet
spot." Copywriting (Jose-led, but Chris contributes) flows from the voice: "smile, your
belongings are on TV," "less stress for your money," "lights, camera, safety." For project
management he invokes the **straight-line system** (borrowed from Jordan Belfort/*Wolf of
Wall Street*) — keep designers, developers, and clients "boxed in" so iteration doesn't spin
out — and argues the *designer* (not a marketer) should write the objective/brief because
only they see it from concept to execution. The engagement snowballs from a logo+site
facelift into office paint, signage, interior design, and even architectural design work.
He closes with beginner advice: educate your design palette, stop using junky fonts (he
shares a curated font list), curate stock photography, and "hire A+ players — better than
you."

## Key claims

- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) The single biggest lever for determining design style is knowing
  *who* you are building for. Without a framework you are "figuratively bumping around in
  the dark" using only intuition and training, alone and stressed; defining the user makes
  the job "exponentially easier."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) A designer must have **empathy and imagination** — "you have to
  walk in their shoes, look at the world through their eyes." This is the prerequisite for
  both design and copy.
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) His user-profiling method (a stripped-down "made for TV" edit of
  Jose's CORE framework): write a short story for each primary customer type — a "Central
  Casting" exercise covering demographics (single/married, male/female, kids, age, job,
  education, income) and psychographics (opinions, attitudes, behaviors) — then derive
  their wants and needs. They time-boxed three user profiles at ~15 minutes each, live,
  with the client in the room, and prioritized the top three needs per user.
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) **"Mind walking"** empathy exercise: map what the customer is
  thinking, hearing, doing, feeling, saying, and seeing; each becomes a prompt (an empathy
  map in all but name).
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Doing user definition *with the client* surfaces truths you could
  never find by searching online — for Trojan Storage: over 90% of clientele is female,
  many are single moms, most are "in transition" (divorce, empty nesters, expanding
  families, widowers) and lower-middle-class; "moving is one of the most stressful things
  you can do in your life."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Those insights convert directly to design requirements: the brand
  must *feel and look* safe and secure (backed by real operations — well-lit, cameras,
  professionally trained staff — "not a mom-and-pop operation"), be kid-friendly (customers
  arrive with kids between soccer and school), and be simple and intuitive: "you have to
  work hard at making it simple."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) He defines the brand across **six categories** — culture,
  customer, voice, feel, impact, and X-Factor — which "mutate depending on understanding."
  He credits the approach to **Marty Neumeier**: pull keywords, plug them in "because it's a
  lot easier to write."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Brand-writing process: start broad and casual, pull out words
  without worrying how they form sentences, "not too self-aware," then write one sentence
  at a time. He names this **"scaffolding"** — taking a small step toward the final goal
  where every step advances it — and credits the term to **Dr. Holtzman**.
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do / Jose Caballer) The positioning statement was written *by the
  client* in the facilitated session (Jose read it out; Chris "just filled it in and
  adjusted"): "Trojan Storage provides storage solutions to local communities in a warm,
  professional and friendly environment while helping them feel secure and giving them peace
  of mind." Chris: entering the session he knew "zero about the client," and left knowing
  their customers, brand, and goals — "more than I could have found out even from getting a
  brief from them."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Competitive audit: quickly research competitors online (Public
  Storage as the national giant, Extra Space as a clean/modern site). Insight: competitor
  sites are dense/cluttered, which fails the time-poor single-mom user; "the more
  sophisticated business operations tend to have better execution of their communications,"
  so better-run/bigger-budget firms communicate better — and a designer can hand a small
  client that same leverage in how people perceive them.
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Logo evolution: Trojan's existing mark used USC crimson + goldenrod
  and a literal padlock, "not totally artfully designed or integrated." The three key words
  from the session — **safe, strong, secure** — drove exploration; he deliberately uses
  "very visual" words, not big abstract ones. Iterations explored structures (boxes,
  overhangs, rollup doors, wedge shapes) and landed on an abstract solid structure that
  *also* reads as a lock, preserving existing brand equity in a more artful, integrated way;
  it flexes to a horizontal configuration and stays modern.
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) **Fit check**: validate the design direction early ("before we go
  too far") so you never surprise the client — and, more importantly, because the client
  reveals valuable reactions you didn't anticipate that guide the process.
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Client relationship stance: "I'm not a vendor, they're not a
  client — we are partners" building the thing together. Bring the client onto the team.
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do / Jose Caballer) The **design brief** for the designers packaged:
  user summaries, brand attributes + brand statement, the competitive audit, the
  communication/revenue prioritization, and a per-designer direction for a stylescape.
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) The client (Brett) volunteered that Trojan has "over a million
  square feet of walls doing nothing" — a cheap canvas for typographic graphics — which
  seeded the whole typography-on-walls direction. (Chris's joke: "putting it on is cheap,
  but I'm not cheap.")
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do / Jose Caballer) **Stylescapes** are typography/mood explorations
  that deliberately *don't look like websites*. They were presented one at a time (not side
  by side); for each, every client stakeholder was asked what they liked and disliked —
  "getting a temperature read" — to build consensus, then a final consolidated stylescape
  incorporating all feedback was shown. Clients were pre-briefed the week before with an
  example stylescape so they knew what to expect.
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) He presented the stylescapes **neutrally** — "I have no horse in
  the game" — deliberately *not* "leading the witness," which he notes is the traditional
  design instinct (sell your idea). The client independently chose the top logo. (Jose
  probes this repeatedly; Chris insists on neutrality while admitting he did privately
  favor options.)
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Messaging flows from voice: because they had defined the culture
  (fun, friendly, flexible, giving, happy) and the user, copy "almost writes itself" — but
  still requires empathy and imagination. Examples map lines to brand attributes: voice =
  attentive/flexible → "you asked, we listened / open 365 days"; voice = professional →
  "white glove service / exceptionally high standards"; feel/secure → "overprotective, you
  bet"; impact → "we're awake when you're asleep."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) `[self-reported]` "I am not a copywriter, I went to design school"
  — yet the branding process (mind walking, seeing through the user's eyes) plus being "a
  really logical person" let him produce the copy. Jose credits it as "Spock doing copy."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Filter principle: "the world is full of stimulation... but when you
  have a very selective filter, a target to hit, only the things that make sense fit within
  that filter."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) **Fun ↔ functional spectrum**: when abundant client feedback
  threatens to "send you into a tailspin," plot the extremes — a fun/quirky version (Jose's
  personality) on one end, a bold/boxed-up Swiss version (Chris's) on the other. The client
  wanted neither; the "sweet spot" sits in the middle. Defining the edges lets you "walk
  your way in."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) **Project management via the "straight line system"**: the ideal
  path from brief to delivery is a straight line, but reality zig-zags. Put "a ceiling and a
  basement" on the process to rein it in — designers, developers, and clients are all
  powerful, but you must keep them "boxed in" so iterations stay within the line and don't
  "go bananas." He credits the straight-line idea to **Jordan Belfort** (*The Wolf of Wall
  Street*), who used it for sales.
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) The **designer should write the objective/brief**, not a marketer
  or strategist: only the designer sees it "from beginning to end," so a marketer's brief
  creates a "chasm" — words that feel good but "don't mean anything" and can't be executed.
  The objective goal must be visual, clear, and written by someone who understands design.
  (Jose pushes back that it can be done collaboratively in the room with the marketer; Chris
  concedes collaboration works in a facilitated session but maintains the designer should
  lead "because I'm a principal.")
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Consultant stance: don't "lead the witness" or "redefine the
  problem as something they don't need." Help the client build what they actually need,
  fact-check them, and bring a better solution only if you genuinely have one — "everybody
  just wants to be heard and understood." He calls it "judo" and "the designer as
  consultant."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) `[self-reported]` Business outcome / land-and-expand: the project
  began as *only* a logo redesign and site facelift and grew into a full relationship —
  office repaint, plexiglass signage, exterior signage (Sacramento building), interior
  design and 3D renders (Minneapolis, other spaces), and even architectural *design* (the
  client offered to hire Chris as architect and pair him with a licensed architect/engineer
  for execution). Reason: blind moves faster than the client's architects because "we're
  used to working in commercials and digital production — we can render images." The client's
  unprompted praise: "you've somehow been able to crawl inside my brain and deliver
  something I was unable to describe... this is the brand I always hoped for." Chris: earning
  that unprompted feeling is "extremely valuable."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) The final site was intentionally minimal (site map by "Purple Pen
  Productions" / Natalie): find storage, location, price, differentiation — "deliver on the
  parts people need to use." The color palette was expanded beyond USC red/yellow (adding
  grays and blues) because red and yellow were "too dominating."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Beginner advice for reaching this quality without a fancy art
  school: (1) **educate your design palette** — collect great reference, find a mentor to
  explain why work is good/bad; (2) **stop using junky fonts** — he shares a curated
  Sans/Serif font list "just use any one of these and you'll be fine," recommending the
  *I Love Type* series (six books, one typeface each — Futura, Bodoni, DIN, etc.); (3)
  **curate stock photography** — good stock has beautiful lighting, the subject not looking
  at camera, no overt expressions, a candid (not staged) feel; (4) **hire A+ players,
  better than you** (credited to Guy Kawasaki) — "do not be intimidated... your client
  won't know or care who you hired, and you'll have access to better clients."
- (2015-03-24, Chris Do) Fonts "say a lot about the personality and voice of the brand...
  they affect the messaging."
