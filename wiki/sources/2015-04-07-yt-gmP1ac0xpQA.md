# How to Design for Startups (The Process)

## Summary

An early "The Process" episode from The Futur's Skool era, hosted by **Jose Caballer**
(lead presenter) with **Chris Do** as co-host/interlocutor. Jose carries the bulk of the
teaching; Chris reacts, questions, summarizes, and adds framing. The episode is a primer
for designers on serving startup clients, structured around three questions Jose poses:
(1) what types of startups exist, (2) how a designer reaches them, and (3) how a designer
delivers to each type.

Jose maps a startup lifecycle — prefunding → funded → mature → legacy → internal
"initiatives" inside legacy companies — and walks through real case studies from his own
work (Huku, One Drop, Scratch-it, a Lucky-Magazine-style shopping startup, Ford Models
media portal, MySpace redesign, a Sony/IO advertising project, and a Scripps recipe R&D
concept). For each lifecycle stage he prescribes what a designer can sell: pitch/investor
deck design, brand and product definition via the "CORE" strategic framework, identity
design, responsive product design, iteration facilitation, and executive alignment. The
recurring thesis (voiced most emphatically by Chris toward the end): startups and startup
culture are "the future" of how work gets done, and legacy companies are increasingly
hiring designers to help them become design-driven. Recommended references: Paul Graham,
*The Lean Startup* (Eric Ries), and the documentary *The Startup Kids*.

## Key claims

> Attribution note: Jose Caballer is the primary presenter and owner of nearly all the
> substantive design-business content and case studies here (marked **[Jose / context]**).
> Chris Do's contributions are reactions, questions, summaries, and framing
> (marked **[Chris Do / persona]**). Where the dialogue does not clearly assign a line to
> one speaker, it is marked **[attribution: uncertain]**.

- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) A startup is defined (via Wikipedia/Paul Graham) as a
  company designed to *search* for a repeatable, scalable business model, built for fast
  growth — not necessarily tech and not necessarily venture-funded; growth is the point.
- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) Startups sit on a lifecycle continuum:
  prefunding/bootstrapped → funded (seed of ~$250k–millions) → mature (exited/IPO) →
  legacy (old tech companies like Apple, Microsoft, SAP, Sun); "initiatives" are
  startup-like projects run inside legacy companies.
- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) Rough financials cited: a startup might begin with ~$250k
  seed; a "mom and pop" might gross ~$300k/year and take home $70–80k; enterprise is
  ~$3–5M; ~80% of US business is small business under ~$500k.
- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) To reach startups, designers should leave the studio and
  attend meetups, Startup Weekends, and conferences (e.g. Digital LA), speak at events,
  and run webinars — an inbound-marketing motion, since tech founders (post-Apple) already
  know they need good design.
- [Chris Do / persona] (2015-04-07) Devil's-advocate objection: the "establish yourself as
  an expert / speak" advice fails if you are not yet an expert or not in the tech space.
  Resolved (by Jose, Chris summarizing) as: play to your existing strengths — a designer
  expert in grid systems and logos already has what funded startups need (they need a
  logo, they need a grid).
- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) Agencies can run a "resident entrepreneur" program —
  host a startup in your office to embed in their community; Jose is himself a resident
  entrepreneur and digital strategist at Blind.
- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) Delivery by stage: **prefunding** → help them get funded
  (investor/pitch deck, define brand/customers/objectives, design identity and product);
  **funded** → communicate vision to stakeholders, prioritize/manage iterations, refine
  brand and product; **legacy** → reinvigorate brand/product/service and deliver a
  strategic framework, translating value to the CMO in ROI terms; **initiatives** →
  provide a process framework and get tech/creative/business/marketing stakeholders to
  agree by putting everyone in one room.
- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) Designers are "really good at telling stories" — helping
  with the deck's narrative (not just the graphics) makes the designer far more valuable,
  but requires being strategic and understanding the business.
- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) The "CORE" strategic framework (user profiles, brand
  attributes, objectives, priorities) is a living document: assumptions/hypotheses about
  users must be re-tested in the market and refined; user profiles were redone repeatedly
  as real market data and industry-savvy team members arrived. "There is no such thing as
  set it and forget it."
- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) Practical speed claims: a startup homepage/sales page was
  sketched with the CEO and designed overnight, reviewed and iterated the next morning;
  Scratch-it went from CORE to stylescape in a 5-day period. Jose designed responsive and
  desktop versions side-by-side using Illustrator templates, and recommends Sketch as a
  better, self-scaling tool.
- [Chris Do / persona] (2015-04-07) Chris is described (by Jose) as "a little bit skeptical
  about agile." Jose's caveat: agile is made for a team; with a small team and no separate
  doers it doesn't work well — the best version locks the whole team in one room for a
  one-week sprint.
- [attribution: uncertain] (2015-04-07) A designer/agency brought into a mature or legacy
  company often acts "almost as a shrink" — the real trigger is that executives/
  stakeholders cannot get alignment (e.g. City Search vs. Yelp pressure; MySpace's
  make-it-work-or-sell mandate), so the outside designer is hired to make it look good
  *and* to mitigate differences between stakeholders, not just to make it pretty.
- [Chris Do / persona] (2015-04-07) Framing/thesis: legacy companies are now asking
  designers "to come help them become design driven" and work in a fluid way — evidence
  the culture has already tipped past the mainstream (referencing HBO's *Silicon Valley*).
- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) Final thoughts: "startups are hot," they are "the future"
  of the workplace and companies, big companies emulate them, and *The Lean Startup*
  (build → measure → learn, i.e. the scientific method applied to product) is the one book
  every designer should read; *The Startup Kids* documentary is a recommended primer.
- [Jose / context] (2015-04-07) Roughly nine out of ten startups don't make it; case in
  point Huku (founder Eric Tong) didn't pursue the product but the founder went on to
  build Tech Armor, a top-selling Amazon phone-accessory company.
