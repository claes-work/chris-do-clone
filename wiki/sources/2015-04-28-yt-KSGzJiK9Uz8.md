# How to Position a Brand ★ L3-candidate

> Early "The Process" show (Skool/pre-Futur era, 2015) co-hosted by **Chris Do** and
> **Jose Caballer**. Both speak throughout; only **Chris Do** trains the persona. Jose
> is context (co-host). Source: [[../../raw/youtube/thefutur/2015-04-28-KSGzJiK9Uz8.en.txt]].

## Summary

Audience-question episode answering "how do I position myself and my design practice?"
Chris Do (with co-host Jose Caballer) builds a positioning method through case studies
(Coca-Cola, Apple, Virgin America, Toyota/Lexus/Scion) and then a personal-brand
framework: a three-circle Venn diagram (what you **love** / what you're **good at** /
what **pays well**), whose center is the "WIN." Core mandate: **pick one thing**, claim
expertise, align it with a client who values it. Chris grounds the "pick one" argument
in the psychology of memory (the "mental box" — people only remember the top of each
category). Second half is tactical: profile your ideal clients, **match and mirror**
their look/tone/vocabulary, gain access via professional circles / trade shows /
business journals, scale down the "supply chain" to reachable clients, and "**say it
and stick to it**" — make the claim before you can fully back it up, then build the
proof over time. Explicitly cites the book *Positioning: The Battle for the Mind*
(Ries & Trout) and attributes framing to Marty Neumeier ("Marty Newman") and Tony Robbins.

## Key claims

- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** Positioning is why the same can of Coca-Cola sells for ~$0.54
  at a supermarket but ~$1.50 at a beach or amusement park — a ~300% price increase for
  the identical product — driven by scarcity, demand, and convenience of location.
  *(paraphrase; his opening illustration of positioning = price + context.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** Apple positions on price via a deliberate 3-tier product
  lineup (e.g. iMacs at $1,799 / $1,999 / $2,499); the high-priced option "defends" the
  middle one, since most people don't see themselves needing the most expensive version.
  *(paraphrase — pricing/anchoring strategy.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** Apple's "Think Different" campaign (Steve Jobs's return)
  repositioned the brand around identity — aligning with rebels/innovators/out-of-the-box
  thinkers — rather than product features or price. *(paraphrase.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Jose, context)** Virgin America is positioned as "Southwest for cooler
  people" — a proven low-cost business model with a redefined delivery/design experience
  targeting young, urban, tech-savvy style-conscious consumers. *(paraphrase — co-host.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** Toyota built Lexus (up-market) and Scion (younger buyers) as
  separate brands on shared platforms/parts but drastically different price points, to
  align each brand with a different customer. *(paraphrase.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** A designer can claim many skills (packaging, typography,
  identity, branding, UX/UI) but from a positioning standpoint should **pick one** thing
  to stand for and claim expertise in — you can still bring the other disciplines in
  later once you have the client's trust. *(paraphrase — central prescription.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** The three-circle framework: love + good-at = "happy but poor";
  love + pays-well (but not good at) = "just a dream / you're a dreamer"; good-at +
  pays-well (but no love) = "rich but bored" (why designers go in-house). The center of
  all three = the **WIN**. *(paraphrase — his signature diagram this episode.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** "Pick one" is justified by cognition: the brain works like a
  "mental box" and remembers only the top of each category (mp3 player → iPod; phone →
  iPhone), so claiming everything makes you unmemorable. Attributed to **Marty Neumeier**
  (said as "Marty Newman"). *(paraphrase; attribution: Chris credits Neumeier.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Jose, context)** Cites the book **_Positioning: The Battle for the
  Mind_** (Ries & Trout) — positioning matters because consumers have too much to
  remember; failing to position is a disadvantage. Jose calls his one-thing his "wedge."
  *(paraphrase — co-host; names the canonical source.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** Align your skill set with an industry/client that can afford
  it — same skill and problem-solving applied to a national restaurant chain vs. a mom-
  and-pop yields full dollar value instead of half. *(paraphrase.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** To choose clients, **profile them and match & mirror**: study
  their look, tone, messaging, vocabulary, and volume so they feel comfortable with you.
  Supports with a Tony Robbins quote: "people like people who are like themselves, or how
  they'd like to be." *(paraphrase + attributed quote.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** Access tactics: look in professional circles (doctors,
  attorneys, accountants, financial advisors), attend industry trade shows, and use the
  local Business Journal lists; if you can't reach a giant like Adidas, scale down its
  **supply chain** (grommet/rubber/textile suppliers) to a reachable client.
  *(paraphrase.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Chris)** "**Say it and stick to it**": most design portfolios "don't
  say anything," just show work and stake no claim. Make a specific benefit-led claim
  ("grow your business with great design, for successful CEOs") — even one you can't fully
  back up yet — then build the expertise to support it over time. *(paraphrase; the
  "say it and stick to it" phrasing is credited to Jose, adopted by Chris.)*
- **[2015-04-28] (Jose, context)** Positioning isn't permanent — industries and desires
  shift, so learn to re-position repeatedly (Madonna reinventing across eras). *(paraphrase.)*

> "You can stand for one thing and you can try to claim expertise in it, it's very hard

> "So what happens is if you try to claim everything, people won't remember you." — **Chris Do**

> "You do not have to give up all the other disciplines that you've learnt… but position

> — **Chris Do**
