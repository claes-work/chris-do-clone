# Critiquing Your Design – Akin.Sidmad.com — @thefutur (2015-05-23)

"The Skool" era episode co-hosted by **Chris Do** and **Jose Caballer**. They review a
viewer-submitted website — a self-described "minimal flat design" landing-page portfolio
by a viewer named **Akin Gündoğan** (a UI/web specialist based in Istanbul; caption
spelling of the name is uncertain), submitted via the Skool Facebook group. Only
Chris-Do-attributed statements are persona training data; Jose Caballer's contributions
are marked as such, and the submitter/site owner is context only.

## Summary

Chris Do and Jose Caballer critique Akin Gündoğan's personal landing page, which he
labeled a "minimal flat design" site for himself as a "web UI specialist." The critique
runs on two tracks. First, a craft/terminology correction: because the page leads with a
large photographic image of a mountain peak, it is not actually flat design — flat design
uses simple color fields, iconography, and near-zero shading/gradients (they pull up
Pinterest examples to define it). Second, and more important, a positioning/business
critique: the site shows the owner's name but no work samples, so a buyer cannot tell what
he does or hire him. Using two other examples — a Skool member's site (Ben Thompson /
"Studio Fluid") and an agency called Guru — Chris argues a portfolio must (a) put your
three best projects up front, (b) specify exactly which role you played on each project so
a sophisticated buyer isn't misled, and (c) lead with why/what you stand for before the
tactical "what." He invokes Simon Sinek's "Start With Why" / Golden Circle, points to
Apple as the exemplar (sell the belief first, specs last), and closes by praising The
Futur/Blind's own homepage as walking a visitor through the whole sales cycle on one page
and telling a story rather than just displaying a portfolio. Jose adds a "show the how"
point, recommending process/behind-the-scenes video (citing a Coldplay interactive project
done at Blind).

## Key claims

- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) The submitted site is not flat design: introducing a photographic
  image (the mountain peak) makes it a photographic site, not flat. Flat design is defined
  by flat color fields, graphic/iconographic interpretation rather than photos, and almost
  zero shading — no gradients; simple and minimalist in color and rendering.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) You can incorporate photography into a flat design, but it should
  be simplified — e.g. black-and-white rather than full color "screaming at you" — to keep
  the overall simplicity.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) The biggest problem with the site is positioning: it shows his
  name but no examples of work, making it impossible to hire him. "You don't have any
  work" — LinkedIn is not where a designer's portfolio gets evaluated.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) Put your best work up front. Assume a visitor treats the top
  three images as your three best projects; they won't scroll to images four, five, six if
  the first ones don't intrigue them.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) On the Studio Fluid example: a strong intriguing headline ("stand
  out with what you stand for") draws the reader in, but the subsequent copy fails when it
  repeats the same idea without adding new information — the body copy must deliver new
  bits of information, not restate the headline.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) In a case study, what you actually did is often buried in the
  narrative; visitors skim and may wrongly assume your role. State your role explicitly.
  Industry-specific terms like "strategic branding" should be made granular (identity, logo
  design, positioning) so a buyer can appreciate what you contributed.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) A sophisticated buyer needs to know which subset of a project each
  person did, because impressive work is often made by many people; hiring someone based on
  work they only partly contributed to is a common and costly mistake. Always ask
  explicitly what they did and for a specific example.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) On the Guru agency example: leading a case study with the concrete
  scope of services ("creative campaign development, digital agency of record, social app
  design and development, website redesign") gives the buyer a lens to understand the work
  immediately. He prefers a blended approach — present clearly what you did (like Guru) and
  also show a little behind-the-scenes detail so the visitor doesn't have to click off the
  site.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) Positioning question for any personal site: decide whether you are
  a UI/UX tactician (then show that tactical work) or a strategy-driven designer (then talk
  at a strategic level) — but always be specific about what you did at the tactical level so
  a sophisticated buyer can read it.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) Applies Simon Sinek's "Start With Why" / Golden Circle: most
  companies know WHAT they do, some know HOW, few know WHY. He reframes "why" as "biology" —
  understanding why people buy. Companies like Apple understand who they sell to and what
  they stand for.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) Good sites lead with what they stand for (Mavericks, creative
  thinkers, rebels — Apple's "Think Different" positioning) and only reveal specs/detail
  (RAM, megahertz) at the last moment when the buyer is ready to buy. Studio Fluid and Guru
  do this by not opening with "UX/UI/strategy."
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) A design company's site shouldn't just show a portfolio — it
  should tell a story, communicate what you stand for and how you work, so the untrained eye
  (to whom all the work looks the same) can appreciate the work at a certain level.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) The Futur/Blind homepage models a "latest startup framework":
  show the whole sales cycle on one page — what we do (summary) → how we're different →
  proof/examples → clients → culture/stats/fun facts → validation/quotes — so a visitor
  gets a sense of whether they want to work with you without having to click deeper.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) They've experimented and found that when you ask people to click
  through, they don't; on sites that have to sell something, present enough on one page.
- (2015-05-23, Chris Do) People don't notice design differences as much as designers think;
  buyers want to know how the work solved the problem and the "why" for themselves first,
  not just how it looks.
- (2015-05-23, Jose Caballer) In addition to the "why," it's valuable to show the "how" —
  process and behind-the-scenes video. Cites a Coldplay interactive project at Blind that
  paired the finished interactive story with a "process" video of interviews, and
  recommends documentary-style process content (though not for every project).
