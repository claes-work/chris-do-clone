# Critiquing Your Design — LeighObrien.com — @thefutur (2015-06-19)

"The Skool" era episode ("the process") co-hosted by **Chris Do** and **Jose Caballer**.
Responding to a viewer (Leigh O'Brien / LeighObrien.com) who posted his new website in
the YouTube comments asking for feedback on "what's not working or what's confusing,"
Chris live-critiques the portfolio site and showreel. **Only Chris-Do-attributed
statements are persona training data** (his critique lens). The site owner, Leigh
O'Brien, is context (a viewer whose work is being reviewed), not persona data. Jose

## Summary

Chris Do critiques Leigh O'Brien's WordPress portfolio site. O'Brien positions himself as
a "motion graphics and Digital development designer" doing 2D/3D animation, VFX, branding,
and illustration. Chris opens with what works (clear "I'm Leigh, here's what I do" intro,
showreel front-and-center, evident drawing/illustration skill, friendly template) and
then goes granular. His core diagnosis: the positioning is muddy — "digital development
designer" is meaningless and the site tries to claim too many disciplines at once, so a
hiring creative director has no lens through which to judge the work. He argues you must
pick ONE clear claim, especially early in a career, because it is far easier to market a
specialist and because big-budget clients won't risk work on someone who is "okay at a
lot of things." He reads O'Brien as fundamentally an illustrator trying to break into
motion (the illustration is the strongest work; the animation reel is spotty and lacks
great camera work / character animation / squash-and-stretch). Concrete fixes: cut the
reel to only the best 3-5 pieces (less is more), replace sleepy music with energy, don't
waste the first ~20-30 seconds on countdown timers or name animations, and never show
work as raw YouTube player frames — grab the best still frames and drop the player UI.
He contrasts O'Brien's site with a former intern's (Maiko) clean thumbnail grid on Vimeo
as the professional benchmark. He frames the whole thing with the "T-shaped" model (Tom
Kelley): a narrow bar of breadth on top, deep expertise in one thing below — and if you
truly want multiple careers, split them across separate targeted URLs. Recurring theme:
"nobody hires student-level work," so stop thinking of yourself as a student.

## Key claims

- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Critique method: start positive, then get more granular and
  constructive — establish what works before dissecting what doesn't.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Positioning must be a single clear claim. Vague self-labels like
  "digital development designer" throw the viewer off — "digital" implies web/UX work
  that isn't actually in the portfolio. Make one claim so the viewer has a lens through
  which to appreciate the work (e.g. "I'm a 3D artist," "modeler/texture artist,"
  "lighting TD").
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) When you need work, be very clear about positioning — pick who
  your client is first (motion firm? direct client? in-house corporate?), then create and
  talk about your body of work with messaging aimed directly at them.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Early in a career you DO need to specialize, even though it's an
  unpopular sentiment in some schools. It's far easier to market yourself as a specialist,
  and on big-budget, high-stakes projects a client won't risk the work on someone who is
  merely "okay at a lot of things."
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Specialization mirrors how real production teams are structured:
  an animation team alone splits into modelers, texture artists, riggers, character
  animators, camera/layout, lighting, and compositing artists — nobody does all of it.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) A showreel must grab the viewer fast: you have roughly 30 seconds
  to keep or lose them, so don't waste the first ~20 precious seconds on countdown timers,
  elaborate name animations, or other non-essential info — get straight into the work.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Less is more: edit the reel down to only your best 3-5 pieces,
  leave them wanting more, then let them drive deeper. Front-load your strongest work.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Music matters — a sleepy track ("puts me to sleep") kills the
  reel; use energy to get people excited.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Diagnosis of the reel: the 3D lighting/rendering pieces are the
  stronger work; the animation is spotty and uneven — he's not seeing great camera moves
  or character work, and the 2D/cel work lacks the timing, breathing, and squash-and-stretch
  he looks for. If the reel doesn't grab him, you lose him.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Reads O'Brien as an illustrator trying to move into motion: the
  flat-graphic icon work and character-design illustration are the strongest, most
  on-trend pieces, but they aren't incorporated into an animated reel.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) You can build a career on a narrow specialty — "you can get a job
  just doing texture mapping and building models" — but you have to be clear that's what
  you want to do.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) The "T-shaped creative" model (credited to Tom Kelley of IDEO):
  the top bar is the breadth of things you might dabble in (generalist); the vertical stem
  is deep expertise in one thing. To get work you must convert from generalist to
  specialist — and the T shouldn't be too wide (don't advertise too many interests).
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Nobody wants to hire someone who's mediocre at everything (stated
  as his opinion).
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) If you genuinely want to pursue multiple disciplines, split them
  across separate, targeted sites/URLs (e.g. "Leigh O'Brien Motion" for motion, a separate
  artist/illustration URL) so you can "have your cake and eat it" without confusing the
  viewer — but expect it to be a lot of work; focus your energy on your strengths and go 100%.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Never present video work as raw YouTube player frames with a bunch
  of clips — it looks unprofessional and unrefined. Grab the best still frames and get rid
  of the player interface.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) Thumbnail benchmark: former intern Maiko (a recent Otis graduate,
  using Vimeo) uses clean thumbnails so a creative director instantly knows what each piece
  is — e.g. "I need a car spot, I know which one to click." Good thumbnails save the viewer
  time and telegraph exactly what they're getting.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) "Nobody hires student-level work." If you think of yourself as a
  student, you'll make student-level work and won't get the work you want. When trying to
  get hired, employers only want professional-level work.
- (2015-06-19, Chris Do) For a recent grad with too much student-looking work: edit down
  ruthlessly to your best 3-5 pieces and make them look as professional as possible.
- (2015-06-19, Jose Caballer) Sets up the counter-question throughout — what advice for
  someone who wants to "keep their options open" and do many kinds of work — which Chris
  answers with the specialize / separate-URLs framing.
