## Summary

Chris Do and Jose Caballer critique Michael Edward's portfolio site. Edward positions
himself in the intro as a "skilled designer with 10 years of combined experience in brand
strategy, graphic design, creative direction, research and leadership," believing in
"human-centered solutions," and the signature reads "design strategist and ideator." The
site uses a polished template with a playful stat block (clients, "impacted lives," cups
of hot chocolate, hours of sleep) and a recurring cutesy refrain ("do you love me yet?").
The core diagnosis: Edward's greatest strength — a very broad skill set — is also his
primary problem, because the positioning is muddy. Are you a strategist or a designer? Jose
argues you must pick ONE clear claim, say it loud, and back it up; he uses his own
repositioning as a "UX strategist / partner to CEOs" as the model. Chris's central craft
point: the work shown is designer's work, not strategist's work — "you're a designer acting
as a design strategist" — and there are no real case studies. A brand-guidelines page shows
logo applications but no brand messaging or strategy; a "campaign" is a single image; project
navigation is buggy (top nav jumps to the next case study, some projects don't scroll). Chris
reframes the fix around client psychology: write the headline to the customer's self-interest
(the benefit to them, not a list of what you do), and pre-empt the three objections a
prospective client has — do you have the experience, what results have you gotten, and are
you credible / who vouches for you (testimonials, named clients). Because the projects look
"gigantic" (Atlanta Hawks, a Weather Channel Super Bowl campaign, MetroPCS), the site reads
like an agency, which can intimidate or confuse a hiring agency into seeing him as a
competitor rather than a collaborator. Both hosts push toning down the personal-brand cuteness,
giving credit to the teams he led (positioning himself as an orchestrator/leader, not the
individual author of everything), and clarifying whether he is an individual or an agency.

## Key claims

- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) The critique format: look at the whole site first, then get into
  the critique — survey the work before dissecting it.
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) A "brand guideline" that only shows logo/user applications with no
  brand messaging is student-level work — "something we all do in school"; it doesn't
  demonstrate brand strategy.
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) A single image is not a campaign — if the case study labels itself
  "campaign, design, video" it must actually show a campaign, not one image.
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) Attention spans are short: saying too many things means the viewer
  won't remember what you actually do. If you're a design strategist, "say it proud, say it
  loud," claim that one thing and then back it up.
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) Write the headline to the customer's self-interest, not yours. A
  design-strategist headline should talk about the benefit you deliver to the other person,
  then address their biggest barriers to saying yes — appeal to what's in it for them.
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) The three objections a prospective client has, which the site must
  pre-empt: (1) do you have the experience, (2) what results have you gotten (high-level
  buyers — CMOs, managing directors, creative directors — look hard at impact), and (3) are
  you credible — what's the track record, who can vouch for you (testimonials, named clients
  for validation).
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) These objections are universal to hiring anyone — "nail salon, dry
  cleaning, or design strategist": we want to know how long you've done this, what results
  you've gotten, and who else has trusted you, because trust from others makes us feel safer.
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) The work on the site is a designer's work, not a strategist's —
  "you're a designer acting as a design strategist." If you claim to be a design strategist,
  a case study must show the strategy: what you did, who for, what role you played, and the
  results (easier delivery, better audience response, whatever the impact was).
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) There are no real case studies here — showing polished deliverables
  is not the same as showing strategy and results.
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) Treating your own name as a brand is fine but can tip into "too cute"
  / egotistical (the "do you love me yet?" refrain) — tone it down; just be Michael Edward and
  let the case studies carry it.
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) Because the projects look "gigantic" (huge-name clients), viewers
  will ask: did you do this as a freelancer, how big is your firm? — which is exactly why
  testimonials and named clients matter for validation.
- (2015-06-25, Chris Do) A site that looks this "tight" and "Pro" reads like an agency; if
  you're promoting yourself as an individual you must show what role you played on a team and
  how a hirer would fit you in — otherwise an agency viewer can't tell if you're a hire or a
  competitor.
- (2015-06-25, Jose Caballer) The broad skill set is both the strength and the primary
  challenge — you must decide who your customer is (direct-to-client? creative director at an
  agency? collaborator?) before anything else.
- (2015-06-25, Jose Caballer) Pick one position and commit — "if you're a strategist, you're
  a strategist." Uses his own repositioning: a UX strategist and "partner to CEOs" who shows
  only UX-strategy work and results, credits the teams he worked with, and markets himself to
  get hired to do UX strategy and manage teams — even though he could do many other things.
- (2015-06-25, Jose Caballer) Separate the brands: split "Michael Edward the person" from the
  work, and simplify the signature (drop "and ideator" — just "design strategist").
- (2015-06-25, Jose Caballer) Give credit down to the project team so it reads like you were
  leading people, not doing everything yourself — position yourself as the orchestrator of the
  work, "a leader versus a doer," which makes you feel bigger and more senior.
- (2015-06-25, Jose Caballer) Designers transitioning to design strategist at a higher value
  level genuinely struggle to reposition themselves — it's a tough, non-obvious road.
