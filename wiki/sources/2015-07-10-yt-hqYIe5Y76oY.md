# My (First) Design Portfolio — @thefutur (2015-07-10)

Co-hosted "The Skool" era episode ("the process") with **Chris Do** and **Jose Caballer**.
Answering a viewer question, Chris shows his own first (analog) design portfolio and lays
out how to prepare a portfolio to get a design job. Only Chris-Do-attributed statements
are persona training data; Jose Caballer's contributions are marked as such, and unclear
speakers are flagged.

## Summary

In response to a viewer request to see his first portfolio, Chris Do walks through his
"How to" framework for preparing a design portfolio to land a job. He gives three tips:
(1) focus your portfolio around one clearly demonstrated core strength (the "T-shaped"
skill), (2) ruthlessly edit your work down because employers are "information rich and
time poor," and (3) be honest and selective — align your work to a small number of
target companies and "matching mirror" the people/place you want to work with. He then
reveals his own first portfolio: just four conceptual pieces sent to Seattle ad agency
Cole & Weber, which got him a job offer within two days. The back half is a nostalgic
tour through his mid-1990s student and early professional/motion-graphics and early-web
work. Jose Caballer contributes counterpoints throughout, especially on when generalist
("multiple skills") profiles are actually valuable (marketing/social-media/account-manager
roles).

## Key claims

- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) Preparing a portfolio to get a design job comes down to three
  things: focus, edit, and be honest/selective.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) Aim to be "T-shaped": have one clearly demonstrated core
  strength (depth) rather than a shallow spread of many skills. Many designers have a
  "T" that has fallen on its side — lots of breadth, no depth.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) From the hiring side of the table, an employer is looking for
  something very specific — the best designer in one category for the role being filled
  — not a bit of clay modeling, puppetry, and graphic design mixed together.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) A "kitchen sink" portfolio (graphics, lettering, UX/UI,
  illustration, packaging all at once) makes it hard to tell what you are good at or what
  job you are applying for; if he wants an animator and you show mostly lettering, he
  hires a specialist animator instead.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) Analogy: you trust a specialist for the best result — you would
  not trust a kebab place to make authentic New York pizza; a buffet lowers your
  standards and expectations because you are optimizing for volume, not quality.
- (2015-07-10, Jose Caballer) There is a distinction between marketing yourself to a
  studio (show one specialized thing) vs. marketing directly to clients as a one/two-man
  "agency" moniker (where showing variety can make sense). The two audiences are very
  different.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do & Jose) At a senior/art-director/creative-director level the
  advice differs — there you want a broader thinker who can write, manage the client, and
  communicate, not a narrow specialist.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) To go deep, pick one focus for now (you don't have to destroy
  the rest of the work), make a clear claim (e.g. "I'm a lettering/logoform designer"),
  and show many examples to establish expertise. He cites Seb Lester as an example of
  someone who went deep — an amazing letterform/calligraphy designer with a huge
  Instagram following (cited as ~571,000 followers) whose mastery lets him do whatever he
  wants because people come to him as the expert.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) Tip 2 — edit: employers are "information rich and time poor."
  On a motion reel you have roughly 30–40 seconds (about a minute max) before the viewer
  hits stop; work does not get better later because people front-load their best. Don't
  waste that time on countdown timers or long logo animations — get right into it and cut
  the filler.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) Tip 3 — be honest and selective: honestly know what you're good
  at, filter out the junk, and target only three or four companies where you have an
  honest shot and whose work/culture aligns with yours. Don't send a broad mass email —
  it doesn't work.
- (2015-07-10, Jose Caballer) The generalist/multi-skill profile can actually be a plus
  for marketing, social-media, or account-management roles — but if your style is counter
  to the company's, you'll likely end up managing rather than doing.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) "Matching mirror": act, talk, and look like the people you want
  to do business with; people want to be around people who are like them (attributed by
  Chris to a paraphrase of Tony Robbins).
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) Positioning is about alignment/value: the closer your offer
  aligns to the target's need, the more value you create (flood-repair services are
  valuable to a flooded house, worthless in the desert). References their earlier
  "positioning" episode.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) On student vs. professional work: the only real difference is
  mentality — the tools and information are the same and searchable. If you keep a
  "student portfolio," you stay a student; study, emulate, and copy the frames of the
  professional work at the place you want to work until your work matches that level.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) Off-brand or overly aspirational work signals student/experimental
  R&D work — e.g. doing something grungy and multicolored for a brand like Target that is
  consistently, simply branded reveals it wasn't a real professional brief.
- (2015-07-10, Jose Caballer) Hiring anecdote: Art Center students were more polished in
  their portfolios; CalArts students were less polished but stronger in thinking/process.
  On a project the CalArts types tend to direct and drive the conceptual framework while
  the Art Center types plug in and execute meticulous production — it's the manager's job
  to position each to their strength.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do — biography) His first job portfolio: a friend, Colleen Mathis,
  who had a job at Cole & Weber (an advertising agency in Seattle, by Pike Place Market),
  invited him to submit because she needed a partner. He sent only four pieces — his best
  conceptual work — even though he had no ad work (he'd taken one advertising class) and
  thought he wouldn't get it. Two days later they offered him the job. This was a
  full-time job taken before he graduated (seventh term; he took a semester off, then
  went back to finish).
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do — biography) The four pieces: (1) a poster for the Museum of
  Tolerance where barbed wire is composed into a swastika; another poster was a gun facing
  a mirror for the Clint Eastwood film "Unforgiven"; (2)/(3) a skateboard-deck design done
  in the "Type Two" class with Simon Johnston; (4) an editorial/magazine-cover piece done
  with Fred Fehlau at Art Center — an anti-drug image of a crack pipe wrapped in thorns.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do — biography) Timeline detail: this early work dates to ~1995;
  Chris and Jose each note they've been working ~20 years (as of 2015). Chris references
  early professional work for clients including Silicon Graphics, "The Island of Dr.
  Moreau" (logotype + keyframes, with Cal Cooper / Inez design), a Saving Private Ryan
  teaser, Drug-Free America, a national Mitsubishi TV spot with typography shot in-camera
  on 35mm film, Nissan, Twinings tea packaging, and appearances/work in Res magazine
  ("Res 10"). (Several names/spellings are caption-garbled and uncertain.)
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do — biography) Self-description: "I'm unemployable… I can't work
  for a place or a person for too long"; he's headstrong and wants to do things his own
  way. Also notes his social energy differs from Jose's — going out drains him.
- (2015-07-10, Chris Do) Practical print-portfolio tip: shoot work as 4x5 film positives
  photographed on a light box by a professional, mount/laminate the glossy prints
  (~$20 downtown) and back them with velvet so fingerprints wipe off.
