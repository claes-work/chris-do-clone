# Is Now a Good Time To Be A Designer?

> Source page for the YouTube video "Is Now a Good Time To Be A Designer?" on **@thefutur** (then branded "The Process"), published 2015-07-26. This is an early ("The Process" era) *Ask Me Anything* episode with a live studio audience (Karen, Matt, Aaron). Co-host **Jose Caballer** is credited in the intro but is absent (on a road trip), so **Chris Do answers solo**. All substantive answers below are Chris Do's. Views are dated **2015** and may have evolved.

## Summary

New "Ask Me Anything" segment of *The Process*, filmed on a work-in-progress set. Jose Caballer is announced as co-host but is away, so Chris Do fields questions solo from three studio-audience members (Karen, Matt, Aaron). The through-line is a career-advice Q&A for designers, especially those just starting out.

Chris argues it has **never been a better time to be a designer**: designers have direct audience access, cheap tools, abundant learning resources, and — most importantly — the world is moving to an information/software economy that needs designers more than ever. The role is expanding beyond making "cool looking things" toward facilitation, user experience, psychology, and product thinking; commodity work (logos, business cards) is what gets eroded by Fiverr / 99designs, while whole-customer-journey thinking raises a designer's value.

He then gives tactical advice: treat the ~6-month post-graduation runway (before student loans hit) as a finite window for maximum risk/exploration; if you can't land a job, pursue an extended internship/apprenticeship under a great mentor (potentially more valuable than the whole degree). He covers how to extract value from an apprenticeship (audit resources, don't waste time, be likable/trustworthy, take a leadership role, over-deliver), and what to do with no access to education or money (the internet has everything — the real skill is learning how to *ask* good questions). He challenges the ROI of design school with a cost-comparison exercise (value ≠ price), and shares his own founder-risk story (impatience, being "too dumb to know the risks," under-promise/over-deliver). A long role-play addresses imposter syndrome on a first real branding job — defeating the negative inner voice, not negotiating against yourself on price, and modeling yourself after people who already have the skill you lack. Closes on how (and why) to find and approach a mentor: just ask, and "scaffold" the ask rather than cold-pitching.

## Key claims

All claims are Chris Do's (2015), paraphrased unless quoted. Co-host Jose is absent; audience members only asked questions.

- **It has never been a better time to be a designer** (2015): designers have direct connection to their audience, all the tools they need, tremendous learning resources, and can publish at a fraction of the former cost.
- The **deeper reason** it's a great time: there's never been a greater *need* for designers — the world is moving toward the software platform / information economy, and designers are best placed to handle it (2015).
- **Commodity design is being commoditized**: logos, packaging, business cards face little demand and are why Fiverr and 99designs work — they give a "limited design experience." Value moves to designers who think through the *entire customer story* (2015).
- The **designer's role is now much bigger** — part facilitator/connector, part cultural anthropologist, part product designer of digital products — requiring awareness of front-end, back-end, and many disciplines; this raises the designer's value (2015).
- **Transitional designers (~5 years or less in) may struggle** because skills they learned are already devalued; decades-in veterans may be better off "doubling down" on what they're great at rather than forcing the transition (2015).
- **Key traits/skills to add**: psychology, philosophy, writing (even for non-native English speakers), visual storytelling, and organizing complex information (2015).
- **"The secret to being a great designer are two things … empathy and imagination"** — empathize with users' needs and imagine the world through their eyes to solve their problems (Chris says he's said this before) (2015).
- **Apple is cited as the "gold standard for user experience"** — every step from deciding to buy through plugging in and turning on has been considered, so no manuals are needed (distinguishing user experience from customer experience) (2015).
- **Post-graduation strategy**: you have a finite runway (~6 months before student loans) — treat it as the window for maximum risk; don't rush into the first corporate job; explore and decide around the 4-month mark. Risk tolerance only shrinks with age, responsibility, and financial burden (2015).
- **If you can't get a job, do an extended internship/apprenticeship** with a firm you believe in under a great mentor — this can be more valuable than the entire four-year education (2015).
- **Getting the most from an apprenticeship**: don't take time for granted; audit available resources (hardware, software, books, training, freelancers, mentors); be likable and trustworthy ("people want to work with people they like and can trust"); take a leadership role; ask for more and over-deliver cheerfully. Doing the bare minimum means no growth (2015).
- **No access / no money?** "There's this wonderful beautiful thing … called the internet and everything you ever want to learn is on the internet — all you have to do is learn how to ask it." Asking good questions is an under-taught skill (2015).
- **Anatomy of a good question**: very specific questions yield yes/no; very broad questions let any answer fit — aim for open-ended questions with enough parameters. Example transformation: "How do I grow?" → "How do I increase my billings to $200,000 annually as a designer?" (2015).
- **School ROI exercise**: isolate tuition (ignore living costs). For a round ~$100k, ask whether you could get an equal-or-better education in less time / for less money by other means — events & seminars for a network (~$1k/month × 12 = $12k), hardware/software, e-learning (Lynda, Skillshare, Udemy, General Assembly, "school Pro member"). **"People oftentimes mistake value with price; they're not related."** School is largely for discovering what you want to learn and self-discovery via the humanities; a confident person who knows exactly what they want may not need traditional school (2015).
- **His own founder risk**: started the company without much work experience; competitors won't help once you're a rival, so you figure it out alone. He was "really impatient," saw a window, asked "what's the worst that can happen," and just went for it — the worst case was going to work for someone anyway. He credits being "just not that smart to know what the risks were," plus a lifelong desire to build a company (secure a project fee, hire the best people, everyone makes money) (2015).
- **Under-promise and over-deliver** is a core operating principle: negotiate two days into three; deliver four versions when asked for three — build in a cushion for mistakes so you don't stress yourself. The common, damaging mistake is projecting confidence via big bold promises ("one day, no problem"; "25 shots I'll do 40") and then shooting yourself in the foot. Over-charging freelancers who then under-deliver "leaves a bad taste" and is hard to recover from (2015).
- **Radical transparency is acceptable**: you can tell a client "I don't know, I'll do my best, I can't make that promise, and I'm willing to do it if you want to work with me" — being open and honest (2015).
- **Reframing loss/imposter syndrome**: "If you lose something it's because you had it and you owned it … you didn't lose anything, you just didn't get that thing." Before the opportunity existed your life was fine; don't build a mental narrative that you *need* to win it (2015).
- **First-big-job mindset (role-play)**: performing on a real job vs. school is "shooting free throws in your house versus in the big game" — same basket, ball, gravity; "it's a mindset." "Gym fighters" who crumble on the big stage fail on mentality, not ability (2015).
- **Confidence framing**: he's been called cocky/arrogant but says "I just believe in what I can do." He earned it by out-working peers ("worked like a demon … when people were out partying I was in the library"). A client can't know more about lettering/typography/identity than the designer — otherwise they'd have done it themselves (2015).
- **Defeat the inner "I can't"**: "do or die and you will do." He always committed to every school assignment ("I won't sleep") rather than deciding in advance he couldn't do it (2015).
- **Don't puff up either** — don't claim you'll "embarrass Paul Rand." Be real, appreciate the opportunity, define the problem, and be an incredible image-maker: pick the right font, a great color palette, lay it out beautifully (2015).
- **Don't negotiate against yourself on price**: wanting to charge $2,000 but talking yourself down to $1,200 (because "I was making $10/hour at Starbucks") is the mistake to stop (2015).
- **Modeling as a skill-acquisition tool**: when he lacks a skill or is afraid, he finds someone who already does that one thing and models himself on them ("faking it") until he becomes that person. He describes himself as "an extreme introvert" who learned social ease by hanging out with naturally social people and imitating them — now he'll strike up conversation with strangers at lunch (2015).
- **Don't give people power over you** (or take it over them) — admired celebrities/teachers are "just another human being just like you and me, no different" (2015).
- **Finding a mentor**: age is irrelevant ("if you want to learn, find a mentor, period"); thinking you must be young to have one is an ego thing. Pick by compatibility — how they communicate, their work, how they carry themselves, and a style that's safe for you to adapt. Choosing a mentor is like choosing an ice cream flavor: try it, see what fits, don't overthink (2015).
- **How to approach a mentor**: "If you don't ask for something you'll never get it." His own example — he asked his typography instructor **Simon Johnston** to be his intern; Simon had never had an intern in years of teaching because nobody had asked. The worst case is a "no," and you're no worse off than before (2015).
- **"Scaffold" the ask** — don't cold-pitch ("can I intern for you?" tapping a stranger's shoulder creeps people out). Introduce yourself, say what you connected with and why, then make the ask so it makes sense. Being asked "so when are we going to work together?" out of context reads as weird/off-putting (this is his personal preference; some people handle directness fine) (2015).
