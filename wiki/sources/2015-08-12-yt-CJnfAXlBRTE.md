# What is UX Design? Defining User Experience Design & Explaining the Process

## Summary

A co-hosted "The Skool" era episode with **Jose Caballer** (his name is garbled as
"Hosea" in the captions) and **Chris Do**, plus a small studio audience (Rishab/"Rashad",
"Archie", "Aron"/"Eron"). The stated question ("what exactly is UX experience?") comes
from an audience member, and the show is titled "The Process."

**Jose leads the great majority of this video.** He runs the live exercise, does the
marker-on-board sketching, and explicitly narrates his own facilitation ("pay attention
to my language as I'm facilitating"). The working example is a real project — the redesign
of The Skool's own site (theschoolrocks.com). Jose walks through: building two customer
profiles (a freelancer "Tim Carson," early 30s, and an agency owner "Ellen Stafford," 38),
each with a story/challenges plus "solutions"/"needs"; prioritizing down to ~2–3 items;
and then translating those profiles directly into a long-form one-page sales/landing page,
module by module (hero, self-selection, value proposition, objections, social proof,
"what is it"/product). Jose also frames the studio setup — variables (brand, users, goals)
on one side, live sketching in the middle, execution on the other — as a "living process."

**Chris Do's role is secondary but substantive.** He supplies the freelancer profile's
lived challenges (transition, growth, complexity, "is there a better way"), contributes the
copywriting/headline point, discusses the technical/product architecture he worked on with
the CS-major audience member, delivers the car metaphor about copying proven structures,
and gives the closing user-centric summary. Because speaker labels are absent in the
captions, several mid-video lines cannot be confidently assigned; those are flagged
`attribution: uncertain` below.

Persona-training value here is moderate: the strongest Chris-attributed material is the
closing "stop talking about yourself, design for the user" summary and the car/structure
metaphor. Most of the mechanical UX-process teaching is Jose's.

## Key claims

_All dated 2015-08-12. Paraphrased unless quoted. Speaker noted where determinable._

- **(Jose)** UX is hard to define like "film" — people hear the finished product, but the
  real subject is "film *making*": the whole collection of activities (research, customer
  profiles, UI/visual design, user testing, development) that produce an experience for
  users of digital devices. _(Film-metaphor framing; attribution leans Jose but is
  uncertain — see note below.)_
- **(Jose)** How you define UX matters less than the underlying move: identify who the
  customer is, what their challenges are, how to solve them, then build the experience
  around that.
- **(Jose)** A customer profile can be distilled to a name + age, a "story"/challenges, and
  "solutions"/"needs" (Jose's product "core" calls these *needs*; Chris names it slightly
  differently — "it's all the same thing").
- **(Chris)** The freelancer's challenge is transitioning from working for other companies
  to having their own clients — graduating from a home studio to an office with employees
  (intern, junior designer, salesperson), driven by a life stage (married / about to have
  a kid) where financial needs differ from their 20s.
- **(Chris/uncertain)** Freelancers also wonder "is there a better way?" — e.g. how to go
  from $5–10,000 websites to $200,000 websites; they need efficiency and bigger clients.
  _(Dollar figures partly garbled in captions: "40501 $200,000".)_
- **(Jose)** You don't need many profiles — three to five archetypes is typical; more than
  ~10–15 becomes unwieldy. More complex sites (e.g. an airline) justify more profiles. A
  common technique: draft ~8 with the client, then consolidate by similarity down to 5 or 3.
- **(Jose)** After listing challenges/solutions you *prioritize* (pick ~3); un-chosen items
  aren't discarded, they move to a later phase (scheduling). The facilitator guides the
  hard third choice.
- **(Jose)** The page format (mobile vs web, long sales page vs multi-page) is dictated by
  the customer plus research and preference; here the client "chooses" a long single-page
  sales page.
- **(Jose)** A sales page maps to the customer journey/objections in order: capture
  attention (hero) → self-selection ("is this for me?" — freelancer vs agency) → value
  proposition → answer "does it work?" (testimonials / social proof) → "what is it exactly?"
  (the product) → call to action at the bottom.
- **(Jose)** The value proposition is the shortest way to name the customer's need/challenge;
  he tests "Grow your design business," pulled straight from the profile and the brand's
  "growth" attribute.
- **(Chris/uncertain)** Good headlines grab attention and appeal to the reader's
  self-interest; designers err by talking about themselves. "If you can't help me solve my
  problem I'm moving on to the next site." _(Introduced as "I'm reading a book on
  copywriting"; speaker not clearly labeled.)_
- **(Jose)** Copy should lead with the customer's *challenges* framed as questions ("Are you
  struggling to grow your design practice?" / "Are your projects getting more complex and
  painful?" / "Is there a better way?"), taken directly from the profile.
- **(Chris)** The one page isn't the whole system — it links out to the e-commerce store and
  product landing pages. Chris worked with the CS-major audience member on the "product
  architecture" / technical architecture, weighing platforms and services (e.g. where a
  "buy" click actually goes), then diagramming the platforms.
- **(Chris)** For functional parts of a website, copy proven existing structures rather than
  reinventing — take the structure, change color/fonts, move things a bit, but don't be "the
  guy with the unique thing." Only reinvent structure when new tech/platforms (e.g.
  responsive/mobile) force it.
- **(Chris)** Closing: user-centric design is all about the user — stop talking about
  yourself and your software/hardware unless the customer asked; empathy and paying attention
  to what customers want is where the skill is.
- **(Jose)** Neither he nor Chris was taught in design school how to go from a customer
  profile to copy; the process is empathy-driven and iterative — landing pages must be
  tested in different arrangements, "not a finite process."
- **(Chris)** The follow-up on site taxonomy / structure / feature prioritization is teased
  as the next episode.

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do (persona voice):**

> "User centric design is all about the user, the customer. And so stop talking about
> yourself, stop talking about your software and your hardware — unless that's a question
> that they have... In most cases it's not. This is where your skills and empathy are going
> to matter a lot. You have to pay attention to what the customers want and need and the
> solutions that are going to solve that, and phrase it in a way that they can absorb — the
> story." — Chris Do

> "Just think about a car. A car has four wheels, a steering wheel, a drive gear or motor,
> and it needs to work the same from manufacturer to manufacturer. They're copying each
> other's structure, but everything else about the experience is different — the way it
> looks, the way it runs, the way it sounds — and we don't have a problem with that." — Chris Do

> "Designers [say] 'no, I'm going to reinvent the web, I'm going to make web 4.0'... and then
> they create a complete disaster. It doesn't function and they spend all their time
> re-engineering something that didn't need to be fixed." — Chris Do

> "[The freelancer is] transitioning from being a freelancer who works for other companies to
> having their own clients... they want to graduate basically from working out of their home
> studio into having an office and having some employees." — Chris Do

**Jose Caballer (co-host — NOT persona voice, context only):**

> "Pay attention to my language as I'm facilitating, because your job as a designer to level
