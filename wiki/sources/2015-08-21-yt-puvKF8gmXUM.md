# Design Advice For Young Designers (AMA / The Process)

## Summary

An "Ask Me Anything" episode of *The Process*, hosted by Chris Do with co-host
Jose Caballer and a live studio audience of young designers/illustrators (Jamie,
Maggie, Karen, Aaron). Format: audience and YouTube questions, answered mostly by
Chris. Three main threads: (1) whether to make time for personal work / developing
one's own voice, and how to raise the value of your creative work over time;
(2) how to recognize "bad taste" and what good taste actually is; (3) the most
efficient way to tackle a design brief and how to write a good one.

Chris's throughline is his distinction between an **artist** (makes work for
themselves, no client, shares a point of view) and a **designer** (solves a
client's problem — still a job). He argues the goal is to align what you love to do
with what you get paid to do so you don't live a "fragmented self." On taste, his
core principle is **restraint**. On briefs, his method is reducing the brief to two
opposing words used as a decision filter, then validating direction cheaply with a
mood board before doing the work. The episode is early Futur / "The Process" era
(2015), consistent with the mindset/design-craft coaching later formalized.

## Key claims

Speaker key: **Chris** = Chris Do (persona subject); **Jose** = Jose Caballer
(co-host, context); **audience** = Jamie/Maggie/Karen/Aaron (context).

- [2015-08-21] (Chris) There is a meaningful distinction between an *artist* and a
  *designer*: an artist makes work for themselves and shares their own point of
  view with no client, which is why it is fulfilling; a designer solves someone
  else's problem. Problems arise when designers act like artists — imposing their
  taste and refusing feedback/criticism. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) The aim is to line up your hobbies / what you'd do in your
  spare time with your profession, so there is no separation — the "fragmented
  self" (unhappy when paid, unhappy when doing your own thing) is where people get
  messed up. Work in an industry as close as possible to what you love. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) He defines his own passion as solving people's problems to
  help them; he frames himself as a coach/therapist/adviser who "speaks the
  language of design." Fulfillment comes from helping a client reach a goal, not
  from solving his own problem. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) The barriers to becoming a recognized fine artist are
  eroding — art critics and gallery owners no longer solely control who gets
  anointed; street artists (Banksy, Shepard Fairey) and illustrators can build a
  following and transition into fine art directly. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) The market price of artwork is driven by the artist's
  popularity and the scarcity of the work; beyond that, buyers pay more for larger
  prints, for more involved reproduction methods (silk screens, high-end litho vs.
  inkjet), for premium paper (heavyweight matte), and for hand-embellishment
  (gold leafing, signing and numbering to signal a limited run). (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) Cheap high-volume reproductions (color laser prints costing
  ~$1 to make, sold for ~$10 at cons) are low scarcity, so buyers won't pay much;
  he prefers matte over gloss because it feels more like art (stated as personal
  taste, invites disagreement). (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) Consider where the work will live — decor, color palette,
  boldness — because most buyers pick a piece to fit a space; a very bold piece at
  large scale is too much for many people. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) Reverse-engineer what people actually want rather than
  making whatever you feel like; artists (and musicians) often can't predict which
  of their pieces/tracks will be the hit. Use an artist friend, curator, critic, or
  creative director to identify your best 3-4 pieces, cut the rest, and repeat
  those themes. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) To be a great designer you need **empathy and imagination** —
  thinking about the customer/user, not just yourself. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) To stand out in a sea of sameness (e.g., anime fan art at
  Anime Expo), do the opposite of everyone else: simple, few solid bold colors
  instead of rendered Photoshop-gradient paintings, or integrate well-set
  typography. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) Good taste = **restraint**: not doing everything, deeply
  understanding materials, proportion, and scale, and not trying to be all things.
  Apple is his exemplar (no extra parts, considered down to internal screws;
  premium matte packaging, selective foil stamping — full foil would be "gaudy").
  (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) Premium is a positioning choice separate from price — good
  taste doesn't require premium price. Uniqlo is cited as delivering more than you
  pay for (strong sourcing/buying power, fashionable cuts without going over the
  top). (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) Bad taste often stems from **inauthenticity** — pretending
  to be something you're not, trying too hard (Ed Hardy given as example). Good
  taste shows restraint; bad taste doesn't. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) Restraint applies even to elaborate work: analyzing James
  Jean illustrations, he notes restraint in the color (complementary orange/blue,
  varied tones so it's not overwhelming), simple well-composed subject matter,
  scale variation, and embedded secondary details to discover. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) Beginners should avoid gimmicks / "being different just to
  be different" — that's where they mess up. For a logo, "pick one trick and go
  with it" (e.g., alter one letterform and make it the hero); don't stack tricks.
  Amateur logo designers pick a bad font (unfamiliar font or Comic Sans) and
  over-embellish. Learn the rules before breaking them. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Jamie, audience) A big part of good taste is being informed —
  studying well-designed typefaces so you learn to identify default/system fonts as
  inferior, enabling better decisions. Chris affirms this is the proper approach
  but notes it requires art education / trained critical looking that many people
  lack. (paraphrase; audience-attributed)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) A design brief is typically produced by a marketing/
  advertising team and should outline the project's boundaries and goals — but in
  his experience briefs are rarely helpful: too broad, generic, full of buzzwords
  (organic, cool, new, fresh) written by non-designers to "cover their butt." A
  useful brief is one you can hold up next to a design to *eliminate* options.
  (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) Method for tackling a brief: reduce it to five words or
  fewer, then down to two (or one) words used as a "decisional filter." Choose two
  seemingly *opposing* words (example: "serious fun") — few things overlap the two
  sides, which narrows and clarifies the goal. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) Validate direction cheaply and early: after agreeing on the
  words, build a quick mood board (Pinterest, Behance, web search) showing what the
  words look like, and check in with the client before investing thousands of
  hours. This avoids the "here's my Mona Lisa / you missed the boat" failure. He
  calls the impulse to rush into making "the squirrel thing" — slow that process
  down. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris) To draft a good brief yourself, use a facilitation framework
  (The Futur's is called "core") and ask clients questions — brand voice/tone, user
  benefits, who it's for, what you're celebrating, what trends you're acting on —
  to build a clear picture. (paraphrase)
- [2015-08-21] (Chris, relaying Kyle Cooper) The core problem designers have is
  they don't listen; if you learn to listen — to the brief, the client's
  objectives, the story — the answers appear and you don't have to make things up.
  Designers who ignore the brief just recycle what they've seen (e.g., wanting to
  do an *Iron Man* or *Se7en* title sequence regardless of whether it fits a
  romantic comedy). (paraphrase; secondhand attribution to Kyle Cooper)
