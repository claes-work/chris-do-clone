## Summary

"The Skool"-era co-hosted tutorial (Chris Do + **Jose Caballer**) — episode one of a
multi-part UX series on designing a website for a real client, the fishing lodge
"Oolie's/OIES" (a client of Chris Do's agency **Blind**, which had already built their
responsive landing page). This installment covers the front end of the UX process: the
**site audit**, **competitor audit**, and reviewing **user profiles / needs**, leading
toward user stories, a site map, and wireframes in later segments.

**Who leads:** Roughly co-led, but split by function. **Jose drives the methodology and
framework** — the overall UX process outline, the "CORE" brand-attributes exercise, the
site-taxonomy/site-audit structure, and the competitor-audit lens ("who is the user and
what is their intent"). **Chris supplies the client-specific expertise and design-craft
judgment** — he introduces the Oolie's client and relationship, delivers the competitor
site assessments ("what works well / what doesn't"), reads and interprets the user
profiles into design implications, and leads the closing discussion on content scope.
Speakers are usually distinguishable, but the caption transcript is heavily garbled
(no speaker labels, misspellings like "Oies/Olies/Oolie's/allies", "submen" for
"submenu", "Laraa/Lgera"), so several exchanges are marked uncertain.

## Key claims

- (2015-08-22, Jose) The web-design process starts with "CORE" — client-discovery
  exercises that surface the brand attributes (culture, customer, voice, benefit, value)
  and the "X Factor" before any UX work. For Oolie's: familiar culture, friendly
  customer, charming voice, "recharge" benefit, exclusive-access value; X Factor =
  family-owned, old-fashioned, unmatched service, exclusive access to protected waters
  (Hakai Pass).
- (2015-08-22, Jose) The next stages after discovery are: site audit → user needs →
  user stories → site map → wireframes.
- (2015-08-22, Jose) On any web project you serve multiple stakeholders — business
  owner, tech, design, marketing — and the goal is to get everyone aligned; skipping
  process and just shipping deliverables produces endless revisions.
- (2015-08-22, Jose/uncertain) Western reading order (left-to-right, top-down) dictates
  visual hierarchy: the logo and most-important nav go top-left, same rules as a
  newspaper or book layout; menu priority runs left (most important) to right.
- (2015-08-22, Chris) Competitor-audit method: assess each site for "what works well"
  and "what doesn't." Praised West Coast Resorts (high-end lodge) for a ready-to-call
  phone number and clean, narrative-driven structure; checks each site's booking form
  for detail, ease, and type size relative to that customer type.
- (2015-08-22, Chris) People try to make their sites do too much — packing in submenus
  and subpages replaces clarity with confusion. A prospect really just needs: what the
  experience is, testimonials, cost, and a way to book; other info can live elsewhere.
- (2015-08-22, Jose) The right amount of menu/nav depends on the user and their intent,
  not on copying big-budget sites (Sandals was shown as a large multi-location resort
  whose heavy menus suit its scale but wouldn't suit Oolie's). Separate the two site
  functions — convincing vs. booking — and decide which the site optimizes for.
- (2015-08-22, both) Oolie's existing site has too many sections, links-within-links,
  and dense legacy content; it needs consolidation. Clients often insist legacy content
  be preserved, which drives revisions.
- (2015-08-22, Chris) User profiles (fictitious personas built from real guests) drive
  design decisions. Reading a profile lets you "glean" concrete feature needs — e.g. the
  repeat-customer CFO ("John," the busy executive) implies an easy login/account, a
  packages-on-one-page view he can download/print, and possibly an assistant doing the
  booking; the tech-savvy family man ("Dan," savvy family guy) implies Facebook sharing
  and photo/community features; the marketer ("Phil," the demanding networker) implies a
  highly customizable booking process.
- (2015-08-22, Chris) Naming and typing each persona (busy executive / savvy family guy /
  demanding networker) lets you reference them by name throughout the design process to
  justify decisions to the client.
- (2015-08-22, audience→both) The introduced personas are all returning customers; a
  first-time / "desired customer" persona (someone you must convince) should be added.
- (2015-08-22, Chris) Content is what screws up every web project. If a site is built
  with Lorem Ipsum and the client is left to fill it, they take months. Nip it in the
  bud at the start: decide up front whether content is in scope, offer to rewrite or
  bring in a copywriter, and consider a separate content audit (pulling all content into
  a Google Doc and reorganizing it). A live "move the stickies" work session with
  stakeholders helps define navigation.
- (2015-08-22, Chris) Redesigning an outdated site responsibly means updating the content
  too — shorter, more concise, more web-friendly — not just a "facelift" (new
  graphics/colors over existing copy). Design without content is "weird," like designing
  a book but ignoring the typos. Whether you offer content depends on who you hire: an
  agency may position as "we'll do the content," a solo freelancer usually won't.
- (2015-08-22, Chris) Enterprise platforms (e.g. Sitecore) offer integrated email
  marketing and personalization ("Hey, welcome John"), but the license is expensive
  (~$25,000) — persona-driven needs like personalization have real platform/scope cost
  implications.
