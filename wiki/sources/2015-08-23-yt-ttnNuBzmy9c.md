## Summary

Part 4 of The Futur's "Unfiltered" conversation with **Imin Pao**, a
designer/creative-director who runs a multidisciplinary design agency operating in
China (works across architecture, product, illustration, and fashion design, and
cites having been commissioned by NEC for phone promotion in China). The host is
presumably Chris Do and/or Jose Caballer (The Futur / Blind), but **no host or
interviewer speech is present in the captured captions** — the file is entirely the
guest's answer. Chris Do's presence cannot be confirmed from the transcript; treat
this as a guest context page.

The single captured segment is Imin Pao's argument that conventional advertising is
"dead": that print/TV/online ads no longer solve a company's real problem, which is
product quality. He uses Apple as the model (product + store experience is what people
pay for, not commercials) and NEC as the anti-model (spent ~$100M in China advertising
a badly designed phone). He closes by framing his agency's edge (cross-discipline
network, credibility in China, owning their own product business) and noting that
advertising sits at the very end of a long product/manufacturing/supply-chain/sales
process.

## Key claims

_All attributed to **Imin Pao (guest)**, paraphrased unless quoted; dated 2015-08-23._

- **Advertising is dead / ineffective** (guest): Conventional advertising — print, TV,
  even online — no longer works, because it doesn't help the client or the business;
  the fundamental fix is to improve the product, not the message.
- **Product + store experience is what people pay for** (guest): Uses Apple as the
  example — few people remember the first iPhone commercial; people buy because they
  like the phone and enjoy the Apple Store experience. Brand spirit must transfer
  through the product first, then the shopping experience, and only lastly a little via
  advertising.
- **The NEC cautionary tale** (guest): His agency was commissioned by NEC in China,
  which spent ~$100M promoting a "disaster-looking" phone (pre-Nokia's decline). He
  calls the spend "a very effective way to tell the Chinese consumer how bad their
  phones are" — the money should have gone into R&D and design instead.
- **Ad agencies can't fix product** (guest): Companies "don't need a better ad, they
  need a better quality of product." Ad agencies have no clue how to design a better
  product and aren't incentivized to, because their money comes from large production
  shoots and media buys.
- **His agency's competitive edge** (guest): They can't out-serve big agencies like
  Ogilvy, so their strength is doing what no other agency can — pulling resources across
  architecture, product, illustration, and fashion design; a strong network ("we know
  everyone"), and high credibility overseas / in China.
- **"Put your money where your mouth is"** (guest): Believes a marketer without
  experience running their own business (loans, inventory, production) is "a naive
  person" who only understands the marketing end. His agency runs its own product
  business.
- **Advertising is the last step of a long process** (guest): Design, manufacturing,
  inventory, supply chain, and sales channels all have to be sorted before marketing;
  advertising is "just one very very end of the process." Where to display the product
  (online vs. store) is another dilemma.
