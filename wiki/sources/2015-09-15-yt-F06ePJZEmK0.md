# Profitable Small Business Ideas — How To Make Money (The Process AMA w/ Kier McLaren)

## Summary

Despite the clickbait title ("Profitable Small Business Ideas — How To Make Money"),
this is a live "Ask Me Anything" episode of *The Process* (Chris Do's pre-Futur / Skool
"Blind" era show) in which Chris Do interviews his longtime business consultant **Kier
McLaren** in front of a small studio audience with a few remote viewers. Co-host **Jose
Caballer** appears briefly in the cold open. The actual topic is **business consulting
for creative entrepreneurs** — specifically the sales conversation, running purposeful
client meetings, financial literacy, and profit awareness. It is not a "small business
ideas" listicle at all.

Kier (whom Chris calls a coach/mentor/friend, though Kier prefers "consultant") does
most of the talking. Chris frames Kier's impact on his own company — Kier helped Blind
break through a revenue "glass ceiling," roughly doubling revenue from ~$2.4M to ~$4M
in their first year working together, and saved Chris from a "catastrophic" decision in
their very first meeting. The bulk of the episode is Kier's advice to the creative
audience: know your numbers (have a P&L, track job actuals, understand gross vs. net
profit), never take underpriced work on the promise of "future work," go into every
meeting with a defined goal, ask direct questions instead of doing "meet-and-greet,"
qualify whether you're talking to the actual decision-maker, "stay out of the results,"
and get the deal terms in writing before doing any work.

Most substantive claims here are **Kier McLaren's (context / guest — not persona
training material)**. Chris Do's own contributions (persona) are mostly framing,
personal testimonials about working with Kier, and reflections on creatives' fear of
asking.

## Key claims

**Kier McLaren (consultant — CONTEXT, not persona):**
- [2015-09-15] He works mostly with entrepreneurs, currently heavily in motion graphics;
  has also worked in music and documentary businesses, and once ran a small ad agency
  "back east." (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] Artists come out of art school knowing art but not business; sheer talent,
  willpower and long hours build the business to a point — then those same traits start
  working *against* further growth. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] He isn't interested in working with people who "simply want to make money,"
  because making money is "not that hard"; making money *and* having a balanced life that
  means something is the hard part. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] Change is the thing most people (himself included) are unwilling to do;
  clients often spend the first six months explaining why they can't follow his advice.
  If you're not willing to change, no one can help you. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] Cites a Nobel-winning psychologist (Daniel Kahneman, unnamed in transcript;
  "Moses/ark" trick illustrates "System 1") — most people default to fast, automatic
  decisions and avoid the effortful thinking that real change requires. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] First qualifying question to a prospective client: "Do you have a P&L?" —
  many people who've made millions don't know what that means. You must be able to read
  it (even on a legal pad) or you don't know how your business operates financially.
  (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] You must "actualize jobs" — track what you actually spent on a job vs. what
  you sold it for — to know your real profit. Businesspeople care about *net*, not gross.
  (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] A $50,000 job can make more money as a percentage than a $500,000 job; you
  must know the numbers to choose the right work. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] Taking a job for little/no money on the promise the client will come back
  with more work "never happens" — analogous to underpaying a car dealer expecting a
  future favor. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] Clients tell you what kind of client they'll be; most people are poor
  listeners with "happy ears" and hear what they want. Ask directly whether the person is
  the decision-maker — usually they aren't and must sell you upward. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] On a sales call the worst answer is "maybe" — worse than "no" — because it
  wastes your time. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] Every meeting must have a goal; "meet-and-greet" wastes time. Anecdote: a
  client who declared a clear goal in a meeting won the deal over someone who wanted a
  vague "get to know each other" meeting. Slade Smiley (Real Housewives of Orange County)
  anecdote: waited a year for a meeting, opened with small talk, and was thrown out.
  (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] Your professionalism/expertise is demonstrated by the *questions you ask*,
  not by claiming to be an expert; he distrusts people who pose as experts. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] "Stay out of the results" (a concept from therapy/Al-Anon): you owe
  yourself only to present yourself and your company as well as possible; you have no
  control over the outcome. Illustrated with the film *My Date with Drew*. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] Get money and some form of deal in writing before doing any work; the
  formality scales with the relationship — a handshake with someone you know up to a
  100-page contract (e.g. a four-paragraph agreement for an unenforceable China deal built
  on trust). (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] Have a short "retellable story" that lets a non-decision-maker sell you
  upward; tells his own Job Corps / Pittsburgh story to demonstrate he can manage
  difficult people. (paraphrase)

**Chris Do (SUBJECT — persona):**
- [2015-09-15] Has worked with Kier as a consultant for over 10 years; found him after a
  friend mentioned a consultant who grew his business 200% in revenue. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] In their very first ~20–30 minute meeting, Kier saved him from a "fatal"
  business decision — "probably saved me hundreds of thousands of dollars." (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] His company was stuck at a "glass ceiling" around $2.4M revenue; the first
  year with Kier it went to ~$4M and "never looked back." [self-reported]. Notes that his
  wife is also his business partner. (paraphrase)
  _(He names his wife in the video; name withheld here per SUBJECT.md family-privacy policy —
  family members' names are treated as not-public.)_
- [2015-09-15] Creatives are sensitive and empathetic and associate "asking/selling" with
  the despised aggressive car-salesman stereotype, so they avoid asking — Kier taught him
  you can ask without being crass. (paraphrase)
- [2015-09-15] He now asks people what they want *before* agreeing to a meeting; often
  someone won't state it until asked three or four times. People who fail to make their
  ask lose the opportunity to the person who does. (paraphrase)
