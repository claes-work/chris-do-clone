# UX Design 3: How To Design a Website: Prioritize Features pt.1

## Summary

Episode in the 2015 "The Process" UX Design series on the (then) Futur channel.
The teaching is **led by Jose Caballer**; Chris Do co-hosts as interviewer/scribe,
asking clarifying questions, standing in for the audience, and writing idea cards.
Jose owns the framework and the walkthrough throughout; almost all instructional
content is Jose's. Chris's contributions are prompts, restatements, and light
participation, so most of this page is **CONTEXT**, not persona voice.

The episode continues a running project designing a website for "OES" ("Ole's"),
described as a real fishing-lodge client. Prior episodes established brand
attributes, user profiles, business goals ("the core"), a site audit, user needs,
and user stories. This episode covers the step **between** those inputs and
wireframes/sitemap: a **feature-prioritization / feature-definition exercise**.
Jose demonstrates a technique he jokingly calls "basket weaving" — laying user
needs on one axis and prioritized business goals on the other, then generating
site features and functionality where the two intersect. He works the example
through one user persona, "Dan" (later also references Phil and Joe), across the
awareness/marketing goal band (revenue and efficiency bands are deferred to pt.2).

## Key claims

- **[Jose, 2015-09-23]** Before wireframes or a sitemap, you must first define the
  site's structure and content; feature prioritization is the missing middle step
  between user stories and building the site. (paraphrase)
- **[Jose, 2015-09-23]** Great user experience = helping users find what they need
  faster by reducing the time between what you see, comprehension, and what you do;
  design "intentional user experience" that guides the user to a target action (for
  OES, booking a trip) in a way that delights them. (paraphrase)
- **[Jose, 2015-09-23]** Apply the Pareto principle (80/20 rule) to features: clients
  want everything and every competitor feature, but budget and timeline (six-seven
  months) force focus — prioritize rather than build it all. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2015-09-23]** States the 80/20 rule as "80% of your revenue comes from
  20% of your effort" (or clients). (paraphrase — Chris answering Jose's prompt)
- **[Jose, 2015-09-23]** The user-journey stages driving prioritization: awareness
  (how they find out) → engagement → conversion (paying is the ultimate engagement)
  → retention (return) → referral (recommend) → revenue (purchase). (paraphrase)
- **[Jose, 2015-09-23]** The "basket weaving" method: take the top ~3 prioritized
  user needs and the prioritized business goals, cross them, and read features out
  of the intersections; done for all users it visually resembles a woven basket.
  Use color-coding (blue = user needs, green = business goals) and Post-it notes,
  ideally with as many team members/client stakeholders as possible to maximize
  ideas before prioritizing. (paraphrase)
- **[Jose, 2015-09-23]** For the "Dan"/fancy-Tech-CEO persona, the key needs are:
  understanding his goals (bespoke service), scheduling a group/mixer event, and
  custom pictures/momentos for his group; his real goal is that his clients have a
  good time and he walks away with business. (paraphrase)
- **[Jose, 2015-09-23]** Business goals in the awareness band — latest trip pictures,
  email newsletter, SEM/AdWords, Facebook ads — are too vague alone; crossing them
  with a specific user's needs yields concrete features (e.g. a "trip planner"
  feature, "mix business and pleasure" newsletter link, corporate/custom-trip SEM
  campaigns, Facebook ads showing prior corporate-trip photos, CEO case studies).
  (paraphrase)
- **[Jose, 2015-09-23]** A revenue-expanding idea: partner with corporate
  team-building / "digital detox" / retreat companies (nature and mindfulness
  focused) to reach a new market segment; cites that large US corporations spend on
  average ~$1,700 per employee on training and coaching. (paraphrase — figure is
  Jose's self-reported recollection from a prior consulting engagement, unverified)
