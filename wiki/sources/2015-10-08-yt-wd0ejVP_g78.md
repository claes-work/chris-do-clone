# How to Start & Run a Design Business: Pricing & Estimating Creative Design Jobs

An "Ask Me Anything" episode of *The Process* (The Futur / The Skool era). Co-hosts **Chris Do** and **Jose Caballer** answer business questions from a guest, **Frank Shi** (2013 Otis College graduate, digital media), who is starting a studio-production business in partnership with a Chinese production company. The bulk of the substantive answers — and the entire pricing/estimating walkthrough — come from Chris Do. Jose Caballer appears mainly in the intro banter.

## Summary

Chris Do walks Frank through the fundamentals of pricing, estimating, and running a small creative/production business. The centerpiece is a live whiteboard demonstration of how to build up a proper bid — starting from a naive hourly-rate calculation ($5,000) and layering in markup, a producer, an art director, workstation/hardware rental, overhead, and a profit margin to arrive at ~$20,400. The core lesson: you cannot charge what you pay a contractor; you must mark up labor, account for people/roles you may not literally hire, cover overhead, and add profit. Chris also covers payment terms (50/25/25 with net 30), the two ways to make money (bid high vs. spend less), value-based mission statements, selling confidence/people over portfolio/process, being upfront about risk with vendors and clients, cash-flow survival (lines of credit, running lean), remote/fluid vs. brick-and-mortar teams, and company culture. He cites *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto* (Blair Enns) and *Delivering Happiness* (Tony Hsieh).

## Key claims

All dated 2015-10-08. Paraphrased unless quoted. Attributed to Chris Do unless noted.

**Payment & cash protection**
- (Chris Do) Get paid upfront — many designers do work and never collect. Getting money upfront is how you know the job is real. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) Recommended payment structure: 50% upfront, another 25% about three-quarters of the way through, and the final 25% on delivery or net 30 (pay in full within 30 days or pay interest). This way you only risk the final 25%, which should be your profit margin. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) The risk of not getting paid upfront: a big client could go bankrupt mid-project and leave you holding the bill — this has happened to many studio owners. [paraphrase]

**Partnerships**
- (Chris Do) In a partnership, discuss the exit/separation scenario upfront. Partnerships eventually fall apart; "nothing is forever." Surfacing the conflict early lets you sleep at night. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) Someone in the partnership must know how to bid; two creatives with no bidding skill is a gap. [paraphrase]

**Sales, roles, and the two ways to make money**
- (Chris Do) Sales and marketing are everything; robust sales and big-budget projects let you fund and run the company how you want. Without cash flow you suffer severely. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) Two types of executive producer: (1) new-business/sales hustler — outgoing, gregarious, short attention span, weak on details; (2) head-of-production type — does the bidding, calls, setup, and closes jobs. Some people do both, but usually stronger at one. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) Two ways to make money: bid high to build a profit cushion, OR spend less to produce the project. A $1M job where you spend $999,999 is really a $1 job; a $100K job where you spend $50K yields $50K gross profit. What matters is what you keep after all expenses (including paying yourself and your partner). [paraphrase — dollar figures clearly stated in transcript]

**Client power & pricing psychology**
- (Chris Do) Clients don't have all the power — "they have as much power as you're willing to give to them." Address budget/schedule concerns upfront like professionals. [paraphrase + quote below]
- (Chris Do, citing Blair Enns / *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto*) We underbid projects and later regret it. Test: when the client calls, are you eager to pick up or grimacing? A client who needs lots of revisions and hand-holding that you resent usually signals you charged too little. Lower-paying clients tend to be harder to deal with. [paraphrase]

**Selling without a track record**
- (Chris Do) You sell your company mostly on confidence, not your body of work. Even an award-winning portfolio won't matter if you don't represent yourself well. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) Chris sells himself as a problem solver / "business designer," not as the person on the software (he admits being rusty in After Effects). He's selling confidence and assurance. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) You don't actually need work samples to get work — if he sold all his company assets including the portfolio he could still go get work, because he sells who he is and what he can do. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) Two selling mistakes: (1) selling the portfolio, (2) selling the process. Instead, sell the people — they're the ones who make the work happen. [paraphrase]

**Mission statements & value**
- (Chris Do) Write a mission statement around the value you provide to customers, not about yourself. Profile your customers (ad agencies, entrepreneurs, startups), identify their pain points, and pick which pain points you solve. References The Skool's "Core 1.7" kit exercises to break it down. [paraphrase]

**Bidding / estimating framework (the whiteboard walkthrough)**
- (Chris Do) How NOT to bid: calculate your hours × an hourly rate and submit that. Example: $50/hr × a 10-hour day = $500/day × 10 days = $5,000. That's not a business — no profit, no room for error. [paraphrase; figures stated]
- (Chris Do) The right frame: ask "how much would I have to pay someone else to do this job?" and bid from that — and pay yourself too. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) Worked example build-up (numbers from transcript):
  - Artist: $500/day × 10 days = $5,000 (raw cost).
  - Mark up the artist's rate because artists underestimate the work: $750/day × 10 days = $7,500 (a potential $2,500 profit if all goes well — "which it never does in visual effects").
  - Add a producer/coordinator to manage the project: $400/day × 10 days = $4,000. (Include it even if you produce it yourself — someone must manage the job.)
  - Add an art director (must be more than the artist's rate): $900/day × 5 days (prorated across the job) = $4,500.
  - Running subtotal ≈ $16,000.
  - Add a workstation/PC (3D) rental — roughly $500/week × 2 weeks = $1,000; roll hardware/software in. Subtotal ≈ $17,000.
  - Add profit/overhead markup, typically 10–30% (uses 20% in the example) = ~$3,400. Final bid ≈ $20,400.
  - Contrast: the naive bid was $5,000; the proper bid is $20,400 (a $15,400 difference).
  > ⚠️ ATTENTION (arithmetic in source): The on-the-fly math is loose. $7,500 + $4,000 + $4,500 = $16,000 (stated); +$1,000 workstation = $17,000; 20% of $17,000 = $3,400; $17,000 + $3,400 = $20,400. The internal totals reconcile, but the numbers are rounded/approximated live and Chris admits "my math is not so good." Treat exact figures as illustrative, not precise.
- (Chris Do) You can adjust a bid via rate OR number of days, but Chris prefers adjusting the rate — deadlines are often fixed, so you can't just add days. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) You can also bill for things outside your control like rendering. [paraphrase]

**Hiring risky specialists / VFX pitfalls**
- (Chris Do) The VFX world is fraught because you come in under budget and scope is open-ended ("we don't like it, keep working"). Few VFX companies survive because they underbid and struggle to manage expensive artists. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) Specialists from big studios rely on infrastructure (dedicated modeling/lighting departments, technical directors optimizing render code from 4 hours/frame down to ~10 minutes). Dropped into a small generalist team without that pipeline, they can't produce — not incompetence, just missing infrastructure. Houdini artists in particular need a whole team. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) Be upfront with vendors/artists (a recurring business theme): state your team size and needs, and set a trial — "I'm willing to try you for X days; if you're not hitting it I need to move on." Cut losses quickly and give people an out to admit they can't do it (ego/pride runs high in the male-dominated effects world). Honesty beats over-booking and then un-booking someone. [paraphrase]

**Cash flow & surviving year one**
- (Chris Do) Get a bank line of credit if you have credit history — companies use it to fill the gap against pending orders (take the invoice/contract to the bank). Chris states The Futur/his company had roughly a $400,000 line of credit with Wells Fargo. [paraphrase; figure stated]
- (Chris Do) Run lean: rent everything, buy IKEA/Office Depot furniture ($79 chair, not a Herman Miller Aeron), work on crates and boxes like a startup or like you did in college. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) When a job comes in, don't spend the money right away (don't buy a new car off a $100K job). Live within your means. The place to spend money you don't have is an educated, calculated risk on a key hire ahead of expected work — "that's what entrepreneurship is about." [paraphrase]

**Remote/fluid vs. brick-and-mortar teams**
- (Chris Do) Pros of a fluid/remote model: hire anyone anywhere, higher quality of life for artists, usually lower cost by leveraging local economies (Brazil, Korea, India, China — noting Japan won't be cheap). Cons: harder communication, more project-management overhead (asset sync, software versions), and the best artists migrate to big markets (SF, LA, NY, London), so remote quality may be lower. Tools mentioned: Trello, Basecamp, Slack. [paraphrase]

**Company culture**
- (Chris Do) Company culture is one of the most important things to get right early. Freelancers/staff weigh pay, quality of work, and how they're treated/managed. If you can't compete on price or project quality, compete on treatment and management — that's your competitive advantage. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do) Protect and nourish people's long-term careers rather than using them up; avoid taking jobs that are too big/short-staffed and burn everyone out. Chris says he's been doing this ~20 years and is as passionate as day one because he learned to manage projects, time, and scope. [paraphrase]
- (Chris Do, citing Tony Hsieh / *Delivering Happiness*) Zappos' thin margins meant culture was their only edge; Hsieh sold Zappos to Amazon for ~$1 billion. Hsieh favors "work-life integration" over work-life balance and lives in a Las Vegas trailer-park community. [paraphrase]
