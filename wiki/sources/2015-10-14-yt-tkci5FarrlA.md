# UX Design 3: How to Design a Website: Prioritize Features pt.2

## Summary

Third installment of the 2015 UX Design teaching series on The Futur, a live
whiteboard workshop demonstrating website-design methodology on a real
example client ("Oolies," an outdoor/adventure business on the west-central
coast of Canada) with a mock stakeholder named Dan. The session is **led
primarily by Jose Caballer**, who drives the feature-generation and
prioritization exercise at the whiteboard while a student/designer participates
and Chris Do interjects as commentator, co-host, and occasional teacher.

The exercise: (1) take the top-scored revenue goals (bookings, online store,
corporate packages / younger customers), (2) generate feature ideas by
"mashing" pairs of goals together, then (3) prioritize those ideas on a 2×2
matrix of **value-to-customer** (low→high) vs. **difficulty/complexity**
(low→high), aiming for the easy-and-high-value quadrant. Jose also names the
"MoSCoW" prioritization method (must/should/could/won't have) as an
alternative. The bulk of the substantive content — idea generation, the matrix
technique, the Steve Jobs "creativity is connecting things" riff, the "you
don't have to do the exercises if you're a genius" bit — is **Jose's**. Chris
Do's contributions are mostly framing/wrap-up: reinforcing that UX design is
about solving a business problem (increasing revenue) rather than "making
pretty stuff," and warning against "feature-ritis."

**Attribution caveat:** This is a two-host conversational transcript with no
speaker labels; several exchanges are hard to assign with certainty. Lines
below are attributed on a best-effort basis. Jose is the "I love that all my
ideas are awesome" self-referential speaker and the whiteboard driver;
Chris Do is the co-host who closes the show ("Thanks very much from Jose and
myself").

## Key claims

- **[2015-10-14] (Jose, paraphrase)** Generate feature ideas by combining
  ("mashing together") two of the client's prioritized goals — e.g. bookings ×
  online store → custom-logo merchandise/swag; corporate packages × younger
  customers → team-building / goals-assessment forms.
- **[2015-10-14] (Jose, paraphrase)** Creativity is pattern-recognition
  (attributed to Steve Jobs): creative people see patterns, and you can only
  see patterns among things you already know — so go out and see more things.
- **[2015-10-14] (Jose, paraphrase)** Prioritize generated features on a 2×2
  matrix — value-to-customer (low→high) against difficulty/complexity for the
  developer (low→high). Target the easy-and-high-value quadrant; use Post-it
  notes so items can be moved as they're compared against a benchmark.
- **[2015-10-14] (Jose, paraphrase)** The point of the exercise is to remove
  subjectivity from design decisions by measuring every feature against the
  ultimate goal (increasing revenue). "Without the revenue, there's no
  business."
- **[2015-10-14] (Jose, paraphrase)** The exercises are designed for groups /
  team participation, ideally including the client and the team that will build
  the site; doing them solo is possible but less effective.
- **[2015-10-14] (Jose, paraphrase)** MoSCoW (must-have / should-have /
  could-have / won't-have) is another prioritization exercise done on index
  cards; Googling "prioritization exercises" surfaces roughly ten methods.
- **[2015-10-14] (Chris Do, paraphrase)** UX design is about deeply
  understanding the company and solving a business problem, not making pretty
  stuff; a great design is done "with a purpose."
- **[2015-10-14] (Chris Do, paraphrase)** Beware "feature-ritis" — the urge to
  throw every feature in (e.g. a video editor on the site) when it provides low
  value to the customer. Low-value features are things you should not build.
- **[2015-10-14] (Chris Do, paraphrase)** Many designers, when asked to "design
  a website," merely give it a facelift / reskin an existing site instead of
  looking at "the bones and the structure" — real UX work is structural.
- **[2015-10-14] (Jose, paraphrase, read aloud)** Definition of good UX: it
  "helps users find what they need faster by reducing the time between
  comprehension and action" — easy to understand and easy to do — and it is
  built around what the user wants, not what you want.
