# The Client Is Not Your Enemy: Redefining Your Client Relationship

★ L3-candidate — packs several distinct client-relationship frameworks (let go of the artist persona; "happy ears"; embrace-and-pivot / mental jujitsu; ask "why" three times; shut up when the client says yes) that go beyond a generic sales-clients hub page and are strong voice-training material.

## Summary

A talk Chris Do gave at Cal State LA (2015-10-20, "The Process" era, before The Futur's later rebranding) on the theme of "leveling up," focused specifically on client interaction. Speaking to freelancers, students, and agency employees, Chris argues that the widespread designer sentiment "my clients have bad taste / clients ruin the work / I need to educate the client" is the wrong frame. He reframes the client not as an enemy but as the person who makes the creative's lifestyle possible, and lays out a sequence of skills for a better client relationship: (1) let go of the "artist persona" and accept you serve clients; (2) build empathy by remembering what it is to be a client (hair-salon analogy); (3) define a clear goal before making anything, using human language and probing questions; (4) become an active listener (illustrated via a "21 questions" audience game and the "Garfield" reveal); (5) handle objections with "mental jujitsu" — embrace and pivot rather than confront; and (6) reframe selling as solving a problem, and stop talking once the client says yes. Solo lecture; all content attributed to Chris Do.

## Key claims

All claims below are paraphrased from Chris Do unless marked as a direct quote in the next section. Dated to the talk: 2015-10-20.

- **Purpose beyond money.** Chris says his early purpose was to make enough money to retire young, but "if you work to retire, it's kind of hard to get up in the morning." He redefined his purpose as "to teach a million people the business of design and to empower creatives to achieve their dreams" (2015-10-20).
- **Goals are about who you become.** Chris claims a big goal matters less as a destination than for the person you become while pursuing it — "it's the journey that matters" (2015-10-20).
- **The "bad client" sentiment is the problem, not the client.** Chris names the popular designer complaints — clients have bad taste, clients ruin the work, "I need to educate the client," the work is thankless — and frames the rest of the talk as flipping that view (2015-10-20).
- **Let go of the artist persona.** Chris draws a fork in the road: the "artist" (Pollock, Van Gogh, Warhol) works only to share their own vision, serves no client, is rarely paid, and only a lucky few reach fame; the working professional accepts they have clients. He argues creatives should stop complaining because clients "are the ones who make our lifestyle, our art, our design, our passion possible" (2015-10-20).
- **Working for your portfolio is the classic mistake.** Chris shares that early on, doing storyboards for main titles (hired by Kyle Cooper at R/GA LA, now Imaginary Forces), he didn't care if the pitch won or lost — he only wanted to make "really cool boards." He calls this "design masturbation," a selfish act that doesn't serve the client (paraphrase + labeled quote; 2015-10-20). Registry note: the R/GA / Kyle Cooper / Imaginary Forces detail is a self-reported biographical claim.
- **He started his company right out of school in 1995** with no experience or clients, and says his business has gone "up and down this arc many times," grown and been "reduced down to nothing" (self-reported; 2015-10-20).
- **Benefits of a great client relationship (three-fold):** (1) you become more valuable — value has dollars attached, moving you off low-end $2k–$5k sites; (2) you get more work and better word-of-mouth — good clients refer you upward toward bigger brands; (3) you become happier. Chris claims that as of 2015, after ~20 years, he was working directly for the first time with a publicly traded company, and that a client will call on a Friday just to say they appreciate him (self-reported; 2015-10-20).
- **Bad word-of-mouth from "educating" clients.** Chris argues that a client saying "my designer tried to educate me / told me I'm ruining the design" is insulting and not the referral you want (2015-10-20).
- **Empathy — remember what it is to be a client.** Chris says he is also a client (of architects, attorneys, financial planners) and wants to "be served well, treated with respect, and listened to." Hair-salon analogy: if a client asks for "the Tom Cruise haircut," a good stylist probes ("which Tom Cruise?"), assesses hair texture and face shape, resets expectations, and personalizes — so the client walks away happy even without looking exactly like Tom Cruise (2015-10-20).
- **Define a clear goal before making anything.** Chris warns against the "maker mentality" of rushing to make things without understanding the problem; when the client hates the result, designers blame the client. Analogy: before designing a house, ask what style (modern, Cape Cod, Mediterranean), who it's for, how it will be used, where it's located (2015-10-20).
- **Use human language, not technical/design jargon.** Asking a client whether a site is "responsive, native, adaptive" intimidates them; nobody wants to seem stupid, so they just go along. Understand the users and their needs, then prescribe a solution (2015-10-20).
- **"Happy ears" (a sales term).** Chris says he learned this term ~a year and a half before the talk: it means hearing what you want to hear and ignoring contradicting evidence — like the "locomotive train" that only goes forward or stops but never turns. You need the ability to turn and maneuver (2015-10-20).
- **Active listening is hard and asking beats talking.** You must quiet the thoughts in your head, keep an open mind, and let the evidence lead you to the next step. "Asking is more important than talking" (2015-10-20).
- **Each question should filter the realm of possibilities.** "Build a website" is too broad and impossible to solve; start broad, cut the possibilities in half each time, form a hypothesis, then get very specific. Beware vague, open-ended questions — vague questions get big answers, and you have only yourself to blame (2015-10-20). Illustrated via the "21 questions" game where the audience failed to guess "Garfield"; Chris also notes active listening includes reading body language and tone (his pause on "yellow").
- **Mental jujitsu — embrace and pivot.** When facing a client objection (after listening carefully), embrace it and pivot so you're both on the same team, rather than confronting head-on. Human analogy: someone walking straight at you forces fight-or-flight; someone walking beside you pointing at something is not confrontational — you want to face the same direction as the client (2015-10-20).
- **The consistency trap.** If a client says "make it bigger" and you say "no, keep it small," you force them to defend their position, because people have a psychological need to be consistent — even if they only meant "a bit bigger," they now dig in. You paint them into a corner (2015-10-20).
- **Ask "why" three times to get to the root.** Chris's example: client says "make it bigger" → asking why reveals the real issue is contrast (yellow type on white, hard to read, poor eyesight), not size. Offer a darker yellow at the next meeting. This addresses the *need* vs. the *want* (2015-10-20).
- **Reframe selling as solving a problem.** People hate selling because of bad experiences with pushy salespeople (car, door-to-door, telemarketer, aggressive retail). Chris's definition: "selling is about providing a solution to a problem — if there's no problem, I'm not selling." Ask about pain points ("tell me a day in your life at work / your pain points with the website"), then ask yourself which of those you can solve (2015-10-20).
- **Qualify on budget and fit early.** Chris says he wants to know up front whether a client can afford him — he won't spend three meetings discovering they have only $2,000 for an e-commerce site he can't build for that. But don't blindside a client by asking "why don't you hire me?" at the start — build rapport first (2015-10-20).
- **When the client says yes, shut up.** People keep talking after the yes and talk themselves out of the sale. Jordan Belfort ("Wolf of Wall Street," a real person) car-buying story: the Mercedes salesperson kept pitching features and lost the sale; at BMW and Porsche, the moment Belfort said he wanted to buy, they just did the paperwork and handed over the keys. "When somebody is ready to buy, shut up and get out of the way." And if they say no, ask why — you can turn a negative into a positive and learn from it (2015-10-20).
