# IS YOUR DESIGN RESUME STILL RELEVANT?

★ L3-candidate — adds a distinct, foundational career/positioning thesis ("the resume is dead; show the work, self-position deliberately, do direct outreach") that likely warrants promotion into `wiki/topics/` (design-craft/career + content-strategy/personal-brand) and `persona/beliefs.md`.

## Summary

Early "The Process" episode (2015) co-hosted by Chris Do and Jose Caballer on designing a resume — and whether designers should bother with one at all. Jose (context, not persona) opens by arguing 20th-century resumes (built on hard skills, experience, education) have been replaced by goals, aptitude, and attitude; he walks through three common resume mistakes (a generic word-template block of text; an unfocused / incongruent work history; being too technically front-loaded), champions the viral Nina Mufleh → Airbnb "resume" as a case study in proactive, value-first self-marketing, and points to online platforms (LinkedIn, Behance, Re.vu, About.me) over paper.

Chris then delivers the episode's contrarian core: **don't work on your resume — nobody cares; show the work.** He argues today's resume lives on LinkedIn and the work lives on Behance/Vimeo; a hirer decides in ~60 seconds from a link; going through HR means "you're already screwed," so do your homework, find the decision-maker/creative director, and connect directly. He also stresses deliberate self-positioning (he now describes himself as a "business design consultant," not a graphic designer), building thought-leadership content (posts, articles, video), and investing time in beautiful in-context mockups (via Behance portfolios, Creative Market templates) instead of resume polish.

## Key claims

Attribution: Chris Do = persona-eligible. Jose Caballer = context only (co-host), labeled below.

- **[Jose, 2015-11-29] (context)** Resumes were designed for the 20th century, based on hard skills, experience, and education; today employers/collaborators look at goals, aptitude, and attitude. (paraphrase)
- **[Jose, 2015-11-29] (context)** Three basic resume mistakes: (1) a generic block of text on a standard word template; (2) an unfocused resume whose stated objective is incongruent with the actual work history (e.g., seeking a media role but leading with unrelated jobs); (3) being too technically focused — leading with software proficiency instead of experience. Fix #2 by cutting irrelevant history and focusing everything around the relevant role. (paraphrase)
- **[Jose, 2015-11-29] (context)** Padding a resume with irrelevant jobs (high school, Taco Bell, etc.) signals fluffing and lack of confidence, not experience — it's an "alarm bell." One relevant thing beats a long list of unrelated ones. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2015-11-29]** Filling a resume with irrelevant information reads as "fluffing" — you're not confident in what you have, so you pad it; that's an alarm bell to him as a hirer. (paraphrase; agreeing with Jose)
- **[Jose, 2015-11-29] (context)** Nina Mufleh's viral Airbnb "resume" is the model: technically simple (big JPEGs), but she led with value — a proactive market analysis and case study of what she'd do for Airbnb, drawing on her experience as an Airbnb guest and host. Focus on value relative to who you're sending it to. (paraphrase)
- **[Jose, 2015-11-29] (context)** Go online: LinkedIn, Behance, Re.vu (visual/infographic resume), About.me (visual + storytelling across Instagram/Twitter) beat paper resumes. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2015-11-29]** Designers over-invest in the resume as a typographic exercise, but it doesn't matter — "nobody cares." All a hirer needs is to see the work. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2015-11-29]** The 21st-century resume: your resume lives on LinkedIn and your work lives on Behance or Vimeo; a hirer makes a hire/no-hire decision in ~60 seconds from a link to the work. School, degree, GPA, valedictorian status — none of it matters. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2015-11-29]** If you're spending more than ~30 minutes on your resume, something else is wrong; stop working on your resume and work on your LinkedIn profile and portfolio instead. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2015-11-29]** Sending your resume through HR means the gatekeeper filters you out — "you're already screwed." Instead, do your homework, find out who makes the decisions / who the creative director is, connect with them directly, and send a link to your work. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2015-11-29]** Self-positioning is an evolutionary process: he stopped calling himself a graphic designer / director / motion graphics person and now describes himself as a "business design consultant" (also facilitator/teacher), testing what sticks and watching how people react. How you describe yourself — and who's looking — matters a lot. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2015-11-29]** Establish yourself as a thought leader by publishing posts/articles and embedding video on your profile; getting comfortable on camera as a "media entity" is increasingly important in the 21st century. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2015-11-29]** Spend time building beautiful in-context mockups (showing the design as a "living, breathing extension" in real environments — via Behance portfolios, or affordable Creative Market templates that comp your Illustrator/PSD files into perspective) rather than polishing a resume. (paraphrase)
- **[Jose, 2015-11-29] (context)** Bottom line: if you use a traditional resume (e.g., in marketing), make it different and unique; either way, go online, research who you want to work for, and build a relationship ("become a creeper"). (paraphrase)
