## Summary

Episode of "The Process" (the 2014–2016 co-hosted "The Skool" era) filmed before a live studio audience. Host **Chris Do** interviews guest **Yo Santosa** — identity/brand designer, founder and creative director of **Ferroconcrete**, and founder of other ventures (Fruit, La Downtowner). Co-host **Jose Caballer** appears briefly (opening banter, one aside). The conversation is a branding masterclass: what branding actually is (versus logo/identity design), how Yo builds brands as "matchmaking," her word-first process, giving brands personality (starting with the name), and reading customer sentiment. Chris repeatedly "reverse-engineers" Yo's instinct-driven process into an analytical framework and layers in his own material on speaking the language of business, taking equity for creative value, and walking into client meetings with self-belief. Audience Q&A covers clients who don't understand their customers, leading meetings, leading teams, and personal branding.

Guest note: **Yo Santosa** is a substantial, recurring-caliber figure in The Futur orbit (Ferroconcrete, Pinkberry branding). If she appears again across the corpus she likely warrants a `wiki/entities/` context page.

## Key claims

_All dated 2015-12-13. Paraphrased unless quoted below. Attributed per speaker._

- A logo is NOT a brand; a logo/identity system is only one facet of branding. A brand is a person's gut feeling about a product, service, or organization (explicitly crediting Marty Neumeier's definition). — Chris Do
- Designers commonly misuse "brand" when they really mean identity or logo; many "branding firms" are really identity-systems designers who don't do messaging. — Chris Do
- People buy what a company stands for, not the logo; we trust companies that believe what we believe (crediting Simon Sinek), and the things we buy signal our tribe/identity (Harley, Mercedes, Apple). — Chris Do
- Branding is deeply personal and instinctive — a "visceral gut instinct" she recognized even as a child (Lego vs. the cheap knockoff). Branding is the "invisible thread" pulling at your emotions. — Yo Santosa
- Branding = matchmaking: you can't change the customer, only the brand, so you make the brand attractive and compatible so the two "fall in love." — Yo Santosa
- Brand-building starts with WORDS before anything visual: gather the demographic, read how people talk about the brand online, harvest their words, then simplify relentlessly down to a single strong paragraph that inspires visual imagery. — Yo Santosa
- To research a brand, read how people talk about it on Facebook, Instagram, and blogs — "when they think the brand is not listening" — not product reviews; this reveals genuine sentiment. — Yo Santosa
- English's richness (many nuanced words for one meaning, e.g. hot/tempered/boiling) makes finding the precise "beautiful word" — witty, visual, precise — central to good brand copy. — Chris Do
- Warning sign: brand strategy full of fluffy, buzzword-laden language that means nothing; good brand statements sound like a real person and contain beliefs. — Chris Do (Yo Santosa agrees)
- Giving a brand personality starts with the NAME (chosen with the target demographic in mind), then a personality trait, then a tone of voice run through filters, then visual look-and-feel. — Yo Santosa
- A name/headline should be self-selecting — the audience instantly feels "that's me" or "not me"; you must plant a flag (e.g. "La Downtowner" = pride in downtown LA, deliberately not the West Side). — Chris Do
- Technique: take every dull, boring operational line and run it through the brand's voice filter (Pinkberry's "yo" filter — bubbly, fun, energetic — turned "open 9 to 5" into "swirling daily"). — Chris Do (illustrating Yo Santosa's Pinkberry work)
- With clients behind the curve, don't argue aesthetics (highly subjective); use business case studies and success stories, and speak the client's language: bottom line, market share, revenue, new markets, innovation (Target vs. Kmart example). — Chris Do
- Credibility often comes from external authority, not from you: a client believed the Harvard Business Review over the consultant's own (identical) advice — "inception"/Trojan-horse influence. — Yo Santosa (anecdote); Chris Do generalizes it (people believe named authorities/famous names over you)
- Designers are trained to argue design/color/aesthetics and therefore lose the argument every time with business clients; learn to talk business instead. — Chris Do
- Creatives create real value and habitually give it away; instead negotiate — take money to cover expenses PLUS equity for the intellectual capital contributed; define a scope, earn your shares, then move on (retainer at preferred rate if they want more). Everything is negotiable. — Chris Do
- Yo turned down multiple offers to shut her studio and go in-house (creative director + shares) because she gets bored on one brand and values the freedom of owning her own business above any money. — Yo Santosa
- Spreading yourself too thin across too many companies is a real mistake — she burned out and wasn't doing her best at Ferroconcrete while managing Fruit; the fix was putting great people in place from the start and guiding rather than doing day-to-day. — Yo Santosa
- "Ferroconcrete" = the technique of steel rebar inside poured concrete; a metaphor for building brands strong enough to become skyscrapers (identity is the concrete exterior, the rebar is the underlying strength). — Yo Santosa
- Life philosophy: make money doing what you love — loving it without money doesn't work, and money without loving it doesn't work. — Chris Do
- Redefine what you "love"/who you are more broadly (motion designer → image maker → problem solver); the broader the self-definition, the more valuable and happier you become (credits his business coach for the reframe). — Chris Do
- Business skills (management, marketing, negotiation, leadership) aren't taught in graphic design; you learn them in the real world where consequences are real (contrast: fail in school, you get a D and nobody cares). — Chris Do (Yo Santosa agrees she learned by doing)
- Leading client meetings: walk in chirpy/happy, show work, but mostly listen — it's about chemistry, reiterating what you heard, and selling your work well. — Yo Santosa
- Client meetings should be entered with extreme self-belief; real confidence is being vulnerable and transparent about who you are (the "I drive a Toyota" story) rather than name-dropping. Flip the narrative — you don't need them, you're equals. — Chris Do
- Chris deliberately goes into pitch meetings unprepared / without researching the client, to avoid jumping to conclusions; leads by saying he'll ask questions, shut up, and listen — which disarms and endears. — Chris Do
- Leadership style (Yo): inspire and excite the team, foster discussion, act as a guide rather than a disciplinarian; comes naturally (attributes it partly to being the oldest child). — Yo Santosa
- Personal branding = business branding applied to yourself: ask people what they think of you (it's their perception, not yours that defines the brand), write down the words, decide if you like them, and change deliberately if you don't. — Chris Do
- Pinkberry's brand voice is essentially an extension of Yo herself (bubbly, fun, energetic); a founder's brands tend to sound like the founder. — Chris Do (about Yo)
- Designing a business as "designer-first" (brand before market/pricing/operations) was a mistake with the Fruit cookie company; La Downtowner improved by prototyping and validating first (mocking up the magazine and asking hotels/restaurants if they'd carry it / advertise) — the idea/validate/iterate cycle. — Yo Santosa (Chris frames it as the software id8-validate-iterate loop)
- Copy example Chris improvised for Fruit ("good cookies get milk, great cookies deserve champagne") to show how brand voice should reflect the founder — while noting he's not the writer and would hire real writers. — Chris Do

## Notable verbatim quotes

### Chris Do — persona voice (training data)

> "I don't believe logos are brands. That's not branding at all. It's a mark, it's an identity system."

> "Marty Neumeier's definition of brand is a person's gut feeling about a product, service or an organization. It's a feeling, it's not a mark."

> "Please do not confuse a logo as a brand. A brand has to have a voice, a personality, has touchpoints — because you're not buying the Apple logo, you're buying everything that the company stands for."

> "Take every dull boring thing that they do that sounds like a voice, run it through the brand filters."

> "Let's stop talking about graphics, colors, aesthetics… those things are highly subjective. Let's talk about real things… bottom line, market share, revenue, new markets, innovation. So let's learn to talk the language of business."

> "As designers we've been trained too much to talk about design, graphics and color… you're losing that argument every single time."

> "We just give it away. We never sit to think, the value has been created — give me equity."

> "I want money to cover my expenses and I get equity for the intellectual capital that I'm bringing, and then we're done. I've earned my shares and I keep doing other things."

> "You need to go to meetings with extreme self belief… To me confidence is about being vulnerable and being transparent about who you are."

> "If I just walk in the room, 'Here, but I drive a Toyota,' it's out in the open and I don't give an F if you think that's cool or not. I just don't care."

> "I like to go in completely unprepared. I don't want to know anything about them, I don't do any research, because I find that then I don't jump to any conclusions… I'm going to ask you a few questions and I'm going to shut up and listen."

> "My whole philosophy in life… is to make money doing what I love. If it's just doing what I love, it's not gonna work for me. Just make me money, not loving it, not gonna work."

> "The broader I go, the more valuable I've become and the happier I've become."

> "It's not about you, it's what they think… you kind of write those words down and you start to think, this is what people know me as — do I like that? If I don't, I need to start to change things."

> "When you f up, somebody quits, you get fired, you lose money, you go upside down, you go bankrupt, you lose it all. When you're in school you get a D, who cares."

### Yo Santosa — GUEST (context, NOT persona voice)

> "For me branding is very personal… as a kid I already knew what a brand is. I don't know how to explain it but there's just something about it that's visceral, it's just gut instincts."

> "It's how a brand speaks, how a brand looks, what it stands for, and how honest and transparent they are as a company."

> "The way I look at branding is you're just a matchmaker. You're trying to make two things fall in love with each other."

> "It's all starts with words, just putting things together… the idea is to simplify, simplify, simplify, simplify."

> "It's more about how people talk about the brand… go on blogs, what people are saying, go on Instagram — it's a lot about listening to what people are saying when they think the brand is not listening."

> "Giving a brand personality starts with the name… then you start thinking about what would this person say, what would this brand say."

> "One of the things that I really really can't give up is the freedom — the freedom of owning your own business, the freedom of the struggle."

> "Ferroconcrete means when you put steel beams and you pour concrete into it… so it became a metaphor for building brands into skyscrapers."
