## Summary

A live workshop/lecture Chris Do gives to (apparently Art Center) design students in the early "The Process" era of The Futur. Chris is the sole teacher; the many named respondents (Eddie, Austin, Nick Hoffos, William, Ben, Simona, and others) are **students role-playing** objection responses — *context/demo, not persona data*. No co-host speaks in the substantive content.

Chris titles the talk "the art of the argument" and covers three things: determining value, understanding the client dynamic, and overcoming objections in the sales cycle. He names the **three most common client objections** ("you're too expensive," "you don't have enough experience," "you don't know enough about [domain]"), has students try to answer them (mostly by fighting head-on, which he uses as the anti-pattern), then teaches his alternative: don't charge in like a bull; use leverage like Jiu-Jitsu — **embrace the objection, make the client dig into their position, then pivot** so the alternative looks less desirable and you look different/better. He frames the sales cycle as **rock-paper-scissor**: you're the mid-size player who loses on experience to the giant firm and loses on price to the small/offshore shop, so head-on arguing is a trap. He grounds the pivot in the *Thank You for Smoking* "chocolate vs. vanilla" clip (nullify the argument / find the hole in their logic rather than prove yourself right), Cialdini's six principles of influence, and Blair Enns' *Win Without Pitching Manifesto* (kill the opportunity, raise objections first, retreat-and-follow, whisper-don't-shout). He closes by reading his own real-world scripts for each objection.

## Key claims

_All dated 2016-01-09. Attributed to Chris Do unless noted. Paraphrased. Student role-play lines are marked as demo/context and are NOT persona data._

**The three objections and the core method**
- The three most common client objections are: (1) you're too expensive, (2) you don't have enough experience, (3) you don't know enough about [vertical/domain — his running gag "digital broccoli websites"]. (Chris Do)
- "Convince" implies dragging the client from their point to yours — high friction; he wants to teach a different way. (Chris Do)
- Most people treat an objection as a one-to-one game — they raise it, you fire back an answer — but there are always other bidders in the equation, so even a "right" answer isn't enough. (Chris Do)
- Objections feel like an attack on your ego ("red meat to a dog," especially for "alpha males/females"); you feel invalidated and rush to prove them wrong — that instinct is the trap. (Chris Do)
- Don't charge head-on: "you're the bull, and you're not the bullfighter." Two head-on positions become two trains colliding; since you don't have the financial upper hand, you lose or the client walks. (Chris Do)
- The alternative is **Embrace and Pivot**, modeled on Jiu-Jitsu/Judo leverage vs. karate's hard power: use the opponent's momentum — embrace them, pivot, and throw them to the side with little force. (Chris Do)
- The method has four beats: (a) **own/embrace** the objection ("you're right, we are too expensive"); (b) **make them dig in** by affirming and validating their position (like the *Thank You for Smoking* setup); (c) **paint the alternative** as undesirable; (d) **pivot** to why you're different/better. (Chris Do)
- You must acknowledge the objection first — changing the subject without acknowledging it makes you look "slippery" and "distrustful," and clients are smart (they hire you because they're smarter than you at business). (Chris Do)

**The rock-paper-scissor / "middle player" model**
- The whole client-objection sales cycle is a game of rock-paper-scissor: you can beat one competitor but not all. (Chris Do)
- You are usually the middle player (Chris says Blind has been "the medium guys" for 20 years). The giant firm (the "rock"/"hammer" — 300–500 employees, global offices, researchers, MBAs/PhDs, better tools) beats you on experience/expertise/resources; the tiny one-or-two-person or offshore shop (the "scissor") beats you on price. (Chris Do)
- If you argue "we're worth it," the bigger, more-established company wins that argument; if you "reduce the price," the smaller player always wins — and reducing price usually means reducing scope, so the client just perceives "less for less." Either way you lose. (Chris Do)
- You cannot out-price a low-cost/offshore bidder (cites finding excellent, far cheaper talent on freelance marketplaces in Eastern Europe, Argentina, Brazil, Asia); reducing scope is giving in to price. (Chris Do)
- Being in the middle is a precarious "balancing act" — a technical term he jokes as balancing "on a chair on top of a table on top of another table on top of a ledge." (Chris Do)

**Why "embrace" works — persuasion / Cialdini**
- People strive for **consistency** with their prior commitments; when you contradict them ("blue" vs. their "red") they dig in harder to avoid looking like a fool — so never force them to dig in against *you*. (Chris Do, citing Robert Cialdini's *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*)
- Cialdini's six principles: **reciprocity, consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity**; five of the six are relevant to embrace-and-pivot. Learn them so you can use them for good and not be a victim of them. (Chris Do)
- The buyer on the other side is usually not the owner; if they make the wrong decision they can get fired — so clients gravitate to the *less risky* (bigger, expert) choice, which is why the big firm usually wins. (Chris Do)
- From the *Thank You for Smoking* "chocolate vs. vanilla" clip: **you don't need to prove you're right — just find the hole in the other person's logic / nullify their argument.** Nick (student) reframes: "to win the argument you have to nullify the argument." (Chris Do; student paraphrase noted)
- The clip's deeper mechanic (per student "Ben" and Chris): the debater first *affirmed and validated* the opponent 100% ("so it's all chocolate for you?") to make him dig in, THEN pivoted — so the pivot "drives the point home." (Chris Do)

**Win Without Pitching / retreat-and-follow (Blair Enns)**
- Recommends *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto* by Blair Enns as a "game changer." (Chris Do)
- When an opportunity first appears, "do your best to kill the opportunity" — it puts you in a position of power/leverage. (Chris Do, attributing Blair Enns)
- Creatives avoid areas of potential objection; do the opposite — **raise the objections first** and lay them on the table for the client to address. (Chris Do)
- **Retreat and follow:** try to kill the opportunity and back up; if they're interested, they follow (like reeling in a fish). Example script to a million-dollar company: "I'm not sure a company of your size can afford us" — now they have to prove to *you* that they can, flipping the client dynamic. (Chris Do) — an "advanced level maneuver" you must genuinely believe to pull off.
- **Whisper, don't shout:** whispering makes people lean in; shouting (selling/convincing) pushes them away. (Chris Do)

**Chris's actual scripts (real-world use)**
- Price objection: fully embrace ("we are expensive, but there's a good reason"), then paint most designers as "pretty makers" whose beautiful logos/foil stamps "won't fundamentally move the needle" or win one new client — "what I do is different." Defeat "too expensive" by not talking about money at all. (Chris Do)
- Domain-expertise objection (**Terry Richardson analogy**): a top fashion photographer often shoots on a cheap disposable camera; you come to him for *what he does*, so would you dictate his process/equipment/lens? "Don't dictate process to me." He tells a story that the last client who dictated process went over budget and didn't get what they wanted — so he'd rather turn the job down. (Chris Do)
- "You don't have enough resources" (real half-million-dollar job; competitor had 4 technical directors, Blind had 0): same Terry Richardson pivot — respect their concern but reframe it as irrelevant to outcome; they still won the job. (Chris Do)
- Experience objection: "we're really good learners… but I will forget, you will forget more than what I can learn — you're the experts." His **process transfers the client's organizational knowledge to his team**, translated into something customers understand and value; "I'm not trying to outgun you 'cause I'll lose." (Chris Do)
- Expert = least-risky-but-undifferentiated reframe: validate that the experts are good and low-risk, then note "they've done it for everyone else, including your competitors… if you want undifferentiated work dictated by a cookie-cutter process, we're not for you." (Chris Do)
- Differentiator/close script: "I help businesses grow and be more innovative by helping them understand their customers… I can't guarantee you success, but I will give you the best shot at winning." Then retreat/take-away: "if you don't need help in this way, save your money, hire the other guys… we'll be here in six months when you need help." (Chris Do)

**Self-reported results**
- In the (brand-strategy) space he'd been working in for only "three or four months," he'd won 100% of the jobs despite not being the incumbent, having no domain expertise, and not competing on the work or on charm. (Chris Do — self-reported)
- Says he's "been doing business for 20 years," always the "medium" player going up against "the monsters." (Chris Do — self-reported)
- Also recommends Cialdini's *Influence* and a closing "hit list" of books on branding, presentations, and motivation (titles not enumerated in captions). (Chris Do)

**Student role-play responses (DEMO / CONTEXT — not persona data)**
- To "you're too expensive," students fired back head-on: "you're poor," "you get what you pay for," "for you I'll bring it down 20%" (a deliberate price-inflation/wiggle-room strategy Chris calls good), and "think of it as an investment — my goal is to make you money" (which Chris praises). One student shifted cost→value (what's the value of the design if it solves all your problems / delivers ROI) — Chris affirms this. (students; attribution: these are demo answers)
- To "you don't have enough experience": "we give you the tools you need" (Austin — Chris notes he wins on charm/liking), "neither do you," "my work ethic overcomes experience gaps," "no experience = fresh ideas" (Chris: "that's marketing/spin"), "but I'm cheaper" (William). (students; demo)
- To "you don't know enough about [domain]": "open up to more perspective," repeated "we give you the [domain] website, we'll fill it." (students; demo)
