# How To Be A Great Facilitator

## Summary

A coaching conversation from The Futur's early "The Process" / CORE era (2016). **Chris Do
leads and teaches** throughout: he answers questions from **Dave**, a real-life member of
the community who is in town for a couple of days and wants to run CORE (The Futur's branded
Discovery / brand-strategy facilitation framework) more effectively. Chris consistently
references **Jose Caballer in the third person** ("Jose has done this before," "Jose is a
very fast thinker... he likes the magic of the reveal"), contrasting Jose's fast, reveal-in-
the-room style against his own more cerebral, process-later approach — which confirms Chris
is the on-camera teacher here, not Jose. Jose is present only as a referenced authority, not
a speaker. Dave asks the questions; Chris does all the teaching.

Core throughline: run the framework by the book first, then adapt it to your own personality
and strengths. Chris covers sequencing (user profiles before brand), the value of capturing
negative/less-desired brand truths rather than sanitizing them, giving voice to marginalized
people in the room, disarming cynics through transparency and calling-it-out, and the
"brands are people too" argument for authenticity (Domino's Pizza turnaround as case study).

## Key claims

All claims below are **Chris Do (2016-01-23)** unless noted; all paraphrased unless in the
verbatim section.

- Do the work the way *you* are comfortable doing it — if you're uncertain, the room picks
  up on your uncertainty and you lose credibility.
- Chris prefers to build **user profiles first, then the brand** (Jose/CORE-by-the-book
  starts with brand, then personas). Rationale: **design thinking** — understand people,
  their jobs, pains, and gains, and solve a real person's problem rather than building a
  product and marketing it outward. Doing personas first also resolves the "are we describing
  the brand as it is today or as we want it to be?" confusion.
- Companies that hire you are usually in a transitionary period (wanting growth, or facing
  declining sales / an aging or departing user base); doubling down on existing strategy
  won't move the needle.
- Learn the basic principles before tweaking them — "it's dangerous to try to tweak something
  before you actually learn the basic principles." Follow the teacher's method first, then
  evolve it once you have a grasp.
- **Martial-arts metaphor (attributed to Jose):** CORE is like "a martial art" generically,
  not a specific discipline. Confrontational/high-friction/big personalities map to karate or
  taekwondo (powerful, fast, immediate results); Chris casts himself as more judo / jiu-jitsu
  — using leverage, fluidity, and intellect. Aaron's addition: some just need "core light,"
  like Krav Maga — enough to survive on the street.
- Practice CORE on each other within the community — experiencing it *done to you* makes you a
  better facilitator (you learn how questions feel from the other end).
- Go **deep on user profiles but broad on customer description** — use an adjective + noun
  (e.g., "concerned activist," "environmentally conscious [X]").
- Contrary to the usual "keep the room positive," Chris likes to surface weaknesses and
  less-desired brand truths because the contrast is useful — "it's okay to describe the brand
  as a less desired state because it's truthful."
- A facilitator should **give voice to marginalized people in the room** — often new/junior
  hires with fresh, un-bought-in ideas who get discredited in meetings. Capture the negative
  statement verbatim (e.g., "we're too exclusive"), then ask "why," then bridge from negative
  to a deeper understanding and pivot out.
- Negative words aren't wasted even if they never reach the final brand statement: CORE
  produces more than the statement (messaging, product/service ideas, campaigns). Knowing
  "we're too exclusive" can spawn a welcoming kit, a personal touch, a goodie bag, etc.
  Pairing a negative with a positive can produce a sharp position (Steve Jobs / Apple:
  "insanely great").
- **Contradiction with a prior CORE teaching, flagged by Dave:** Dave notes the CORE videos
  (he thinks by Jose) teach the "Trojan storage" move — turn a negative word into a positive
  because the brand should end up positive. Chris's stance is more nuanced: keep the negative
  for a holistic, authentic sense of the company rather than discarding it.
- **"Brands are people too."** Using the 2015 Star Wars / Kylo Ren example (complex, not pure
  evil nor all good), Chris argues ~80% great + ~20% "quirky/off-center" is what makes a brand
  unique and authentic; a one-note personality feels disingenuous. Ties to a line he
  attributes to Jose: "people don't fall in love with companies, they fall in love with
  personalities."
- **Domino's Pizza case study:** Domino's (known for "30 minutes or it's free," not quality)
  heard via social media that people hated the pizza, reformulated the recipe, tracked down
  the most vocal critics, and sent them free pizza asking for honest feedback. Many critics
  publicly reversed on social media. Lesson: run *toward* the problem, embrace it; you can't
  out-market a bad product — customers now talk to each other independently of the advertiser.
- Handling a cynic / disruptive participant: name it up front without asking them to
  self-identify — tell the room "somebody here has a lot of institutional knowledge and is a
  bit closed to new ways; I'll ask you to suspend disbelief for the next 6–8 hours and give me
  feedback afterward." Calling it out makes them self-conscious, and you can "have fun with
  it" while affirming their value to the organization.
- **Transparency as a client-trust tool:** state your agenda openly ("this is what I need to
  get out of this, and I hope you'll love me by the end"). Agendas are fine; *hidden* agendas
  are the problem. Confronting things head-on is disarming and endears you to the client.
- "We're in the age of the customer / the user — brands need customers more than customers
  need brands." Understand that and you develop, deliver, and speak about products differently.
- CORE (or any framework) is **not a rigid system — it's an open framework**; adapt it to how
  you work and think, and play to your strengths.
