# 3 Tips on starting your own design agency

## Summary

An early "The Process"-era (2016) Q&A episode of The Futur in which Chris Do answers
three questions submitted via Twitter by Grace, a French self-taught graphic/web designer
planning to launch her own design agency by 2017. The advice draws on Chris's experience
running his agency Blind. A co-host (referred to on camera as Aaron) contributes one
observation; Chris delivers the substance. The three questions concern (1) how to market
a multi-service agency without confusing clients, (2) how to integrate a social cause /
values into the business without being mistaken for a nonprofit, and (3) whether to create
separate logos for each service line.

The closing segment (roughly the last third of the transcript) is a separate on-location
riff by Chris about the tension between *making* design work versus *managing* a design
firm — that a firm owner who is producing the work cannot manage, and that his role is to
transfer knowledge to young designers so they can do the work to the standard he promises
clients.

## Key claims

Speaker = **Chris Do** unless noted. All paraphrased unless quoted below.

**Tip 1 — Marketing a multi-service agency: offer ONE thing at first** (Chris, 2016)
- When first marketing a company, offer one thing and show as many examples of that one
  thing as possible, because people are "information rich and time poor." Showing too many
  services is confusing.
- Chris frames this as a mistake he made personally over 20 years of experience.
- Over time you can expand services; start from your core strength — whatever you genuinely
  do really well.
- The only way to lead with more than one service is to sell **branding**, because branding
  is the "umbrella" offering — but you must have enough examples to support the claim, stake
  a claim as an expert, and prove it.

**Tip 2 — Integrating a social cause / values: don't lead with it** (Chris, 2016)
- A social cause is a good thing to do, but when starting out don't confuse people about
  what your business actually is. Work it into the copy, but don't "hit somebody over the
  head with it."
- Clients should hire you first and foremost for your expertise; feeling good about
  supporting a community-based design firm is a secondary benefit, not the lead.
- (Attributed by Chris to the co-host "Aaron"): the questioner's graphic-design-plus-
  business background is itself a differentiator — messaging like "with my business
  background I understand the bigger business problem, not just design" ties the values and
  the cause into the mission.

**Tip 3 — Separate logos per service: don't** (Chris, 2016)
- Advises *against* creating a separate logo for each of the ~six services.
- Adobe and FedEx use sub-brands/systems because those are distinctly different products
  appealing to different people; a design agency's services are not separate products —
  they support the parent brand.
- "Just making one great Mark is hard" — stick with one strong mark rather than
  over-complicating.

**Closing riff — make vs. manage** (Chris, 2016)
- People who ask whether the principal personally touches the design don't understand how
  design firms are run; they are "stuck in the I need to make something mentality" where
  they find their value.
- If you're making the work, you can't manage; you choose to manage or to make. At any real
  volume of new opportunities, there's no time left to make.
- Chris's own role: transfer his knowledge to young designers so they can do the work to the
  level he promises clients. (Illustrated by declining to forward a designer's work to a
  client until he has personally reviewed it — quality gate.)
