# Client Red Flags and Warning Signs — "The Process" (2016)

★ L3-candidate — adds a distinct client-screening/qualification framework (a taxonomy of bad vs. good client archetypes and a gut-instinct/self-awareness screen) that likely goes beyond current `topics/sales-clients` coverage.

## Summary

An early episode of "The Process" hosted by Chris Do with co-host Jose Caballer, framed as "good clients versus bad clients" (jokingly "The Good, the Bad and the Ugly"). The two run through an extended catalog of bad-client warning signs, then a catalog of good-client traits, and close with Chris's core thesis: there are no bad clients, only your own choices — self-awareness and trusting your gut determine which clients you accept. Chris argues that the warning signs are felt as a "vibe" in the very first meeting, that hourly-rate thinking diminishes value (talk budget, not hours), and that resentment toward demanding clients is a pricing problem, not a client problem ("charge more"). Recurring analogy: choosing clients is like dating.

## Key claims

Bad-client warning signs:

- (Chris, 2016) A client who makes you feel you have to *prove yourself* to them is an immediate bad sign. Paraphrase; illustrated by a story: invited to meet a "creative business partner," was greeted with "so what are you going to do for us?", found it off-putting, wrote off the meeting and left.
- (Chris, 2016) He measures bad clients "from a vibe standpoint" — e.g. a client opening with "wow you guys are really expensive" sets a bad tone, versus one who opens by complimenting your work. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) Rates-versus-value: clients who pin you to hours and a rate misunderstand the project; "hourly rates diminish the value of what you provide." A client fixated on scope/timeline/budget in the initial meeting isn't separating their business goal from the tactical. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) He does not mind talking about *money* upfront — distinct from talking hours — to avoid spending 90 minutes only to learn the client has $4,000 for a $400,000 project. Ask what resources/budget they have against the initiative, not the hours. Paraphrase.
- (Jose, 2016) "The Pete Campbell producer" — interested only in themselves, climbing the internal career ladder, inexperienced, fronts, and grinds you. Overcompensating; gives off negative vibes. Attribution: uncertain (transcript speaker unclear; likely Jose).
- (Jose/Chris, 2016) "The Squirrel CEO" — changes their mind every few seconds, shiny-object syndrome, wants a "unicorn," no real direction. Attribution: uncertain.
- (Jose/Chris, 2016) "The Donald Trump CEO" — "I'm better than you, I know how to do this, let me tell you how" — lots of bluster, not enough substance; may or may not actually have the budget they claim, and will be a pain regardless. Attribution: uncertain.
- (Chris, 2016) Micromanagers who tell you *both* what to do and how to do it negate your ability to contribute creatively. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) Clients with unreasonable expectations and no boundaries — expect 24/7 availability, call Sunday night about something due on a holiday Monday. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) Clients who give obscure feedback — "I'll know it when I see it," or repeated "I don't like this" with no positive direction. It's easy to say what you don't want, hard to articulate what you do. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) Clients who think everything is easy — "how hard would it be to do this?" — and needle you through endless revisions by trapping you into admitting it's "not hard." Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) Indecisive clients who solicit opinions from everyone (neighbor, PTA, spouse, kids). Better: a client with the courage to say "I don't know, so I trust you — guide me and make a recommendation." Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) Clients who get personal and make personal attacks ("you're lazy," "bad ideas," "total hack work," "I thought you were the best"). Chris theorizes some do this deliberately because artists have fragile egos — breaking someone down emotionally makes them work harder. His counter-move: "this isn't working for me, let's try to work toward the goal." Paraphrase + quotes.
- (Chris, 2016) Opaque vs. transparent clients — "poker face syndrome"; you can't tell if the work is good or bad, which breaks the collaborative spirit. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) Clients who blame everybody and never take responsibility — success is theirs, failure is everyone else's fault. Paraphrase.

Good-client traits:

- (Chris, 2016) The best clients pick vendors they trust, treat them as experts, and let them do their job. Paraphrase.
- (Jose, 2016) "The Zen CEO" — calm, not blustered, picks the right people, speaks plainly, tells you honestly whether they're happy in a mindful/present (not emotional) way. Attribution: uncertain (likely Jose).
- (Chris/Jose, 2016) A great client coaches you to win the account, tells you what's going on internally and how to maneuver — a real partner who cares about your well-being. Attribution: uncertain.
- (Chris, 2016) Invokes the Steve Jobs line that A-players hire A-players while B-players hire C-players — always hire better than yourself and trust those experts. No point hiring the world's greatest designer/architect then telling them how to design. Paraphrase of Chris citing Jobs.
- (Chris, 2016) Clients who value design as a *competitive advantage / differentiator* — not "sauce you pour over a faulty product" — are golden, and they'll see you as valuable too. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) Fair clients: how they treat their own staff predicts how they'll treat you. Great clients are "servant leaders versus boss dictators" — in the trenches with their team, not commanding from an ivory tower. Paraphrase + quote.
- (Chris, 2016) Open-minded clients genuinely want to hear solutions even if unlike what they planned — as opposed to those who claim openness then pull back at anything new. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) "Social" clients share credit, extol your virtues, and refer you to more work rather than taking the credit. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) He personally prefers business-focused clients whose skills complement his as a designer — they handle sales/marketing/business goals and leave the creative to him. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) Counterintuitively, *creative clients are the worst* — they don't value creativity (they see it "as a byproduct of breathing"), so they either say "just do it, it's easy" or take over and reduce you to a "mouse jockey," at which point Chris emotionally checks out and is no longer vested in the solution. Business clients pay fairly and don't expect free work. Paraphrase.

Closing thesis:

- (Chris, 2016) "I don't believe in victims, I believe in volunteers" — if you keep getting bad clients, it's because you keep accepting unhealthy relationships; time to change. Quote + paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) You already know the red flags in your gut but choose to ignore them "at your own peril." Analogy: in a dark alley you stay alert and don't flash your iPhone/wallet/Rolex, yet in business we ignore our instincts and pay a heavy toll. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) There are no bad clients — your degree of self-awareness determines your choices. "Seek to develop the self first," then define your journey and which clients you want. Fear (or financial pressure) makes people talk themselves into bad clients against their instincts. Paraphrase + quote.
- (Chris, 2016) Keeping overhead low, especially when starting out, gives you the power to say no — and "no is an incredibly powerful word." Paraphrase + quote.
- (Chris, 2016) The more self-aware and mature you become, the more binary you get — a protective yes/no about your energy and space. Example: he and the team literally walked away from a real (not figurative) million-dollar job because the client was evasive and dodgy in the first meeting and gave the wrong vibe. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) But there are also "many shades of gray" — they work with a wide variety of clients (pet food to electronics) because he wants to do good work; the criteria isn't the project, it's the *relationship*. Paraphrase.
- (Chris, 2016) A high-maintenance, nitpicky client is workable *if boundaries and rules of engagement are defined up front*; resentment comes from underpricing. Cites "Blen[d]": the reason you resent a client's Saturday call is you haven't charged enough — solution: charge more. Paraphrase (Chris relaying a source).
- (Jose, 2016) Getting good clients is like dating — you'll have bad dates / bad clients, and it takes experience to find the right fit. Attribution: uncertain (likely Jose affirming Chris).
