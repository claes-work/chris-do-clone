# How To: Tell A Great Story — 5 storytelling tips

## Summary

A solo vlog monologue by Chris Do ("it's Chris here") teaching five practical
storytelling tips aimed at everyday communicators — vloggers, presenters, anyone
telling a story — not just filmmakers. This is a single-speaker piece; Jose Caballer
does not appear, so all substantive content is Chris-Do-attributed.

The core thesis: **stories are about conflict** — somebody wants something and can't
get it. The five tips, each drawn from a named storytelling authority, build a toolkit
for injecting conflict, structure, and drama into any narrative:

1. **Conflict = a want plus an obstacle.** A character must want something and face an
   obstacle in the way. If they get it immediately, there is no story. Illustrated with
   the opening six lines of *Romeo and Juliet* (two feuding families whose children
   fall in love) — enough to hook the audience.
2. **Subjective expectation vs. objective reality** (from Robert McKee, *Story*).
   Friction comes when a character's subjective expectation meets cold, hard objective
   reality. Illustrated with *The Matrix* (Neo expects the truth to be a simple
   conspiracy; the reality is humans harvested for energy) and everyday framings
   ("getting a job should be easy but it's not").
3. **Normal → Explosion → New normal** (from Kendra Hall, storytelling consultant). A
   simpler, more useful structure than beginning/middle/end: set the normal (with
   details and emotion), the explosion disrupts it, and the new normal is the progress
   report on the changed reality. Illustrated with Steve Jobs introducing the iPhone
   (smartphones aren't smart → the iPhone → it changes how you communicate).
4. **Story delaying** (from Francis Glebas, *Directing the Story*, a book on
   storyboarding). The secret is to drag out the conclusion for as long as possible
   while feeding the audience meaningful details and posing dramatic questions ("will
   the character live?"). A whodunit loses its point if you reveal the culprit up front.
   Humans love stories because we learn lessons vicariously — through the character's
   growth — without having to suffer ourselves.
5. **Make it dramatic by exaggerating.** Treat the moments your characters face as
   life-and-death even when they're not (missing the bus feels like the end of the
   world). Tap into the raw emotion of childhood-scale stakes (a bad haircut, fear of
   approaching a stranger) to make a story dramatic and interesting.

Chris closes by applying the framework to a vlog: before an upcoming event (a new
client meeting), record your expectations and hopes/fears (normal), let the event be
the explosion, then reflect afterward on what happened and the lessons learned (new
normal → transition to lesson).

- Stories are fundamentally about conflict: somebody wants something and can't get it;
  a want granted immediately kills the story. (Chris Do) [paraphrase]
- A story needs an obstacle or challenge in the way of the character getting what they
  want. (Chris Do) [paraphrase]
- Storytelling doesn't require a cinematic or documentary scale — it applies to
  something as simple as a vlog. (Chris Do) [paraphrase]

  whose children fall in love, hooking the audience into what happens next. (Chris Do,
  citing Shakespeare) [paraphrase]

  that gap is the friction. (Chris Do, attributing the idea to Robert McKee's *Story*)
  [paraphrase]

  movement) collides with objective reality (humans are harvested as energy for the
  machines). (Chris Do) [paraphrase]

  it's not"; "winning a client should be easy but it's not." (Chris Do) [paraphrase]

  explosion → new normal. (Chris Do, attributing to Kendra Hall) [paraphrase]
