# UX Content Strategy w/ Karen McGrane

## Summary

Interview episode of "The Process" (early The Futur / thefutur channel, 2016) in which
**Chris Do** hosts a conversation with guest **Karen McGrane**, a longtime UX and content
strategy consultant. Chris frames the episode in the opening voiceover, noting he and
Karen used to work together at Racerfish, and he conducts the interview — asking the
questions and steering the conversation. **Jose Caballer** appears briefly in the intro
banter (the "business of design" bit) but does not lead the interview. The substance of
the episode — content strategy, content modeling, editorial process, the shift from print
to web/mobile — is Karen's expertise and is **CONTEXT, not persona**. Chris's role is
that of interviewer/framer; only his own attributed statements feed the persona.

Karen introduces herself as a UX consultant with a "soup to nuts" background (interaction
design, information architecture, content strategy, user research). She has authored
*Content Strategy for Mobile* and *Going Responsive*, worked with large publishers
(Hearst, Time Inc., Condé Nast, Fast Company, Inc., WWE) and led the New York Times
redesign ~10 years prior. The core thesis (Karen's): good web design means designing for
content fluidity — the web is not a finished artifact like a magazine but a "living,
breathing" system that must account for ongoing content maintenance. She explains content
modeling as defining the "Lego blocks" (types, purposes, sizes/shapes, rules) of content
before design begins, rather than waiting for final copy.

This is the first in a planned multi-part series with Karen (next episode teased: design
for mobile).

## Key claims

Per-speaker, dated 2016-03-18, paraphrased unless quoted below.

**Chris Do (host — persona-eligible):**
- Chris frames the episode's premise: user experience is not only about form — it is
  about the people who put together devices and content and how to facilitate the
  creation of content (his read of Karen's *Going Responsive* conclusion) [paraphrase, intro].
- Chris positions Karen as a foundational UX authority ("grandmother of user experience")
  and says he has known her a long time, having crossed paths at conferences and worked
  together at Racerfish [paraphrase].
- Chris describes the audience for the show as three segments: freelancers growing into
  agencies, small/medium agencies, and principals (like himself) transitioning from
  motion into digital/web work — and notes these audiences may be unfamiliar with UX
  jargon, so the goal is to demystify UX terms [paraphrase]. *(business/positioning of
  the show's audience — attribution clearly Chris.)*
- Chris articulates the pain point of designers transitioning from print/motion: content
  is "fluid," "lives in multiple places at the same time," and "it's a mess" — he asks
  Karen to validate and explain this [paraphrase; see quote below].
- Chris closes stating content is the first thing to deal with, especially with mobile,
  and that he is passionate about content strategy because it affects how people
  collaborate and removes friction from projects [paraphrase, outro].

**Karen McGrane (guest — CONTEXT only, not persona):**
- She is a longtime UX consultant (interaction design, IA, content strategy, user
  research); much of her content-strategy work was "paradoxically sparked by mobile" [paraphrase].
- Author of *Content Strategy for Mobile* and *Going Responsive*; the latter is about
  organizational process and culture change needed to deliver a great responsive site [paraphrase].
- Editorial/content-management processes should be treated as a UX problem — tools and
  processes must work for content managers/owners [paraphrase].
- Client roster: Hearst, Time Inc., Condé Nast, Fast Company, Inc., World Wrestling
  Entertainment; led the New York Times redesign ~10 years earlier; currently working
  with a large hospital chain on print-to-web strategy [paraphrase, self-reported].
- Publishing decisions are often mistaken for technology decisions but are really UX and
  people decisions [paraphrase].
- UX mindset shifts decisions from "what the business wants to say" to "what the person
  coming to the site wants to find and do"; users are "in the driver's seat" [paraphrase].
- The web is not a finished artifact (like a shipped magazine) but a living system
  requiring ongoing "care and feeding"; designers must "think in systems" and account for
  content flexibility/fluidity [paraphrase].
- **Content modeling** defined: figuring out expected content types, purposes, audiences,
  and sizes/shapes ("the Lego blocks") before design starts — you need the structure of
  content, not every final word [paraphrase].
- A content model / editorial style guide (voice, tone, word choice) connects language to
  brand and keeps the process moving [paraphrase].
- Content strategists should be brought in early — often before design/dev, in a ~6–8
  week engagement to audit content, define the content model, guide copywriters, and
  liaise between content and design/dev teams [paraphrase].
