# How To: Stay Creatively Motivated and Inspired & Dealing With Clients With BAD TASTE

★ L3-candidate — the "client with bad taste" handling introduces a distinct, reusable move (reframe taste as subjective → replace with objective business measures → refund-and-refer if truly misaligned) that goes beyond generic sales-clients material.

## Summary

An early The Futur ("The Process" era) Q&A in which Chris Do answers two audience questions, apparently from Art Center students. The first: what keeps him creatively motivated and inspired after 20 years. The second, prompted by a co-host relaying a roommate's situation: how to deal with a client who has "horrible taste."

On motivation, Chris credits a love of learning — he'd rather stay up past midnight learning the correct/shortest way to do something than take the easy familiar path — plus curiosity about the world and continual exposure to teachers, coaches, and conferences. He warns graduates that a burnout typically hits ~5–6 years out, when the creativity, self-expression, and autonomy they enjoyed in school get "beaten out" of them, largely because they define success as imposing their own aesthetic on others.

On bad-taste clients, Chris reframes taste as purely subjective and reminds designers that commercial work is a value exchange: the client pays to have a problem solved, not to fund the designer's art. His prescription: shift from subjective measures ("is it good/cool/clean?") to objective, client-agreed measures (deadline, print budget, message, audience), diagnose the problem WITH the client (not for them), and thereby move from order-taker to expert. If the work truly destroys you, refund the money and refer another designer rather than resent the client. He recommends Blair Enns' *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto*, and closes with his information-diet and Pinterest-bucket workflow for aligning on visual language before doing real creative work.

## Key claims

Chris Do (subject) — motivation:
- What motivates him after 20 years is a love of learning; every time he learns something new he feels he has grown. (2016-04-01)
- He'd rather spend the time learning the correct, shortest way to do something than default to the easy, familiar way — even with a deadline the next morning. (2016-04-01)
- He pushes his designers/interns on efficiency; small repetitive inefficiencies compound — "40 years later you could have gone on vacation for 3 weeks in the time that it took you to do that same repetitive task." (2016-04-01)
- He automates repeated tasks (actions), looks things up, and uses Google — "if you learn how to ask the right question you'll have the answer" in four or five clicks. (2016-04-01)
- Growth is what makes him feel alive; he seeks out teachers, coaches, and conferences. (2016-04-01)
- He has felt burnt out only once, about 5–6 years in, and predicts new graduates will hit burnout ~5 years out. (2016-04-01)
- Cause of that burnout: defining success as how much of your own design aesthetic you can impose on people; when clients reject your taste you feel injured as an artist, but you are really forcing subjective choices onto others. (2016-04-01)
- Young designers often produce solutions unrelated to the brief (e.g., "dark and cinematic" when "flat graphic" was asked for) because they do what they love rather than solve the problem. (2016-04-01)

Chris Do (subject) — clients with bad taste:
- Taste is purely subjective; there is no certified "king of good taste," and even highly-educated designers only argue subjective preferences. (2016-04-01)
- Agreeing to do work is a value exchange: the client gives money to have a problem solved, not to fund the designer's art. If receiving money for your art feels cheap, become a fine artist; commercial art is a service. (2016-04-01)
- Analogy: a dressmaker or hairstylist who imposes the style they think is "the hotness" instead of what the client wanted leaves the client upset — change that mentality first. (2016-04-01)
- If the work is genuinely destroying you, own up, refund the money, apologize, and refer other designers — but don't take the money while hating them and the process. (2016-04-01)
- Prescription: define an OBJECTIVE measure of success, not a subjective one. Objective measures = delivery date, print budget cap (e.g., under $3,000), the message (e.g., a BOGO / two-for-one), and the audience (e.g., expectant mothers). Then solve and measure against that. (2016-04-01)
- Creatives should define the objective business/marketing goal WITH the client, not for them — this is diagnosing the problem. (2016-04-01)
- Diagnosing the problem (vs. selling parts and services) changes the dynamic from order-taker to equal or expert; becoming the expert requires a different skill set. (2016-04-01)
- Recommends *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto* by Blair Enns (~$27, short book); its point is not literally "never pitch again" but that careful practice gets you seen as an expert whose advice/services command high fees. (2016-04-01)
- Cites the manifesto's commandment: "Thou shalt not prescribe a solution prior to diagnosing the problem." Whenever someone says "I need this" and you're ready to start, you've already screwed up. (2016-04-01)
- Points people to his Facebook page "Chris Do business designer" for his book list and business ideas. (2016-04-01)

Chris Do (subject) — staying informed / workflow:
- Warns against learning from people no longer in the field; their information may be 20–30 years out of date in a fast-changing industry. (2016-04-01)
- Says the future for designers is to learn business principles, not more design/motion-graphics/illustration tricks — that's how he creates value; he then hires specialists. (2016-04-01)
- His daily information diet: checks social media on waking, then CNET/CNN and photography blogs; NPR while driving to work; TED Talks at home. He no longer reads design-trick sites, having learned that material already. (2016-04-01)
- Has ~20,000–30,000 pins on Pinterest, sorted into buckets — not for inspiration but so that when a client says e.g. "punk rock look," he can show a bucket and confirm they mean the same thing. (2016-04-01)
- Workflow: think, diagnose the problem, and get visual alignment with the client (and sometimes surface adjacent options they didn't ask for) BEFORE doing any real creative work — "peeling back the layers." (2016-04-01)

Co-host / roommate's situation (context, `attribution: uncertain` — a second speaker, possibly Jose, relaying a roommate's experience):
- Was reviewing a roommate's work; the client "had horrible taste," and the roommate reasoned "I go to school so I know what to do." Asks whether to just go along with a bad-taste client or drop the client entirely. (2016-04-01)
