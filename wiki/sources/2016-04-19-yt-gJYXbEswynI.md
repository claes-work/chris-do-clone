## Summary

Chris Do interviews an Atlanta-based startup founder about what it actually takes to start a
business. The guest walks through his arc: dropping out of college (2.5 years in, a marketing
major) to take a VP-of-Sales offer in LA; becoming employee #2 at a college-marketing company and
scaling its sales force from 50 to 250 reps over five years; then co-founding **Campus Special**
(a two-sided marketplace connecting local merchants with college students via print/mobile/online,
run ~8.5 years across 500 colleges / 165 cities, ~4,000 sales reps, sold for over $20M in 2013);
and finally launching **Hire Wire**, an on-demand hiring marketplace ("anti job board") for the
service industry, raised on $2M angel funding (Aug 2015), 90 days live at recording. Recurring
themes from the guest: trusting your gut, grit/"burn the bridges" 100% commitment, managing
expectations (halve your projections), the emotional/dark side of startups the media romanticizes,
building an MVP, solving a validated problem rather than chasing an idea, 10x-not-incremental
disruption, and family/work sacrifice. Chris's own contributions are interspersed as commentary:
his definition of creativity, the primacy of analytics/data, framing the guest's story in
design-thinking terms, and closing on giving back.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona — subject-attributed)
- 2016-04-19 — [paraphrase] Creativity is not draftsmanship or rendering; a creative person is
  **"somebody who is able to connect [two] disparate things."** Chris applies this to the guest,
  arguing the guest *is* creative because he connected two needs (job seekers wanting work,
  employers wanting talent) into a novel solution (video-interview marketplace).
- 2016-04-19 — [paraphrase] Analytics/data is the **first and last thing Chris looks at each day**
  ("that's what I do").
- 2016-04-19 — [paraphrase] The guest's approach — figure out what people want, then build that —
  is textbook **design thinking / UX**; Chris ties it to what IBM and big management-consulting
  firms are doing: don't build a product then market it, discover the want first so the marketing
  is built in.
- 2016-04-19 — [paraphrase] Chris relays a Jose Caballer line he likes: building a startup is
  **"building the runway while you're landing the plane."** (Attributed by Chris to Jose, not
  original to Chris.)
- 2016-04-19 — [paraphrase] Closing, Chris invokes a line he attributes to Obama: once you're at
  the top, **"send the elevator back down"** — an obligation to share what you know and help others.

### Guest founder (CONTEXT — not persona; some lines `attribution: uncertain` due to garbled audio)
- 2016-04-19 — [self-reported] Describes himself as a **problem solver** who likes to solve big
  problems, in this case through technology and startups.
- 2016-04-19 — [self-reported] Dropped out of college at ~2.5 years (junior, marketing major) to
  move to LA as VP of Sales; says he trusts his gut, was never afraid to "break the mold," and had
  a safety net (could return to school) but couldn't risk missing the opportunity.
- 2016-04-19 — [paraphrase] Advice on dropping out: it depends on the person and the opportunity;
  entrepreneurship is **weighing risk vs. reward and deciding when to pull the trigger**; you need
  grit, determination, persistence, and will that supersedes everything.
- 2016-04-19 — [self-reported] Was employee #2 at a college-marketing company at age 21 with "no
  playbook, no process"; his job was to scale — **"the real value was in the ability to clone
  myself on a large scale"** — growing sales reps from 50 to 250 (~5x) over five years.
- 2016-04-19 — [self-reported] Co-founded **Campus Special** (funded initially on "Visa and
  MasterCard" — i.e., debt, from his dining-room table); projected $2.5M in year-one sales, hit
  $1.2M; calls the first year "one of the hardest, darkest times of my life." He and his partner
  each put in $250K ($500K total).
- 2016-04-19 — [paraphrase] Managing expectations: **whatever projection you think you'll hit, cut
  it in half, then cut it again.**
- 2016-04-19 — [self-reported] Campus Special ran ~8.5 years: ~4,000 sales reps, 10,000+ brand
  ambassadors, 500 colleges, 165 cities; grew 20–40% every year through the recession; sold in
  2013 for over $20M (to a recently-public company growing by acquisition).
- 2016-04-19 — [self-reported] After a two-week "retirement," started **Hire Wire**; discipline
  lesson: **don't start a company just to start one or on a hunch — find a real, existing problem
  you have the background/team/skills to solve**; try to kill the idea first (due diligence to talk
  yourself out of it).
- 2016-04-19 — [paraphrase] The most common startup killer is **building something nobody wants**;
  build an **MVP (Minimum Viable Product)** — the bare minimum to validate/invalidate — rather than
  "the Taj Mahal."
- 2016-04-19 — [self-reported] Hire Wire: raised $2M angel (Aug 2015), a two-sided on-demand hiring
  marketplace for the service industry ("anti job board" — you can't post a listing or apply);
  employers search candidates by skill/availability/location/pay and watch pre-recorded 4-question
  video interviews. Revenue model: charge employers per "connect" (paywall on contacting a
  candidate); ~1 hire per 2–3 connects. 90 days in: 1,200 employers, ~15,000 job seekers, one city
  (Atlanta), one vertical (restaurants), ~10% market share, targeting 25%.
- 2016-04-19 — [paraphrase] Disruption requires a product an **order of magnitude (10x) better**
  than the status quo, not 2–3x; won't scale until they reach 10x. Deliberately focused on one
  city + one vertical for **saturation, critical mass, and liquidity** ("a million users in two
  cities" over "a million users in twenty").
- 2016-04-19 — [paraphrase] Startups are romanticized by media/recent success stories (Snapchat,
  Facebook, Twitter); reality is **"like giving birth"** — the hardest, most painful thing.
  Calls himself "chief problem solver"; if you have no problems you're not growing.
- 2016-04-19 — [self-reported] Sleeps ~6 hours, works ~18; stresses hiring a team with
  **complementary skills** — "my goal is to be the least smart guy at the table."
- 2016-04-19 — [self-reported / attribution: uncertain] On family: married, two kids; credits his
  wife as "the secret to my success" for letting him be "100% of who I am uncompromised";
  emphasizes compartmentalizing and that startups aren't a good fit unless you truly want it.
