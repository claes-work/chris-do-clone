# Big Client Meeting: How to Sell Large Digital Projects (2016-04-30)

★ L3-candidate — contributes a distinct, structured "first sales call / big-project"
framework (diagnose-not-sell, elicit all stakeholders' objections, listen-and-mirror,
be direct, discovery phase as budget-negotiation, walk-away move) worth promoting into
`wiki/topics/sales-clients`.

## Summary

An episode of *The Process* in which Chris Do and Jose Caballer role-play the **first
sales call** for a large digital project. Jose plays "Jill," a marketing associate at a
fictionalized version of Tumi (a ~$1B bag manufacturer) whose e-commerce experience is
"a mess," is being driven by the design/marketing team (tired of tech leading), and must
integrate with existing ERP/CRM platforms. The prior development partner cost "a little
over a million dollars" and underperformed. Chris plays the seller (design firm "blind" /
"RCT") and demonstrates his approach to running the call. After the role-play, both step
out of character to debrief: Jose critiques from the client's perspective (the church/state
tech-vs-design separation made him nervous; a real Tumi would more likely hire a large
integrated agency), and Chris breaks down his technique meta-level (listen, write down every
concern, elicit all stakeholders' objections, mirror them back, be direct, land a paid
discovery phase, and walk away if the money doesn't work).

Attribution note: This is an explicit **role-play**. Chris Do speaks both as himself (the
debrief/coaching commentary = persona voice) and in-character as the seller. Jose Caballer
is the co-host (context only) and plays the client "Jill." Lines spoken in-character as the
client/seller are labeled as role-play below; only Chris's own-voice coaching is treated as
persona training material.

## Key claims

Dated 2016-04-30. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Per-speaker.

**Chris Do (own voice — coaching / method):**
- The seller's job on the first call is to **diagnose the problem, not sell a solution**.
  Clients arrive asking for a specific deliverable ("we need an app"); resist and diagnose
  first to be sure that's the real need.
- **Listen more than you talk.** Ask a question, then just listen. Write down every concern
  the client raises; you can circle back and address each one, prioritizing the biggest.
- **Elicit the objections of everyone in the room**, not just the person you're talking to.
  Directly ask what the top three objections will be from each stakeholder (CTO, CMO/marketing
  VP, CEO/boss). If the contact doesn't know, ask "who else is concerned and what are their
  concerns" to unearth them.
- **Mirror concerns back** to the client: repeating them proves you listened *and* validates
  the person; it also tells you what your pitch deck must address head-on to win.
- **Be direct without being a jerk.** People like directness; say things plainly ("I'm not
  sure that's going to work for us / maybe we're not the right people") without being crass,
  rude, or confrontational.
- Frame design in service of the business goal: for the CEO whose concern is revenue, position
  yourself as entrepreneurial/creative and make sure the work serves the client's bottom line,
  not just aesthetics/UX for their own sake.
- **Don't separate the problem from the scope and cost too early** — "you can do anything for
  $250,000 if you do the discovery right." Divide the *problem* from the *scope/cost*; a good
  discovery lets you address the main problem within almost any budget.
- **Land a paid discovery phase** ($30–40k) as the immediate next step when scope/budget are
  unresolved — that's where scope and budget get determined together. (Chris frames this as a
  fair compromise in the role-play.)
- **Walk away** if the budget genuinely can't work: say plainly that other firms can do it,
  "we're probably not the right vendor," and decline rather than waste the client's time in
  discovery for a job you can't deliver at that price.
- On the mobile/responsive craft point raised in-role: **design mobile-first** (desktop-down
  designs "break in translation"); optimize for thumbs — big, friendly, tap/swipe targets;
  the phone is "the one device we never leave home without."
- On apps: don't add an app just because the client asks — forcing a download is "another step,"
  so an app "has to do something magical," give something not otherwise obtainable, or users
  delete it. UX = giving people what they want in the fewest number of steps.

**Chris Do (in-character as seller — role-play, positioning tactics):**
- Openly admit the limits of your expertise ("obvious technical concerns... well beyond my
  level of experience — I need to bring somebody in") and promise to bring in a technical
  partner rather than fake competence.
- Reframe a "tech" problem as a design/experience problem you can own while "playing nice
  with the tech."
- Position self as a **unifier** of internal teams, sensitive to the CTO's turf, "not here
  to castrate anybody" — reduce internal political friction.
- Toward a large investment already made (the ~$1M platform): go in open-minded to make it
  work, but if together you find it's "pouring good money after bad," don't continue.

**Jose Caballer (co-host — context; debrief in own voice):**
- Chris "is a master... he did a great job on that first call."
- Two things made him (as the client) nervous: the **separation of design from technical**
  ("church/state") and not talking to the technical person, plus the difficulty of a
  non-technical buyer vetting a non-technical vendor.
- Reality check: a real ~$1B Tumi would more likely hire a **large integrated agency**
  (e.g., "huge") that positions itself as solving business problems and integrating tech
  strategy + design, so the tech-competence question never even comes up. Such agencies have
  technical limits too but "figure it out" by structuring teams.

**Jose in-character as "Jill" (client — role-play, context):**
- Coaching feedback: Chris "sold me on himself," was honest/straightforward, addressed the
  technical concern by promising to bring someone in.
- The one thing she'd coach before the next meeting: speak to how the **entire internal team
  is also a user group** — the site/platforms must be usable internally and externally, not
  just pretty to the customer — which would reassure both the marketing VP and the CTO.
- Client-side details established in-role: 4-month product launch deadline; prior dev partner
  cost "a little over a million"; budget target revealed as **$250k–$500k** ("under half a
  million"); the boss dislikes splitting into separate tech/design partners; the boss dangles
  future "Tumi Global" work as a carrot; a discovery phase is the smart client's way to
  negotiate scope down during the engagement.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Only Chris Do's own-voice lines are persona training data. Role-play lines labeled.

Chris Do (own voice — coaching):
> "Don't be afraid to be direct. That doesn't mean you're going to say things like a jerk... people I think genuinely like people who are direct, and I try to be as direct as possible without being crass or rude or confrontational."

> "Notice a lot of times what I did was I would ask a question and then what did I do? I just listen. You have to really listen. And so every time I heard a concern I wrote it down."

> "By saying it back to them, not only did I tell them I listen, I heard you, but I'm validating you at the same time."

> "You can probably do anything for $250,000 if you do the discovery right... this is where you have to remove or divide the problem from the scope and the cost."

> "During the discovery phase you're negotiating the scope, and that's where you cut back and you add and you come back with a proposal."

Chris Do (in-character as seller — role-play):
> "[in-role] I'm not here to try and sell you a solution. What I want to do is spend some time diagnosing the problem with you."

> "[in-role] Tech-led firms sell tech at all costs. They sell you the tech, and when the tech doesn't work out there's not much else to fall back on."

> "[in-role] If we force people to download an app it's got to do something magical... otherwise, I don't know about you, but I delete apps on a pretty regular basis off my phone."

> "[in-role, the walk-away] I'm sure there are companies out there that can do what it is that you want. We're probably not the right vendor because I just don't know how to do what it is that you want... I don't want to waste your time."

Jose Caballer (co-host — context, NOT persona):
> "[Jose, debrief] Chris Do is a master, so don't feel intimidated — he did a great job on that first call."
