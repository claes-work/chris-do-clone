# How to Get a Graphic Design Job in LA (The Process)

## Summary

An episode of *The Process* (Chris Do's pre-Futur / Blind-era show) built around a
recorded exit interview with a young female graphic designer from New Delhi, India,
who traveled to intern at Chris's studio Blind after Chris found her on Behance. It's
her last day. The audio-only conversation (her camera failed) is framed by Chris as a
lesson in "what it's like to be a foreign-born person looking for work in America."

The conversation is a two-way exchange: Chris explains how he actually hires
designers (portfolio over pedigree/location, how he wrote the job post, how he
screens for English and personality), and the intern pushes back with pointed
questions about whether Western agencies exploit Indian freelancers for cheap labor
and how much a designer should defend their design against a client. Core threads:
(1) talent is talent — location and school don't matter, make yourself discoverable;
(2) specialize / show depth, not breadth ("Trojan horse" analogy); (3) design is a
service business — serve the client, don't die on the sword for your taste; (4)
practical career mechanics for international applicants (English, a short specific
cold email, matchmaking fit). Jose Caballer appears only in the show's opening title
gag.

## Key claims

Speaker key: **Chris** = Chris Do (persona subject); **Intern** = the New Delhi
designer (context, unnamed in transcript); **Jose** = Jose Caballer (co-host,
context, intro cameo only).

- [2016-05-20] (Chris) His studio works with international designers because "talent
  is talent" — he doesn't care what city you're from, your nationality, gender, or
  whether you went to school; good designers are hard to find, period. (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) He hired the intern after posting a graphic-designer job on
  Behance; hundreds responded, most were not good, and hers stood out. Lesson to the
  audience: make your work discoverable, reach out, and open a dialogue with
  potential clients/employers — Behance is a good format for being found. (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Intern) Many designers abroad won't respond once they see a location
  pin in New Delhi, Bombay, or Bangalore; she was scared and didn't expect a
  response, so Chris looking past her location gave her hope that location shouldn't
  be "binding." (paraphrase; context-attributed)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) He deliberately wrote the job post to say school/degree
  doesn't matter — just have a good portfolio and get in touch — specifically to
  avoid discouraging good designers; most Behance job posts have long, intimidating
  requirement lists. His intent was "I just want good designers to apply and then I
  will sort the rest out." (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Intern) Perceived difference between design in India and the US:
  India favors quantity over quality because there are so many people and businesses,
  so work is faster and more spread thin; she described being able to "push one block
  100 feet forward" (deep focus on one thing) in the US versus "pushing 10 blocks one
  foot forward" back home. (paraphrase; context-attributed)
- [2016-05-20] (Intern) In the US studio, a project is like an assembly line where
  each person is assigned one part, which makes the overall output better; back home
  she did most of a project alone, so weak areas dragged the whole outcome down.
  (paraphrase; context-attributed)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) He runs his studio to be transparent and inclusive — the team
  openly shares client emails/feedback (naming staff Nicole and Greg) rather than
  filtering it behind closed doors; he's not there to hide things or manipulate to
  get a desired result. (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) He's a firm believer you must **specialize** to get hired or
  win business: a very diverse portfolio doesn't show depth or expertise, and many
  skills are confusing in the marketplace. Analogy to buying a purse or shoe — you
  want the best of one thing, not something trying to be all things to all people.
  Show the one thing you love (logo/brand identity, or UX/UI, or illustration) and
  get the job first. (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) His newer framing of specialization is a **Trojan horse**:
  don't storm the gate showing every skill (they won't let you in) — present one
  desirable thing the employer/client wants; once inside you reveal the rest and
  "blow them away." (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) Showing work you're not good at or new to signals insecurity
  and lack of confidence; you also risk getting hired for a skill you can't actually
  deliver. Better to be hired for a specialized skill, build a relationship, and then
  be "stretched" into new work as trust grows. (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Intern) Some Western agencies hire Indian freelancers not for a
  specific skill but because it's cheaper; a designer flattered by a big brand (e.g.
  BBC) getting in touch is often "roped in" before learning the low budget — the real
  reason they were contacted was the assumption they're cheaper. (paraphrase;
  context-attributed — this is the intern's challenge to Chris)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) He says he has never targeted a country to exploit cheaper
  labor, but he knows people who look up a country's median income vs. the US/UK and
  advertise only in Eastern Europe, the Philippines, India, or Vietnam because it
  costs less. He frames hiring a foreign designer who solicits him at a lower rate as
  supply-and-demand, not exploitation — if a designer earning ~$5/day is happy to
  make ~$50/day, "that's what they want"; if the quality is good he'll hire them.
  (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) It's relative: US salaries vary by cost of living too (rural
  Midwest homes cost a tenth to a fifth of the coasts), so pay adjustments aren't
  unique to India. (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) The world is a smaller, global marketplace, so American
  designers face downward wage pressure; to stay competitive on a global market you
  must develop skills that can't be easily replaced. (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) His hiring criteria, in order: (1) portfolio quality and fit
  with his clients — he wants low risk, people who do work he likes aligned with his
  client base; (2) ability to communicate in a common language (English), tested via
  email replies then a Skype call; (3) personality/fit, judged through email tone and
  the video call. Weird, nervous, or tense presentation makes him reluctant to bring
  someone in. (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) On the intern specifically: their initial hang-up was that she
  had a very strong, particular style he didn't have many clients for; they weren't
  sure "how far she could swing to the right," and luckily she proved flexible. His
  advice: within your specialty (e.g. brand identity) show **versatility** — don't do
  a thousand different things, but demonstrate range within the vertical (industrial,
  corporate, etc.). (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) Develop English speaking skills if you're from India, China,
  or anywhere else — design feedback is abstract and nuanced (e.g. "that's not
  whimsical enough"); if you don't understand the words, communication breaks. If
  the work is in France, learn French. Language common to the employer is essential.
  (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) A cold-outreach email should be short and specific, not a
  generic form letter: admire the company's work, name specific projects you liked
  (e.g. "I see you worked on the Audi project, on Coldplay"), state your skill set,
  and link your portfolio. The email won't get you the job — the portfolio does — so
  keep it short. (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) Applying is **matchmaking** — people whose work is totally
  incongruous with what his company does get deleted within two slides. Like a
  personal relationship, both sides need compatibility and good energy; some look
  good on paper but aren't a fit in person. If it's not a match, find a different
  person — "a lot of fish in the sea." (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Intern) Challenge: many design startups (especially in India) are so
  afraid of losing the client that they don't defend the design at all, which breaks
  a designer's confidence; how much should you alter a design just to please
  somebody, since design is "like a science" you should keep intact? (paraphrase;
  context-attributed)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) His (deliberately unpopular) answer: **we're in the service
  business** — we create designs/illustrations as the service, and without happy
  clients you go out of business fast. It's immature for a young designer to tell a
  client running a $100M company that their taste is wrong and that the kid's design
  sensibility will save the business — that's pompous and presumptuous. (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) Reframe: your job is solving a problem. If you help *define*
  the problem with the client you're viewed as a peer/consultant/expert; if the
  client hands you a diagnosed solution to execute, you're an order-taker. Don't
  impose your own taste (Hello Kitty pink on an Armani client). Don't tell the client
  they're wrong — they run a business and know their customers better than you.
  (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) How he *does* push back: "I will do whatever you want because
  you're the client and it's your decision — having said that, here's why you should
  approach it this way." If you've built trust and have a strong rational,
  business-impact reason, they may go for it; but "red is better than blue" or "this
  font is better than that font" is purely subjective. Design "feels like science but
  it's really arbitrary." (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) The real move is to **align your style and the work you want
  to do with the right client** who wants that — that's where you win or lose. This
  is the core philosophy of his company Blind: put customer needs first, solve that
  problem, don't begin with preconceived answers before diagnosing — "that's why
  we're called Blind." (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) Great **customer service** is the differentiator: all
  designers can make designs, but service is what separates companies and earns
  loyalty and referrals (referencing the good service at the cafe they visited).
  (paraphrase)
- [2016-05-20] (Chris) Closing message: if you live in a country that values design,
  make yourself discoverable (e.g. via Behance); when he looks for talent he doesn't
  care about your city, school, or degree — talent is talent. (paraphrase)
