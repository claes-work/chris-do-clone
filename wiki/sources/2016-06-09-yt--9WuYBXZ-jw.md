# How to Get Hired as a Graphic Designer 2016

## Summary

A short Q&A clip from The Futur's early "The Process" era (2016). An audience member
(context) asks how a new graduate can make themselves valuable — a commodity/asset — to
a motion or identity firm they are targeting. Chris Do answers from his perspective as a
hiring principal at his design firm Blind, laying out the two concrete craft skills he
screens for, why he'd rather hire pre-skilled people than train from scratch, and the
origin rationale for turning that training bottleneck into a scalable paid typography
course (an early articulation of the education-to-hiring funnel that becomes The Futur).

The clip overlaps heavily with his other 2015–2016 hiring/career material (portfolio
tailoring, "know your craft/tools," corporate-cool logos, typography as the tell of a
good designer). It does not clearly break new ground, so it is left at L2.

## Key claims

All claims below are Chris Do (2016-06-09), paraphrased unless quoted.

- When evaluating anyone joining — intern, apprentice, staff, or freelancer — he screens
  for "certain skills I can work with." Right now (he stresses this changes over time) he
  looks for two specific things. (paraphrase)
- Skill 1: the ability to design logos — specifically **corporate but interesting**
  ("boring corporate" done well). Rustic-restaurant or hipster/crest/"x's" logos are
  cool but not the kind of client he serves. He cites **Glandor and Associates** (his
  spelling; likely Chermayeff & Geismar or similar big identity firm — see note) as a big
  firm that makes corporate look cool, naming H&R Block, Kinko's, and FedEx as examples of
  big corporate logos. (paraphrase) *(Firm name is a transcript mis-hearing; the logos
  cited — FedEx especially — point to Landor / a major branding house.)*
- Skill 2: **typography** — how well you set type. Those two skills together usually tell
  him you're a really good designer. (paraphrase)
- He needs candidates to already know the craft and the tools: "I can teach you a lot but
  I don't want to spend time teaching you Photoshop, Illustrator, or... Premiere" (for
  editors). Coming in with tools you know plus eagerness to learn puts you in a good
  position. (paraphrase + quote below)
- Observation on schools: regardless of which school people attend, their typography
  skills are "not sharp enough" for him to consider them good. (paraphrase)
- The "evil business plan" (origin of the productized education model): rather than paying
  people and spending his own time training them, he'll teach a **6–7 week typography
  course**, charge money for it, take ~30 students at a time, videotape it, and put it out
  — so he can teach at scale and then **hire the ones who are good.** (paraphrase) This is
  an explicit statement of the education-as-recruiting-funnel logic behind The Futur.
- Broader answer on making yourself valuable to a company: study the work the target firm
  does and align your own work more closely with it. (paraphrase)
- Tactical portfolio advice: it's fine to make **multiple landing pages / sites / tailored
  portfolios** — send a fashion client only your watercolor illustration work, send a
  corporate-ID prospect only typography and graphics, etc. Match the portfolio you send to
  the job. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (persona voice):

> "I can teach you a lot but I don't want to spend time teaching you Photoshop,
> Illustrator, or if you're an editor for me I don't want to teach you Premiere."

> "I've devised a new evil business plan... rather than you come here, me to pay you and
> then spend all my time teaching you, I'm going to teach a typography course that you can
> do in six or seven weeks, I'm going to charge money for it, take about 30 students on at
> a time, teach it, videotape it, and put it out there — so I can teach a lot of people...
> the ones that are good, I'm going to hire."

> "There are a lot of people who have the potential of being great, I just don't know it
> based on what you're showing me."

Audience question (context, not persona):

> "As a new graduate, how do you become valuable or a commodity to the motion or identity
> firm that you're targeting?"
