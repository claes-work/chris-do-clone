# How To: BE A HAPPIER, BETTER DESIGNER (AMA)

★ L3-candidate — adds distinct mindset material (redefining success as personal growth; "absent a narrative people invent one"; how-to-apologize framework) that is worth promoting into `wiki/topics/mindset/`.

## Summary

An early AMA episode of "The Process" (2016), hosted by [[../entities/chris-do]] with co-host Jose (spoken intro banter only). Two recent Art Center College of Design graduates — Michelle Fong and Emily Shay — put questions to Chris, with a third person, Rashab, contributing a relayed question. The through-line is designer wellbeing and how to be happier and better at the craft: reframing what "the end result" means, taking credit for growth, not disappointing clients, pricing yourself as a recent grad, maintaining client relationships, and why designers avoid transparency. Chris answers as a mentor/faculty figure (he taught these graduates).

## Key claims

Speakers: **Chris Do** (subject/persona), **Jose** (co-host, context — intro only), **Michelle Fong** / **Emily Shay** / **Rashab** (guests/questioners, context).

- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — Design turns abstract ideas into tangible things, and the idea in your head is always better than what you make; disappointment is inherent to translating the "amorphous thing" in your mind into something concrete. [self-reported / paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — Instead of measuring a result by how closely it matches the vision in your head (which it never will), redefine the end result as how much you grew: how much risk you took, how far you stretched the idea, how far you pushed yourself. [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — Designers/creatives fear the work won't measure up, so they stay in their comfort zone making unchallenging work "where we cannot fail" — but that itself should be the disappointing outcome. [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Michelle Fong (guest, context) — In critique culture you get used to cataloguing what went wrong; it's equally important to count what went right and to celebrate new skills learned. [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — Critique-based structures (school, faculty) constantly "chip you down"; take an accounting of what you did right — new tools, styles, techniques, shortcuts. After 20+ years he stays motivated by giving himself credit for the little things he does right, which "gives me fuel to keep pushing forward." [self-reported / paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — On client expectations: "under promise, overdeliver." Don't promise 14 versions; promise two and bring four. Overpromising (e.g. eight, delivering four) sets you up to underwhelm. [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — He dislikes framing work as "impressing" the client because that implies validating himself through them; he already knows whether the work is good or working. [self-reported / paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — To avoid client disappointment, elevate the conversation away from purely subjective aesthetics ("wrong shade of red") toward marketing/messaging goals and defined success criteria — harder to answer and deliver, but it creates more value and avoids client-vs-designer battles. [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do (advice to recent grads on pricing) — Don't overprice your skill level; price is relative to your skill vs. peers and to what the company can afford. Overpricing "creates friction where none existed." Rather than stating a price, turn it into a question: express genuine preference to work there and ask what makes sense to them, which invokes their sense of responsibility instead of a negotiation. [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — Take the offer (unless it's exploitative), put a trial time period on it (weeks/a month), then review against expectations. It's easy to raise rates later; it's hard to recover a reputation after coming in overpriced and underdelivering — "your reputation is all you have." [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — Be of service: if you finish early, don't pretend to look busy — ask for more work and "endear yourself." In 21st-century businesses it's dangerous NOT to be noticed (inverting the old corporate adage that the nail that sticks out gets hammered); flying under the radar flies you "straight out of a job." [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — Once you get the "yes" in an interview/pitch, stop selling — "going past the sale" risks showing work that makes them reconsider. He only needs to see three-to-five consistent pieces; needing more means he's unsure of your work. [self-reported / paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do (Rashab's relayed question, on maintaining relationships) — Little mistakes, taking the client for granted, and poor communication "will kill the job every single time." If clients are calling to ask what's happening, that's bad client management. [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — "Absent a narrative, people invent one" — without status updates clients fill the silence with an unflattering story (imagining you're on Facebook Live instead of grinding on their project). Build visibility/transparency via frequent check-ins and status updates. [self-reported / paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — Give yourself a time cushion (estimate 3 hours, promise 6–12) so delivery lands early and delights rather than late and angers. Covering up gets you into more trouble than telling the truth. [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — Don't act like you're gifting clients your creative genius; they support your livelihood ("you have rent, you have food, because somebody agreed to give you money"). Cites his business coach Kier: all companies are the same — every company does marketing and every company is a customer-service company, whether it makes widgets, tacos, or graphic design. [self-reported / paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do (Emily Shay's "philosophical" question, why designers aren't transparent) — Opacity is a confidence problem and an "imagined battle" in your head; harsh self-judgment amplifies in "the echo chamber of your brain," but "the truth will set you free." [paraphrase]
- [2016-06-16] Chris Do — When you mess up, first diffuse the anger: come in "hands up," apologize plainly, take fault, give NO excuses, and commit to making it right. People come looking for a fight and stop once you own it; arguing may win the battle but loses the war (they won't want to work with you again). [paraphrase]

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (persona voice):

> "My motto is under promise, over deliver."

> "Nothing is ever as good as you imagine it."

> "Measure on the success and failure of how much you've grown. How much risk did you take? How far did you stretch an idea? How far did you push yourself?"

> "I don't like to use the word impressed because it implies I'm trying to validate what I do through them. I know that the work is good. I know it's not good. I don't need them to tell me that."

> "The most dangerous thing that you can do for yourself is overprice your skill level, because you're going to create friction where none existed."

> "Your reputation is all you have."

> "In 21st century businesses it's dangerous not to be noticed... if you're flying under the radar you're going to fly yourself straight out of a job."

> "It's called going past the sale, and you don't want to go past the sale."

> "Absent a narrative, people invent one... we make up a story, and usually the story is not very flattering."

> "You'll get into more trouble covering up stuff than just telling people the truth."

> "Don't act like they're — you're doing them a favor by gifting them your creative genius."

> "All companies do marketing, and all companies are customer service companies. Period." (attributing the idea to his business coach, Kier)

> "The truth will set you free."

> "I'm so sorry. I screwed up. This is my fault. I'm going to make this right... Notice how I gave no excuses."

> "At the end of the day you might win the battle, but you'll lose the war."

Michelle Fong (guest, context — NOT persona voice):

> "In critique we're very used to taking in like what went wrong, what could have been better... but it's also important to remember to count what went right — like what new skills did I learn — and being really happy with that and celebrating that too."
