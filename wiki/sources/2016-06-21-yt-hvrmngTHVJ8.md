# Where Do Good Ideas Come From? Plagiarism or Influence?

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do lays out a distinct, articulated creativity/originality framework ("everything comes from something," copy-transform-combine, sell ideas not craft, ban the word "plagiarism") worth promoting into `wiki/topics/` and `persona/beliefs.md`.

## Summary

A panel episode of *The Process* (an early @thefutur / The Skool Network format, 2016)
recorded on a "brand new set." Host **Chris Do** convenes three others to debate
authorship, originality, plagiarism, and influence, sparked by an Art Center faculty
meeting on students borrowing work without crediting sources. Panelists:

- **Chris Do** — SUBJECT / persona voice.
- **Jose Caballer** — co-host ("I designed sandwiches… I talk about the design of
  business"). CONTEXT, not persona.
- **Alison Goodman** — Art Center faculty. CONTEXT.
- **Petrula Vrontikis** — Art Center faculty. CONTEXT.

The debate splits along a fault line: the two faculty (Goodman, Vrontikis) treat
plagiarism as real, definable, and tied to intent, process, and referencing sources;
Chris takes the provocateur position that there is "no such thing as an original idea,"
that everything is remix, and that he would "ban the word plagiarism." Much of the
value is Chris's own creativity framework and origin story (his early ad-industry
mentor who assembled storyboards from a film library, teaching Chris to "sell ideas,
not the crafting of the frame"). The group agrees the truly bad case is lazy, fearful,
uncredited ripoff sold for profit; the productive case is deep research, remix, and
being able to articulate the *why*. Shepard Fairey's career is used as the contested
test case.

## Key claims

Dated 2016-06-21 (source date). Paraphrased unless quoted. Attributed per speaker.

**Chris Do (SUBJECT / persona):**
- Believes there is no such thing as a truly original idea; all his ideas come from what
  he reads, people he talks to, professors — a shared "universe of ideas." (paraphrase)
- Cites the film **Everything Is a Remix** by **Kirby Ferguson** — his citation, not his
  origination — and its formula **copy, transform, combine** as "the basic elements of
  all creativity." (paraphrase; Ferguson quoted below)
- Cites **Picasso**: "good artists borrow, great artists steal" — his citation — to
  justify telling students to "steal away." (paraphrase + quote below)
- In his classroom he gives students "free permission and rein to steal" to accelerate
  learning; he does NOT require them to footnote/cite where they stole from — "I don't
  care" — because he prioritizes getting work done and learning over attribution. (paraphrase)
- Distinguishes black (blatant, lazy, one-font-swap copying — bad), white, and a large
  "gray" middle where reuse of un-ownable elements (Helvetica, clean minimal layouts) is
  legitimate: "I don't think the idea of Helvetica or simple clean layouts is something
  that anybody owns." (paraphrase + quote below)
- For him plagiarism reduces entirely to **intent**, which is unknowable from outside —
  so he refuses to police it and instead asks only: is the work good, is it interesting,
  did you improve on the source? (paraphrase)
- Origin story / core lesson: early in his career he struggled shooting his own photos
  and could barely comp "two frames of one lame idea"; a friend produced three full
  storyboards fast by pulling clips from a film library and re-sequencing them. Chris
  first reacted "you cheater… that's plagiarism," then realized the friend "was selling
  the idea and not the crafting of the frame." This became his stance: **sell ideas, not
  making skills.** (paraphrase + quote below)
- In pre-production this practice is legitimate and named: borrowing clips to "block out
  the idea" is **pre-visualization** ("previs"), because live shooting is expensive and
  you must know timing/angles first. (paraphrase)
- Master-copy pedagogy (as practiced with his students): assign a beautifully crafted
  frame and have students reproduce it as closely as possible *without* using the
  original's elements; the technical gap guarantees it changes — "it can't be a pixel for
  pixel copy… it's not trying to stand in as a forgery" — so genuine ripoff is nearly
  impossible and learning is forced. (paraphrase)
- "Stand on the shoulders of giants" — progress in medicine, technology, and art is
  cumulative; asks why design should be an exception. Cites **Steve Jobs at Xerox PARC**
  seeing the GUI and building Apple as proof that "everything comes from something and we
  keep building." (paraphrase)
- On collaboration: schools wrongly train students to "build a trench" around someone
  else's idea and not touch it, which cripples brainstorming; better to teach "can I say
  what you said and add one new idea or reframe it" — that additive move is "the genius
  part" of collaboration and progress. (paraphrase)
- Would "ban the word plagiarism" if made king of design because he "believes in
  progress"; wants students focused on articulating ideas and making good work rather
  than crediting. (paraphrase + quote below)
- On **Shepard Fairey**: calls the OBEY/Andre-the-Giant career "heavily borrowed" —
  the word "obey" and imagery trace to the cult film *They Live* (Roddy Piper), and the
  art style to Russian Constructivism; but by episode's end says he no longer cares —
  "I'm thrilled for Shepard to be a fine artist… just make something good that the world
  enjoys… and make it a little bit better." Notes Fairey only acknowledged sources "after
  the fact… a little revisionist history." (paraphrase + quote below)
- Analogy: a person raised from infancy in a white room with no stimulation "may make
  work that's original to me but not original to the world" — originality is relative to
  exposure. (paraphrase)
- Catalogs advertising as especially prone to unoriginality — claims there are only
  ~"seven ideas in advertising" and that he has "cataloged them"; companies pay millions
  for unoriginal ideas because clients aren't savvy enough to spot the ripoff. (paraphrase)

**Alison Goodman (CONTEXT — Art Center faculty):**
- School is for growth: hitting a wall, admitting it, asking for help; plagiarism is a
  "lessening of the entire educational experience." (paraphrase)
- Plagiarism is a fear-based reaction that occurs when people feel they lack talent,
  time, or resources; rarer in an elite, supported school. (paraphrase)
- Master-copy assignments are research/being-influenced, not plagiarism — the productive
  end of the spectrum. (paraphrase)

**Petrula Vrontikis (CONTEXT — Art Center faculty):**
- Plagiarism is a "blatant ripoff" of someone's ideas, visual language, style, tone, and
  concept — done "shamelessly"; concept theft is as bad as visual theft and more common
  than discussed. (paraphrase)
- The test is **process and intention**: openly collecting/researching and building from
  a "conglomeration" of appropriate references spares you "the agony of plagiarism." Asks
  Chris "what's the exit plan from plagiarism?" (paraphrase)
- Wonders aloud whether "Pinterest is just social media plagiarism." (paraphrase)
- Plagiarism = profiting from someone else's work (meeting a school deadline, selling to
  a client); imitation as part of learning is different. (paraphrase)
- Shepard Fairey's Obama poster was clear-cut ("absolutely plagiarism… he admitted it");
  cites the ramifications: awards, being sued, the client sued. (paraphrase)

**Jose Caballer (CONTEXT — co-host):**
- Frames the definitional question ("what constitutes plagiarism to you… who's the
  judge… what is influence?") and repeatedly presses the panel toward parameters.
  (paraphrase)
- On Star Wars: George Lucas cut WWII dogfight footage into the Death Star trench run;
  raises it as a plagiarism-vs-remix-vs-previs test case. (paraphrase)
- Notes "digital promiscuity" among the millennial generation — and explicitly credits
  the term to "Scott," flagging it isn't his own (a live modelling of citation).
  (paraphrase; attribution of the coinage: uncertain)

**Cited third-party works surfaced in the discussion (as references, not originations):**
- Kirby Ferguson, *Everything Is a Remix* (copy/transform/combine; Danger Mouse's *Grey
  Album* example). — Chris's citation.
- Picasso: "good artists borrow, great artists steal." — Chris's citation.
- Magritte (picture-within-a-picture / collapsing foreground-background). — surfaced by
  Chris and Goodman.
- Walter Benjamin, *The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction*. — Vrontikis's citation.
- John Kenneth Galbraith, *The New Industrial State* ("everything that was going to be
  invented was invented already"; toothpaste example). — Vrontikis's citation.
- Steven Heller / Mirko Ilić, *Anatomy of Design* (tracing Sagmeister's influences). — Chris's citation.
- Battleship Potemkin (Odessa Steps) reshot as homage in *The Untouchables* (Brian De
  Palma). — group.
- Robert Doisneau / Henri Cartier-Bresson-type "man jumping over the puddle" photograph
  as a medium-changing moment (photographer name not recalled on camera). — Goodman.

> "My question that I want to ask you, Chris… is what's the exit plan from plagiarism?"
