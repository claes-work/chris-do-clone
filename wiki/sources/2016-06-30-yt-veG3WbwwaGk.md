# Rethinking Design Education 2016 ★ L3-candidate

> ★ **L3-candidate**: This is one of the clearest early statements of Chris Do's
> design-education-reform thesis — the intellectual seed of The Futur's mission
> ("a private art school without the crippling debt"). Recommend promotion to L3
> for topic + persona integration.

## Summary

An episode of *The Process* (2016 era) recorded at Art Center College of Design,
in which Chris Do debates the future of design education with two Art Center faculty
members, Allison Goodman and Petrula Vrontikis (transcript renders her name as
"Patula Veronicus"). Co-host Jose Caballer (transcript: "Jose Cawette") frames and
provokes the discussion.

The jumping-off point: an ethical scenario the faculty raise — elite design students
(with money) secretly hiring other designers to do their coursework, then receiving
praise for a sudden "breakthrough." The faculty treat this as academic dishonesty and
a personal loss for the student. Chris inverts it: he argues that hiring people to
fulfill a creative brief *is* the entrepreneurial business model, and that a student
who did this well should be congratulated, not punished.

From there the conversation broadens into Chris's central thesis: design schools
overwhelmingly train "makers" (production), but in a globally connected 2016 world the
economic value of making is collapsing. Elite schools, he argues, should elevate
students past the maker role into strategic, entrepreneurial, client-facing "art
director / creative director" work that is harder to offshore — so graduates carrying
a quarter-million dollars in debt have a realistic chance of paying it off. The two
faculty push back, defending the necessity and dignity of making (Goodman: "somebody
needs to lay those bricks and they need to lay them well") and warning against
front-loading everything into school. The group converges on "aligning expectations"
with each student's core essence — some are true designers/entrepreneurs, some are
true artists — rather than forcing one path.

The episode foreshadows The Futur strongly: the debt critique, the moral-obligation
framing of educating creatives differently, the maker-vs-strategist distinction, and
Chris positioning himself as the voice of a "revolution in creative culture."

## Key claims

**Chris Do** (2016-06-30):
- Hiring someone else to fulfill a creative brief is not cheating — it is the business
  model of an art director / creative director. If a student did it well, he would
  "hug him, high five" him rather than penalize him. [paraphrase]
- The entrepreneurial mindset: "If you have more money than time, you hire everybody."
  Directing others is more valuable than executing yourself. [paraphrase]
- What design schools teach is fundamentally "maker mentality" = production. Anyone who
  makes something is "in production," however conceptual it feels. [paraphrase]
- In 2016 the world is globally connected; there are far more makers, so the value of
  making is "going really down… really fast." [paraphrase]
- Elite schools should "teach them not to be makers" and elevate students to find
  clients, understand business problems, distill vague creative briefs, assemble and
  manage a team, and make money — work that is "hard to offshore" because it requires
  cultural context, awareness, and command of English. [paraphrase]
- Personal practice: as "a matter of pride" he does as little hands-on work as
  possible. A business coach taught him the economics — if he bills at (illustratively)
  $1,000/hr and does a task a $50/hr person could do, he costs the company $950,
  because few people can do his higher-value work. [self-reported]
- "That's what I've been doing in the last 10 years… that's how I'm able to make the
  kind of money I'm able to make, because I don't get into that anymore." [self-reported]
- Autobiography: his parents were refugees who emphasized education, warning him that
  failing at school meant becoming a bricklayer / ditch-digger. He internalized wanting
  "thinking work," not blue-collar work — and now regards making itself as "brick
  laying… all production." [self-reported]
- Creativity cannot be taught because it is innate in all of us; it is "beaten out"
  through culture, parents, and teachers, so most people go into accounting or Wall
  Street. Survivors who can "connect two disparate ideas" are rare and extremely
  valuable — the source of billion-dollar companies. [paraphrase]
- Designers should be "at the tip of the spear," the vital part of every organization
  if not leading it — changing dialogue and the world, not making "a new typeface" or
  "an original poster." [paraphrase]
- These conversations must happen *now*, before students leave school — he already has
  them with his own children, aged 12 and 10. [self-reported]
- Autobiography: if he hadn't encountered Lynda Weinman teaching After Effects 1.0
  (at "Kosa" / Cosa), he "would not have the career I have today" — a warning against
  gatekeepers who insisted graphic design "will always be about print." [self-reported]
- Advocates introducing advanced/strategic ideas pre-Art Center or early in the program
  so students can "skip past the maker and just go straight into the advanced skills";
  believes essentially no class at the highest level teaches this yet. [paraphrase]
- Frames it as a near "moral obligation" of a top school to lead this revolution in
  creative culture; says he still needs, respects, loves, pays and treats his makers
  well — but a quarter-million-dollar education demands expectation management. [paraphrase]

**Jose Caballer** (co-host, context) (2016-06-30):
- Predicts/agrees that paying others to work for you *is* the business model — "that's
  what you do every day." [paraphrase]
- Presses Chris with the counter-scenario: if you hire that art director who can't
  actually produce his own portfolio work, "have you done him a disservice?" [paraphrase]

**Allison Goodman** (Art Center faculty, context) (2016-06-30):
- Objects to devaluing the maker: "somebody needs to lay those bricks and they need to
  lay them well"; devaluing production is one of the problems in the global economy.
  [paraphrase]
- Rejects the notion that everything must be learned in school — there is time to grow
  and manifest skills afterward. [paraphrase]
- Warns that education has become "transactional" (classes for a degree) largely
  because of cost; college debt and what's learned in vs. after school are bigger
  cultural issues. [paraphrase]
- Prefers "manifestors" over "makers"; wants students to have the "heuristics to be
  learners." [paraphrase]

**Petrula Vrontikis** (Art Center faculty, context; transcript "Patula Veronicus") (2016-06-30):
- A student who secretly outsourced work harms themselves — they get used to it and
  can't recover the ability to produce what their portfolio claims. [paraphrase]
- Insists freshmen must make, because "making is an expression and a nurturer of more
  thinking" and gives them an "internal gyroscope of what it takes to make"; moving from
  maker to "gatherer of resources" should be a choice, never a necessity born of
  inability. [paraphrase]
- Good art directors know how things are made; the "little victories" of individual
  creation are cumulative and build confidence. [paraphrase]
- Endorses Chris's pro-business stance as a valuable part of the faculty *mix* — a long,
  multi-career life means students benefit from instructors who practice design
  differently (business people and "poor artists" alike). [paraphrase]
- Reframes the debate as "aligning expectations" with each student's core essence:
  some are true design-entrepreneurs, some are true artists (often pushed into design
  by parents), and clarity sometimes comes only years later. [paraphrase]
