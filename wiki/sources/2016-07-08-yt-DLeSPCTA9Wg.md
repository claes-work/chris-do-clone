## Summary

A live art-direction session in the co-hosted "The Skool" era of The Futur (then "The Skool"). Chris Do reviews many typographic layout explorations by a designer (referred to as "Minnie"/"Min") for an editorial spread / newspaper for a new internal initiative (a "business of design" journal, masthead working title "thirst"/"THIRST", issue "001 Summer 2016"). Jose Caballer appears only in the joking channel-intro bit. The video frames art direction as giving feedback that lets designers make their own changes and grow — "not sitting on the box moving the mouse around yourself."

Chris walks layout-by-layout, praising and critiquing type treatment, hierarchy, alignment, negative space, and graphic elements, and twice hand-sketches on camera to demonstrate fixes. He closes with a lesson on how to direct a designer humanely (give them permission to say no, set a concrete deadline).

## Key claims

_All dated 2016-07-08. Attributed to Chris Do unless noted. Paraphrased._

- The point of art direction is to give designers feedback so they grow AND the work gets done — not to take over the mouse yourself. (Chris Do)
- Prefer justified/flush-left type over centered type in modern layouts; centered type reads old-fashioned and only works for a strong display piece or when there's too much going on. (Chris Do)
- Contrast is king: commit to a clear big-type / small-type relationship and let everything else breathe rather than sitting in "no-man's land" between two similar sizes. (Chris Do)
- Avoid filling the entire page/space; cutting things off edge-to-edge just makes the page feel smaller and fragments it into separate pieces. Inset boxes (run them a bit past halfway) so space isn't trapped. (Chris Do)
- Repeat elements (a color dot, an angle, a shape) to create harmony and cohesion — like a beat or chorus in music; without repetition a graphic element feels arbitrary/random and "sounds like noise." (Chris Do)
- A stray graphic element (red dot, red line, registration marks, an angle) should tie into / align with something real in the layout, e.g. snap a red dot to the grid alongside a photo credit; otherwise remove it. (Chris Do)
- Use figure-ground ambiguity: overlap type and shapes in a deliberate stacking order (e.g. blue over pink over yellow over white) to create dimension even in a flat 2D space. (Chris Do)
- Establish proportion by working in small / medium / large sizes of a repeated element to tie a composition together. (Chris Do)
- Bleeding elements off the edge looks modern and strong, but costs more in print (a non-issue for purely digital). (Chris Do)
- A thick-line + thin-line ("double line") rule and drop caps read old-fashioned; loosen spacing / add vertical separators or drop them for a modern feel. (Chris Do)
- Big headlines can overlap a photo, but a headline bleeding INTO a photo usually doesn't help — crop the photo higher instead. (Chris Do)
- Mixing a very modern grid/layout with a classic condensed serif can be a nice unexpected contrast; but for body text prefer a sans-serif and reserve the serif for the masthead / as accent contrast. (Chris Do)
- Watch for trapped white space inside letterforms (e.g. inside a "C", or a little white triangle) and awkward tangencies where a thin font's stroke matches a box's stroke weight — a bolder sans gives more "wiggle room"/forgiveness. (Chris Do)
- Don't get "stuck in the middle": push a type treatment to an extreme — avant-garde/postmodern expressive typography OR clean Swiss-modern — rather than landing halfway. (Chris Do)
- On typefaces: Univers is flexible but has "almost no personality"; Chris prefers something with more character like Akzidenz-Grotesk, Helvetica, or (caption-garbled) "Victoria." He praises DIN as a beautiful font and dislikes a Eurostile-looking face as one that "will get old fast." Futura ("Futura"/caption "feature") is part of the established identity. (Chris Do)
- Don't set one word in two different point sizes just to fill space; make them the same size and scale to fit. (Chris Do)
- Treat the layout as three-dimensional — let a band/ribbon of color wrap around the back of the page and reappear on the other side. (Chris Do)
- When a layout isn't working and you don't know why, strip elements away one at a time; e.g. crop an image smaller / off the edge to let white space move around it. (Chris Do)
- A designer exploring nearly every possible expression (not getting stuck on one idea) is a good sign for a designer. (Chris Do)
- On the project brief: a newspaper needs high content density (many writers' perspectives on business/creative entrepreneurship); only one or two spreads should be expressive — don't spread content thin, because printing many pages is costly. (Chris Do)
- Designers often won't show work "in progress" until they feel it's resolved — that's the designer's dilemma, and it's understandable. (Chris Do)
- Directing a designer well: give a concrete deadline (end of day Thursday) AND explicitly give them permission to say no, so they don't over-commit out of intimidation, panic, and disappear. (Chris Do)
