# Freelance Tips Motion Design Los Angeles

★ L3-candidate — contains a distinct, transferable freelance-negotiation framework (hold/book/challenge terminology, "put yourself on hold first" tactic, rate-anchoring psychology, bringing conflict upfront) that goes beyond generic business advice and is specific enough to seed a `wiki/topics/business` page on freelance rate negotiation.

## Summary

An episode of "The Process" (2016) in which Chris Do (owner of Blind, 21 years in business) coaches two working motion-design freelancers — Angie (CalArts grad, 2014) and Julia Wright (Art Center illustration grad; formerly in-house at Urban Outfitters, went freelance in motion ~late 2013) — through the freelancer's core anxieties in the Los Angeles motion-graphics/mograph industry: setting and defending a day rate, hidden long hours, getting paid on time, the feast-or-famine cycle, self-promotion, and dealing with "nightmare" understaffed projects. Chris repeatedly frames the advice as one person's point of view (his), grounded in the LA motion industry, and emphasizes bringing conflict/terms upfront, honesty, and confidence. Two role-play scenarios close the episode: Chris plays a freelancer negotiating with an exec producer ("Bob") over an under-budgeted project, demonstrating both the "stay in" and "walk away" scripts.

Speakers: **Chris Do** = subject/persona. **Jose** = co-host (intro banter only; context). **Angie** and **Julia Wright** = guest freelancers being coached (context).

## Key claims

**Chris Do (2016-07-13) — paraphrase unless quoted:**

- Motion-industry freelancers generally work on a **day rate**, ranging from ~$150/day (low end) to ~$800–900/day (high end); rates were higher when advertising budgets were healthy, and the industry is now in a "adjustment cycle" where work slows, budgets shrink (half or a third), but the same output is expected.
- When someone asks your rate, **just name a number** — "it doesn't have to be your real number" — to practice. New grads should say their rate out loud repeatedly (to family, friends, partner) until the mouth/tongue get used to it, because an unrehearsed rate said with a rising, uncertain voice "gives room to manipulate and negotiate you down."
- Be aware of the value you deliver: don't name a rate you can't deliver at, because faster/better people in the office will "run circles around you" and overpricing yourself creates tension. Chris prefers to be on the **over-delivering** side.
- A **day rate should mean about a day = 8–10 hours** (with a 1-hour lunch); anything beyond that is unreasonable. Crunch near delivery is "par for the course" (the exception, not the norm); a mandatory 12–13 hour day is not.
- If a company negotiates your rate down, **negotiate back** — clarify the hours, because agreeing to a lower rate and then discovering a 12-hour day means "working more hours for less money."
- Companies that deliberately negotiate freelancers down and squeeze them are being dishonest and are damaging themselves: the industry is "very small and tight," talented designers won't return, and word spreads. Chris references an "underground forum" where freelancers blacklist companies and share payment/practice information.
- The producer/staffing side's job is also to make money — get as much from the client, minimize expenses — because companies face feast/famine too; there's real "elasticity" in rates.
- Getting paid: **1–2 months is a long time** for a freelancer (though not for a car company that pays in 6 months). Agree payment terms (2 vs 3 weeks) upfront, confirm in writing so you have documentation.
- **Recourse for non-payment is limited** — small-claims court is rarely worth the time, energy, and emotional toll unless the amount is large. Chris has known people who sued and won but paid an emotional/opportunity cost. Better to trust your "spider sense / gut feeling": if you don't trust that you'll be paid, minimize your loss and walk away.
- You can add **late-payment penalties** to a contract (e.g. 2% interest per month past due, or 5% — whatever is fair) so the invoice bumps up monthly.
- Chris says Blind has been in business 21 years, is not run "hand to mouth," keeps runway, treats a booking as a commitment, and he'd "pay you before I pay myself."
- **Terminology — hold / book / challenge** (Chris says freelancers must know this):
  - **Hold** = a request for right of first refusal for a period; no payment, no commitment, but you can't take another booking for that time without telling the holder first. (Chris warns people have moved cross-country thinking a hold was a full-time job — "a total nightmare.")
  - **Book** = a financial commitment and promise to bring you in at a certain rate for a period.
  - **Challenge** = when a second company wants time you're already held for, they must explicitly "challenge" (which puts them financially on the hook); "I was just inquiring" ≠ a challenge. Get them to say the special words and agree.
- **Cancellation of a booking:** it's up to the freelancer to set fair terms; you're entitled to ask for up to 100% of the booking. Put a clause in email (e.g. cancellation two weeks out = 75% of fees). Analogy to a hotel's 48-hour cancellation window — but a designer can't fill a slot in 48 hours, so pick a comfortable timeframe and create a win-win.
- **"Put yourself on hold first"** — a tactic so callers must commit to *booking* you rather than holding you. Alternatively, just say "I don't accept holds, I'm available at this time." You can charge a reduced cancellation fee (e.g. 30% instead of 75%) as a trade-off. Chris warns this can backfire (people move on) if you're not in demand.
- **Always confirm terms by email and get a "yes" reply** — sending terms without a response is not enough.
- **Rate bumps:** if you're constantly held/challenged, you've "graduated" — bump your rate in $25/$50 increments until you hit the same friction again. If people are "falling all over themselves" to book you at $300, make a bigger jump (e.g. to $500).
- **Managing feast/famine self-promotion:** create a suppressed-recipient email group ("availability group" / "my favorite people") of studios you like and send one blind mass email announcing availability + a link to latest work. Showing 20 visible names is "super tacky" (competitors see each other) — suppress the list; "corporations have been doing this for decades." Higher-touch alternative: personalized one-to-one emails; or use a bulk tool like MailChimp for merged names (but that's "too much work" for Chris).
- **Prefer longer bookings** over shorter, all things equal; push back and ask how long a booking is — a company thinking of you for one short job may commit to two weeks + holds if you put it back in their court. Blind books freelancers it likes for long stretches.
- **Three questions that protect you:** Do you do good work? Do you work hard? Are you a likable person? If yes, and you deliver/exceed the value you charged, honesty about how you feel "should not jeopardize you at all" — and if it does, "they're not a company we're talking to anymore."
- **Nightmare (understaffed/under-bid) project:** have a conversation with whoever booked you once you see a pattern (usually takes 2–3 instances); ask directly whether the long hours are the norm or an anomaly, and get help/staff. Chris demonstrates both a "stay in" script (commit, keep evaluating, ask for more money if the project comes out ahead) and a "walk away" script (it's unfair, give me a day or two to be replaced).
- **Who broke the rule:** if they told you 8–10-hour days and then imposed a 13-hour day, *they* broke the booking first, so leaving isn't breaking your contract. If they never told you the hours, "that's your bad" — you're supposed to establish the hours upfront.
- **Overtime:** you can charge it (e.g. time-and-a-half) but some companies won't book you for it. At Blind, Chris tells booked freelancers *not* to work overtime ("I don't want to see an Overtime Bill, I can't afford it") — when they finish their day, they walk; if he specifically needs more, he'll pay more that day.
- Chris "hates" the overtime/hours fight: a company should do a good job anticipating and managing the work required. He doesn't care if a freelancer is on the internet or watching TV — "I just need you to get your job done and do it well"; if you finish in two hours, "it's because you're that good."
- Recurring meta-principle: **bring the conflict upfront / early**, have terms out in the open, in writing, so you can work "with peace of mind."
- Designers are often **conflict-averse** and end up taking "the short end of the stick," which isn't fair; don't cede all your control or you walk away "unhappy and resentful."
- Perspective/mindset: if you manage expenses and live within your means, "the amount of money you make per day is actually ridiculous" — a commercial artist in motion design can do better than after 10 years of school for doctors/lawyers. And "we're making cartoons all day long, we should have some fun."

**Angie (guest, context) (2016-07-13) — paraphrase:** Her biggest concerns: studios ask her rate then talk her down; hidden long hours (e.g. told she'd work 10am–11pm as the daily norm); and getting paid late (studios taking 1–2 months, requiring repeated harassing emails).

**Julia Wright (guest, context) (2016-07-13) — paraphrase:** Has had an easy time getting paid (she's often the one invoicing); her concerns are self-promotion / getting a foot in the door at new places, and managing feast-or-famine offer flow. She prioritizes a few consistent studios she enjoys, uses the hold system, and keeps an email log of who she's contacted.
