## Summary

An episode of "The Skool" (2014–2016 co-hosted era of The Futur) in which Chris Do introduces creative director **Matthew Encina**, who then teaches a design-theory tutorial on making **dynamic compositions** by **adding contrast**. Matthew adapted the lesson from a Medium article he wrote. He demonstrates in a design app across three worked examples: (1) an elk-and-wolf illustration showing how contrast in size and value establishes predator/prey relationship and can flip the story's point of view; (2) a lineup of knights and a king showing how contrast establishes visual hierarchy (first/second/third "read"); (3) the numerals 1–2–3 in type showing the same principles create movement/rhythm. The core thesis: contrast in size, weight, value, and color controls the eye, defines hierarchy, creates movement, and tells better stories — across photos, illustrations, and typography alike.

## Key claims

All dated 2016-07-16. Speaker labeled per fidelity rules. Note: nearly all substantive claims are **Matthew Encina** (context, not persona training data).

- (Chris Do, intro) Matthew Encina is one of The Futur's two creative directors; he has directed spots for Xbox (E3 launch of the Xbox One S) and music videos for bands including Coldplay. — paraphrase, self/host-reported.
- (Jose Caballer / Chris Do, show open — garbled) The show frames itself around "the design of business" (Jose) and "the business of design" (Chris Do). — paraphrase; see verbatim below.
- (Matthew Encina) "Adding contrast" means manipulating the size, weight, value, and color of the elements in a frame; doing so lets you control the eye, define hierarchy, movement, and meaning, and tell better stories — regardless of whether the frame is a photo, illustration, design, or typography. — paraphrase.
- (Matthew Encina) Tip 1: when adding contrast, go **extreme first, then pull it back** — a little contrast often does too little to read. — paraphrase.
- (Matthew Encina) Foreground elements typically carry more contrast (brighter brights, darker darks); as elements recede into the background they have less contrast and lighter values. — paraphrase.
- (Matthew Encina) You can flip a scene's point of view purely by swapping the relative size and value of subjects — without changing the poses at all (demonstrated by re-telling the wolf/elk scene from the elk's perspective). — paraphrase.
- (Matthew Encina) Tip 2: the thing that should be most important in the frame should be the most prominent — give it the darkest values and most extreme contrast so it becomes the first "read"; secondary and tertiary reads support the story/context. — paraphrase.
- (Matthew Encina) The same size/weight/value principles that work on illustrations apply identically to typography; adjusting type weight and value across elements creates rhythm and directs eye movement (e.g., a clockwise 1→2→3 flow). — paraphrase.
- (Matthew Encina) Tip 3: for those without formal typography training — "type is not scary"; treat type like any other shape or object and the same composition rules apply. — paraphrase.
- (Matthew Encina) He used the typeface Museo Sans in the type example, chosen because it has many weights to work with. — paraphrase.
