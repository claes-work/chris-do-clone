# Graphic Design Tutorial: Web Design Review

## Summary

An episode of **"The Process"** (2016 era) in which **Chris Do** reviews an
in-progress web design with **Jamie Van Wart**, a designer at Blind (Cal Arts MFA
graduate) who has been building the redesign of Blind's own website
(theblind.com). Co-host **Jose Caballer** appears only in the intro banter. Rather
than a harsh critique, Chris frames it as a walkthrough — he explicitly notes at
the end that there wasn't much to critique "because the design was already solid."
Instead the video functions as a teaching tour of how wireframes translate into
visual design and the UX/craft reasoning behind each decision.

Chris uses the review to explain several web-design fundamentals: site maps,
responsive grid systems, the hero / masthead / "above the fold" concept, headlines
as attention hooks, featured-work sorting/curation, logo quilts as social proof,
case studies vs. standard project pages, breaking up monolithic copy blocks into
scannable editorial chunks, hover interactions, filterable work galleries, and
calls to action. The second half pivots into live copywriting/positioning — Chris
brainstorms a "we build Bridges" headline framework tied to Blind's strategic
repositioning from a motion-design house toward direct-client branding/strategy
work.

Attribution note: the **critique lens and design/business principles are Chris Do
(persona)**. Jamie Van Wart is a **context** figure describing her own design
choices; the website (Blind) is the subject of the review, not persona material.

## Key claims

- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) Blind is repositioning: for 21 years a
  motion-design-centric company, it is now shifting toward working with clients
  direct — serving clients who don't need a full advertising agency, doing
  rebrands, video-related (not commercial) content, and digital collateral
  marketing.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) A site map should be sketched and referenced
  before layout; Blind's structure = Home → Work, Feed, Events, About, Contact.
  Assume visitors land wanting to see the work right away, so Work sits first.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) A responsive grid re-configures itself as the
  window changes; Blind's four-column desktop grid collapses to two or one on
  mobile — "a number that works" that holds up across breakpoints.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) Much web terminology is borrowed from newspaper
  editorial layout — "hero," "masthead," and "above the fold" all come from
  newspapers. Everything above the fold is critical: if a visitor doesn't
  immediately see or hear what they want, they never scroll below it.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) The headline matters more than the hero image —
  the image is striking, but the headline is what grabs attention and drives the
  visitor deeper. "If we lose somebody right here, there's no point."
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) Featured work needs a sorting system: default
  chronological, but taggable/assignable by number so the team controls the
  display hierarchy. Tell the developer to build this.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) Good UX gives people multiple ways to find
  something (e.g. a "view all projects" link) so they don't have to scroll back up
  once they've had enough information.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) A "logo quilt" exists because companies need
  validation — clients like to work with firms that have a proven track record and
  who've worked with companies of their vertical, size, and stature. Logos must be
  real clients; faking them backfires when a prospect asks for specifics.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) UX means designing for the user: giving them
  what they want in the fewest number of steps. Visitors want to find work like
  what they're envisioning — filterable/sortable galleries serve that.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) Hover states can surface project info on demand,
  keeping the layout from being cluttered with typography (with a dimming overlay
  so text is legible) — but note that mobile has no hover equivalent and must be
  handled separately.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) Case studies are more valuable than standard
  project tiles because they tell the story of what the studio actually did —
  strategy, discovery, messaging, positioning, naming, identity — countering the
  fact that many firms misrepresent their true contribution.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) A case-study structure of Client → Challenge →
  Solution/Outcome, plus an intro block, makes the work legible from the client's
  point of view.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) Designers add value by extracting information —
  making type "more dense" by pulling more structure (headline, subhead, callouts,
  bullets, body) out of a monolithic copy block, breaking the single-column grid
  for visual variation and easier digestion.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) Pair text with the image it corresponds to so
  there's always context. The web is not designed for giant blocks of copy —
  "watch out for the Big Blocks of copy" unless you're a Wall Street Journal / LA
  Times.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) A solid layout avoids overlapping images and
  type — that overlap is "usually where you get into a lot of problems" when it
  comes time to program it; design sensibility should not make a page impossible to
  build.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase) Positioning insight: the work product is not
  what you sell — it's the byproduct of the approach you take. Firms must give a
  contextual lens on how and why they work, not just show finished pieces.
- [2016-07-23] (Chris, paraphrase / opinion, dated) To be relevant "in the 21st
  century, in 2016," a design company must offer services that position it to be
  valued for its ideas and thinking, not just executional/production work — framed
  explicitly as his opinion and "just one way."
- [2016-07-23] (Jamie Van Wart, context, paraphrase) Design choices she describes:
  mobile version presented as a single strip; the hero shows work imagery plus a
  strong statement of who Blind is; placeholder images stand in until copy is
  written; case studies add block quotes, captions, and more grid variation while
  standard projects flow as a more template-able image carousel.
