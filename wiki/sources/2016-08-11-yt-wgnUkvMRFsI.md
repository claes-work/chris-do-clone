# How to Write Content for Web

## Summary

An episode of *The Process* (2016) documenting Chris Do and his company (then
"Blind") relaunching and repositioning their website. Chris leads a live
brainstorming/working session with **Janica**, a hired copywriter, to hammer out
the landing-page copy that will reposition the studio from a motion-graphics shop
into a "brand strategy agency" and "business design consultancy."

**Who leads:** Chris Do leads throughout. This is Chris coaching/directing his
writer — he does ~90% of the talking, laying out his positioning thinking,
metaphors, user profiles, and copywriting frameworks; Janica mostly responds,
asks clarifying questions, and describes her own writing process. Co-founder
**Jose Caballer** appears only in the cold-open montage (the show's stock intro,
where he says "I talk about the design of business" vs. Chris's "I talk about
the business of design") — Jose is NOT part of the working session. So the vast
majority of substantive content is Chris-attributed = persona-eligible. Janica's
process description is CONTEXT.

## Key claims

**Chris Do** (2016-08-11, paraphrase unless quoted):
- The core problem the studio solves is a "gap" — between what you think and the
  words that express it, between goals and results, between brands and consumers.
- They identified three keywords/search phrases to rank top-10 on Google; they
  want to be known as a "brand strategy agency" and a "business design
  consultancy," and are writing copy to attract people searching those terms.
- Historically the old site was portfolio/reel-driven ("just show me the work");
  the new site must be user-centered — think about what the user (a prospective
  client) actually wants, which is to see if the studio is the right fit.
- Invokes Simon Sinek's "Start With Why": most companies talk about *what* they
  do, some know *how*, very few know *why*; Apple leads with why/beliefs/values.
- Central metaphor: **"we build bridges."** Ideas (the "spirit") → form (the
  tangible thing); "strategy conceived" → "strategy realized" (the word strategy
  must appear twice). The bridge normally breaks down because smart
  strategists/writers have no connection to how the work is made, and the most
  creative people only give the solution *form* rather than helping solve the
  problem.
- Warns against copy that leans on "artful," "beautiful design," or
  "award-winning" — "that's not what matters"; focus on what's in it for the end
  user.
- Value proposition: they facilitate dialogue, build **user profiles** (pains &
  gains, wins & losses) to surface users' needs vs. wants, then form a strategy
  and give it form — the "golden triangle": understand users → figure out
  strategy → give it form.
- On the elevator pitch: "We bridge the gaps between business needs and user
  wants to design innovative brand strategies" is the right sentiment but a
  mouthful; needs a condensed version explainable "to your mom or a fifth
  grader" (cites Apple's "Think Different" / "Just Do It" as clarity benchmarks).
- Likes structuring things in **threes** ("phrase one, two, three"); proposes a
  game of picking one anchor word per side (e.g., bridge X to Y) — candidate gap
  pairs: strategy→execution, **goals→results** (his favorite: simple, everyone
  gets it), brand→users, external perception→internal value.
- CMO user profile: on the site to increase revenue, grow market share, increase
  awareness, drive engagement, and get better marketing materials — often
  because their outward brand feels out of alignment with who they feel they are
  internally.
- "Making a million-dollar brand look like a billion-dollar brand" is a job
  designers are good at — making things look more expensive, credible, or
  trustworthy.
- Second user profile: the "discerning executive/CEO" — brand-conscious,
  design-savvy, wants best-of-the-best, great service, a fair (not cheap) price,
  and minimized risk.
- **Design thinking** = taking a magnifying glass to users' lives (pains/gains,
  +/−) and helping solve those problems, from a point of **empathy** ("it's not
  about us, it's about them"). Notes both he and Janica came to the term
  recently; it's a relatively new (last 10–15 years) part of the design lexicon.
- **AIDA copywriting framework** (he calls it "ADA/AIDA"): learned via a
  breakdown of the *Glengarry Glen Ross* Alec Baldwin scene — **A**ttention (the
  headline should be self-selecting and appeal to self-interest; cites *How to
  Win Friends and Influence People* as an example of a self-selecting title),
  **D**etail (the how), **D**esired state (create the aspirational picture), and
  **call to Action** (the clearer the better — start a project, get a quote).
- On tone/positioning: prefer "we're here to help" over "trust us" ("when you say
  trust us, I don't trust you"); show empathy by speaking directly to what's
  going on and by asking questions rather than selling — "the quality of the
  questions you ask will then express your value."
- If a company is searching for outside help, something isn't working ("if you
  are happily married, you don't search"); they only want clients who want to
  grow, innovate, and push — high-performing "alpha" executives who aren't
  complacent.

**Janica** (writer, CONTEXT — not persona):
- Describes her writing process: go at it and let it flow, write a bunch, then
  delete and refine; spends a long time on one sentence via iterative editing.
- Asks clarifying interview-style questions (whether CMOs are aware of the "gap,"
  whether to frame the approach as new).

**Jose Caballer** (intro montage only, CONTEXT): "I talk about the design of
business" (vs. Chris's "the business of design").

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (persona voice):

> "My name is Chris Doe and I talk about the business of design." (intro)

> "Most companies talk about what they do and some of them know how they do it
> but very few of them know why they do what they do." (paraphrasing Sinek)

> "So, I want to be careful about any language that talks about artful this or
> that or just beautiful design or award-winning because that's not what
> matters."

> "The way I like to think of it is if you had to explain it to your mom or a
> fifth grader... when you can explain it that way, like Think Different, I don't
> have to explain it to anybody. It's that clear."

> "We make stuff look more expensive than what it really is. Or we make it look
> more credible or more trustworthy."

> "Change is inevitable, but progress is a choice."

> "The hardest thing in the world is to know yourself. So when we're talking
> about companies and why they hire outside brand consultancies is because
> they've lost their way. They forgot who they are. They forgot why they exist."

> "I want to have a point of view. I'm okay with it being wrong, but I want to
> have a point of view."

> "When you say trust us, I don't trust you."

> "Don't sell. Don't tell them you're an expert. Don't tell them trust us. Don't
> tell them you're valuable. Just ask them some questions. And the quality of the
> questions you ask will then express your value. It demonstrates your value."

> "I literally can go into a room and say, 'I don't know anything about this at
> all,' but say it in a way that earns me respect."

## Notes

- ★ **L3-candidate.** Chris contributes two distinct, reusable copywriting
  frameworks he clearly owns/teaches: (1) the **AIDA** structure applied to web
  headlines (self-selecting headline appealing to self-interest → detail →
  desired state → call to action) and (2) the "bridge the gap" positioning
  device with the "explain it to a fifth grader" clarity test and the rule of
  threes. These, plus "don't sell — ask questions; the quality of your questions
  expresses your value," are recurring Chris Do teachings worth promoting into
  topics/persona during synthesis.
- Attribution is clear/high-confidence: single-speaker-led session; Chris is
  named on screen and does nearly all substantive talking. Janica's contributions
  are marked as context. Jose's one line is intro boilerplate.
- Company was called "Blind" at this time (line 563 references "blind"); the
  studio later became known through The Futur. Note for entity cross-linking.
