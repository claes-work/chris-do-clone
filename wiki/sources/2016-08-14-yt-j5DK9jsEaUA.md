# How to design a website from wireframes

## Summary

An episode of **"The Process"** (early The Futur / Blind era, 2016). Chris Do leads a
live design review of the in-progress **Oles fishing lodge** website, sitting beside
**Charlene Chen** — an MFA candidate at CalArts interning at his studio for the summer,
who did the actual visual design. Chris drives the session: he narrates the wireframe
(built from an earlier discovery/planning session with **Jose Caballer**), then walks
through Charlene's iterations from blocky first pass to near-final comps, critiquing each
in real time. Jose appears only in the standard cold-open ("I design the businesses… I
talk about the business of design") and is referenced as the strategist who ran the
discovery/wireframe stage; he is **not** present in the review itself.

**Who leads:** Chris Do (critique, art direction, teaching). Charlene Chen is the
designer being coached and explains her own choices. Because the persona clones only
Chris Do, Charlene's and Jose's lines are **CONTEXT**; Chris's design-craft commentary is
persona voice. Attribution is clear throughout (distinct on-screen speakers), so no
`attribution: uncertain` flag is needed.

## Key claims

_All dated 2016-08-14. Paraphrased unless quoted. Speaker attributed._

**Chris Do — process / method:**
- Design is an iterative process, especially for web; do not try to reach perfection on
  step one, because leaving work rough creates room for the designer to talk back,
  collaborate, and try ideas without over-committing — the goal is the best of both
  people's thinking. (paraphrase)
- His model of the process (crediting Jose Caballer's framing): it's like sculpture —
  you chip away at the marble to find the form, get closer and closer, then use
  "sandpaper" to refine; first you just block things in. (paraphrase)
- A designer must be given **space** to explore and **time** to do it — you can't ask for
  four versions "in ten minutes"; then you have conversations about the work. He'd rather
  a designer explore the problem than come back with a single option. (paraphrase)
- Wireframes are "just chunks of boxes and copy that are roughly representative of the
  site architecture and certain features and functions." (near-verbatim)
- Strategy should anchor the visuals: he points back to the original strategy document,
  which included photographic references to define the brand — and says that reference is
  what Charlene ultimately dialed the color correction toward. (paraphrase)
- Two brand keywords guiding the whole aesthetic: **"refined rustic."** The corporate/
  digital/template look is the failure mode to avoid; the target is outdoorsy cabin /
  "lumberjack" vibe, "perfectly imperfect." (paraphrase + quotes)

**Chris Do — craft / Photoshop technique:**
- Depth beats flatness: flat comps (where the wood, photo, and map feel 1/16" apart)
  need color correction, vignettes, a light source, and cast shadows to create depth.
  (paraphrase)
- Bent/uneven shadows create the optical illusion that stacked photos aren't sitting
  perfectly flat "sandwiched between two pieces of glass" — makes a layout far more
  dynamic. Demonstrated live with a curved sheet of paper. (paraphrase)
- Avoid fully-blurred images (they read as fake / "you don't know how to focus"); instead
  fake **selective focus** to simulate shallow depth of field — keep some parts sharp.
  (paraphrase)
- Blending a landscape photo's edges into the whole composition erases boxiness so "we
  don't know where the photo ends and begins." (paraphrase)
- Photo-detail realism: representative photos should show the white edge / correct margins
  so they read as photographs, not a "photo monster on a piece of paper." A Polaroid has
  thicker bottom margins — get the proportion right. (paraphrase)
- Decorative elements (stars, motifs) exist largely to fill negative space so letterforms
  and elements don't feel "trapped." (paraphrase)
- Meaning via juxtaposition: cites **Scott McCloud's *Understanding Comics*** and the idea
  of **"closure"** — placing two images (a man fishing / a fish being caught) makes the
  viewer's mind assemble them into one story even when unrelated. (paraphrase, cited)
- Sketching a layout solution with marker/pencil takes ~30 seconds versus doing it
  perfectly in Illustrator, which gets complicated — sketch to explore. (paraphrase)
- On his own skill: claims he only knows "five or six tricks in Photoshop," but reused in
  combination they're enough to craft and make an image pop. (paraphrase; self-reported)

**Chris Do — content-strategy points (site UX):**
- Landing page needs clear calls to action (sign up / sign in — they want account
  sign-ups) and a newsletter opt-in framed as a **value exchange** ("give us your email…
  we want to give you something valuable back," e.g. a fishing guide). (paraphrase)
- Don't over-focus on fishing alone — that excludes women and younger people; show
  trophy catches *and* other activities, corporate/team-building trips. (paraphrase)
- Embedding the client's fishing-school YouTube videos on the site is a good way to make
  sure those videos get watched (one, the miter-box herring-cut tutorial, had huge views).
  (paraphrase)

**Charlene Chen (CONTEXT — designer):**
- Her first pass deliberately stuck close to the wireframe / interpreted it in her own
  way; later feedback was that it looked "too digital / template-y," which pushed her
  toward distressed type, wood textures, added shadows, layering, selective focus, Dutch
  angles, and color correction. (paraphrase)
- She added the shadows/layering herself to make elements "look more real"; added all the
  videos from the lodge's fishing-school channel. (paraphrase)

**Jose Caballer (CONTEXT):** ran the discovery + wireframe/planning stage the design was
built from; source of the "chipping away at marble / sculpture" process metaphor Chris
repeats.

## Notes

Not an L3 candidate. The design-craft principles are useful but are hands-on Photoshop/
art-direction tips tied to one project rather than landmark, portable frameworks. The
process/collaboration ideas ("give designers space and time," "iterate, don't perfect,"
"perfectly imperfect," McCloud's closure) are the strongest persona-relevant material and
can be promoted during a synthesis pass if they recur in other sources.
