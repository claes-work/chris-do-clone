# Graphic Design Career Tips: Integrity vs Profitability pt. 1/3

★ L3-candidate — Chris Do lays out a substantial, distinctive negotiation-and-values
stance (don't-want-the-work leverage, kill the engagement 3x, red-flag "relationship"
language, spec-work refusal). Core persona material worth promoting to `topics/` +
`persona/beliefs.md`/`voice.md`.

## Summary

**Part 1 of 3** in a live studio episode of *The Process* (2016 era of The Futur,
then still branded "The Futur"/"the process"). Host **Chris Do** (branded "I talk about
the business of design") and co-host **Jose Caballer** ("I design sandwiches… the design
of business") sit with studio guests **Michael Stinson** and **Rachel Elnar** of **TypeEd**
(a typography education program bridging school and the professional workplace), plus other
studio-audience designers (Zach, Jessica, and others referenced).

The episode frames "the **designer's dilemma**": must a graphic designer choose between
doing soul-sucking corporate work to eat, or starving as a happy artist — or is there a
middle ground? Part 1 works through: doing work *for love* vs. for margin, the danger
signals in client "relationship" language, **spec work** and **RFPs**, and the psychology
of **negotiation and leverage** established in the first minutes of a client dialogue.
Chris repeatedly returns to a values-vs-profitability tension: he is running a business
that must hit margins, but he refuses engagements that start by devaluing him.

## Key claims

_All paraphrased unless quoted. Dated to the 2016-08-25 source. Speaker-attributed._

**Chris Do (subject — persona material):**
- (2016) There is a recurring inverse pattern: the cooler the brand / better the project,
  the lower the budget; the more they pay, the less lovable the work — designers must find
  the balance. [self-reported observation]
- (2016) He runs a small business like many in America; he *has* to take projects that hit
  a certain margin or he will go out of business. Projects done "for love" are justified by
  wanting to build/keep the **relationship**, not by the budget.
- (2016) The word "relationship" is often **used against** designers — sales reps / executive
  producers (biz-dev) dangle a bad, low-budget "stinker" job as the start of a relationship.
  That is a **red flag**; he wouldn't accept a devaluing first project.
- (2016) Buzzword red flags to train your ears for: "it's good for you / good for us,"
  "exposure," "good for your portfolio," and the word "**just**" ("we just don't have the
  budget / the time"). When you hear them, something's up.
- (2016) On **spec work** and **RFPs**: in motion design you must do spec **pitch decks** —
  3–5 firms pitch against a script, costing thousands to tens of thousands; it's a numbers
  game/gamble (e.g. spend $30k chasing a $400k job). But he treats client-direct **RFPs**
  differently: he *does* respond, because he "plays the game under his own rules."
- (2016) His RFP method: don't answer every question (that puts you on equal footing = bad
  odds); figure out the **three things** they need to know to give you the job; reuse a
  **master template / deck**, shuffle it, give **bulk (not granular) numbers**. He claims a
  very high conversion ratio on proposals. [self-reported]
- (2016) **Don't break down your numbers.** Quote a logo as one chunk price + revision count,
  not a line-item breakdown, to defeat competitive line-item bidding — "we're in or we're
  not." If they say no, you learn you're out of the ballpark and stop wasting time.
- (2016) **Terms of engagement, leverage and power are set in the first few minutes** of a
  dialogue — just like real-life relationships. Neediness/desperation can be "smelled";
  if your rate's tone rises into a question mark, the client knows it isn't your real rate
  and will cut it down.
- (2016) Core tactic (credited to **Blair Enns**): **don't want the work** — try to "kill
  the engagement at least three times" during the initial conversation. Not being desperate
  removes the leverage the client has over you (portfolio, exposure).
- (2016) Creative types are "other-validated" people-pleasers who hand over their self-worth
  on a silver platter; clients play on that. To have long-term healthy client relationships
  you need a bit of a "**mastery mindset**" — genuinely not wanting it. "The more I say no,
  the more they say yes."
- (2016) Anecdote: a prospect offered $5k for a ~$50k job; Chris said no; the prospect
  "found another five" to make it $10k — someone doubling a budget that fast is itself a
  **red flag**. He referred them out instead. [self-reported]
- (2016) Caveat to saying no: you must not be "eating Wonderbread for dinner" — if you have
  to eat first, you have no leverage. Financial stability underwrites the ability to walk.
- (2016) On discounting: never just drop 50→10; if you reduce price you must **pull scope**
  (fewer versions, less time). Start high, chisel back so they feel they got a deal. Set a
  caveat up front: if scope "spirals out of control" you'll have the price conversation again
  — and raise it *before* "DEFCON 5."
- (2016) You can be "too tough a negotiator — winning the negotiation but losing the job";
  it's a fine line and an art that needs practice. Practice on the next job you *don't* want:
  act as if you don't need it, genuinely try to help them find someone else, and see what
  happens.
- (2016) He's "been doing this 21 years" — going in, doing everything for nothing hoping
  they'll love you back, "never works out." [self-reported — implies career start ~1995]
- (2016) Business context: his firm is "**pivoting pretty hard**" from a **motion design**
  firm to a **brand strategy** firm; leaving the agency/pitch-deck world behind ("a mutual
  breakup… conscious uncoupling… it's healthy"). [self-reported]

**Jose Caballer (co-host — CONTEXT, not persona):**
- Co-hosts and pushes back; jokes about the show ("I design sandwiches," "show me the
  Benjamins"). Probes TypeEd's headcount/overhead economics.

**Michael Stinson & Rachel Elnar / TypeEd (guests — CONTEXT, not persona):**
- (2016) TypeEd: ~3-year-old typography education program bridging education and the
  professional workplace; in-person seminars (~3h) and workshops (8h) in LA plus online;
  an invite-only "varsity" peer-mentoring/alumni group. (type-ed.com)
- (2016) They'll go above and beyond, unbudgeted, for clients/projects they love (e.g. a
  10-year CWS Capital annual report where they hand-redrew and rewrote currency — new
  typefaces, serial numbers = staff birthdays/wedding dates). Some concepts render a higher
  budget; a long-term loyal client gives latitude (some years earn more, some less).
- (2016) Rachel walks away from spec-work / RFP competitions; TypeEd targets **10–20% of
  work** for pro-bono/investment projects (1–2 nonprofit clients at reduced-but-still-
  profitable rates). Michael's screen: it's about the project (and people with **integrity**).
- (2016) Rachel's negotiation nuance (Chris endorses): it's okay to take a lower price if you
  want the project, but always **trade off scope**; always start higher.
