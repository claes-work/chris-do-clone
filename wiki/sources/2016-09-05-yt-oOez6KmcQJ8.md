# Marketing and Social Media Tips for Designers pt. 3/3

**Series note:** Part 3/3 of a marketing/social-media series aired under *The Process*
(2016 era). This installment centers on "the designer's dilemma" and how designers
should market themselves through content.

**Format:** Panel/live taping. Chris Do co-hosts with Jose (Caballer). Two additional
guests, Rachel and Michael, participate, and audience members (including one named
Paul, and one named Michael) pose questions. Only **Chris Do** trains the persona;
Jose, Rachel, Michael, and audience members are **context** speakers.

## Summary

The panel debates "the designer's dilemma" of 2016: must a designer choose between
doing soul-sucking corporate work to eat, or starving as a happy artist? Chris argues
the game has fundamentally changed — "your work gets you work" is dead; now "you and
your work get you work," meaning designers must promote themselves, their personality,
and their point of view. He frames two survival paths for creatives: **entrepreneurship**
and **authorship**. Pure service providers face erosion as design commoditizes (the
"good enough revolution," offshore work for a few dollars). Chris positions himself as
"the entrepreneurial canary in the coal mine" warning that classical design's value is
trending downward.

On self-marketing tactics, Chris advocates making something great, distributing it
everywhere, then "leaking out little bits of your process" in whatever medium feels
natural (video for him). He attacks perfectionism ("F Perfection") as the reason
designers don't post, and shares his personal content system: he reads books by
sketching bite-sized takeaways in Keynote and publishing them — "you're reading my
diary with me as I write it."

The panel also debates specialization vs. being multidisciplinary. Chris's synthesis:
**externally specialize, internally diversify** — the outside world needs you to focus,
but your own learning should be broad. He closes on "balance" as the answer to the
whole topic: deep-dive to earn money, take side jobs to stay creatively happy.

## Key claims

**Chris Do (persona):**
- (2016) Starting a business right out of school was one of his regrets — he "didn't
  know anything" and lost more money through inexperience "in a very short period"
  than the audience could imagine. [self-reported]
- (2016) "Poor" is passing an opportunity repeatedly; people build up an infinite list
  of excuses (wrong family, wrong school) for why they aren't where they should be.
  Cites Jim Rohn's car-dealership story: the one thing the complainer forgot was to
  take responsibility for where he is.
- (2016) The market has changed: work alone no longer gets you work — you must also
  promote yourself, your personality, and "what you're made of." It's more work than
  it used to be.
- (2016) His literal advice when asked how to balance getting work vs. doing work:
  "find someone that promotes the work for you" and partner up.
- (2016) Maker culture pressures everyone to make and post everything; you can't do
  everything. Young designers should specialize on one task early; breadth of guidance
  comes later as a creative director with staff.
- (2016) Just posting "I made it" is like putting work on your mom's refrigerator —
  add insight, say what you learned, help/educate the viewer instead.
- (2016) An entrepreneur asks what the most valuable thing is that only they can do —
  that's their differentiator. The moment you stop hustling/selling/promoting/
  brand-building, you stop being an entrepreneur, because the making can be done by
  someone else.
- (2016) Classical design's *value* is going away; the market is "imploding." Cites a
  design-school figure claiming work could be done in the Philippines for five bucks
  and be amazing. [attribution: the five-dollar anecdote is Chris relaying a third
  party's claim]
- (2016) The future for creatives is two things: **entrepreneurialism** and
  **authorship**. Authors of content are back in favor; pure service providers are in
  a dangerous place — the best last longer "in the sun," but market forces win.
- (2016) The "good enough revolution": when others do it at 1/10th the cost and it's
  not 10x worse, good-enough wins. Examples: military drones replacing billion-dollar
  jets, GoPro cameras. Cites a *Wired* article ("read the good enough revolution").
- (2016) The entrepreneur's role: find the person with a problem, find the person with
  a solution, and thrive in the gap between them (both require art and skill).
- (2016) We live in an amazing, democratized time — little barrier between creator and
  client, direct communication with fans, Kickstarter access.
- (2016) For artists: design something wonderful, put it on every site to be
  discovered, then leak little bits of your process in your most comfortable medium so
  it doesn't feel like work.
- (2016) "F Perfection" — perfectionism (lighting isn't right, not perfect) stops
  people sharing. Being genuine and imperfect onboards more people and builds clients
  and fans. Someone documenting a worse process is "already blowing up."
- (2016) Design is a "blue collar deal," not white collar — it's never about you,
  always about the customer (analogy: bricklayers/masonry).
- (2016) Entrepreneurship isn't mandatory, but it's an "unfair competitive advantage."
- (2016) On what sets a standout apart (re: Gemma O'Brien): it's her *craft* and deep
  expertise, not merely sharing her process — plenty of bad lettering artists share
  process. All things equal, the one who documents and builds an audience beats the
  equally-good artist "working in a cave."
- (2016) The best designers don't always win; name recognition and audience matter.
- (2016) Many designers are introverts; promoting yourself and being center-stage
  isn't the natural instinct, but it's a skill developed over time. Good things started
  happening for him once he stopped fighting it and embraced it. He encourages his
  whole company to develop their social presence — "it's not for me, it's for you."
- (2016) His content process: he's an "information junky," reads books by sketching
  takeaways in Keynote and publishing them as bite-sized pieces — a public notebook
  ("you're reading my diary with me as I write it"). Translating an idea into your own
  words/drawing inserts yourself into it; the most painless way to build an audience.
- (2016) Endorses the **T-shaped skill** model: deep on one thing, shallow on
  everything else.
- (2016) You'll make more money if you specialize; establish yourself as specializing
  first. If you drill deep, be prepared to pivot when the market changes (notes his own
  20-year motion-design run now requiring a pivot).
- (2016) Synthesis: **externally specialize, internally diversify.** The world needs you
  to focus; how you diversify on your own time is up to you.
- (2016) On learning: be a deep diver, learn as much as you can then move on; surface-
  level knowledge doesn't help. Building "neuron networks" of ideas lets you relate to
  anyone and find unexpected connections in a room.
- (2016) The whole topic comes back to **balance**: deep-dive for money, take side jobs
  to stay creatively happy.

**Jose (co-host, context):**
- Running a business is itself a school; sometimes to learn to swim you jump in and
  drown a bit.
- The digital age opened up the audience; you now must cast a wider (or more specific)
  net — from calling ~50–60 companies a year to needing far more reach.
- Don't promote yourself if you're not doing good work; criticizes the "dribble age" of
  people rushing to post mediocre work.
- Attributes design's changes to the web/digital age.

**Rachel (guest, context):**
- Craft itself can create authorship — getting genuinely good at your design builds a
  recognizable style/process others idolize or copy; sits between entrepreneurship and
  authorship.
- Multidisciplinary work is a strength: designers get to take on varied clients and
  bring a fresh point of view (unlike a heart surgeon); knowing a little about
  everything feeds the design result.

**Michael (guest, context):**
- We're in a "maker culture" pressured to make and put everything out there.
- Standing out is partly about name recognition.

**Paul / audience (context):**
- Posed the framing question about balancing getting work vs. doing the work.
