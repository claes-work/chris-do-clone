## Summary

An early Futur / "The Process" era (2016) instructional video on the **pre-design
discovery process for competitive pitches**, aimed at the motion design and
post-production community. Presented by **Matthew Encina**. The core argument:
diagnose before you design. At the creative kickoff meeting with a new potential
client, your job is to *gather and distill information* so you can walk away and
synthesize an effective solution — not to rush into making things.

Matthew lays out **three things to do before any pitch**: (1) **define the goal**
in one sentence, (2) **diagnose the problem** (the who / what / how), and (3)
**narrow your design exploration** (set creative parameters and, critically, ask
the client how they will make their decision). Doing these three things keeps the
pitch "hyperfocused" and stops designers from wasting time guessing what the client
wants. The video closes with a promo for Matthew's "pitch kit" (a step-by-step pitch
guide with worksheets, deck template, and archive of real pitches) and a beta-tester
callout.

## Key claims

Speaker key: **Matthew** = Matthew Encina (presenter; The Futur creative director —
**context, NOT the persona subject**). Chris Do is absent from this video.

**The 3 things (before any pitch):**

- [2016-09-23] (Matthew) At the start of every competitive pitch you hold a creative
  kickoff meeting with the potential client; your objective is to gather information
  and distill it so you can walk away and synthesize an effective design solution.
  (paraphrase)

- [2016-09-23] (Matthew) **Thing 1 — Define the goal in one sentence.** State what
  the project is and what it needs to do. Clients may ramble about hopes and dreams;
  be present, listen, then distill it to a single sentence. Using more than one
  sentence waters down the interpretation and the proposed solutions. Example goal
  sentences: "This video will announce our new product X at CES"; "This branded
  content video will educate consumers about Wi-Fi technology"; "This teaser should
  build hype around the upcoming release of our new sneakers." (paraphrase)

- [2016-09-23] (Matthew) **Thing 2 — Diagnose the problem.** Identify what challenge
  you must solve and what is getting in the way of the client reaching their goal,
  via the **who / what / how**:
  - *Who* — the target audience/user: who will see this, why it matters to them,
    why they care. Expect more than one user; identify the top three and prioritize
    the single most important. (paraphrase)
  - *What* — what the project must communicate: the key takeaway message and how the
    audience should feel afterward. If the client has too many things to say in a
    short time, prioritize the message and define the hierarchy *with* them — doing
    this shows your value as a partner because you use core design skills to provide
    clarity and organize their thoughts. (paraphrase)
  - *How* — where and in what context it will be shown, and what stage of the sales
    cycle the viewer is in. Context changes the viewer's state of mind and attention
    span: a YouTube pre-roll ad must hook in ~5 seconds or it gets skipped, whereas
    a convention hall / theater audience allows more time. Early in the sales cycle
    the viewer needs to be made *aware*; late in the cycle they are *ready to buy*.
    (paraphrase)

- [2016-09-23] (Matthew) **Thing 3 — Narrow your design exploration.** Establish the
  criteria and "define your sandbox." Narrowing the possibilities upfront saves time,
  focuses effort on what matters, and stops you guessing what the client wants.
  (paraphrase)
  - *Creative parameters* — ask what it can look like and sound like. Watch out for
    **"coded language"**: subjective words like *epic*, *elegant*, *energetic* that
    leave wide room for interpretation. Get specific, cite examples, use simple
    language. (Matthew references his own Medium article on translating coded
    language, linked in the description.) (paraphrase)
  - *The most important question* — ask the client **how they will make their
    decision on who to work with.** This is "almost like cheating" / the answer to
    the test: the client will tell you the single most important thing they need to
    see or hear. Is it **money** (shopping for lowest price), **results** (best
    quality), or **rapport** (the people they get along with best)? Whatever it is,
    make it a priority to address during the pitch. Matthew admits he didn't bother
    asking this for years and wasted time guessing. (paraphrase)

**Framing / rationale:**

- [2016-09-23] (Matthew) Do these three things and the pitch process becomes
  hyperfocused so you don't chase bad ideas; the established goal becomes your "north
  star and blueprint" — everything you and your team create should be driven by the
  goal and filtered through the criteria, which delivers real value and (hopefully)
  increases your win rate. (paraphrase)

- [2016-09-23] (Matthew) Design education emphasizes execution and thinking but
  offers no classes on how to pitch; Matthew learned pitching only in the field from
  other directors across studios over ~10 years — motivating his "pitch kit"
  step-by-step guide (creative brief, structuring pitch decks, articulating and
  presenting ideas; includes worksheets, a deck template, and an archive of real
  pitches). (paraphrase; promotional)
