# 3 Keys To Success — How To Run A Design Business pt.1

★ L3-candidate — adds a distinct "success principles" running-a-business framework
(moderation / patience / humility, each inverted) plus a structured word-of-mouth
referral-ask technique not yet captured in `wiki/topics/business`.

## Summary

Live audience Q&A recorded for **The Process** (2016 era, Chris Do's earliest show
format, shot from his studio/Blind context). **Part 1 of a series** ("How To Run A
Design Business pt.1"). Chris Do fields questions on running a design business,
opening with two priming stories and his personal "success principles," then pivoting
to the audience's main question: how to generate leads.

Chris opens with the **five-monkey ladder experiment** (a parable about inherited,
unexamined "that's just how it's done" behavior) and a Stephen Covey line ("if we keep
doing what we're doing, we're gonna keep getting what we're getting"), leads a short
breathing/mind-clearing exercise, then presents **3 success principles** — each
deliberately inverted from conventional virtue.

The back half is a practical lead-generation clinic: word-of-mouth as a *passive*
channel you can make active, a scripted 3-step referral ask taught to him by his
business coach, a skeptical take on formal "networking," reframing networking as
genuine relationship-building / connecting people, and Cialdini-style **reciprocity**.
Closes on a reframe from Ben Burns: stop asking "how do I find better clients?" and ask
"how do better clients find you?"

Co-host **Jose Caballer** appears in the cold-open banter; other voices are live
audience members (Frank prepared the questions; Eduardo, Neal, and others speak).
Only Chris Do is treated as persona voice below.

## Key claims

**Chris Do (persona) — priming / mindset:**
- [2016-10-11] The **five-monkey ladder experiment** shows that learned behaviors
  prevent us from doing what we want "just because somebody said that's the way it's
  supposed to be." (paraphrase; parable Chris retells, not claimed as his own research)
- [2016-10-11] Invokes Stephen Covey: "if we keep doing what we're doing we're gonna
  keep getting what we're getting" — new results require openness to new information.
  (paraphrase, attributed by Chris to Covey)
- [2016-10-11] If you're not primed to take in new information with an open mind,
  "you're gonna be fighting me the whole time and there's really then no point."
  (paraphrase)

**Chris Do (persona) — the 3 success principles (each inverted):**
- [2016-10-11] **1. Moderation → intensity.** Conventional wisdom says everything in
  moderation / work-life balance; Chris's take: "if you do everything in moderation you
  live a life of mediocrity." He is intense, goes all in, does something well or not at
  all. (paraphrase; direct quote below)
- [2016-10-11] **2. Patience → urgency.** Patience is called a virtue; Chris is
  deliberately impatient. Your **time is your most valuable asset** — worth more than
  the money paid. He lives as if tomorrow could be his last day: act now, don't defer
  learning/doing to "tomorrow." (paraphrase)
- [2016-10-11] **3. Humility → self-love / self-acknowledgment.** We're taught to be
  humble and not take credit; Chris argues creatives especially must learn to love
  themselves, acknowledge their gifts almost daily, and use that confidence as fuel.
  Suggests writing a "love letter to yourself" as a daily ritual to reprogram the brain
  when confidence is low. (paraphrase; direct quote below)

**Chris Do (persona) — lead generation:**
- [2016-10-11] Word-of-mouth is the **most effective** lead source (warm leads that
  close) but as normally practiced it's **passive** — you have no control over whether a
  client refers you. (paraphrase)
- [2016-10-11] **Ask for referrals before the job ends**, while feelings are good — once
  you're out of sight you're out of mind; clients aren't sitting around thinking about
  how to send you work. (paraphrase, attributed to his business coach)
- [2016-10-11] Some clients won't refer you because they see you as a competitive
  advantage / "secret weapon" they want to keep in their back pocket. (paraphrase)
- [2016-10-11] **Scaffold the ask — never open with the hard ask.** Build up in ~3 steps:
  (a) confirm they're happy with the work, (b) establish your *need* ("I'm expanding my
  practice, added a designer"), (c) ask specifically "is there somebody you know that I
  could reach out to?" — phrasing that puts the work on your plate, not theirs, and then
  follow up. (paraphrase)
- [2016-10-11] Invokes Jack Canfield: "you are the average of the five people you spend
  the most time with" — successful entrepreneurs cluster (country clubs, EO/YEO), so
  their referrals reach other good clients. (paraphrase, attributed to Canfield)
- [2016-10-11] **Skeptical of formal "networking"** — throwing people in a room to
  "magically do business" feels contrived; the term implies a hidden agenda that
  poisons real interaction. Chambers of Commerce are long-haul relationship plays, not
  quick wins. (paraphrase)
- [2016-10-11] What works instead: find a **connector / matchmaker**, or *be* one — have
  genuine conversations, look for how you can help (even connecting someone to your
  chiropractor at no profit), and build real relationships. (paraphrase)
- [2016-10-11] Because of his skill set (identity/logo, messaging/copy, websites, video)
  he can help almost anyone in business — "almost everybody in the 21st century needs a
  website and a video and an identity." (paraphrase)
- [2016-10-11] His mental sales sequence: first establish *need* (usually present), then
  ask **"can they afford me and do I like them / want to work with them?"** — if he
  doesn't like them, he won't ask for the sale. (paraphrase)
- [2016-10-11] **Reciprocity** (attributes to Robert Cialdini's *Influence*, "seven
  principles"): society runs on evening the score; genuinely helping someone makes them
  feel indebted and more likely to help back. Cites the Hare Krishna flower/book
  experiment. His own financial advisor, whom he pays, now refers clients back to him.
  (paraphrase, attributed to Cialdini)
- [2016-10-11] Closing reframe from **Ben Burns**: the wrong question is "how do I find
  better clients?"; the better question is "**how do better clients find you?**"
  (paraphrase, attributed to Ben Burns)

**Audience / co-host (context — NOT persona):**
- Eduardo (audience): gets business from **trade shows/events**; is outgoing, can walk
  in and close. (context)
- Neal (audience): considering business networking groups, wary of wasting time.
