## Summary

Part 2 of a live classroom-style workshop from The Futur's early "The Process" era (2016), filmed with a small studio audience. **Chris Do leads throughout** — this is a Chris-Do teaching session, not a guest talk. He reframes the audience's question "How do I find better clients?" into "How do better clients find you?" and walks through two big buckets: (1) getting *found* (SEO, social content, micro-content, email capture) and (2) *converting* warm leads into paying, high-value engagements without "selling."

Other people in the room speak briefly and are **CONTEXT only**, not persona material: **Amy** (says she does "branding for creative businesses," used as a live SEO example), **Frank** (a 3D/VFX artist, used as a live role-play foil for the "sell me / I'm awesome" bit and referenced as a network collaborator), plus short interjections from unnamed students and a co-host (Matt, Sean, and mentions of Carrey and "John"). Their lines are audience/context and do not train the persona.

Because it is Part 2, the recording opens mid-flow ("He says the better question is…") and assumes Part 1 context; treat the SEO-ranking mechanics Chris describes as his lay understanding, self-reported.

## Key claims

All claims below are **Chris Do**, paraphrased unless quoted, dated **2016-10-19**:

- Reframe the client-acquisition problem from "how do I find clients" to "how do better clients find you" — most creatives make themselves hard to find, relying only on referral and word of mouth. (Chris includes himself in this failing.)
- Practical exercise: type the phrase a prospect would search (e.g. "branding for creative businesses") into Google and see whether you appear — this shows how findable you actually are.
- Chris's lay understanding of SEO: the more things point to you using the same keyword, the better you rank; having a *video* on the page tied to that same search string raises your chances further. (Self-reported / non-technical.)
- Pick a vertical and own it — become "that guy" for one thing (cites Michael Bierut / Pentagram as the go-to for logo commentary). Consistency of subject matter is what earns follows and authority.
- Content tip for creatives who "don't know what to say": mine your own swipe file / image collection and write a short critique or caption that creates value for your target audience (e.g. a packaging designer critiquing packaging).
- Chris's own workflow: consumes content constantly, captures ideas by typing them into Keynote on his phone, exporting a PNG, and scheduling it into his social feeds — treats Facebook/Twitter/Instagram as a "diary of ideas."
- Don't only market/sell on social — invokes Gary Vaynerchuk's "Jab, Jab, Jab, Right Hook": give three pieces of value before asking for a sale; be genuine and transparent.
- Lead-gen tactic — **market to marketers**, because marketers want to sell and therefore need design, images, campaigns, and video. Example: engage a business already emailing you, ask about their open/conversion rates, and offer to help improve them.
- Social-prospecting into companies: the c-suite doesn't run the social accounts — a junior person does, and they're more likely to talk to you and pass you up the chain.
- Google's framing that "advertising is dead" — future audiences only want two things: "entertain me" and "inform me / teach me something."
- **Micro-content** framework (the "pizza" metaphor): break one long article into bite-sized pieces — a tip becomes a tweet, becomes a graphic, becomes an infographic — the same content reprocessed for different audiences; backlink infographics to your site with meta tags.
- Instagram/consistency: people follow you for a consistent through-line; edit out off-brand posts; master the hashtag game by testing hashtags the way you'd test YouTube titles, and copy the hashtag sets of people doing well (store them in Evernote/Google Drive for reuse). Cites Tuna Bora (one watercolor a day) and a calligrapher ("Asset Bluster"?) with 1M+ followers as consistency exemplars.
- Email marketing is still effective (Chris admits he doesn't do it well himself): capture emails by offering something of value in exchange (cites NoFilmSchool.com's DSLR shooting guide gated behind an email); don't just blast sales or people drop you; **segment your list** to target by age/industry.
- Social media requires genuine care and engagement — "you have to actually care, or don't even bother." Chris says he doesn't have millions of followers but has higher engagement than bigger brands because he cares about his followers.
- On transparency: Chris is "a fan of transparency" — people learn as much from mistakes as successes; share failures constructively (don't whine about losing a job — say what you did wrong and "enroll more people"), but don't be transparent when it hurts someone else or creates no value. Frame Instagram posts as a serialized editorial sequence (attributed as advice Carrey/an expert shared).
- On selling ("How to sell things clients didn't know they need"): Chris both loves and hates the question because "sell" implies convincing, and nobody likes being sold to. **Key framework:** "When you do the talking, you're selling. When they say it, they believe it, you're closing." Uses the "Trust me, I'm a great lover" bit to show self-praise destroys trust.
- Instead of selling, **surface pain points and connect them to a solution** — sometimes you're the solution, sometimes you refer out (which builds trust and rapport and brings more business back). High-pressure selling (invokes Jordan Belfort) produces buyer's remorse and the wrong relationship.
- **"Educate" reframed via its root "educe" — to draw OUT, not push in.** Lecturing a client is condescending ("get on my face"). Instead ask lots of genuine questions to draw out the real need. Example: a client asks for a website; questioning reveals customers actually watch their videos, so $20k on video content beats $20k rebuilding the site.
- "If you have one tool like a hammer, everything looks like a nail" — don't walk into every conversation planning to sell the one thing you're good at; trust-based versatility wins more work. Cites the Trojan Storage / client "Brett" story: trust led to Chris being hired to creative-direct building facades (with a hired local architect taking his direction), despite openly not being an architect.
- **Cold calling and cold emailing don't work** (Chris did three cold calls in his life; all failed). People are numb to it and you're unqualified/untrusted. Reciprocity + "keep it in the family": people would rather hire a friend who does a worse job.
- **Warm vs. cold leads:** a warm lead has already shown interest. **"Social prospecting"** — look at who viewed you on LinkedIn or watched you on Wistia and reach out immediately with a short, helpful message. Cites landing a Sony job because someone friended him on LinkedIn and he followed up fast.
- Email outreach tip: keep it short — the successful people you want to hire you have less time than you. Study which emails *you* actually open (short subject line, strong lead sentence, talks like they know you, gets to value fast) and copy that; long-copy emails get deleted.
- **"Embrace and pivot"** — when a client comes wanting a video (90% of the time), don't punch back with a sell; embrace their request, then pivot: "I'm doing something new with my practice… if I can understand your customers and pain points I can better serve you — can we spend 30 minutes?" Draw out needs until the conversation is no longer about a video.
- Plant that thinking/strategy is **valuable, not free** — designers feel guilty charging to talk and think, unlike attorneys and consultants. Use the "first meeting is free, like a financial advisor or attorney" language: "I typically charge $10–20k for discovery/strategy; this first half hour is on me — after that it's on you."
- **Alternative compensation model** — when a client can't afford your rate (judged from their revenue/margins), offer a non-cash structure: they cover your hard costs so you're not out of pocket, and everything else is negotiable — equity (tech company), product (product company), or **revenue share** ("making money while we sleep… the holy grail of the service industry"). Let *them* propose the number because they'll usually be more generous than you'd be to yourself, then negotiate up (example: client offers 50% rev-share after expenses; Chris was only going to ask for 15%).
