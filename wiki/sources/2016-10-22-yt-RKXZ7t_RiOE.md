## Summary

A live Futur/Skool workshop (late-2016, filmed at Blind's studio) in which **Chris Do** answers a student's question — "What do you charge for a logo, and can you break it down?" — and turns it into a full teaching on pricing creative work. He first surveys the room ($500, $1,500, $20,000) to show that price for the *same* logo varies wildly, then argues that price is a function of **value to the client and risk to the client**, not time. He gives concrete rates for his own studio (Blind), dismantles hourly billing through live role-play, and demonstrates his discovery-question sales method (the "meta moment": never assert value, ask questions until the client says it themselves). He closes with an extended worked example: interrogating a hypothetical client's revenue streams, profit margins, and growth potential to size the true dollar value of a project, then pricing off that number and subcontracting the production cheaply while keeping the spread.

Primary speaker throughout is **Chris Do** (host/teacher). Other voices are workshop participants (students named Jung, Rosille, Gabrielle/Gabriel, Frank Shih, Sean, "Earth," Amy Gooseman) and at least one Futur instructor assist ("Thanks, Matt" — likely Matthew Encina, line 122). Only Chris-Do-attributed statements are treated as persona data below.

## Key claims
_All dated 2016-10-22. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Attributed per speaker._

- **[Chris Do]** The same logo has wildly different worth depending on who owns it — a mark on a blank T-shirt is worth only the cost of materials, but the same mark on a branded product lets the company charge a large premium. So the logo's value is not intrinsic; it comes from the brand and the client. (lines 68–77)
- **[Chris Do]** Price the client, not the job. Everyone in the room is talented enough to draw the logo; the price should change based on *who* receives it (mom-and-pop vs. giant corporation). You charge for value, not time. (lines 85–101)
- **[Chris Do]** Big-brand agencies (he cites people who worked at Landor, which did FedEx, Cisco(?), H&R Block) can get ~$1,000,000 for a logo because what a large client is really buying is **risk reduction** — the logo goes on planes, brochures, hundreds of millions in physical assets, "forever," and a bad logo carries huge cost and backlash (he cites Uber's rebrand backlash). (lines 88–99)
- **[Chris Do]** A logo doesn't reliably create positive ROI (the mark "could've been anything"), but it *can* create large negative impact if people hate it — which is why pricing is variable and why risk, not upside, is the main lever for a pure identity mark. (lines 102–105)
- **[Chris Do]** His studio (Blind) prices roughly: **$15,000–$30,000 for the logo**; **$20,000–$50,000 for strategy done before the logo**; **~$10,000 to write messaging**; **~$10,000 for applications** (pick ~3, e.g. business card, letterhead, email blast; brand/usage guide included as a template). Overhead — big building, staff, electricity, limited number of jobs per month — forces his rates to start much higher than a freelancer's. (lines 106–128)
- **[Chris Do]** He does not bill hourly. A proposal line item reads simply "Logo — three rounds — $18k," with no hours shown. (lines 130–134)
- **[Chris Do]** Hourly billing is self-contradictory and punishes skill: if a client wants to pay less for fewer hours, they must logically pay more for more hours — meaning slower work is rewarded and efficiency is punished. Clients can't have it both ways (demand an hours breakdown but also cap the hours). The logic must be symmetric. (lines 194–222)
- **[Chris Do]** Most business people value time above money ("time is the most important thing I have"), so a great logo delivered in 5 minutes is worth *more*, not less — it lets the client go to market sooner. (lines 215–221)
- **[Chris Do]** "Symmetry of logic": test a pricing argument by checking whether the same logic holds elsewhere in life (a spotless house by the time you're back from vacation — you value the result, not the hours; you don't care how Nikes are made, only that they're Nikes). Used to move clients off time-thinking onto outcome-thinking. (lines 256–275)
- **[Chris Do]** Confidence is the real gating factor, not credentials or awards. He charged $10,000 for strategy the very first time by just "going for it," then raised to $20k after it was accepted, and kept escalating — "you just have to believe that this is what it's worth," relative to the client's risk. (lines 147–160, 348–360)
- **[Chris Do]** Clients don't choose the best option; they choose the **least risky** option. Your job is to appear as the least risky choice. Ask the client directly what criteria they'll use to decide (case studies, referrals, credit history, office space, headcount, social proof, expertise) — most of which a bigger/established vendor can win on. (lines 324–370)
- **[Chris Do]** The "meta moment" / questioning method: never *state* the value yourself — ask questions until the client says it, because "when you say it, you believe it." You lose the argument every time if you assert your own worth; the client must draw the conclusion (demonstrated when Jung concludes Chris is "better" though Chris claims he never said so). (lines 249–255, 299–307)
- **[Chris Do]** Discovery is done live, not in your head. Ask how the business makes money — channels (e-commerce, retail, reps, international), the % split across channels, and the profit margin on each — to find where branding/marketing effort has the highest leverage. Direct/e-commerce usually has the highest margin (no middleman; cites Apple), so "sell direct" is usually the answer, and web work usually points there. (lines 297–301, 384–486)
- **[Chris Do]** He teaches the **double keystone**: retail $1 → wholesale $0.50 → cost $0.25; margin roughly halves at each middleman step, so direct sales carry the fattest margins. Designers need basic business fundamentals to have this conversation. (lines 450–475)
- **[Chris Do]** Value-sizing math: estimate a realistic (not optimistic) percentage lift the work could drive (e.g. +5% on the biggest channel), translate it to revenue and gross profit (e.g. ~$20M revenue → ~$12M gross profit), and let the client see the project is "worth" that. Then a website that yields $12M gross profit justifies spending a slice of it (client offers ~10% → $1.2M) — so quantify value, don't argue quality qualitatively. (lines 487–528)
- **[Chris Do]** He can reverse-engineer company size from the conversation (a channel worth $2M at some % implies a ~$400M company), but usually doesn't need the math — once you talk business fluently, the client volunteers their real numbers. (lines 507–515)
- **[Chris Do]** The entrepreneur's arbitrage: price to the client's value (e.g. $1.2M), then hire the best producer at their *full* rate (Sean quotes $6,000 for the site) and keep the spread. "I sell what the world can do," not just what you personally do. (lines 530–543, 593–594)
- **[Chris Do]** On guarantees: he won't guarantee results by default — but if a client demands a guarantee, that is a risk transfer, so he triples the price (e.g. $3M) to carry it, and prefers a pro-rated deal (hit half the goal, earn half). Framed via a returns argument: spending $0.75 to make $1.00 is a 25% return, far better than a bank or the stock market, so any positive lift is money in the client's pocket. (lines 545–592)
- **[Participants, context]** Student price points offered for "a logo": Amy(?) $1,500 for a package (logo, colors, typography, brand strategy, supporting elements); "Earth" $500 for just a logo; Gabrielle $20,000 for the logo and ownership. Used by Chris to show price dispersion, not as his own claims. (lines 16–47)
- **[Participant, Matthew Encina — attribution: uncertain]** Prompts the "Letterhead" application item; addressed as "Matt" (line 122). Likely a Futur instructor assist, not persona data.
