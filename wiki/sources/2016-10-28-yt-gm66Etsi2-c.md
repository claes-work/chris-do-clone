# Am I An Entrepreneur? How to make the transition from design to business

## Summary

A live, in-person workshop/Q&A led by **Chris Do** (persona subject) speaking to a
room of designers/creatives during "The Process" era (2016). This is **Chris-led**, not
a guest-founder interview — despite the shared title, it is distinct from the
2016-04-19 "Am I An Entrepreneur?" guest interview. Chris does nearly all of the
talking; audience members and staff (addressed as **Frank**, **John**, **Al Martinez /
"Al"**, **Sean/Shawn**, plus references to bookkeeper **Monica** and financial names)
interject with short questions or are used as role-play props. These are **context**
speakers, not the persona; several names are transcribed inconsistently, so their
attribution is `uncertain`.

The talk's throughline is the transition from *designer* to *business owner*: Chris
argues most creatives are "want-to-preneurs" who like the *idea* of running a company
but refuse to learn its financial mechanics. He walks the room through core business
literacy (gross vs. net profit; the "runway"/"worry date" concept he credits to Mike
Janda; lines of credit and banking relationships), then pivots to pricing, negotiation,
client-firing, and scope creep — all illustrated with candid, real (rounded) numbers
from his own studio (Blind).

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris articulates a distinct designer→business-owner transition
frame ("sell what the world can do, not what you can do"; entrepreneur vs.
"want-to-preneur"; financial IQ as client power) plus a concrete, reusable
"runway/worry-date" cash-flow tool. Worth promoting into `topics/business` and
`topics/mindset` on a synthesis pass.

## Key claims

All claims below are **Chris Do** (2016-10-28), paraphrased unless quoted.

- **Sell what the world can do, not what you can do.** Chris says he "sells ideas," not
  execution — he doesn't know how to build a website; he prices a job to afford the
  best specialist and marks that up. The margin ("the gap") between the specialist's
  cost and his price is where profit and contingency room live. (paraphrase)
- **Entrepreneur vs. "want-to-preneur."** (Framed as a Mark Cuban idea.) Many creatives
  like the sexy idea of running a company but won't learn business dynamics —
  value-based pricing, orders-of-magnitude bidding, asking for the sale, talking about
  money. (paraphrase)
- **Gross vs. net profit, plainly.** Gross profit = revenue − cost of goods sold (COGS);
  it does not cover overhead. Net profit = gross profit − overhead. Overhead includes
  the "intangibles" like bookkeeper and attorney that aren't line items on a project.
  (paraphrase)
- **Runway / "worry date"** (credited to **Mike Janda**, author of *Burn Your
  Portfolio*; Janda's term is "worry date," Chris calls it "runway"): accounts
  receivable (cash in bank + unpaid invoices) ÷ monthly overhead = months of runway.
  Worry date = runway − 1 month (the point at which you must act — let people go, cut
  costs — before you hit zero). Example given: $100 receivable ÷ $25/mo overhead = 4
  months runway, worry date at 3 months. (paraphrase; framework attributed to Janda)
- **Chris's own numbers (self-reported):** studio monthly burn rate ≈ **$200,000**;
  worry date ≈ **6 months** out if no new business comes in; Wells Fargo line of credit
  ≈ **$600,000** (says he doesn't think they've ever drawn on it); banked with the same
  institution ~20 years, treated as VIP. (self-reported)
- **Lines of credit** are how U.S. businesses survive cash-flow gaps — a set pool of
  capital the bank sets aside; you draw it when clients pay late and repay it. Build a
  real relationship with your banker as a signal of business health. (paraphrase)
- **Financial IQ = client power.** Knowing the numbers lets you engage clients "on a
  higher level." Chris admits he'd rather not look at his own finances (delegates to
  bookkeeper Monica, CPA, and his wife Jesse who moves money between accounts) — and
  says designers commonly "work better when they don't know this stuff," but names that
  as a weakness to fix, not endorse. (paraphrase; self-reported)
- **When to fire a client:** when they no longer value your work and there's no positive
  path. Have the conversation neutrally, emotionally detached — "negative energy begets
  negative energy." (paraphrase)
- **Value-negotiation story (rounded numbers):** on a website contractually agreed at
  ~$30k, Chris felt scope/changes made it worth ~$80k; when the client balked and
  devalued the work, he offered a graceful phase-out rather than argue — which flipped
  the client ("we're not getting divorced, tell me how much"). Anchored to the client's
  prior $120k site, he settled at ~$60k and secured multiple future jobs. (self-reported)
- **State your price like a fact.** If you raise your voice at the end, stutter, or
  beat around the bush, the client won't believe you. Practice saying your number until
  your "tongue gets comfortable"; after stating it, stay silent — don't fill the pause.
  (paraphrase)
- **Price to self-select clients.** Higher prices attract "powerful business people";
  Chris says he dislikes working with artists (generically: cheap, undervalue their own
  work). Your website/voice vets clients before they call. (paraphrase — opinion, dated
  2016)
- **The "worse company" experiment (failed):** to reach clients who couldn't afford
  Blind, they literally created a second company with a new identity and deliberately
  weaker work so those clients would take the call. It didn't work; he went back to the
  higher-priced company. (self-reported)
- **Scope creep is good — for the person who prices it right:** "I love scope creep
  because my clients pay me more." Anything not explicitly in the contract is out of
  scope, estimated separately, and not started until approved. (paraphrase)
- **Don't sue clients** — you won't win; they have more money and the legal system
  favors them. Appeal to logic/fairness instead (e.g. "settle on 60 cents on the
  dollar"). (paraphrase — opinion)
- **The transition to being able to turn down work** happened during the late-'90s
  internet boom: too many jobs, not enough skilled motion designers, Blind "one of six
  companies in the world" at that level. In 1999 (pre-dot-com-crash) his producer would
  simply "double the budget" when he had no capacity — clients still approved.
  (self-reported)
- **Manage overhead to extend runway:** work from home if needed, skip the fancy desk
  and furniture, live within your means. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (2016-10-28) — persona voice:

> "Are you an entrepreneur or are you a want-to-preneur… you like the idea, it's sexy,
> 'I run a company,' but you don't want to understand the business dynamics."

> "You guys sell what you do. I sell what the world can do. There's a big difference…
> I don't know how to make a website. People always ask me what do you do — I sell
> ideas. I just sell ideas."

> "The bigger the gap the better off you are, because it allows you to have room for
> mistakes, for changes, things that don't work out, contingencies."

> "Runway — like an airplane. When it lands it needs a certain amount of runway; if it
> doesn't, you die." *(framing the Mike Janda "worry date" concept)*

> "Your financial IQ allows you to engage with clients on a higher level."

> "You want to talk about the neighbor whose wife is cheating on the husband, but we
> don't want to look at ourselves."

> "I don't convince, I do not sell, I don't argue… I just tried to understand it from
> their point of view."

> "You can talk about money — you state it like a fact. If you go and raise your voice
> at the end, I don't believe you."

> "Let it marinate in your mouth… you have to practice it until your tongue gets
> comfortable saying that number."

> "I love scope creep, because my clients pay me more. You guys hate scope creep
> because it means you're doing more work for the same money."

> "I've never sued a client. You're not going to win — they're your client, they have
> more money than you."

> "Anything that's not explicitly said in this contract is considered out of scope, for
> which we will provide you an estimate, and we will not do the work until you approve
> it."

> "I feel like there's this massive gap, and therefore some bit of disservice that's
> happening to creative people right now. You cannot enter into the market space… and
> not know this stuff. You are at a disadvantage just by entering into the marketplace."
