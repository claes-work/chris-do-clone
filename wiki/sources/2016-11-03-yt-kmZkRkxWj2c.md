# How to create a creative culture in a company

/ L3-candidate — adds a distinct, repeatable culture/leadership framework (values-based hire/fire, autonomy-mastery-purpose, low-cost culture tactics) beyond generic business topics.

## Summary

A The Futur podcast / Facebook Live episode from the "The Process" era. Host **Chris Do**
(subject) is joined by **Kier McLaren** (his business coach of 10+ years; context) and
guest **Ben Burns** (context), plus audience questions from staff (Aaron/Aron, context).
Prompted by a Twitter question, they discuss how to build and sustain a creative culture —
both as an owner and as an employee.

Chris frames the episode around his own story of running his studio **Blind**: he initially
hired purely for talent, ended up with an unmanaged, toxic culture, and became unhappy to
show up to work. Kier assigned him to define his core values; unable to articulate them,
Chris discovered **Tony Hsieh's book _Delivering Happiness_** (Zappos) and adapted Zappos'
published core values into Blind's own. The bulk of the episode covers hiring/firing on
values, managing millennials, incentives beyond money, the downsides of a bad culture, and
concrete low-cost tactics. Chris and Kier also plug their joint consulting venture
("Second Shift") that helps creative companies evaluate and improve culture.

## Key claims

- **[Chris, 2016]** [self-reported] At Blind, Chris hired the best people he could find
  regardless of whether they shared his values; the culture became unmanaged and he stopped
  being happy to come to work. (paraphrase)
- **[Kier, 2016]** The basis of all change is company culture; most companies have one but
  never consciously consider it. He assigned Chris to write his core values, hire around
  them, and fire against them. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2016]** [self-reported] Unable to write his own core values, Chris copied and
  lightly adapted Zappos' published core values (from Tony Hsieh's _Delivering Happiness_),
  changing the few that were too service-oriented to fit Blind. (paraphrase)
- **[Kier, 2016]** Bosses usually have the worst view of their own culture and know the
  least about daily operations; a healthy test is whether staff ever close the door to
  raise real concerns — if no one does, the boss isn't as open as they think. (paraphrase)
- **[Kier, 2016]** People hire people they're comfortable with (his restaurant-waitress
  anecdote); some creatives feel entitled to be difficult, and those people eventually
  moved on from Blind and were replaced by equally creative people who shared Chris's
  culture. (paraphrase)
- **[Kier, 2016]** Millennials don't watch traditional TV or respond to traditional
  advertising, consume less than prior generations, and want direction plus collaboration —
  they like a leader to set direction but want to be involved. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris + Kier, 2016]** [self-reported] Incentive experiment at Blind: cash incentives
  worked for senior creatives but younger animators/designers rebelled against more money —
  they wanted pizza parties and monthly bowling instead. Lesson: people put a premium on
  time and energy, not just money. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2016]** Downsides of no core values / bad culture: high turnover (expensive to
  re-hire and train), the "side hustle" (staff coast at work and save real passion for
  after hours), dehumanization, self-interest, throwing colleagues under the bus, and
  fractured, toxic teams. (paraphrase)
- **[Ben, 2016]** [self-reported, context] Ben started freelancing on the side not out of
  passion but for empowerment/autonomy — he felt poorly managed and without a say in his
  full-time job. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2016]** Cites **Daniel Pink** (autonomy, mastery, purpose) as the key
  components for cultivating culture, and **Simon Sinek** on why time/energy is valued more
  than money. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2016]** [self-reported] Hiring on values went wrong when Blind grew past
  Chris's network of friends and he valued talent over values. Blind now uses tests to
  filter for values/beliefs; talented people who don't fit "don't get through the net."
  It took ~1.5 years to cycle out everyone who didn't fit (they quit or were asked to
  leave); afterward it felt like a brand-new company that could move fast. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2016]** [self-reported] "It's okay to fail / make mistakes" is set from the
  top; Blind won't fire someone for a mistake — only for repeating the same mistake without
  learning. Fear of mistakes makes people reuse the same safe tricks in Photoshop/After
  Effects. (paraphrase)
- **[Kier, 2016]** Don't copy Chris — his fast, high-risk, failure-tolerant style fits him;
  other successful companies (e.g. Space Junk in Columbus, OH) deliberately move slower and
  gather more information. Build the company around your own values. (paraphrase)
- **[Kier, 2016]** On the "cat food commercial" problem: quoting **Kyle Cooper**, if you
  call yourself a creative you should be able to make a spectacular cat-food commercial;
  owners can also rotate boring jobs across staff. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2016]** There's no such thing as an uncreative job, only uncreative people;
  cites _The Art & Fear_ study (quantity group vs. "make your masterpiece" group) — the
  masterpiece group finished little, the quantity group produced more good work; constraints
  and deadlines help creativity. (paraphrase)
- **[Kier + Chris, 2016]** Advice to an employee in a bad culture: Kier says approach the
  boss carefully, bring solutions not attacks, and frame issues around company performance;
  Chris says bluntly "you just need to quit" and find a company whose values fit — but Kier
  tempers it: most workers need their job, so plan an exit strategy rather than walking out.
  (paraphrase)
- **[Kier, 2016]** A "flat" org with no clear reporting lines is a warning sign — people
  feel comfortable knowing who they report to; you need a chain of command without running a
  military operation. (paraphrase)
- **[Chris, 2016]** [self-reported] Concrete low-cost culture tactics used at Blind: let go
  of control and let people personalize/decorate their space; rearrange furniture / route
  people through one main entrance to force interaction (from _Delivering Happiness_);
  empower staff to make decisions ("act as if"); a mandatory 5:00 break with loud music
  (from Greg Gunn / Casey Hunt) to get people off machines; goofy dress-up / pajama days to
  break comfort zones; group hikes and physical exercise so staff see the boss sweat and
  everyone is on equal footing. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (persona voice):

> "I let creativity be my guiding force I wanted to hire the best people I could find and
> people that didn't necessarily share my same values or beliefs but they were really good
> and something started to happen the the company culture was unmanaged and I got to a point
> in which I wasn't happy to show up to work anymore."

> "These are things that you have to live and breathe by so you hire based on your values
> and you have to consequently fire based on those values too."

> "With people who weren't aligned with my own values it was I was feeling like dragging my
> foot through like really thick mud... but once the company was purged of everybody didn't
> share my beliefs and values we were moving really fast."

> "We want to say that it's okay to fail it's okay to make mistakes we will never fire
> somebody because they made a mistake now if you make the mistake repeatedly now that's an
> issue because you never learn from it."

> "I don't think there's anything such thing as a uncreative job there's just uncreative
> people."

> "The one resource that you can't get more of is your time so really they wanted time and
> energy spent together."

> "You only got one life to live... go find a place where you can thrive."

> "The only person holding you back is you."

Context speakers (NOT persona voice):

> Kier McLaren [context]: "The boss usually knows the least about what goes on every day in
> his or her own company than the employees."

> Kier McLaren [context]: "Just because you own a company just because you're a creative
> director doesn't mean you have all the answers sometimes the person who's the lowest on
> the totem pole has an answer."

> Kier McLaren [context, quoting Kyle Cooper]: "If you call yourself a creative you should
> be able to make a spectacular cat food commercial."
