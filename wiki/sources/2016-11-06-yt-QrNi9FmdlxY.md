## Key claims

All rules authored/attributed to **Chris Do (Blind)**, dated 2016-11-06. Paraphrased unless quoted below.

1. **Justify left, rag right** — When in doubt, set type flush-left/ragged-right. Western readers scan top-to-bottom, left-to-right; a hard left edge gives the eye a consistent starting point and is easier to read. Avoid first-line paragraph indents for the same reason.
2. **Use one font** — Combining two typefaces well requires deep knowledge of both; until then, stay on one font. In particular, avoid pairing two faces of the *same* classification (two sans, two serifs, two slabs, two scripts) — the point of mixing is contrast, and same-class faces don't give it. Master one font first.
3. **Skip a weight** — When changing weight, jump (light → bold, or medium → extra bold) rather than stepping one notch. Great design relies on contrast; small weight changes are hard for the audience to perceive. Try bold headline + light body.
4. **Double the point size** — Rule of thumb: double or halve the point size between elements (e.g., 30 pt headline → 15 pt body). For a more dramatic effect, go 3× or 4×.
5. **Align to one axis** — Build type along one primary axis and align elements to that gridline. Vertical axis → align the left edge (works regardless of font or size). Horizontal axis → align on the strongest horizontal element (sometimes the cap height, sometimes the baseline).
6. **Pick any font (from a curated list)** — "Use any font you like, as long as it's one of the following," then lists a canon of classic/workhorse typefaces: Akzidenz Grotesque, Avenir, Avant Garde, Bell Gothic, Bodoni, Bembo, Caslon, Clarendon, Courier, DIN Mittelschrift, Franklin Gothic, Frutiger, Futura, Garamond, Gill Sans, Gotham, Helvetica, Letter Gothic, Memphis, Meta, OCR-B, Rockwell, Sabon, Trade Gothic, Trajan, and Univers. (A tongue-in-cheek "freedom within constraints" gag — a designer's shortlist of trusted faces.)
7. **Group by using rules (lines)** — Use rules/lines to group related blocks of information; this also makes dissimilar objects read as more orderly.
8. **Avoid the corners** — Don't place elements on the edges or corners of a page unless you deliberately want to crop/cut them off. Negative space is good — let the design breathe.
9. **Mind the gap (spacing)** — Typography is all about spacing. Never use forced-justified type (it creates "rivers" running through the copy). Avoid a single word alone on a paragraph's last line (a **widow**). Don't let a new page/column open with the final line of the previous paragraph (an **orphan**). Use a single space after punctuation. Watch the shape the rag creates to avoid awkward angles. And: the closer things sit, the more the reader assumes a relationship between separate blocks of information.
10. **Be bold or italic, never regular** — Closing maxim; a play on words (design attitude *and* type weight): commit — don't be "regular."
