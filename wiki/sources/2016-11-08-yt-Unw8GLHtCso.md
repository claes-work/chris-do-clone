## Summary

An interview-format episode where Chris Do talks with Ben Burns (a Futur team member / former designer) about why designers undercharge for logos. Ben recounts having designed roughly 20 logos for $50 each via oDesk (a Fiverr-adjacent freelance platform) early in his career, competing on spec for those low-paying jobs. Chris uses Ben's story as a case study to dismantle the psychology of undercharging: lack of price context, treating a logo as a low-ceiling commodity, guilt around charging overages, and — above all — a confidence gap. Chris contrasts Ben's mindset ("sell as many logos as I can, as fast as I can") with his own ("charge as much as possible; create a luxury/premium item, not a volume-discount product"). Central lessons: your time is your most valuable, non-recoverable resource; pricing logic should be symmetrical (what's fair for the client is fair for you); and the same technical work can command wildly different prices based on positioning and confidence, not craft.

## Key claims

All dated 2016-11-08. Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section.

- **[Chris Do]** Your time is the most valuable resource you have, and unlike raw materials (wood, cement), you can never get it back — giving it away cheaply undermines your own value and self-worth.
- **[Chris Do]** People show a "lack of symmetry in their logic": they value a logo at $50 yet think nothing of paying $150+ for mass-produced designer jeans made in a factory — even though design work requires thinking, empathy, creativity and craftsmanship.
- **[Chris Do]** Overages are fair and should not induce guilt. Analogy: a bar pours you two free refills, but after 14 glasses of beer there is a finite limit — likewise a designer who has re-poured a design 14 times is right to present the "bar bill." Feeling "shady" while being in the right is a signal the guilt is misplaced.
- **[Chris Do]** The core difference between a designer charging $50 and one charging $5,000–$20,000 for the same technical work is **confidence** and **positioning**, not the quality of the work itself.
- **[Chris Do]** He aspires to create a "luxury/premium item" (like $600 designer eyeglass frames from Alain Mikli, Philippe Starck, or Gucci) rather than being a "volume discount person" (like $15–20 swap-meet frames). Both models can survive and thrive, but they are opposite mentalities.
- **[Chris Do]** When Chris saw the AIGA pricing guide, his reaction was to charge five times the listed rates; the takeaway is a mindset that his work was worth more than any reasonable rate — the opposite of Ben's "that's unrealistic" reaction.
- **[Chris Do]** Success in design pricing is "all about mindset" — technically the work of a "rockstar" designer (he names Michael Bierut) is the same; the difference is positioning. (Positioning deferred to a future episode.)
- **[Chris Do]** For an insignificant sum like $50, Chris would rather not do the job at all and go watch a movie — the amount is too small to be worth the time.
- **[Ben Burns]** He charged $50 per logo (not an exaggeration) and did about 20 of them, because he had no context for what others charged and got immensely positive client feedback — the affirmation/validation, creative fulfillment, and control of taking a project start-to-finish mattered more than the money.
- **[Ben Burns]** His searches were about how to get *more* clients and do the work *faster*, not how to charge more — he treated a logo as a commodity with a fixed ceiling on value.
- **[Ben Burns]** He avoided charging overages to dodge client conflict, eating extra revision time; only after revisions hit "the teens" did he ask, and even then he felt "shady" because his time "didn't cost anything more."
- **[Ben Burns]** A "burritos" TED talk by "Aaron Draplin" (bearded Portland designer) — who mentioned doing a logo for a burrito shop and also a logo for $220,000 — blew his mind and set him on a quest to figure out what Draplin did differently; the answer was "nothing different," the work was comparable. (Attribution: Ben's recollection; the $220,000 figure and TED-talk framing are Ben's, uncertain.)
- **Origin story (biographical, Ben-attributed):** Ben studied media arts, served in the military, worked as an undercover cop, then returned to design. He got back into design after he and his wife fostered a litter of puppies; to fund saving a sick puppy the shelter wanted to euthanize, he built a logo, a website, and a Kickstarter-type campaign and raised ~$1,800. This is Ben Burns' biography, not the subject's.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (persona voice):

> "You freely gave away your time and undermine your own value and self worth."

> "I'd rather not do that at all for 50 bucks and go watch a movie... It's such an insignificant amount of money that why even do it."

> "One thing that I realize with people is the lack of symmetry in their logic."

> "You've ordered 14 cups of beer, glasses of beer, and our policy is I give you two for free — two refills for free — but there's a finite amount of refills I can give you."

> "The difference is confidence."

> "I always thought that I want to charge as much as possible cuz whatever I did was worth way more than what I could charge... I got to charge five times as much as what's in the guide."

> "I've always looked at myself as one who aspires to create a luxury premium item versus a volume discount person. It's a whole different mentality."

> "Technically you were doing the exact same thing as [a] Michael Bierut or one of these kind of rockstar designers... but they're positioned differently than you."

Ben Burns (NOT persona voice — Futur team member, context speaker):

> "When I was charging $50 for a logo I had no confidence in myself to charge anymore."

> "I had this mentality that a logo would be a commodity and so it had a certain value, there's a ceiling... I was going to sell as many logos as I could, and a lot of the searches that I did was how to get more clients, not how to charge more."

> "Even though the overage was built into the agreement, I felt shady asking for it." (Ben, on charging overages)
