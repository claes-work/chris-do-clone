# I Wish I Knew This When I Started: Logo Design Pt. 2

★ L3-candidate — Chris Do contributes distinct, quotable logo/pricing/confidence material (the "$5,000 logo" client-empathy framework, "double the price," and "you are good enough" imposter-syndrome coaching) that goes beyond mere restatement.

## Summary

Part 2 of a two-part "The Process" era (2016) conversation on The Futur. Two speakers:

- **Chris Do** (host / mentor, [[entities/chris-do]]) — drives the teaching: how to move from a $50 logo to a $5,000 logo, how to find and empathize with higher-value clients, content-marketing to reach them, and the internal-confidence work required to charge more.
- **Ben Burns** ([[entities/ben-burns]], Futur team member) — the mentee whose story is the case study: a self-taught, ex-veteran / ex-undercover-cop designer who started on oDesk/Elance charging $50 per logo and, by the time of filming, is signing a $30,000 identity project. **Ben's autobiographical material is CONTEXT, not persona.**

Framed as a mock "time machine" — what would Ben tell his younger self on oDesk — but Chris supplies the frameworks and Ben supplies the lived example. Only Chris-Do statements feed the persona.

## Key claims

Per-speaker, paraphrased, dated 2016-11-12.

**Chris Do (persona):**
- Designers' core edge is making things look more expensive / higher-quality than they are — it's about packaging (analogy: bottled water is ~100% marketing, all in the bottle/label/logo/brand).
- To escape the $50-logo market, first decide WHO your client is: not the $50 oDesk buyer or the $500 Elance buyer, but the $5,000 business. Then empathize with their decisions and pain points before selling.
- Every small-to-medium US business needs three things: an identity system (logo + business card), a website, and social-media marketing.
- $5,000-logo clients are ordinary local entrepreneurs who want to differentiate — barber shops, tattoo shops, shoe stores, dentists, dog groomers, real-estate agents — not just funded startups.
- Reach those clients with content marketing: write guides answering their pain points ("how to pick the right logo designer," "how to give feedback that empowers the designer," "are you getting the most out of your logo?") and break them into micro-content.
- A logo must scale up and down and work across applications — favicon, ~150×150 Facebook icon, email signature, business card, a mural — so design for future applications.
- Clients rarely feel their external brand matches their internal self-image ("I'm a million-dollar company, I want to feel like a billion-dollar company"); closing that gap is where designers add value.
- Confidence to charge more starts from internal dialogue; a self-authored letter congratulating yourself for achievements is a "gold" exercise. Sometimes people need permission from someone they trust just to let themselves charge more.
- Imposter syndrome is the "grass is greener" complex — desiring what you're not and seeing only what you lack. Fix: change the conversation, and (per Gary Vaynerchuk) double/triple down on strengths, go all-in on them.
- Pricing method: test higher prices, keep pushing the ceiling until you get real pushback ("that's too much, I can't afford"), then offer to work with them. When you have success and praise, double your price on the next call (with judgment — 10k→20k may be too much); school, degree, city, résumé don't matter, only internal belief.
- The moment a client calls you, you're already qualified — your existing work earned the call; the "game" is then whether they're a good fit for you, not proving yourself.
- Personal example: went from speaking unpaid to $1,000/hour for speaking, learned incrementally rather than jumping straight to a high fee.

**Ben Burns (CONTEXT — his own story, not persona):**
- Any platform where the client dictates the price (oDesk/Elance) will never pay enough — get off it.
- People refer people like themselves, so $50 clients refer more $50 clients; earning more required leaving that market, not just referrals.
- Self-identified with an imposter/identity crisis as a self-taught designer (no private art school, not top of class); reframed himself as "an emerging self-taught graphic designer."
- No longer charges $50 for a logo — signing a $30,000 identity project (logo + style guide: primary/secondary colors, fonts, do's and don'ts) the next day.

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do (persona voice):**
> "That's where designers excel — they can make things look more expensive, of higher quality than they are."

> "Not the $50 oDesk woman, not the $500 Elance [buyer], but the five-thousand-dollar company that's going to hire you. Who's that person? What decisions are they making? What are their pain points?"

> "If you look at bottled water, that's almost 100 percent marketing… it's all in the bottle, the packaging, the label, the logo, and therefore the brand."

> "I'm giving you the permission to tell you right now: you are good enough. When the client calls you… you already qualified for the job, because something you've done, your reputation, the body of work… got you the call to begin with."

> "The imposter syndrome is the grass-is-greener complex. You desire something that you're not and you can only see the things that you're lacking and missing. So let's change the conversation."

> "Next time out — anybody calls you, including that same client — I want you to double the price… keep pushing the ceiling on this until you hit the limit. And don't take into consideration what school you went to, what degree you got, what city you live in… none of that matters. It's the internal belief that matters."

> "When you add a zero, when you double the budget, just don't forget your friend — send me ten percent. Hashtag ten percent."

**Ben Burns (CONTEXT, not persona):**
> "Get off oDesk, because any platform where you allow the client to dictate the price… it's never gonna be enough."

> "People refer people like them, and so it was difficult for me to earn more."

> "I don't charge $50 for a logo anymore… we're signing a contract tomorrow on a thirty-thousand-dollar identity project."
