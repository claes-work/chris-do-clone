# How to be more relatable? Jose is back and tells Chris how.

> **Format / attribution note.** This is a "The Process"-era (2016) conversation in
> which **Jose Caballer returns as a guest to coach Chris Do** on being relatable and
> connecting with an audience. The bulk of the framing, advice, and spiritual/
> psychological concepts are **Jose's — CONTEXT about Chris, not Chris's own beliefs.**
> Only **Chris's own reflections and responses train the persona**; those are marked
> `[CHRIS — persona]` below. Jose's material is marked `[JOSE — context]`. Where a line
> could be read either way it is flagged `attribution: uncertain`.

## Summary

A special-episode reunion prompted by a YouTube comment (from a viewer, "Nasif Salam")
noting that the future videos felt less relatable and less followable after Jose stopped
appearing. Jose Caballer returns to coach Chris on **relatability and connecting with an
audience**. Jose's thesis: relatability comes from being **real and authentic** — letting
the "little Chris" (the inner child / essence beneath social conditioning) show through —
not from manufacturing struggle. He argues Chris keeps a protective "wall/force field"
around himself (rooted in a control adaptation), which limits how deeply he connects, and
urges Chris to "invert" that — put the vulnerable, generous inner person on the outside.
Jose layers in spiritual/psychological framing (inner-child work, attachment patterns from
an India workshop, Burning Man imagery, astrology, Myers-Briggs, meditation) and introduces
a **collaboration vs. co-creation** distinction.

Chris pushes back throughout in his characteristic mode — asking Jose to translate abstract
"woo" concepts into concrete, actionable advice — while candidly **acknowledging relatability
as a genuine weakness he struggles with**, and reflecting on his own patterns (caretaking/
people-pleasing, being a social "wallflower," valuing self-awareness of one's flaws). The
episode is heavy on live audience comments read aloud, and closes with the takeaway that the
two are a complementary "odd couple" who need not become each other.

## Key claims

_All paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Dated 2016-11-19. Per-speaker._

**[JOSE — context, about Chris]**
- Relatability is not about being one-in-a-million or not; anyone can be relatable
  regardless of success — it comes from **being real and authentic**, not from
  manufacturing struggle. (Jose corrects his own earlier framing that made Chris focus on
  struggles.)
- Beneath social conditioning ("the little Chris"/inner child) is a person's true essence;
  letting it shine through creates far more relatability, spontaneity, and joy. Who we
  present is often an **adaptation** formed in childhood to secure love.
- Chris's core adaptation is **control**: he only lets connection go "so deep" because he
  needs to control every situation; he keeps a "wall / force field" of relatability and a
  small, exclusive circle. This protects "little Chris" but limits connection.
- Advice: **invert the relationship** — put the vulnerable, caring, generous inner person on
  the outside; be wholly true to who you are and stop worrying what others think.
- Chris's low-relatability read is partly structural, per Jose: as a "Capricorn / sensing"
  type he climbs gradually with well-anchored footholds, so his failures don't drop him to
  the bottom the way they do others — making his experience harder to relate to.
- Design industry is over-attached to ego and awards, which harms relatability.
- Oprah is the model of relatability: she is "real," shares her ups and downs, and gives
  people an entry point despite her success. `attribution: uncertain` on the biographical
  Oprah details Jose asserts (offered speculatively; not verified).
- **Collaboration vs. co-creation**: collaboration serves a shared goal that supersedes the
  individuals (e.g. building a website to a brief); **co-creation** is when each person
  brings their true self so the result exceeds what any one could dictate (his film analogy).
  Co-creation requires first finding and creating from your own center.
- Challenge to Chris: "dance with your audience" when creating content — connect from your
  authentic center and transmit that energy across the screen.
- On the designer as **consultant vs. vendor**: once you work on yourself enough to show up
  in "full power," clients meet you as a co-creator and style/taste stops being a battle.

**[CHRIS — persona]**
- **Relatability is something he genuinely struggles with** and has thought about a lot,
  including after a question at the Santa Fe "motion" conference. He can't fulfill his
  purpose (to teach and share what he knows) if people can't find a "pathway / conduit" to
  graft their own experience onto his.
- He believes people are one-in-a-million "if we say we are."
- **Understanding your flaws matters more than your strengths** — strengths are easy to
  recognize; the flaws are the human parts. He says he has done the work of holding up a
  mirror to see where he's weak and where he's strong, and simply tells people where he's weak.
- **Pretending to be someone you're not consumes brain power** (like maintaining a lie),
  which prevents being present; better to show up knowing who you are, flaws included.
- Self-described **social wallflower**: awkward at parties, unsure of the social rules, jaw
  tenses up, tends to "check out." Coping tactic: verbally naming his state so others can
  adjust the energy and keep him engaged (cites a Portland example).
- **Caretaking / people-pleasing** pattern he traces to being a middle child (his therapist
  calls it caretaking, not "pleasing"); since art school he has struggled to say no and puts
  others' needs before his own to his detriment — which is why he keeps a small, selective
  circle and the "wall."
- He plays with others' perceptions of him almost as a "social experiment" because he
  believes he knows who he is, so he doesn't much care what people say back.
- Agrees strongly with self-acceptance / being comfortable in your own skin ("zen / knowing
  yourself").
- Frames the difference between himself and Jose: Chris is "logical / robotic," Jose is
  "spiritual, deep, connected"; they should borrow from each other rather than become each
  other. He does not want to be Jose.
- Repeatedly asks Jose to translate abstract concepts into concrete, actionable language —
  his signature "get specific / time is money" mode.
- He is drawn to relatability partly for a **higher purpose**: money is nice and he's made
  plenty, but at what he half-jokingly calls a midlife point he wants to serve something
  higher. (Jose notes Chris draws energy from the connection/praise and asks whether Chris
  can source it internally — `attribution: uncertain` whether Chris fully accepts this.)
