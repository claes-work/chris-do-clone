# Should You Go To Design School: Doing The Math on Education

★ **L3-candidate** — This is one of the most complete early statements of Chris Do's
design-education-reform thesis: the cost-vs-value framing, the "$1,000-a-second demo
reel" argument, and the "$100k buys you 10 rock-star mentorships" thought experiment.
It directly underpins The Futur's founding mission (see
[[wiki/sources/2016-06-30-yt-veG3WbwwaGk]]) and should be promoted to topics/persona at
synthesis.

## Summary

An episode of **The Process** (2016, "The Process" era; co-hosted at the top by
**Jose Caballer** — context, not persona) in which Chris Do runs a live seminar with
design students and alumni on whether a private art-school education is worth its cost.
Chris' central move is to separate **cost** (an expense) from **value** (what you
actually get), then crowdsource a list of everything students hope to get from school
(connections, network, discipline/process, portfolio, mentorship, confidence, creative
voice, professional mindset, role models) and pressure-test how much of that list a
$200k–$500k private art education actually delivers. His verdict: art schools over-index
on hard skills (the "making/manifesting"), while the soft skills that actually get people
hired and paid — communication, leadership, management, storytelling, negotiation,
entrepreneurship — are learned mostly on the job, if at all. He reframes a 90-second
demo reel as "$1,000 a second" and proposes an alternative model: spend the same tuition
paying to shadow/apprentice under industry masters around the world. The episode doubles
as a manifesto for rethinking design education — the seed of The Futur's "private art
school without the crippling debt" mission.

## Key claims

_Paraphrased unless quoted. Dated 2016-12-06. Attribution per speaker; only
Chris Do = persona. Jose Caballer and all students/alumni/audience = context._

**Chris Do (subject / persona):**
- Cost and value are different things: value is what you get out of the education; cost is
  the expense. A $200k education that returns $500k of value is a bargain; a $200k
  education returning $100k of value is just an expense. (2016-12-06)
- Art schools tend to focus heavily on **hard skills** — making, "manifesting," technical
  craft (Illustrator/Photoshop, After Effects). Most of a student's time is spent there.
  (2016-12-06)
- The things that actually get you hired and paid — **soft skills**: concepting,
  communication, leadership, management, storytelling; plus business skills like pricing,
  estimating, proposals, negotiating, closing, getting paid, entrepreneurship — are mostly
  learned on the job, not in school. He repeatedly hires people who lack all of these and
  finds it "disappointing." (2016-12-06)
- **Self-reported:** As a hiring manager, he never looks at where you went to school, your
  degree, whether you got a degree, whether you attended class, or your GPA — he calls the
  value of the degree "a myth." (2016-12-06)
- **Self-reported:** He has hired and worked with people who went to "some school or no
  school you've never heard of" who went on to very successful careers. (2016-12-06)
- The "network" students describe is mostly a **butterfly-effect / domino** network of
  chance encounters, not the **Harvard/Stanford/Berkeley** network where classmates become
  captains of industry who hire you and start companies with you. (2016-12-06)
- **Self-reported:** When he, "Matt," and "Greg" asked which peers from their own cohort
  they could call on today as successful company-runners, the answer was "less than five
  people" — "I'm the most successful one in my group." Their art-school network is not the
  Harvard network. (2016-12-06)
- A degree buys **perception/credibility** that worked in the 1980s but no longer holds in
  2016 — "Nobody cares." (2016-12-06)
- **The demo-reel math:** a good motion grad-show reel is ~90 seconds; at ~$100k tuition
  that's "$1,000 a second," so "that better be a really good demo reel." (2016-12-06,
  self-reported figures / illustrative math)
- **Alternative model (the core thought experiment):** instead of tuition, pay industry
  masters ~$10,000 each to shadow/apprentice for three months. $100k = ~10 masters; or pick
  five, spend $50k, add ~$5k each to live in Paris/UK/Brazil/Japan/Berlin ($25k), and still
  keep $25k — studying under five masters around the world. (2016-12-06, illustrative math)
- Reaching these people: **email is a bad channel** ("If it's important to you pick up the
  phone"). It takes courage, leadership, entrepreneurial spirit, persistence — "If you want
  something, you ask for it, and you don't give up." (2016-12-06)
- He credits the pay-to-apprentice idea to *The Apprentice* — a millionaire who offered to
  pay to learn from someone for six months. (2016-12-06)
- Bigger education critique: the lecture model repeats the same lecture "decades after
  decades"; better to record the single most engaging authority once, have students watch
  at home, and do the hard creative-problem-solving work at school with teachers acting as
  **coaches**, not lecturers — i.e. flipped classroom. He notes the current model does the
  exact opposite. (2016-12-06)
- Creatives are strong on intuition/"gut" but weak at translating feeling into words; his
  own communication ability came from paying someone for 10 years to teach him how to
  speak. (2016-12-06, self-reported)

**Jose Caballer (co-host — context):**
- Intro banter framing the show: "I talk about the design of business" (vs. Chris' "the
  business of design"). (2016-12-06)

**Students / alumni / audience (context — NOT persona):**
- Sol (Otis): the value was gaining access to working creative instructors; also "finding
  your heroes" and faculty who sacrifice for students; recounts having his ego "completely
  shattered" by a Pixar visitor, which changed his direction.
- Various audience members: value = connection (with teachers in the industry and peers),
  discipline/process, inspiration/competition, community, portfolio ("so I can get a job"),
  transferable/problem-solving skills, creative voice, professional mindset, role models.
- **Marcel:** the biggest thing students get is **confidence**, and soft skills are why
  people get hired; degrees change others' **perception** of you (analogizes the
  degree/no-degree stigma to the gender pay gap); cites the **T-shaped skills** concept and
  argues job descriptions now demand many "barcode" vertical skills, which deters
  creativity; argues Chris is trying to quantify something qualitative and that there's only
  a **finite supply of rock-star mentors**; voices a generational duty to mentor/give back.
- **Matthew** (Art Center; now on Chris' staff): got concepting and storytelling from
  school, developed the rest in the field; disagrees on the network — says his Art Center
  network significantly changed his path via a chance friendship (Chris Real) that led into
  motion graphics, Chris' class, an internship, freelance, then staff; as a recruiter he
  sees Otis portfolios as largely interchangeable ("10 to 15 candidates … the same exact
  thing").
- **Ceasar** (UCLA, ~10–12 yrs out): got concepting from school; learned communication and
  leadership on the job; argues education **is** quantifiable because the schools themselves
  put a price on it.
- **Vin:** value = professional mindset; names Ash Thorp as his idol and role-plays the
  pay-to-shadow phone call with Chris.
- An audience member: "Academia is the most profiting industry since the 50s," alongside
  health care (audience-reported, uncited).

> "Value talks about what you're getting out of it; cost is an expense." — Chris Do

> "If your private school education in design costs $200,000, but you get $500,000 worth of

> well, that's an expense." — Chris Do

> "I'm a big believer … that your degree don't matter. I never look at it. I hire people
> here all the time. I really don't care where you went to school, what degrees you got, if
> you got a degree, if you ever even made it to class, what your GPA is. It's a myth."
> — Chris Do

> "There's this thing that they feed to you. It's like the Kool-Aid. You drink it up …
> 'I went to Art Center, man.' … They don't care." — Chris Do

> "That's what you're saying — a thousand dollars a second. That's how good your reel is?"
> — Chris Do

> "So for a $100,000, how many rock star, trailblazing, industry titans can you get at ten
> thousand bucks a pop? … the number is 10." — Chris Do

> "Just think for a moment how different your life would be right now if you just got to
> know those rockstar, trailblazing, amazing … [people]. … You get to live in Paris, in the
> UK, in Brazil, in Japan, in Berlin, to study under five masters." — Chris Do

> "You've been sold a bill of goods, that going to school gives you something. It did in the
> 80s. It doesn't in 2016. Nobody cares." — Chris Do

> "If it's important to you, pick up the phone. Have the courage to use those amazing
> $100,000 communication skills that you picked up … and call somebody, knock on the door.
> And be persistent. … If you want something, you ask for it, and you don't give up."
> — Chris Do

> "Why don't we just find the foremost authority on the subject … record that video once,

> "This is the way it is, this is the way it always will be — that's just dogma. I don't
