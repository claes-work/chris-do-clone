# How I Became A Graphic Designer — Chris Do origin story & struggles Pt. 1

## Summary

First-person autobiographical account from Chris Do, prompted by an off-camera interviewer
(likely a Futur co-host / student), telling the story of how he first discovered graphic
design as a teenager. This is **Part 1** of a multi-part origin story and it covers only the
earliest stretch of his path — from a strict Asian-American upbringing in the 1980s, through
high-school signals that he had artistic talent, to his very first paid design job as a
"tracer" at a silkscreening shop, and finally the pivotal "glowing room" moment (age ~17)
when he first saw what a professional designer's studio looked like and decided design was
his path. The video opens with a teaser image of struggle to come (lying in fetal position
next to a futon), foreshadowing later parts.

Everything below is **[self-reported]** primary autobiography. Chris also references, in
passing, that he later taught design at Cal State Northridge ("Cal State North") for a long
time — a present-tense (2016) framing detail, not part of the teenage narrative.

## Key claims

All dated 2016-12-16, paraphrased, `[self-reported]` unless noted. Tagged
`biography-relevant` where they add or sharpen dated life facts for a later biography pass.

- **[self-reported] [biography-relevant]** Chris grew up in the 1980s and came of age /
  developed self-awareness during high school in that decade. (Consistent with a birth
  around the mid/late 1960s, though he states no birth year here.)
- **[self-reported] [biography-relevant]** He was raised in a "strict Asian upbringing" and
  describes himself as "a very practical person," which is why he did not initially let
  himself pursue art as a career.
- **[self-reported]** As a teenager, "graphic design" was not in his vocabulary; the only
  living proof of "an artist" he could point to were caricature artists drawing portraits at
  the fairgrounds, whom he admired for their talent.
- **[self-reported] [biography-relevant]** In his **junior year of high school**, his
  commercial art instructor told him he should think about a career in art. Chris just
  nodded it off and did not act on it. He notes this realization came "very late."
- **[self-reported] [biography-relevant]** People kept "tapping" him to draw — e.g. he did a
  drawing for the high-school newspaper — but he treated it as a side thing and returned to
  studying or games.
- **[self-reported] [biography-relevant]** His **younger brother's wrestling coach** knew
  Chris liked to draw and introduced him to a man named **Brad Shuboya**, who owned his own
  company and ran a silkscreening shop called **Screen Designs**. (Proper-noun spellings
  transcribed by ear — see attribution note.)
- **[self-reported] [biography-relevant]** Brad hired teenage Chris off a "lame portfolio."
  Chris's **first real design job was as a "tracer"**: Brad drew pencil drawings, and Chris
  laid acetate over them and inked the lines with a **rapidograph pen (Koh-I-Noor brand)**.
- **[self-reported]** Chris found the inking hard — his hands shook and he could not keep the
  lines from jiggling like the master examples Brad (who had "been doing this for decades")
  produced. At this stage he still doubted both his skill and whether he liked the lifestyle
  (the shop had "a bunch of stoners" working there).
- **[self-reported] [biography-relevant]** The turning point: Brad sent Chris on an errand to
  pick up "the typeset" from **Dean**, a typesetter who worked out of a single-story suburban
  tract home not far from the office. (Chris says he did not even know what the word
  "typeset" meant at the time.)
- **[self-reported]** At Dean's house Chris turned a hallway corner into the home office and
  experienced a revelatory "glowing room" moment — he compares it to the glowing briefcase in
  *Pulp Fiction*. Inside were two drafting tables (left and right), a well-organized set of
  colored **Tombow markers**, and a clean professional setup — utterly unlike the ink-and-
  spray-paint mess of the screen-printing shop. He realized professional designers exist and
  "that's what one looks like."
- **[self-reported] [biography-relevant]** Chris frames this as literally "crossing the
  threshold" from his old world into "the design world." At that moment he decided: "this is
  it, this is what I'm gonna do." He states he was **about 17** ("seventeen, I think") when
  this happened.
- **[self-reported]** (Present-day framing, 2016) He has taught at **Cal State Northridge**
  ("Cal State North") "for a long time" and observes that some students' "light" (the
  realization / calling) still hasn't come on.
