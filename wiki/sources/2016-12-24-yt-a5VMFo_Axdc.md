# How to Overcome Self Doubt When No One Will Support Your Passion

★ L3-candidate — adds distinct mindset material (the "hijack your mentors" tactic, the Kanye "reality distortion field" self-belief exercise, the "own the key to your sanity" reframe on external validation, and Chris's autobiographical dark-night-of-the-soul narrative) that would enrich `wiki/topics/mindset`.

## Summary

A "The Process" era (2016) in-studio conversation in which Chris Do counsels a young design student who is struggling with self-doubt, lack of family/peer support for his passion, and intimidation about breaking into design. The student describes feeling sorry for himself, being driven by a desire to "prove everybody wrong," and being scared of the sacrifices required. Chris responds with a mix of autobiography (his parents cutting him off, working his way through school, his father's lecturing) and a toolkit of mindset reframes: transitioning from self-pity to "prove them wrong" to genuine self-belief; setting small goals rather than intimidating giant ones; the "explorer vs. GPS" metaphor for navigating an uncertain path; choosing your peer group ("average of the five people you spend the most time with"); "hijacking" mentors who don't know they're mentoring you; and owning your own emotional state rather than handing "the key to your sanity" to teachers, parents, or critics.

Speakers: **Chris Do** (subject; the mentor/host giving advice) and a **design student / seminar participant** (context only — referred to in the transcript as "Giancarlo," an audience member seeking advice). Only Chris-attributed material feeds the persona.

## Key claims

**Chris Do** (2016-12-24):
- [self-reported] His parents cut him off when he told them he wanted to be an artist, and he paid his own way through school. (paraphrase)
- [self-reported] His father lectured him for hours instead of slapping him as a kid; those lectures embedded a voice in his head that later became his own inner voice. (paraphrase)
- Negative emotions (depression, frustration) can be channeled into "prove them wrong," but that state is still negative because it is rooted in anger at others; the mature endpoint is doing the work because you believe in who you are, letting go of the bitterness. (paraphrase)
- [self-reported] He describes a personal "dark night of the soul" in which an internal conflict was resolved — "the boy" was killed and "the man emerged" — after which he became serious and focused. (paraphrase)
- [self-reported] He made a promise to focus entirely on school — "eat, drink, sleep school," no time for girls, parties, or anything else — starting in his first semester, distancing himself from everyone else; sacrifice led to reward. (paraphrase)
- Effort compounds: every ounce of effort invested returns roughly a "10x return." (paraphrase)
- The mind and body crave the reward of achievement; self-fulfillment and mastery are confidence boosters, and this positive reward loop is addictive — but you have to stay in the zone. (paraphrase)
- Video games are engineered to deliver small rewards (badges, sound effects, stickers) that tap into how the brain works; the same small-reward mechanic can be applied to self-improvement. (paraphrase)
- A person can choose a single day as their "defining moment" and decide to become whoever they want to be from that point forward. (paraphrase)
- Citing **Jim Rohn** (business philosopher): you are the average of the five people you spend the most time with — so upgrade your friends/peer group. (paraphrase, attributed to Rohn)
- Citing **Michael Beirut** (Pentagram): "hijack your mentors" — you can have mentors without them knowing they are mentoring you; Chris counts Rohn, Anthony Robbins, Jack Canfield, and Blair Enns among mentors he has never met. In the information age nearly everything you want to learn is already available, mostly for free. (paraphrase, attributed to Beirut)
- Don't set a goal so big that all you can do is measure how far away you are from it; set small goals (one more hour in the library, one video a night, one book a semester, drop one negative friend). (paraphrase)
- The "explorer vs. GPS" metaphor: some people want a GPS that dictates every turn, but they don't go far because they wait for all the pieces to fall into place; better to head toward a distant peak, watch the ground in front of you, and adjust the path as conditions change. (paraphrase)
- Story of a woman who rowed across the Atlantic solo — some days the current gave positive miles, some days negative — as an illustration of always trying to move forward and not letting setbacks tear you down. (paraphrase)
- Everyone has an inner voice that can be their worst critic; you must have a conversation with it and give yourself permission to screw up a little, because that voice usually originates from an authority figure (teacher, coach, parent) who limited you with good intentions. (paraphrase)
- Parents have the best intentions, but their "operating system" is not equipped for the rate of change in the world; their default advice ("get a college degree and get a job") no longer reliably applies in the 21st century, so you must set your own compass and goals. (paraphrase)
- On the student's assumption that graphic designers need 3D/animation/web skills to be employable: [self-reported] the last three people Chris hired were graphic designers with no motion, animation, or 3D skills — the assumptions about what makes someone employable are flawed. (paraphrase)
- The "Kanye West trick" / reality distortion field: imagine a bubble around you that filters out self-criticism and negative talk and lets you believe fully in yourself. (paraphrase)
- Own your emotional state: don't let your well-being be governed by someone else's praise or criticism — "would you give the key to your sanity to any person on the street?" Assess your own work honestly before showing it, so external critique (positive or negative) can't move you; you are not looking for validation or criticism. (paraphrase)
- [self-reported] His wife is very risk-averse and pessimistic (looks for the 99 ways out of 100 that will fail), yet in 21 years of business they have only been "burned" about twice. (paraphrase; wife not named)

**Design student / participant** (context, 2016-12-24):
- Says his parents cut him off after he said he wanted to be an artist and that he's paying his own way through school; feeling sorry for himself has been "one of the worst things ever." (paraphrase)
- Is drawn to Japanese culture and connects the "prove everybody wrong" drive to the concept of *kuyashii* (the feeling of frustrated determination to prove someone wrong). (paraphrase)
- Wants to be great but is scared of the sacrifices and uncomfortable telling his family/others to back off. (paraphrase)
- Gave up video games to develop his design thinking. (paraphrase)
- Feels intimidated: his school focuses on three Adobe programs (Illustrator, InDesign, Photoshop) while he sees people in Chris's studio using tools like Maya and Cinema 4D, and worries graphic design alone isn't valued without web/other add-ons. (paraphrase)
