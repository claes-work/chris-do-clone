# Overcome Your Fears: Motion Design Industry Changes, Public Speaking, Insecurities

★ **L3-candidate** — carries distinct, highly quotable mindset and self-aware persona
material (public-speaking anxiety, introvert/extrovert, "modeling," growth as the
metric of success, learn-by-copying). Strong voice-training source.

## Summary

An early "The Process"-era conversation (published 2017-01-05) in which Chris Do speaks
to what appears to be a group of design students/young creatives, fielding questions
from an interviewer/host and audience members. Chris walks through three threads: (1)
why he pivoted his studio (Blind) from a motion-design shop to a **brand strategy firm**
as the motion industry contracted; (2) the mindset he says a creative needs to thrive —
"creative elasticity," measuring success by personal growth, and learning by
deconstructing and copying good work; and (3) an unusually candid autobiographical
account of his own **social anxiety, introversion, and public-speaking terror**, and the
concrete tricks he used to overcome them.

Speakers: **Chris Do** (persona subject). One or more **interviewers/students** ask
questions (context only — not persona). Individual student questioners are not named;
their prompts are recorded here as context. No co-host is clearly identified by name in
the transcript, so any non-Chris speech is treated as context.

## Key claims

**Motion-industry change & the pivot to brand strategy (Chris, 2017):**
- For ~21 years his studio did motion design for big brands and the world's largest
  agencies; it "made a lot of money" and was great, but audiences stopped watching TV
  and moved to an on-demand world, so the industry changed and opportunities shrank.
  [paraphrase] [self-reported]
- He told his whole company the motion business has "limited days" / a limited shelf
  life and they had to plan for "this other thing" he couldn't yet name — a deliberately
  vague, "cloudy" future direction. This scared people; some quit, and he let others go,
  shrinking the company as it changed direction. [paraphrase] [self-reported]
- The pivot landed: they transitioned from a motion-centric company to a **brand
  strategy firm** serving companies "in the millions and billions" that want to "look
  like a billion dollar company and not spend like one" — the gap between giant brands
  with huge agencies and mom-and-pop shops. This is why they were newly hiring
  designers who understand branding/brand strategy, can design and mock up, and are
  agile. [paraphrase] [self-reported]

**Creative mindset (Chris, 2017):**
- To thrive (not just survive) in the 21st century you need **"creative elasticity"** —
  like a rubber band, how far can you stretch beyond one specialty. Specialists (the
  "logo assassin," the "motion graphics assassin") are valued, but he wants designers
  with range who don't panic at an unfamiliar brief (a showroom, a 3D interactive
  experience, an AR thing) and will just research and figure it out. [paraphrase]
- "Most creative people measure success as to how closely what they produce resembles
  what they had imagined. I measure success by how much I've grown." [paraphrase of the
  claim; verbatim below] One of his greatest fears is looking back and still recognizing
  the person he was a year ago. [paraphrase]
- He gets excited by learning — used to read Mac World like an "adult magazine,"
  thrilled by imagining a future empowered by new tools/skills. Excitement about the
  future became "a pattern of behavior." [paraphrase] [self-reported]

**Introvert/extrovert & confidence (Chris, 2017):**
- Introvert vs. extrovert is about **where you get your energy** (a student cites
  Myers-Briggs; Chris affirms). Being around people (public speaking, teaching, groups
  of more than two or three) used to drain all his energy. [paraphrase]
- He does **not** believe he's an extrovert; the transition to how he presents now
  "only happened a couple years ago." [paraphrase] [self-reported]
- **Confidence ≠ extroversion.** He says he was always confident and knew his work was
  good — he just didn't like being around people. [paraphrase]

**Public-speaking anxiety & the fix (Chris, 2017) — self-aware persona trait:**
- As a shy kid he avoided eye contact so hard that peers read it as arrogance and nearly
  picked fights; a casual "what's up?" would throw him off. [paraphrase] [self-reported]
- His **business coach of 10+ years** told him his reluctance to talk to clients was
  "killing" the company: clients who visit and don't see him feel unimportant, and when
  employees eventually leave, the client bonds to the employee, not to him. The coach's
  prescription: **volunteer to do public speaking.** [paraphrase] [self-reported]
- The first year of speaking was "absolute torture." He describes the physical panic:
  quickened heart, gassy throat/burping, pacing in circles, shaky voice, once
  frantically rewriting a lost keynote and telling the audience he was nervous. He
  didn't get good — he "just sucked less" until he found his own trick. [paraphrase]
- **The trick:** stop trying to sound smart/smooth (it backfires and you sound dumb);
  go with what you know — "they'll forgive you for the words, they'll remember your
  energy and your tone." And build the talk like a **storyboard**: image-only slides, no
  words, each frame a visual prompt, arranged for flow like a movie (establishing shot →
  tighter → transition). [paraphrase]
- **"Modeling"** — a named tool he offers: watch someone and behave like them. He copied
  a man named Bart (an ordinary-looking guy who greeted people easily) to learn casual
  social greetings, and studies comedians like Louis CK for cadence, pauses, pacing, and
  delivery. [paraphrase] [self-reported]

**Insecurity, letting go & learning by copying (Chris, 2017):**
- To grow confidence: **let it all go** and accept imperfection. "Trying to be perfect
  makes me more imperfect. Accepting it makes me more perfect." Trying to prove you're
  smart/powerful makes you less so — and annoying. [paraphrase]
- **Bullies are insecure people** — braggarts, show-offs, cocksure advice-givers are
  "messed up on the inside." He contrasts this with spiritual figures (Dalai Lama, the
  Pope) whose faces show calm; the Dalai Lama "has nothing" but inner peace and is
  comfortable in his own skin. [paraphrase]
- Whatever you fear someone discovering about you, say it to yourself out loud — the more
  you let it out, the more it's all right; hiding it makes you an "impostor to yourself,"
  and lies drain your energy. [paraphrase]
- Emotional arc of a creative: starts in confusion/insecurity/frustration/anger/self-
  pity/jealousy → turns it into rebellion / "prove them wrong" (a bridge) → eventually
  reaches self-awareness, mindfulness, and being at peace/comfortable in your own skin.
  [paraphrase]
- **Learning method:** good design is just the base level and is "easy" today; expose
  yourself to lots of good work and **deconstruct and copy it**. Analogy: tasting a
  complex soup — an unrefined palate makes spaghetti instead; his method is to break the
  soup down to its ~17 ingredients (salt, acid, herbs), replicate it repeatedly, then
  move "less away from the formula and more into intuition," but always built on
  **fundamentals**. Masters trained apprentices one distilled skill at a time.
  [paraphrase]
- He frames his own talent as **analysis** ("that's where the robot comes in") — breaking
  things down and regurgitating them into bite-sized pieces others can understand. Cites
  **Kyle Cooper** (visited ~a week and a half earlier) reviewing animation in layers
  (typeset → motion → compositing → concept), and positions his own gift as being able to
  tell you *where* it breaks, not just that it's bad. [paraphrase]
- A student/interviewer pushes back (context): a "be true to yourself" philosophy is
  great for life, but in a design class you still need criteria for good vs. bad design
  and can't just pursue your own journey and dismiss critique. Chris agrees there must be
  criteria. [paraphrase, non-Chris prompt = context]
