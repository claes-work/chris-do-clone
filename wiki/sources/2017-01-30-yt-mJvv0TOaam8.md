# 101 Things I Learned in Business School — Book Review

Solo episode of "The Process" (2017 era), filmed by Chris Do from his home bedroom
rather than the studio, as an experiment to ship more content. Chris reviews the book
*101 Things I Learned in Business School* and walks through selected tips, adding his
own creative-industry interpretation to each.

**Book credited to its authors** — Michael Preston (Harvard MBA, PhD George Washington
University; management consultant / executive / business owner) and Matthew Frederick
(architect, urban designer, teacher; creator of the bestselling *101 Things I Learned*
series). The book's format: an illustration on the left page, a numbered tip on the
right. The persona-relevant material is **what Chris takes from each tip** — his
translation of the book's business-school ideas for creatives, designers, and studio
owners.

## Summary

Chris organizes the review around an agenda: running a meeting, management/leadership,
sales & negotiation, clarity, and pricing. For each of ~20 numbered tips he restates
the book's principle (authors' idea) and then reframes it for creative professionals —
e.g. how a designer becomes a facilitator rather than an expert, why servant leadership
and delegation grow a team, how to diagnose before selling, how to hold negotiating
leverage by staying lean, and how to price on value rather than cost. The pricing
section is the most developed: Chris draws out his own layered model of a bid
(cost of goods sold → overhead → profit → value to the client), which foreshadows his
signature value-based-pricing teaching. He also demonstrates his book-annotation habit
(highlighting, underlining, writing notes) as a learning tool.

## Key claims

Book ideas are credited to the authors (Preston/Frederick); the reframing and examples
are Chris's own takeaways (persona-relevant), all dated 2017-01-30.

**Running a meeting (book tip 10)**
- The book prescribes: circulate an agenda in advance, invite additions, prioritize
  items (most important first), begin on time, set speaking rules, stay on subject,
  record minutes, one person speaks at a time, outline next steps, distribute notes
  afterward. Chris endorses all of this. (paraphrase)
- Chris's additions: begin on time so people don't "straggle in late"; set time limits
  because open-ended discussion lets a long-talker monopolize the meeting; as chair,
  actively encourage participation and debate "regardless of title or salary" because
  "the best ideas come from the least likely person"; the point is a safe space to
  share "without fear of retribution." (paraphrase)

**Management & leadership**
- (Book tip 65) An expert isn't the person who knows the most — experts identify and
  create new patterns that reorganize known information. Chris's takeaway: creatives who
  fear "I can't do that work, I don't know anything about it" miss that a facilitator
  need not be the expert — the value is in creating space, listening, and recognizing/
  connecting patterns others can't see. This is a shift "from a maker mentality to
  thinking and connecting dots." (paraphrase)
- (Book tips 70 + org ladder) A manager lacks specific craft knowledge but understands
  personnel, training, motivation, evaluation, compensation, budgeting; a VP handles
  long-range planning, product development, financing, strategic direction; the
  executive level concerns philosophical direction, mission, and the meaning of the
  brand. Chris's takeaway: as you climb, skills get broader and less connected to
  "moving pixels on a box" — reaching the C-suite requires "vastly different skills
  than you were taught in school." (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 75) A good manager makes imperfect decisions, weighing benefits against the
  dangers of delay. Chris's takeaway, a line he repeats: "not making a decision is in
  itself a decision"; inaction is catastrophic to organizations, and you can help
  fence-sitting clients decide by asking "what if we wait — are there threats looming?"
  (competitors gaining advantage, lost market share, customers tiring). (paraphrase)
- Pareto / 80-20 principle (attributed in-book to Joseph Juran): 20% of causes produce
  80% of results. Chris's takeaway: entrepreneurs err by spending time on the 80% that
  doesn't matter; e.g. 80% of revenue often comes from 20% of clients, and among a staff
  of 10 the two top performers get neglected while attention goes to the intern or the
  drama-bringer. (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 78) Good managers delegate and serve those under them. Chris ties this to
  servant leadership (citing Simon Sinek, and Tony Hsieh's *Delivering Happiness*):
  "we live to serve our people... it's not about our win, it's about their win."
  Micromanaging inhibits growth — subordinates stop thinking for themselves; delegation
  is about growing your people, not just saving time. (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 79) Tell others the result you need, not how to get it. Chris's takeaway:
  be clear on goals, deadline, parameters, budget — but don't dictate "open Photoshop,
  do X, save here," which throws away the team's thinking and creativity. Creatives /
  knowledge workers prize autonomy highly. (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 99) Who to hire first: resist hiring another creative just like you —
  instead "hire your own boss," someone to run the business of the business (executive
  producer, producer, salesperson, operations person) so you can stay focused on the
  work you enjoy. Hire someone who complements your skill set. (paraphrase)

**Sales & negotiation**
- (Book tip 55) Customers whose complaints are resolved quickly and satisfactorily
  often become very loyal; your unhappiest customers are your greatest source of
  learning. Chris (relaying Michael Preston): a customer willing to complain wants to
  stay loyal. Illustrated with the Zappos story of a rep who flew shoes to a customer's
  wedding — treated not as an expense but as marketing, because the customer tells the
  story repeatedly. (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 52) A good salesperson first seeks to understand the true nature of the
  customer's problem, then offers a solution. Chris's takeaway (his recurring line):
  "amateurs give advice, experts diagnose" — don't leap to "you need a new logo /
  website / video / VR"; understand the problem first, and sometimes you're not the
  answer. (paraphrase)
- Talk a customer out of a wrong purchase to gain respect and build trust — even
  redirecting them to something you don't offer. "Trust is worth its weight in gold."
  Few creatives grasp this; they think great work alone is what clients value.
  (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 9) Act as a fiduciary — bound to act for another's benefit (like an
  attorney managing a beneficiary's trust). If you can be perceived as acting in the
  client's best interest, you've achieved something great. (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 23) The party that cares less (is indifferent about the outcome) holds the
  stronger negotiating position; wanting the sale badly makes you weaker and likelier to
  accept less. Chris's two routes to leverage: genuinely — keep overhead lean (no fancy
  office, expensive furniture, new car) so you don't *need* the income; or mentally —
  keep saying "no, it's not right." He adds a note from another book: whisper and people
  lean in; shout (i.e. act desperate) and people pull away. (paraphrase)
- Meta-strategy: push for deeper needs to create a win scenario. When a client says
  "we can't afford this," keep asking what they're really trying to do / what business
  problem they're solving — you may surface a more meaningful problem with a different
  budget, sidestepping the money conversation. (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 14) Connectors are people outside your limited family-and-friends circle who
  introduce you to entirely new networks. Chris's example: his brother in Silicon Valley
  partnered with "Eli Cohen," a connector whose former sales contacts became clients —
  won by delivering beyond what was promised so he'd recommend them. (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 54) A feature is a fact; a benefit is how it helps the customer (often a
  feeling/intangible). Example: call forwarding/waiting is a feature; never missing a
  call is the benefit. "Benefits, not features, ultimately sell." (paraphrase)

**Clarity**
- (Book tip 15) A mission statement should guide daily decision-making and performance.
  Chris (citing Guy Kawasaki): don't waste money writing a mission statement; reduce it
  to three words or less — e.g. Nike = "genuine athletic performance." Ties it to users
  of his "core" positioning/mission framework: make it clear enough to guide decisions.
  (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 80) Do fewer things but do them better. Chris's takeaway: creatives want to
  be everything to everyone; asked their target market they say "anybody alive," which
  means they can reach no one. Focus doesn't mean going narrower — it means going deeper
  and delivering a greater customer experience. (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 100) Some stress is good, a lot of stress is bad. Chris relates personally:
  his creative juices don't flow until a deadline approaches (which frustrates his
  family), but taking on too many things is when stress overwhelms him. (paraphrase)

**Pricing**
- (Book tip 1) A business transaction works because each party assigns higher value to
  what it receives than what it provides; when value exceeds price, a deal is made.
  Illustrated with a hat: seller = material value + profit (selling price); buyer =
  material value + emotional reward ("I'll look cooler," "keep sun off my eyes")
  = purchase price. Chris's takeaway: if a client doesn't see value, work on creating
  the emotional reward (the benefit). (paraphrase)
- (Book tip 92) Set prices according to what the customer will pay, not necessarily
  what it costs — i.e. price on the customer's perception of value. This launches
  Chris's value-based-pricing model. (paraphrase)
- **Chris's layered bid model (his own elaboration):**
  1. *Cost of goods sold (COGS)* — labor (you, staff, freelancers, outsourced
     specialists such as web development) and materials (stock photography, commissioned
     illustration, fonts, video production).
  2. *Overhead* — rent, equipment, hardware, software, utilities, insurance, janitorial
     — everything absorbed as a business owner; must be factored into the bid.
  3. *Profit* — a separate layer on top; paying yourself is NOT profit, and covering
     costs alone means you only broke even. Businesses need profit (roughly 5–40%) to
     survive; The Futur's video/post work typically adds a 30% margin — "a rainy-day
     fund" for slow periods.
  4. *Value to the client* — the highest layer: helping the client hit a business goal
     (downloads, email signups, event registrations, purchases/conversion), campaign
     value (reach, exposure, engagement metrics), product development, clarity, and
     value from *avoiding* loss — lost sales, confusion (illegible logo, bad messaging),
     lower conversion, slow-to-market, or wasteful spend (e.g. a bad 50,000-unit print
     run). (paraphrase)
