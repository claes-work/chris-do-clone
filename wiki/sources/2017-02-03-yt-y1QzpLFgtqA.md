# Live Design Critique w/ Chris Do via Facebook | Brand Identity, Motion Graphics, Print Design

Episode of *The Process* (2017 era). Chris Do live-critiques viewer-submitted work —
portfolios, websites, logos/identity, calligraphy/lettering, motion/web copy, and print
posters — over Crowdcast while Ben Burns (introduced as the new digital director, "working
here for a couple of days") relays Facebook Live comments. Format is improvised and
occasionally hampered by screen-share glitches. Critique subjects: Miguel (Portugal, branding
+ hand lettering), Freddy (Splashboard, brand strategy site), an unnamed student (university
application portfolio, Levcon/Levon APS logo + sports posters), and Fernanda (recent grad,
print).

## Summary

Chris walks through several submissions and applies a consistent set of portfolio/craft
principles. The recurring throughline: **buyers browse fast with short attention spans, so the
first image ("above the fold" / thumbnail) must do the selling, and the "splashiest" work
should lead — not the sketches/process**. He repeatedly tells submitters they buried their
best, most polished imagery at the bottom and led with weaker sketches. Secondary themes:
present process as deliberately as the finished work (or don't show it at all), title/tag work
with searchable buyer-facing terms, edit out weaker pieces, and use application mockups rather
than bare logo marks. Craft-level notes cover lettering refinement (stroke weights, thick/thin
flow, endings), logo integration (symbol angles echoed in the wordmark), color contrast
(yellow-on-white reads poorly), and drawing symbols as filled forms rather than lines.

## Key claims

All claims are paraphrases of Chris Do's stated positions during the critique (2017-02-03)
unless marked as a direct quote below.

- **Go where the buyers already are, not where they'd have to search for you by name.** For
  branding/lettering, buyers spend time on Behance and Dribbble, so post there rather than
  relying on a standalone site people won't find. (2017-02-03)
- **Thumbnails / cover art must draw the viewer in because attention spans are short and people
  browse fast.** Each thumbnail should relate clearly to the discipline you want to be hired
  for (branding thumbnails should look like branding). (2017-02-03)
- **Show the application of a logo, not just the logo mark, as the thumbnail** — it is "much
  more enticing visually." (2017-02-03)
- **The "above the fold" rule (borrowed from newspapers) applies to portfolio pages:** if the
  top of the page/piece doesn't capture attention, the viewer won't scroll further. (2017-02-03)
- **Lead with your splashiest, best work; put sketches and backstory farther down.** Multiple
  submitters made the same mistake of hiding their strongest imagery at the bottom. (2017-02-03)
- **Present the process with as much care as the finished work — or don't show it at all.**
  Show mood boards, outline/vector-in-progress views, and thinking; label it (e.g. "simple,
  clean, plain") with the same typography and layout care as the rest. (2017-02-03)
- **Title/tag pieces with searchable, buyer-facing language.** "Logo and identity design" or
  "hand lettering" tells the buyer what it is; a client name like "Sandbox Arts and
  Entertainment" tells them nothing. (2017-02-03)
- **Edit ruthlessly — remove the weaker pieces.** If the curator (as a prospective hirer) likes
  some marks more than others, weaker ones raise doubt about "which version am I going to get."
  (2017-02-03)
- **A few excellent marks can win work.** "If you draw this really, really well... you're going
  to get work from just drawing two or three marks." (2017-02-03)
- **Great mockups sell design work; you don't have to build them yourself** — mockup templates
  can be downloaded (e.g. Creative Market) to save time. (2017-02-03)
- **But real photography can beat a mockup:** shooting your own physical piece (well-lit) is "a
  much better approach" than a generic mockup when you can do it well. (2017-02-03)
- **Lettering craft = refinement of weights and stroke endings.** Judge thick/thin, whether it
  "flows," and how strokes end; refine awkward letters (called out specific T, U, G, A, S
  strokes). Prefer a clean single pen-weight stroke over added highlights. (2017-02-03)
- **Draw logos/symbols as solid filled forms, not lines** — fill shapes in (demonstrated live
  in Photoshop with the paint bucket); with a pencil, lay the broad edge down and fill fast.
  Symbol design "is more about solid forms." (2017-02-03)
- **Integrate the symbol with the wordmark.** The angle/form of a symbol (e.g. a triangular L)
  should be echoed elsewhere in the logotype so the pieces don't feel separate. (2017-02-03)
- **Watch color contrast:** yellow reads poorly against white (low contrast); pick a darker hue.
  (2017-02-03)
- **For web copy, prefer static written text over animated type.** Animation forces the viewer
  to your pace, is disorienting if they tune in mid-sequence, and a four-sentence linear
  structure read at the viewer's own pace works better. (2017-02-03)
- **Thin process pages should be "blown out":** give each stage (brand audit, brand discovery,
  etc.) its own card with description and examples rather than a thin outline. (2017-02-03)
- **Typography/layout note (print):** don't jam type into a corner — treat the whole page and
  build in negative space. (2017-02-03)
- **On the insecurity question ("is my work good enough?"):** even a highly regarded designer
  with thousands of fans still privately struggles with it, and that feeling may never fully go
  away. (2017-02-03) *(attribution: Chris relaying a peer's vulnerable admission; peer unnamed.)*
