## Key claims

**The "lies" of design education (Chris Do, 2017-02-15):**

- **Lie 1 — "Just get in [to Art Center] and you're set."** [paraphrase] Chris says the
  mythology built up around "the school on the hill" told him that mere admission meant he
  had it made. He calls this a lie: getting in is not being set — you have to "give it all
  you've got." Petrula (context) debates him, arguing it's a matter of interpretation and
  that admission gives you access to strong faculty and network. They openly disagree.
- **Lie 2 — "Your degree matters / the more education, the better off you'll be."**
  [paraphrase] Chris calls this a "total lie," attributing it to his parents (the American
  belief that you can't be taken seriously without a bachelor's, and that higher degrees
  mean a better life). [self-reported] He cites cousins, friends and family pursuing
  master's and doctorate degrees who "are suffering." Core line: **the amount of education
  you get is not proportionate to the value you get out of it.**
- **Qualification — degrees do matter *within academia*.** [paraphrase] To be a teacher /
  get tenure and pay, the highest possible education is required; within academia the
  degree matters. But Chris argues **actual field practice matters a lot more** for
  teaching design, and criticizes educators focused only on schooling over practice.
  [self-reported] His own Art Center title is still "assistant adjunct instructor" after
  ~15 years teaching / 20 years in the field, because he has no master's and doesn't want
  one — which he finds insulting given someone from "Podunk University" with a master's and
  no work experience could become a professor. Petrula (context) agrees that in graphic
  design significant practice is essential to teach; they reach agreement here.
- **Lie 3 (mindset, not education) — "if you're fit and you exercise you have more energy."**
  [paraphrase] Chris says he used to roll his eyes and assume this was "a lie to get me to
  exercise," but found it true: more exercise → less sleep needed, more focus, more energy.

**Education / future-of-schools framing:**

- Petrula (context) argues degrees are important and that multiple, increasingly-accessible
  sources of information (especially online) are key; schools that don't keep up will
  struggle, those that teach what you *can't* get online will become more valuable.
  [attribution: this is the host's view, not Chris — do not train persona on it.]

**Mindset & self-development (Chris Do):**

- Admires **Tony Robbins** [paraphrase] — despite once thinking him "the cheesiest guy
  ever" / a get-rich-quick scam; now respects that Robbins helps people change their mind,
  build confidence, and remove negative associations blocking their path.
- Coaching insight [paraphrase]: he can teach all the tools, scripts and bid forms in
  hours, but the real work is **helping people get over their own mental blocks** — "it's
  not about the tools."
- Persuasion came from **loving to debate as sport** [self-reported] — with his
  girlfriend, college mates, and deliberately seeking out "religious zealots" to argue
  with for intellectual challenge; the more questions he asked, the harder it was for them
  to stay consistent.
- Grew up in **a very traditional, near-silent Asian family** [self-reported] ("if my dad
  said two [words] to me that's a lot"); despite being introverted, he wanted conversations
  to be intellectually challenged.
- Two formative classes [self-reported]: (1) **Philosophy 101 at De Anza College** — an
  instructor who "masterfully" led the class to conclude "God cannot exist," using short
  stories each teaching one small concept building to a large one; Chris repeatedly took the
  lone contrarian side in debates (e.g. said at 18 he *would* give his life to end world
  hunger). (2) **Jay Chapman's film-critique class at Art Center** — taught him he could
  think critically/analytically and articulate ideas; made him hyper-aware while watching
  films. Being broke and finding it unethical to sit through a film twice without paying,
  he **trained himself to watch a film twice in one sitting** — once as a seduced audience
  member, simultaneously once as a critic tracking shot structure and narrative. He calls
  it "what great training" for the motion-design work he didn't yet know he'd do.

**Business / the future ↔ blind flywheel (Chris Do):**

- Being visible on YouTube makes you more vulnerable to criticism, but if you get past that,
  **inbound leads arrive** [self-reported]: clients now call blind from YouTube — and not
  just for design, but for **consulting**. Examples: a corporate-sales leader wanting help
  "designing a sales problem," and a company wanting help managing its creative team — both
  found him by searching "the business of design." He enters as a facilitator using design
  thinking to solve business problems, not to build identity systems.
- **blind is the proving ground for what he teaches on the future** [paraphrase] — not
  theoretical pontification; he documents real client calls into sales scripts (objection →
  response). The line between the two companies is "permeable."

**Managing creative people (Chris Do):**

- Managing creatives is "like herding cats" [paraphrase] — emotionally driven, ego-laden,
  the hardest people to manage; the same traits make them powerful. Early on he handled
  off-brief work passive-aggressively (just not presenting it), which went badly.
- Now he **hires for it upfront** [paraphrase]: designers with emotional maturity ("it's not
  about me and my work, it's about solving the problem"), who take direction, have many
  ideas, and work autonomously — screened via freelance/internship trial.
- **Culture is safeguarded deliberately** [self-reported]: hires only like-minded people who
  share the published core values (don't take themselves too seriously, drive change, seek
  fun in the little weirdness). Interview process once put "the three weirdest people in the
  company" to vet culture fit — survive them and you're "family."

**Health & creativity (Chris Do):**

- Believes health, happiness, fitness and creativity are all tied together [paraphrase]:
  exercise = stress release → more access to intuition → better decisions; and happiness
  comes partly from perceived progress and control — diet and movement are among the few
  things you fully control, and you see results.
- [self-reported] For most of his life he was "not an athletic person" — "a nerd" who drew,
  read comics, played video games. In the last quarter of his life he hit "another emotional
  bottom," felt "soft," ashamed. The trigger: on a family vacation he felt **too timid to
  take his shirt off at the pool** with his young sons, leaving that to his wife. He
  committed to change a bit at a time and now says he is **"in the best shape of my life."**

**Parenting (Chris Do, self-reported):**

- Two sons: Otto (13, drifting toward science — wanting to impact the world "in ways art
  cannot") and Matias (10 — more like Mom and Dad: music, drawing, wears emotions on his
  sleeve; his Facebook drawings are colored in Photoshop with Chris's wife Jesse's help).
- Home overflowing with art supplies/making tools ("an art store times something").
- Says with total sincerity he **doesn't care what his sons do** — whether artist or
  businessman — as long as they apply themselves, are happy, and don't hurt others; no
  pressure to be a doctor/lawyer or run the family business. Hopes in 50 years they'd thank
  him for **loving them unconditionally, giving them space to be who they are, setting
  boundaries, and being a fair, positive role model.** Petrula notes the question reveals
  what he values most in the world.

**Quitting / firing (Chris Do, live role-plays):**

- **How to quit well** [paraphrase]: shake the boss's hand, genuinely thank them, say you've
  grown but need to pursue other things, and offer a smooth transition — but only quit once
  you've *already made the decision*; don't reopen the negotiation ("I'm sorry you feel that
  way, I've already made my decision").
- **How to fire well** [paraphrase]: start by articulating your own feeling ("hey Petrula…"
  → "you're fired" with a lot in between); be transparent, illuminate your reasoning, own
  shared failure ("I feel like we failed you, you failed us"), and if you've treated the
  person consistently and fairly all along, they sometimes *apologize to you.* He references
  a podcast he did on the art of communication covering firing.
- As a manager he prefers people just say they want to quit — "I only want people who want
  to be here."

**"Selling out" & sustainability (Chris Do):**

- "Selling out" usually means achieving success and leaving Indie roots; communities get
  protective of under-the-radar artists and brand success as commercial betrayal
  [paraphrase].
- Chris's stance [self-reported]: if selling out means his financial success lets him
  **share his vision, build a safe home for creatives, and protect the community and
  livelihoods** entrusted to him, then "call me a sellout and I gladly do it." He *does*
  care about the art and craft. **Business sustainability** — making decisions that let the
  company keep thriving, keep good clients and good people — is where "the proof is in
  creativity and business."
