# Legally Speaking | Copyright & Trademark Infringement Attorney Stuart Carroll PT 1

## Summary

Episode of "The Process" on The Futur (@thefutur) in which host **Chris Do** interviews
his long-time attorney and friend **Stuart Carroll**, an intellectual-property lawyer
based in Southern California who works with tech, fashion, and entertainment companies.
This is **Part 1** of a multi-part conversation, filmed with a live studio audience plus
Facebook Live questions.

Chris frames the episode with a personal story: in ~1996, about a year and a half into
his business, he was served with a copyright-infringement lawsuit related to web-design
work featured in a book. He uses it to introduce Stuart and the topic of how creatives
should protect themselves legally. Stuart then answers audience/host questions on:
unpaid clients and the value of contracts, implied vs. signed contracts, attorney's-fee
clauses, the cost of having a lawyer draft a contract, work-for-hire, non-disclosure
agreements, idea-vs-expression in copyright, copyright registration, and the risks of
sourcing images from sketchy (e.g. "Russian") archives vs. reputable, indemnifying ones.

> **Attribution note:** Stuart Carroll's legal explanations are **context / guest
> expertise only** — NOT Chris Do's beliefs and NOT to be encoded as legal advice from
> the persona. Only Chris Do's own framing, questions, reactions, and hard-won business
> lessons feed the persona. Nothing here is legal advice.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (host — persona-eligible framing)

- **[2017-02-23]** In ~1996, roughly a year and a half into business, Chris was served
  with a copyright-infringement lawsuit — his "introduction into the American legal
  system." (self-reported personal anecdote; paraphrase)
- **[2017-02-23]** The dispute arose from web-design work Chris did for a large beauty/
  cosmetics company being featured in a book. Chris signed a release granting whatever
  rights he had, and told the author to get asset rights from the client directly; he
  says he sent the author none of the actual assets — they came from the client.
  (self-reported; paraphrase)
- **[2017-02-23]** The book's text portrayed the client unflatteringly ("the client
  handed us a box of images... we had to figure out the game plan"); Chris speculates the
  client, no longer working with him, disliked the portrayal and/or wanted money back,
  and "hid behind the copyright." Framed explicitly as his own speculation. (paraphrase)
- **[2017-02-23]** It became a four-way lawsuit (publisher, author, Chris, old client);
  the claim was "at a minimum" $100,000. Chris had no insurance and considered simply
  closing the business rather than fighting. (self-reported; paraphrase)
- **[2017-02-23]** Chris's takeaway from seeing the legal system up close: the party with
  more money, more time, and who "could care less" usually ends up on the better end.
  (Chris's own belief; paraphrase — see quotes)
- **[2017-02-23]** Business lesson Chris repeatedly draws: it's better to "get the job
  done" and move on than to fight on principle; principled legal battles drain cash,
  cause stress, and strain relationships. (Chris's belief; paraphrase — see quotes)
- **[2017-02-23]** Chris advises creatives: have a contract, have it signed, and have an
  attorney write or at least review it. He confirms The Futur/Blind uses a standard
  boilerplate service agreement attached to every estimate/proposal. (paraphrase)

### Stuart Carroll (guest attorney — CONTEXT ONLY, not persona, not legal advice)

- **[2017-02-23]** Background: born/raised in Southern California, studied at Brandeis and
  Boston University School of Law, worked as an assistant DA in NYC, then opened his own
  firm focusing on internet/tech/entertainment IP; taught copyright law at Pepperdine;
  met Chris via the California Lawyers for the Arts referral panel. (guest self-report)
- **[2017-02-23]** He does both litigation and transactional (contract) work. (context)
- **[2017-02-23]** On unpaid clients: the first question is always "what does the
  contract say" — disputes usually trace to no contract or a client/designer
  misalignment. (guest legal opinion — context)
- **[2017-02-23]** An implied contract can exist (e.g. accepting terms by sending a
  retainer check), but a signature is far safer; he tells people to print, sign, scan,
  and email back rather than wait for a wet signature. (guest legal opinion — context)
- **[2017-02-23]** Without a contract you can recover filing fees but generally NOT
  attorney's fees — unless the contract has a clause that the prevailing party in a
  dispute is entitled to reasonable attorney's fees. (guest legal opinion — context)
- **[2017-02-23]** Having an attorney draft a contract is a modest investment — roughly
  two to three hours of attorney time; Southern California rates cited as ~$150–200/hr up
  to ~$800/hr, so roughly $700–$1,500 for a contract. (guest estimate — context)
- **[2017-02-23]** Work-for-hire defined: copyright is owned by someone other than the
  author in only two ways — (1) the author assigns it in a signed written agreement, or
  (2) it's created within an employer/employee relationship or under a signed
  work-for-hire agreement (owning all results/proceeds in perpetuity, all formats,
  throughout "the universe," now known or hereafter devised). (guest legal explanation —
  context)
- **[2017-02-23]** NDAs are common boilerplate (agree not to disclose shared business
  ideas); before signing, ask what you might lose and check the terms/jurisdiction. Notes
  creatives struggle with NDAs because they like talking about their work. (context)
- **[2017-02-23]** Copyright protects the **expression** of ideas, not ideas themselves
  ("ideas float in the wind"). Infringement analysis has two prongs: access to the work,
  and substantial similarity (the latter often "in the eye of... juries"). (context)
- **[2017-02-23]** You can register a copyright at copyright.gov for $35, uploading the
  image digitally; registration gives added protections if you must sue. (guest legal
  info — context; may be dated)
- **[2017-02-23]** On sourcing images: reputable archives (e.g. Getty, Bettmann, stock
  services) can **indemnify** you; a sketchy source (e.g. a "$5 Russian" archive) leaves
  you with no recourse if the rights turn out invalid. Tools like TinEye (tineye.com,
  Canada) fingerprint images to find copies — which also lets rights-holders find
  infringers. Giving an infringing image to a client exposes the client too. (context)
