# Legally Speaking | Copyright & Trademark Infringement Attorney Stuart Carroll PT 2

**Part 2 of 2.** Continues [[wiki/sources/2017-02-23-yt-LOeJ3be9vJE]] (Part 1).

## Summary

The second half of a live Q&A between Chris Do (host, The Futur / Blind) and
intellectual-property attorney **Stuart Carroll**. Carroll fields legal questions
from a live audience of designers/entrepreneurs and from the internet, covering:
suing infringers who stole a portfolio, collecting on judgments (seizing bank
accounts, domain names, artwork), NDAs and employee/contractor portfolio-use
agreements, enforcing contracts against overseas subcontractors, fair use and
derivative works (the Shepard Fairey / Obama "Hope" poster case), brand/logo
appearances in film ("product placement" vs. blurring labels), copyright in an
original painting vs. its physical canvas, making agreements enforceable ("meeting
of the minds"), and registering software copyright.

Attribution note: **Stuart Carroll's legal statements are CONTEXT** — expert legal
commentary from a guest attorney, not the subject's own frameworks, and not legal
advice attributable to Chris Do. **Only Chris Do's questions, framing, and
business-philosophy asides train the persona.** Chris's recurring theme across the
episode is a business-realist reframe: the legal system is not practically built for
small creatives, so protect yourself up front (contracts, money up front, integrity)
rather than relying on litigation.

## Key claims

### Stuart Carroll (guest attorney — CONTEXT, not persona; not legal advice)
- You can sue portfolio thieves under trademark + copyright claims in federal court
  (e.g., sue in Los Angeles, serve in Michigan); even a non-responsive defendant can
  yield a default judgment that stops them. (paraphrase, lines 45–64)
- A federal filing fee was ~$450 at the time; drafting a complaint to show "this is
  your work, this is what they stole" is relatively easy. (paraphrase, lines 45–49)
- A judgment lets you seize assets: find an asset (e.g., a bank account), get a court
  order, serve it on the bank, collect. This difficulty is why cases settle.
  (paraphrase, lines 79–89)
- Collection is the hard part and depends on the defendant; sketchier people are hard
  to find. Leverage points that work: domain names and copyrightable artwork the
  defendant is attached to. (paraphrase, lines 94–125)
- Employees can be bound by an employee handbook / policies (often signed day one)
  including confidentiality; independent contractors need a work-for-hire or
  rights-assignment agreement plus NDA and a portfolio-use provision. (paraphrase,
  lines 182–226)
- Portfolio use is an ongoing negotiation; clients often require written permission in
  advance, or credit/watermark ("bug") conditions. (paraphrase, lines 223–258)
- Enforcing contracts against overseas subcontractors: a contract is only as good as
  you can enforce it; you generally must sue where they are. Canada, EU/UK generally
  enforceable; some countries won't respect a US judgment. China won't respect US
  judgments, but is enforceable if the contract specifies the dispute be litigated in
  China under PRC law. (paraphrase, lines 321–360)
- **Fair use / derivative works:** there is NO magic percentage you can change to reuse
  a work. Under 17 U.S. Code § 106, the exclusive right to create derivative works
  belongs to the copyright owner. (paraphrase, lines 415–423)
- The Shepard Fairey "Hope" poster (derived from an Associated Press photo) — Fairey
  filed first to pick venue (New York vs. the AP photographer in Pennsylvania); it was
  ultimately determined he created an unlicensed derivative work and needed a license.
  Carroll says "He lost." (paraphrase, lines 434–470)
- Practical test for any reuse (parody, existing work, brand): "Are you going to upset
  someone enough that they're going to do something about it?" Getting sued is losing,
  because most creatives can't afford a lawsuit regardless of who would win.
  (paraphrase, lines 481–499)
- Brand/logo appearing incidentally in a film isn't necessarily illegal but is risky —
  "anyone can sue anyone for anything at any time." Labels get blurred/genericized
  largely to avoid conflicts with paying advertisers or brand-tarnishment/association
  risk. Paid product placement must be disclosed (FCC regulations). (paraphrase, lines
  549–597)
- **Documentary fair use:** for the film *Room 237* (on Kubrick's *The Shining*), clips
  from the critiqued films were free under fair use (you can't critique a film without
  showing it); ironically the non-Kubrick generic audience/theater shots had to be
  licensed. (This is Chris's story; Carroll's principle underlies it — lines 514–535.)
- **Copyright vs. physical object:** buying an original painting gives you the canvas,
  not the copyright — "The copyright subsists separate from the physical manifestation
  of the work." A studio that recreated a foreclosure-sold student painting for a film
  set settled: paid a license fee and returned the painting. (paraphrase, lines
  656–718)
- **Enforceability of agreements:** an agreement is legally binding even if oral;
  enforcement is the hard part. Get intent in writing ("meeting of the minds"); to
  guarantee a minimum number of booked days you must state it explicitly. (paraphrase,
  lines 736–780)
- **Software copyright:** code is a "literary work," registered like a book; the
  Copyright Office lets you deposit a segment (e.g., first/last ~50 lines) to keep
  source proprietary. You own the copyright as soon as you hit save. Code is NOT a
  work-for-hire category, so a buyer needs an explicit assignment. May need to register
  both the underlying software and the GUI/look-and-feel. Registration adds leverage
  (attorney's fees, punitive damages). (paraphrase, lines 868–922)

### Chris Do (host — persona-eligible framing/opinions)
- Business-realist reframe on the legal system: it effectively works for large
  companies (Samsung/Apple sue each other routinely), not for small creatives chasing
  small-time infringers with no money. Suing costs six months and yields no money.
  (paraphrase, lines 128–154)
- Recommends a cease-and-desist to try to make infringers stop, while being mindful of
  how much you'll spend "to try to get no money." (paraphrase, lines 148–154)
- Opinion on employees "walking away with the company's portfolio" (common in motion
  design): "I don't think it's right. I don't think it's fair." At Blind, employees
  sign contracts with non-compete/work-for-hire terms; people may show work case-by-
  case following procedures. Taking Blind's work onto ANOTHER company's portfolio is
  what enrages him and triggers litigation. (paraphrase, lines 265–311)
- Business lesson repeatedly stated: get your money up front, protect yourself, use
  incremental payments, build in a "fudge factor" because there's inherent risk in
  business and in hiring subcontractors — that's why you charge more. (paraphrase,
  lines 376–393)
- "Getting sued is losing" reframe applied to his own creative ideas — Stuart's risk
  questions kill many of Chris's ideas. (lines 500–512)
- Closing moral: integrity applies even when you're "small time" — respect others'
  work when small if you want your work respected when you're big. (paraphrase, lines
  1013–1021)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do (persona-eligible):**

> "The legal system works for a certain size company and a group of people. It's not
> like for you and me... Samsung sues Apple. Apple sues Samsung... But you sue a guy
> who's like tiny in podunk USA. He's got no money. And you spent six months chasing
> this money. It's not going to come to you." (Chris Do, lines 129–144)

> "So the lesson here, and it's we've heard this in many different ways, is get your
> money up front, you guys. Protect yourself." (Chris Do, lines 382–385)

> "It's a very common practice especially in the motion design industry for you to go
> work somewhere, get a nice salary... and then when you feel like you've had enough to
> walk away with that company's portfolio... I don't think it's right. I don't think
> it's fair." (Chris Do, lines 269–277)

> "There's risk inherent in doing business. When you hire a subcontractor, sometimes
> they do the work for you, sometimes they don't. That's why you got to charge more
> because you assume certain risk." (Chris Do, lines 376–382)

> "If you want your work respected when you get bigger, then respect others even when
> you're a small fry." (Chris Do, lines 1018–1021)

> "Let's start acting like real business people and conduct ourselves in a bizlike
> manner. This is the part that you need to learn. If you want to be an entrepreneur,
> if you want to be successful, this is what you got to do." (Chris Do, lines 963–969)

**Stuart Carroll (guest attorney — CONTEXT ONLY, do NOT train persona):**

> "You can't take someone else's work and create a new work from it. That is called
> creating a derivative work. And under 17 US code section 106, the exclusive right to
> create derivative works lies in the author, the owner of the copyright."
> (Stuart Carroll, lines 415–421)

> "Getting sued is losing. And so the question... is 'Can I do this?' And I'll say
> 'Will they be upset?'" (Stuart Carroll, lines 493–497)

> "Anyone can sue anyone for anything at any time. It's just a matter of whether
> they're upset enough to find the resources to do so." (Stuart Carroll, lines 550–554)

> "They own that canvas, but they don't own the right to reproduce it." (Stuart
> Carroll, lines 710–711)

> "An agreement is legally binding. It doesn't even have to be on paper. It could be
> oral. Enforcing the agreement is the hard part." (Stuart Carroll, lines 736–738)

> "Code is considered a literary work. It's the same copyright registration as a book...
> You own the copyright in it as soon as you hit save." (Stuart Carroll, lines 868–884)
