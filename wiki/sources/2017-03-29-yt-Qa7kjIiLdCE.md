# Design Legends — Kyle Cooper, Main Title Designer PT 1 (Braveheart…)

## Summary

Part 1 of a live "Design Legends" stage interview on The Futur, hosted by **Chris Do**
("The Process"), with guest **Kyle Cooper** — the legendary film main-title designer
(Se7en, and work at R/Greenberg Associates and his companies Imaginary Forces and
Prologue). Kyle is a documented influence/reference for Chris and appears in Chris's
biography; he also gave Chris some of his earliest motion-design work.

Kyle does most of the talking, recounting his craft and career: why film titles must be
"appropriate to the movie" rather than trendy so they age well; studying under **Paul
Rand** (plus **Armin Hofmann**, **Bradbury Thompson**, **Herbert Matter**, **Hugh
Dubberly**) at Yale; mentorship, riddle-based idea-making, contrast, and "make every
frame beautiful"; immersive research over "bag of tricks" reuse; and stories about Paul
Rand (Ford/NeXT logos, Steve Jobs) and being yelled at by Steve Jobs over a Pixar/Apple
sequence. Chris also tells shared-history stories about Kyle hiring him for his first
title-design gigs (Island of Dr. Moreau, Celtic Pride, Se7en connection).

**Attribution note:** Kyle Cooper's life, craft, and anecdotes are **CONTEXT, not
persona** — they must NOT enter Chris Do's biography. Kyle also frequently quotes his own
mentors (Paul Rand, Armin Hofmann), so those design maxims are third-hand context. The
persona value here is only in **Chris Do's questions, framing, and business commentary**
(see below). PT1 — the interview continues in a Part 2.

## Key claims

All dated 2017-03-29, PARAPHRASED unless a verbatim quote is given below. Attribution
per speaker.

**Kyle Cooper (CONTEXT — NOT persona; not Chris's biography):**

- Has worked on titles since 1988; the montage of his work spans pieces up to ~20 years
  old, so "it's not current."
- Film titles should be "appropriate to the movie," not fashionable/trendy; film is more
  forgiving than a 30-second commercial because a film title lives "forever," so chasing
  the trend of the moment risks it looking dated later.
- Cites mentors: studied with **Paul Rand** at Yale (drove to Rand's Connecticut house,
  shoveled his driveway as the "tutorial"); also learned from **Armin Hofmann**,
  **Bradbury Thompson**, **Herbert Matter**, **Hugh Dubberly**, and studied lineage from
  **Lou Danziger**.
- A mentor's key gift: helping you understand what you're good at, sometimes better than
  you know yourself (Dubberly told him he was "good at ideas").
- Paul Rand maxims Kyle relays (third-hand context): "an idea is only as good as its
  execution"; "form can be an idea"; emphasis on **contrast** in composition; a good
  design should read like a **riddle** — "a + b doesn't equal a, it equals a new idea" —
  which, per Gestalt psychology, involves the viewer and is more memorable.
- His own working principle: "make every frame, one frame at a time, beautiful."
- Believes in immersive **research** (read the script, talk to people) over "going online
  and pulling reference and cobbling together Photoshop things," and warns against
  applying the same "stylistic bag of tricks" to every job.
- Paul Rand charged flat fees (~$100k) and, if the client rejected the logo, would not
  redo it — he made a presentation book arguing his case (Ford, NeXT for Steve Jobs) and
  walked away; Armin Hofmann's "telephone designer" put-down = an art director who
  "phones it in" instead of working the form by hand.
- Steve Jobs story (context): Kyle presented a trendy Apple/Pixar sequence briefed by
  middle management; Jobs was upset ("who told you to do this"), swore, but did not throw
  anything; Kyle just "took notes" and did not argue.
- Enjoys paid consulting where he's flown in "just to tell them what's wrong" with others'
  work (poster books, troubled title jobs) — compares it to Harvey Keitel's "The Wolf"
  from Pulp Fiction.

**Chris Do (PERSONA — his framing and business teaching):**

- Frames Kyle and the named Giants (Rand, Hofmann, etc.) as designers who could "command
  the money… with the kind of respect" because they had **direct relationships with the
  CEO/executives**; today, with "a thousand middlemen… everybody covering their butt,"
  designers drift into being **order takers** the further they are from the decision maker.
- Business concept **"going past the sale"**: once the buyer says yes / "enough," stop
  selling — continuing to over-explain can only lose the deal. Illustrated with Kyle
  cutting his portfolio review off at "1, 2, 3, enough" and still giving him the job the
  next day; analogized to an argument where the other person already apologized but you
  keep pushing until they take it back.
- **Go all-in on your strengths**: a great teacher tells you what your strengths are;
  don't try to do everything (logo + Photoshop + animation) — focus where you're great
  (Kyle = ideas), which builds a long career where "people now just call you for your
  opinion and your ideas."
- Reads Paul Rand's presentation books (Ford/NeXT) as **storytelling** — a sequenced flip
  through images "almost like an animation" that arrives at the solution so the last page
  feels like "there's nothing else to talk about."
- Encourages the audience to look up the named design Giants; positions the interview as
  giving younger designers historical **context**.
