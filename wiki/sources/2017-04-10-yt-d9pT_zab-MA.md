# Design Legends — Kyle Cooper Main Title Designer PT 2 (Braveheart, Se7en, Dawn of the Dead)

Part 2 of a live "Design Legends" on-stage conversation between **Chris Do** (host,
The Futur) and guest **Kyle Cooper**, the main-title designer (Se7en, Braveheart,
Spider-Man, Argo, etc.). Continues [[wiki/sources/2017-03-29-yt-Qa7kjIiLdCE]] (PT 1).

> **CONTEXT ONLY** and must NOT enter Chris Do's biography/persona. Only **Chris Do's**
> own framing, coaching principles, and business philosophy are persona-eligible, and

## Summary

Chris explicitly steers PT 2 away from design craft toward the "business and personal"
side of Kyle's story. Kyle recounts why he left RGA to co-found Imaginary Forces
(Bob Greenberg thought main titles were a weak business model and wanted Kyle to build
an internet/digital studio in SF; Kyle wanted to stay in LA doing titles), and why
Imaginary Forces ultimately didn't work for him: bad "job mix," the strain of holding
world-class creatives together, and his own refusal to compromise on the work. He
reflects on fear-based choices, regrets about not pursuing directing, negative
self-talk ("the Dark Passenger"), his faith ("God gave me an idea when I needed one"),
and his creative process (breaking an edit into one component at a time, "listening to
the work," letting the problem tell you what it wants to be).

Chris interleaves his own commentary throughout: he reframes Kyle as "an artist before
he's a business person," contrasts their differing philosophies on running a studio,
delivers his own coaching maxims, and gives his own single hiring criterion
(typography). Interwoven is a personal anecdote: years earlier Kyle tried to buy out /
absorb Chris's young company (Blind) into Imaginary Forces, and Chris jokingly priced
himself at "a million dollars," ending the deal.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (host) — PERSONA-ELIGIBLE

- **Framing of the interview**: deliberately shifts away from "design mentoring" to
  "business personal related questions" because Kyle's process has been covered
  extensively elsewhere. (paraphrase, lines 27–39)
- **Coaching maxim — look for the one that works**: says he coaches people, and that
  most people waste energy scanning the "99 [options] that will not work" instead of
  hunting for the one that will; claims this is core to who he is and how he focuses.
  (near-verbatim below, lines 1144–1153)
- **Gain vs. loss framing**: focusing on what you have to gain, rather than what you
  have to lose, changes the whole outlook. (paraphrase, lines 1141–1144)
- **Single hiring criterion = typography**: his answer to "who gets hired" is simpler
  than Kyle's — he looks for command over typography (interesting, timeless, a sense of
  good type), and hires on that alone. (near-verbatim below, lines 626–635; self-dated
  by Chris to "2016 … November")
- **The creative's inner battle**: the real battle for creatives is internal —
  judgment, acceptance, ego, fear — and the same traits that make us creative amplify
  the negative voices. (paraphrase, lines 1129–1138)
- **Interpretation of "listening to the work"**: reframes Kyle's process as "when
  you're ready the concept will appear" (echoing "when the student is ready the teacher
  will appear"); requires leaving out ego, award-chasing, impressing/destroying others.
  (paraphrase, lines 1200–1216)
- **Differing studio philosophy**: openly notes this is "where you and I may differ" —
  Chris signals he runs companies / handles clients differently than Kyle's
  never-compromise, go-over-budget stance. (paraphrase, lines 424–428)

### Chris Do — biographical (persona-eligible, about Chris)

- As a young designer Chris was **freelancing for Kyle**, never staffed at Imaginary
  Forces; his own company **Blind** was then "just about a year old." (paraphrase,
  lines 84–94) [registry-unverified; self-reported by Chris on stage]
- Kyle tried to convince Chris to close his shop and merge into Imaginary Forces,
  framing it as "a big part of a small thing or small part of a big thing." Chris,
  after talking it over with his brother, named a buyout price of **"a million
  dollars"**; Kyle laughed, ending the deal. (paraphrase, lines 76–101)
- Chris once asked Kyle (privately) **"why do you need partners? … just do it on your
  own"** — reflecting his younger self's aversion to business partners. (paraphrase,
  lines 143–150)

### Kyle Cooper (guest) — CONTEXT ONLY (do NOT use for Chris's persona)

- Left RGA / co-founded Imaginary Forces (~1996–97) because Bob Greenberg saw main
  titles as a weak business model and wanted Kyle to start an internet/digital studio
  in San Francisco; Kyle wanted to stay in LA and do titles. Greenberg owned a stake in
  Imaginary Forces and was bought out in a "gracious transition." (context, lines 40–69)
- Why Imaginary Forces didn't work for him: poor "job mix," the difficulty of keeping
  world-class creatives together, and his refusal to compromise on quality. (context,
  lines 261–304)
- Regret: passed up directorial opportunities to be a "company man." Advice to young
  people: don't have a fallback / plan B; be specific about what you want. (context,
  lines 166–235)
- Client philosophy: evaluate the person/relationship, not the brand or high-profile
  logo; repeat business comes from people who had a good experience, "good design is
  good will" (attributed by Kyle to Paul Rand). (context, lines 293–355)
- Kept working after leaving because clients called him directly — Laura Ziskin
  (Spider-Man, Oscars — Kyle credits her for the Marvel logo and 13 Oscars gigs), ESPN,
  Joel Silver, John Frankenheimer. (context, lines 359–396)
- Process: break an edit into one component at a time (kerning, then color, then …);
  "take the next indicated action"; a meditative focus on one thing amid noise.
  Inspired by movies (Hacksaw Ridge, Manchester by the Sea), not motion-graphics
  reference. Cites Chuck Close ("inspiration is for amateurs") and Sergei Eisenstein
  ("immutable fragment of actual reality"). (context, lines 637–740)
- "Listening to the work": stare at the problem/script and let it tell you what it
  wants to be; look for serendipitous accidents; on his one directed film he taped his
  glasses and wore headphones to block interruptions. (context, lines 900–1054)
- Faith: attributes ideas to God (Argo storyboard epiphany, Spider-Man "type caught in
  webs like flies," Island of Dr. Moreau pitch); "God has pulled me out of some jams."
  Name "Imaginary Forces" comes from Shakespeare's Henry V ("spotted soldiers").
  (context, lines 515–534, 788–884, 1290–1331)
- Negative self-talk / "the Dark Passenger"; the name "Prologue" is Kyle's later
  company (cpr.com); has done 350+ main titles. (context, lines 416, 1091–1113)
