## Summary

A live classroom Q&A (recorded at CSUSB / "Cal State San Bernardino," with graphic
design instructor Dave Moon of CSU Northridge present) in which Chris Do fields a
student's question: what do employers look for in a recent design graduate? Chris uses
Socratic questioning to lead the room to two answers — **talent** and **passion** — then
drills into how each is demonstrated in a portfolio. Talent is shown through the ability
to execute designs; passion is shown through going beyond the assignment, focused range,
and evident quality/devotion. He argues you cannot fake passion at a student level, that
you should never show work you don't love doing, and that a strong portfolio needs
relatively few, genuinely great, job-relevant pieces. He closes with his own origin
story of landing his first advertising job by sending only three or four pieces, and a
reflection on the intense peer competition that drove him at Art Center.

## Key claims

_All dated 2017-04-22. Paraphrased unless quoted below. Attributed per speaker._

**Chris Do:**
- Employers look for **talent** and **passion** in a recent graduate. (2017-04-22)
- Talent is demonstrated through the portfolio — specifically the ability to execute
  designs and art. Passion is demonstrated separately and is harder to see in a
  static portfolio. (2017-04-22)
- Portfolios today (2017) live on a website, not a physical book. (2017-04-22)
- There is no fixed number of pieces; what matters is showing only your strongest work.
  A reasonable minimum is around **eight** pieces — but only ones you are *really* proud
  of (he stresses "really," not just "kind of"). (2017-04-22)
- A somewhat objective test for whether work is portfolio-worthy: show it to someone
  without telling them it's yours, and see whether it reads as **professional**. (2017-04-22)
- **Never show work you don't love doing** — a viewer can spot it "a mile away."
  If you're not passionate about something, the work won't be good, and students aren't
  skilled enough yet to fake it. (2017-04-22)
- Showing weak work you dislike (e.g. editorial/layout you're not passionate about) only
  invites more of that kind of work; it's illogical to include it just to "show range." (2017-04-22)
- Passion is shown by **focusing and going deep** — pick what you love (e.g. movie
  posters) and show range *within* it (sci-fi, tentpole, documentary, romcom). A focused
  portfolio raises your chances of working where you want to work. (2017-04-22)
- Passion is also shown by **going beyond the parameters of the assignment** — when three
  comps are requested, the passionate student brings far more; it's evident and can't be
  faked. (2017-04-22)
- Word choice / precision matters: to solve a hard problem, break it into small pieces
  (analogy: a recipe is a complex dish broken into finite steps). (2017-04-22)
- Chris landed his first professional (advertising) job by mailing only **three or four
  pieces** in a FedEx box — not all the work he had, only what was relevant to that job —
  and got the offer days later. (2017-04-22, self-reported)
- **Three establishes a pattern** (cites editor Steve [O'?]: "one won't, two might, three
  will"); like an IQ test showing three items to infer the fourth. Show as few relevant,
  really-good pieces as possible. (2017-04-22)
- Your portfolio quality was effectively decided long before you apply — the work you did
  as a sophomore/junior/senior already determined whether it would be good. (2017-04-22)
- Personal reflection: at Art Center (~two decades earlier) intense peer competition drove
  him; he wanted to out-work and "embarrass" rivals through volume of work, ideas, and
  thoroughness of exploration. (2017-04-22, self-reported)
- Very young creators (13–16) are already better at some skills (e.g. hand lettering) than
  many in the room; a 16-year-old freelancing for 3 years is believable once he sees their
  work. (2017-04-22)

**Dave Moon (instructor, CSU Northridge) — context, not persona data:**
- Teaching mantra: "do more than what is expected of you"; most students here have gone
  beyond expectations since the start of the semester. Going beyond (10 thumbnails when 5
  are asked) is how you differentiate and signals the passion employers want. (2017-04-22)

**Students (attribution varies) — context:**
- (uncertain student) A piece is portfolio-worthy if it "passes off as professional" when
  shown to someone blind to its authorship. (2017-04-22)
- (student "Dominic") Passion projects = things you have a personal interest in (e.g.
  movie posters). (2017-04-22)
- (uncertain student) Passion shows in the quality of the work — passionate people make
  time for it and it shows; Chris reframes this as "quality and devotion." (2017-04-22)
- (student "Christina" + "Alisa") "Take it outside" = go beyond being a confined student:
  explore beyond class, use resources like Lynda.com, and push a project beyond its stated
  parameters. (2017-04-22)
