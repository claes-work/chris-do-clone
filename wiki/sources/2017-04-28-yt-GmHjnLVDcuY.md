## Summary

A live classroom Q&A (same CSU-series session with instructor **Dave Moon** of CSU
Northridge present; students from Cal State Northridge, including "Zachary/Zach" and
"Sam"). A student opens by asking about the work culture at Chris Do's studio Blind;
Chris pivots the discussion into a larger argument that **character, brand, and culture
are the same thing under different words** ("did you do what you say you're going to
do"), then into **how designers should design for business goals rather than aesthetics**,
and finally into **client empathy**.

The core teaching runs in three linked moves. First, Chris makes the creative process
**visible and repeatable** — a process is "a repeatable formula that has predictable
results" — and insists designers start not with a competitive audit but with the
**business goals** behind a logo request. He names three generic, transferable goals:
**increase revenue, repositioning, and building awareness**. Second, he layers on
**competitive audits** (vertical + horizontal analysis) and **customer feedback** (trolling
forums, Yelp, Facebook) as the real inputs, citing Douglas Davis's *Creative Strategy and
the Business of Design* and Tony Hsieh's *Delivering Happiness*. Third, he defines
**empathy as acting as a fiduciary for the client** — willing to do what's best for the
client even when it's bad for you — illustrated by the "Sam the logo designer" parable
(refer the client away, lose the job, gain a customer for life). He closes on the
"disarming" sales posture of standing **shoulder-to-shoulder** rather than face-to-face,
the anti-pattern of discounting, referral finder's fees / reciprocity, and a final riff
on **designers' guilt about charging for work they enjoy**.

## Key claims

_All dated 2017-04-28. Paraphrased unless quoted below. Attributed per speaker._

**Chris Do (persona data):**
- **Character = brand = culture** — the same idea in different (old-school / overused /
  misapplied) words. Character is simply "did you do what you say you're going to do."
  (2017-04-28)
- Fulfilling a brand promise reduces to two simple things: **do what you say you're going
  to do, and say what you're going to do** — and most people/firms do this poorly. (2017-04-28)
- The creative process is too **opaque** to clients ("the logo is here, that's the ta-da");
  the job is to make it **visible and transparent** by breaking the work into finite,
  concrete steps. (2017-04-28)
- **Definition: a process is "a repeatable formula that has predictable results."** Clients
  paying large sums shouldn't feel like they're gambling in Las Vegas. (2017-04-28)
- A designer is a **problem-solver, not an artist** — "I solve people's problems and they
  pay me good amounts of money"; being unpassionate about the client's category (e.g. a
  bank) is irrelevant. He is "passionate about solving my client's problems, not fulfilling
  my own personal need." (2017-04-28)
- Before the competitive audit, **start with the business goals** — why does the client
  want a new logo at all (e.g. United-style bad brand association, change of ownership,
  refresh)? A mere "refresh" is not a business goal. (2017-04-28)
- **The three generic business goals** (transferable across any business): **increase
  revenue**, **repositioning** (new look → new/different target market), and **building
  awareness**. They are interconnected and form a repeatable formula. (2017-04-28)
- Design schools rarely teach business goals; if a designer isn't *saying* these things,
  it's evidence they aren't *thinking* them — and "what chance do you have of doing it?"
  (2017-04-28)
- **Competitive audit has two axes: vertical analysis** (direct competitors — other banks)
  and **horizontal analysis** (unrelated industries — Apple, Google, Uber, Airbnb) to draw
  transferable ideas. (2017-04-28)
- Cites Douglas Davis, *Creative Strategy and the Business of Design*: **"Aesthetics are
  not a compelling reason for a business to change their operation,"** so the designer must
  **learn the language of business** to justify the work. "It's cool, it's what the kids
  like" won't justify $100M. (2017-04-28)
- Gather **real customer feedback** — troll forums, read Yelp/Facebook reviews "when people
  don't think they're listening," good and bad — because what a company thinks it's saying
  and what customers experience are often "a whole different story." (2017-04-28)
- Cites Tony Hsieh (Zappos founder), *Delivering Happiness*: **"Forget brand — get the
  culture right and the brand will follow."** You cannot have a toxic culture and a positive
  brand. (2017-04-28)
- **"We trust people who believe what we believe."** (2017-04-28)
- **Empathy = "I feel what you feel, I want what you want."** (2017-04-28)
- **"Designers are terrible at empathy"** because many are **self-centered** — every client
  gets "the treatment" (my fonts, my colors, my idea of luxury). "We're not Picasso... we're
  here to solve a problem." (2017-04-28)
- Empathy can be trained; Chris runs a class exercise having students role-play an
  impossible character (e.g. a 1,000-year-old being from outer space) — students "fail
  pretty hard because all their answers sound just like them." (2017-04-28)
- **The true test of empathy: are you willing to do what's best for the client even when
  it's not good for you?** That means **acting as a fiduciary** — in the client's best
  interest, not your own. (2017-04-28)
- **"Sam the logo designer" parable:** the world's best logo designer discovers, through his
  process, that the client doesn't need a logo but needs new customer-service agents, a new
  website, and campaign literature — so he refers them away. He "may have lost a job but he
  may have gained a customer for life." That is empathy / being in service of the client.
  (2017-04-28)
- **Sales strategy — tell potential customers "you don't need me, you don't want this, you
  can't afford it."** It's disarming; it moves both parties from **face-to-face
  (adversarial)** to **shoulder-to-shoulder / side-by-side collaborators** looking at the
  problem together. Chris says he uses this himself. (2017-04-28)
- **Detour analogy (pickup-artist book):** the worst approach is head-on confrontation (or
  a tap on the back — "even creepier"); the best is **approaching from the side**, no eye
  contact, signaling "I'm not a threat." Same as how you collaborate without being too
  aggressive. (2017-04-28)
- **Discounting is a losing proposition:** an ad saying "call now, 50% off your first logo"
  reads as "super desperate" and signals your prices were too high. In retail, 50%-off
  sales **train customers never to pay full price**; post-recession retailers, having only
  the discounting tool left, are in a losing position (e.g. the Gap perpetually 30–50% off).
  (2017-04-28)
- **Referrals should be paid — reciprocity.** A referred, budget-qualified client is a **hot
  lead** (vs. a cold lead). Sales reps work on commission (roughly **5–18%**, some 2–5%);
  designers should build finder's fees into budgets and pay for leads — "that's how you
  guarantee you get more leads." Chris admits he mostly hands off work for goodwill and
  "would probably have a lot more money" if he charged every time. (2017-04-28)
- **Designers carry guilt about charging** for work they'd do for free / derive pleasure
  from. Kobe Bryant, LeBron James, Jack Nicklaus, Serena Williams, poker players feel no
  guilt earning millions doing what they love — designers shouldn't either. (2017-04-28)
- Chris gets attacked online ("you're just about the money") but frames his mission as a
  **"random act of kindness"**: empowering creatives to adopt a mindset where they're paid
  what they're worth — "there's a lot of unlearning that must be done." (2017-04-28)
- Self-reference: The Futur/Blind had made **"probably 160, 170 videos"** by this point;
  the show "is called The Process." (2017-04-28, self-reported)
