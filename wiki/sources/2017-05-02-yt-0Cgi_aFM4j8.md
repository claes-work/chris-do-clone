# Is strategy for creatives who can't design? Is there too much hype around strategy? Pt. 1/3

★ L3-candidate — Chris Do lays out a distinct, substantial thesis that strategy is
inseparable from design ("we can't extract, we can't remove it, otherwise we just create
shiny objects") and reframes design as consultative discovery over the craft-first "big
reveal." Directly relevant to Blind's brand-strategy positioning and the design-as-business
thesis; the debate structure surfaces the counter-argument too.

## Summary

Part 1 of 3 of a recorded panel/debate. Chris Do (host, The Futur) sits down with **Mark
Posh** — a graphic designer who runs his own agency of 20+ years, same design-school
"pedigree" as Chris — because Mark sent an angry message to their Facebook PR group
complaining that "strategy thinking, as valuable as it is, is too domineering and for some
used as an excuse to enter the creative industry." Rather than argue online, Chris called
Mark and invited him to debate on camera. The framing question: is the current emphasis on
strategy in the creative field **hype**, or for some is it **hope**?

Chris opens by playing a video clip from **Jose Caballer** (founder of a school / The Skool,
prior Futur guest) defining strategy: strategy is a "bad word" in many companies, often
seen as boring meetings that yield no actionable results; designers/developers often say
"that's not my job." Jose's core point — strategy is simply **getting from point A to point
B**: a plan, framework, or road map, especially when there's no brief, multiple
stakeholders all wanting everything, or when *you* are the one who has to brief the team.
He notes strategic road maps usually fail because the people who execute (designers) are
never invited into the strategy room.

Mark then reacts: several of Jose's points ring true for him (juggling multiple
stakeholders, no common objective, and especially "nobody follows the brief anyway").
Mark's actual grievance is with **bloated, jargon-filled marketing briefs** — hundreds of
pages, often written by people who never executed a creative thing, sometimes just to
justify cost and headcount. He is skeptical of "strategy" and "design thinking" as
buzzwords/labels that get "kicked around."

Chris and Mark actually agree more than they disagree. Both hold that strategy is
intrinsic to design, that empathy for the end user is central, and that design must be
justified/sold to clients. The disagreement is more about **labels and hype** than
substance. Chris signals this is a debate where he's "just warming up" and predicts Mark
will "make my argument for me." A third participant, **Dan**, is present (Chris tells him
not to help him along). Chris closes Pt. 1 by contrasting the craft-driven "Tada!" big
reveal with the consultative "of course, of course" — a natural progression of shared
discoveries — setting up Pts. 2/3.

**Who's on it:** Chris Do (host — persona). Mark Posh (guest/debate opponent — context).
Jose Caballer (via video clip — context). Dan (in-room participant — context).

## Key claims

**Chris Do (persona):**
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) For Chris, strategy **supports** the design — not the other way
  around; strategy is intrinsic and cannot be removed from the design business, or you just
  "create shiny objects."
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) The **kinds of questions you ask control the whole
  conversation** — so what matters is what you're actually trying to get at with a client's
  intake/discovery questions, not just having a questionnaire.
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) A great designer / problem-solver has **empathy** — understands
  the end user's experience (is the interface intuitive, ergonomic, beautiful, clear).
  Chris argues many designers have *low* empathy and care only about their own portfolio.
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) In traditional design training the method was "create 100 ideas,
  cut the worst 75, refine the best 25." Chris frames **Discovery / core** as a smarter,
  more accurate methodology that reaches the solution in a "shorter distance."
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) Endorses Blair Enns' *Win Without Pitching Manifesto*: to be
  positioned as experts, designers must move away from the exciting "Tada!" reveal toward
  the anticlimactic "here it is → of course" — the outcome of a series of shared
  conversations and discoveries. Chris calls this a "radically, fundamentally different way
  of designing" from how he practiced for two decades.
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) The **justification reflex**: when a reveal lands badly,
  designers reach for "this is what your customers want" to defend the work — a pattern he's
  critical of. He notes he's tracking the words *justify, formula, questions* in the debate.
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) Says labels (strategy / discovery / design thinking / core) can
  misdirect the conversation — proposes setting the labels aside to talk about what the
  thing actually *is*.

**Mark Posh (context — NOT persona):**
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) Strategy thinking has become "too domineering" and is used by
  some as "an excuse to enter the creative industry."
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) Marketing briefs are often bloated (hundreds of pages), written
  by people who never executed creative work, sometimes just to justify cost/headcount;
  "nobody follows it anyway."
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) "Design thinking" is often drama/labeling created so people can
  charge extra — but he concedes design thinking has always been part of the process.
- (2017-05-02, paraphrase) Agrees strategy/questions are essential to define scope and
  objectives, and that work must serve a purpose and solve a real problem.

**Jose Caballer (context, via video clip — NOT persona):**
- (clip, paraphrase) Strategy = getting from point A to point B; a plan/framework/road map,
  needed most when there's no brief or multiple stakeholders wanting every feature.
- (clip, paraphrase) Strategic road maps fail because executors (designers) are excluded
  from the strategy sessions.
