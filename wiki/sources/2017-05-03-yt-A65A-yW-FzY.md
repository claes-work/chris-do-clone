# Is strategy HOPE for creatives who want to play a more important role with the Client? Pt. 2/3

★ L3-candidate — In Pt. 2 Chris Do adds substantial, distinct strategy material beyond
Pt. 1 ([[wiki/sources/2017-05-02-yt-0Cgi_aFM4j8]]): the **symptoms-vs-root-cause**
diagnostic frame, the **"common language of business"** (as opposed to the common language
within design), an **autonomy → creative control** argument drawn from Daniel Pink (FedEx
days, Google 20% time, ROWE), the **"design is the application of strategy… a byproduct,
not the product"** thesis, and a **design-as-commodity / supply-and-demand** economic
argument. New source authority introduced: Douglas Davis, *Creative Strategy and the
Business of Design*. Directly feeds Blind/Futur's design-as-business positioning and the
value-of-strategy debate; the live disagreement with the opponent surfaces the counter-case.

## Summary

Part 2 of 3 of the recorded panel/debate on the value of strategy in creative work. Chris
Do (host, The Futur) continues sparring with his debate opponent — the graphic designer of
20+ years, self-described as "practically German," identified in Pt. 1 as **Mark Posh** —
in front of a room of ~15 designers, several of whom (Liz Marie Maria, Kyle, Katie) step to
the mic. Chris opens by proposing they **drop the labels** ("strategy / discovery / design
thinking / core") and talk about what the thing actually *is*, because the labels
misdirect.

Chris's central new frame is **symptoms vs. root cause**: most designer intake questions
ask the client to self-diagnose and then treat surface symptoms (a "topical cream" for a
rash), when new frameworks let him instead surface the underlying **root cause** — a skill
he says came from neither a typography nor an identity-design course. He introduces a second
author, **Douglas Davis** (*Creative Strategy and the Business of Design*), quoting Davis's
professor **Margery Culter** — "subjectivity is not a compelling rationale for a business
decision" — and Davis himself: "I lost creative battles because I was ignorant of the larger
business or marketing considerations that informed aesthetics." This anchors his push to
change the narrative from font sizes and RGB/CMYK to a **business dialogue**.

He develops a **common language of business** point (distinct from the earlier "common
language within design," e.g. the "rock and roll" example a panelist raised): to be seated
"at the very top" and involved in business decisions, designers must speak the client's
business language. He then makes an **autonomy** argument via Daniel Pink: when designers
have no control over the outcome, creativity shuts down; autonomy is a precondition for
solving creative problems. Examples: Atlassian's "FedEx days," Google's 20% time (source of
Gmail, Google News, etc.), and the Results-Only Work Environment (ROWE) — building toward
**autonomy, mastery, and purpose** as the new operating model. He also cites the Blair
Enns/Neumeier line **"design is the application of strategy… a byproduct of what we do, not
the product,"** warning designers it will "tear your soul apart."

The debate sharpens near the end. Mark argues the industry over-sells strategy and lets
**design fall off the wagon** ("$100,000 strategy and $100 for the design") — and that
**strategy is easier to sell than design**. Chris disagrees "100%" and challenges: if
strategy is so easy to sell, "go sell strategy and do a bait and switch." He closes on a
**design-as-commodity** economic argument: a global proliferation of highly skilled, cheap
designers means supply is up, demand is static, and price is down — pushing Mark on whether
design is a commodity, which Mark resists ("it's both / it's a spectrum"). Chris teases the
in-house designer Katie's constrained situation as a thread to pick up in Pt. 3.

**Who's on it:** Chris Do (host — persona). Mark Posh (debate opponent, the "practically
German" designer — context). Panelists Liz Marie Maria (brand strategist/copywriter at a
"strategic"-named agency), Kyle (SEO/UX/app design), Katie (in-house designer at a beauty
startup) — context. A participant Chris addresses as "Rob" is referenced re: the common-
language point (see attribution note).

## Key claims

**Chris Do (persona):**
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) **Drop the labels.** Whether it's called strategy, discovery,
  design thinking, or core, the labels misdirect the conversation from what the thing
  actually is.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) **Symptoms vs. root cause.** Typical designer questions ask the
  client to self-diagnose and describe symptoms, which the designer then treats
  superficially — like a topical cream for a rash actually caused by a food. New frameworks
  give Chris tools to diagnose and surface the **root cause** — a skill that came from no
  typography or identity-design course.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) Introduces **Douglas Davis**, *Creative Strategy and the Business
  of Design* (a designer who then got a business degree) as authority; quotes his professor
  Margery Culter that design subjectivity/aesthetics "is not a compelling rationale for a
  business decision."
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) Knowing the **target audience does not by itself dictate the
  design**; a 65+ vs. a 15–25 audience implies genuinely different UX, but the same brief
  handed to 10 people yields 10 results — lots of interpretation.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) **Change the narrative** from font sizes and RGB/CMYK (clients
  don't understand or care) to a **business dialogue** — purpose, audience, direction — which
  opens a different conversation and lets creativity be injected with purpose rather than as
  decoration.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) **Autonomy is a precondition for creativity.** When designers
  feel they have no control over a project's outcome, creativity shuts down (cites Daniel
  Pink on autonomy — attributed to *A Whole New Mind*, but the autonomy/mastery/purpose
  thesis is Pink's *Drive*; see attribution note). To solve creative problems people must be
  self-directed.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) Cites **Atlassian "FedEx days"** (24 hrs to build/deliver
  anything outside your normal job), **Google 20% time** (birthed ~half of new products in a
  year — Gmail, Google News), and the **ROWE** (Results-Only Work Environment) as evidence
  that radical autonomy raises productivity, engagement, and satisfaction and lowers
  turnover. **Autonomy, mastery, purpose** are the building blocks of a new way of working.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) To capture that value in client relationships, designers must be
  **positioned and prove their value** so clients want them involved in business decisions —
  "at the very top."
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) There are **two common languages**: the common language *within*
  design (distilling vague briefs like "rock and roll" or "bold and graphic" into specifics)
  and, more importantly, the **common language of business** — the one designers must learn
  to speak.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) Endorses the line (attributed to "Mr. Blair" / Blair Enns) that
  **"design is the application of strategy… a byproduct, not the product"** — an unsettling
  idea for craft-first graphic designers.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) **Disagrees 100%** that strategy is easier to sell than design;
  challenges that if strategy were so easy to sell, one would "go sell strategy and do a
  bait and switch" — a practice he dislikes.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) **Design has become a commodity**, driven partly by design
  education being widely/freely available online. Supply of skilled designers worldwide is
  up, demand is static, price is down (simple supply/demand). Presses that this is a
  spectrum but that the economics are real.

**Mark Posh (context — NOT persona):**
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) Clients don't wake up needing design; there is always an
  underlying business situation (competition, new product, bad reviews, a merger) that
  triggers a design change — but for him what ultimately sells is the **aesthetic**.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) Recounts a **soap-manufacturer** project ($1/unit, big-box
  retail, ~59% women, lower-income Midwest) where the winning move was an empowerment
  campaign that "doesn't even sell soap" — an example where **strategy drove the car and
  design was almost an afterthought**, which worries him.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) His grievance: the selling has become **too focused on strategy
  and not on the result/design** — "a $100,000 strategy and $100 for the design"; design
  falls off the wagon. The ideal is a **hybrid**.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) **Strategy is easier to sell than design** because business
  people understand strategy but not (good vs. bad) design; strategy provides the guidelines
  that let you sell design.
- (2017-05-03, paraphrase) Resists calling design a commodity: "it's both," a spectrum, "no
  black and white."

**Panelists (context — NOT persona):**
- Liz Marie Maria (2017-05-03, paraphrase): her agency runs an extensive brand-strategy
  process interviewing 6–12 stakeholders, seeking work that is differentiating, true to the
  brand, unique, and relevant; argues it takes **more** creativity to solve within all those
  constraints than with none — "design is a creative execution of the strategy that fits a
  business purpose."
- Katie (2017-05-03, paraphrase): in-house designer ~2 yrs out of school at a beauty
  startup; works under an art director, rarely presents her own ideas, wishes she were more
  involved in the conversations — the setup Chris uses to illustrate loss of creative
  control.
