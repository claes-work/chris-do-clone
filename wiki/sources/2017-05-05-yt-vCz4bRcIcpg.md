# Design Strategy: Hope or Hype — The conclusion 3/3

★ L3-candidate — Chris Do delivers a large amount of distinct, quotable persona
material here: the "blur rules of collaboration" (5 rules), the "three golden
questions" strategy framework, the origin story of how Blind/The Futur pivoted to

## Summary (Part 3/3 — conclusion)

Concluding installment of a live, audience-facing debate between **Chris Do** and
**Mark Posh** (a designer/friend framed as the "context" opponent) on whether design
strategy is "hope or hype." This is the resolution of the argument opened in Part 1
([[wiki/sources/2017-05-02-yt-0Cgi_aFM4j8]]) and Part 2
([[wiki/sources/2017-05-03-yt-A65A-yW-FzY]]). It belongs to the 2017 "The Process"
era of The Futur.

Chris's core thesis: much of *classical* design (typography, lettering, identity —
"the things you and I were taught in school") has become an undifferentiated
commodity, while **strategy** is what commands value and rescues the "squeezed
middle" of the design industry. He tells the personal origin story of how his own
studio (Blind), a two-decade motion-design shop, declined after the 2008 crash, tried
and failed at a $5,000 website project, and was then shown a **strategy framework** by
Jose Caballer that changed everything. He generalizes the framework by analogy to
therapy ("experts ask questions, amateurs give advice") and hands the audience two
concrete tools: the "ask why three times" technique and the "three golden questions."
He also lays out blur's five "rules of collaboration" and closes on the idea that all
of it centers on **the user — the customer's customer**.

Mark Posh (context) largely agrees by the end but pushes back on the framing: he
argues design was never separate from strategy ("design thinking is always part of the
process"), that design is still in high demand, and that strategy vs. creative is not
a zero-sum game. Chris concedes strategy and design are not separate concepts but
insists most practitioners fail to actually surface anything with their questionnaires.

Also touches the episode's secondary theme — **how to market creative services**:
differentiate on a clear, case-study-backed strategic process rather than on shiny
visuals ("bringing a knife to a gunfight"), and apply the same client-facing framework
to your own studio.

## Key claims (paraphrased unless quoted; dated 2017-05-05; per speaker)

**Chris Do (persona):**
- A large portion of *classical/traditional* design (typography, lettering, identity
  design) has become a commodity; it hurts him to admit it but he considers it true.
- IBM hiring ~1,000 designers is not evidence design-as-craft is thriving — they hired
  a *specific* kind: designers with **design thinking / strategic thinking**, not
  traditional classical designers.
- The industry has an "inverse bell curve": tiny 1–2-person garage studios do fine,
  giant 200–400-person firms do fine, but the **middle gets crushed** — "the decline
  of the middle class of the graphic designer." Blind was in that squeezed middle.
- His own studio (Blind), a motion-design company of 20+ years, declined after 2008;
  recovery never fully returned, so he had to choose between pining for the past or
  evolving. What they found was **strategy**, and the business turned back upward and
  is hiring designers again.
- A failed early $5,000 website project (he had no UX training, just re-skinned the
  client's existing site; it reverted months later) taught him design-without-strategy
  doesn't work. Jose Caballer then showed him a **structured question framework** — the
  "Trojan client" / "core kit" videos.
- The framework is "just a series of carefully designed questions." Its power is
  **self-discovery**: clients realize things about themselves/their users for the first
  time; that is "the magic" that marketers get paid a lot for.
- Analogy: a therapist is an expert because they have a framework to ask questions and
  surface the *root* problem, not the symptom — and let the client feel they reached
  the answer themselves. He spent ~10 sessions ("best $1,000 I spent") studying *how*
  the questions worked.
- "Educate" comes from a root meaning to **draw out**, not to put in — so educating a
  client isn't condescendingly lecturing them; it's asking and listening. He argues
  the whole education system is "busted" because it forgot this.
- Practical tool 1 — **ask "why" three times** (gently) to get past the prescribed
  request (e.g., "we want a new website") to the actual business problem (maybe the
  product, not the site, is the issue).
- Practical tool 2 — **the three golden questions**: (1) "What's your goal / what do
  you want to accomplish?" (2) "What's getting in the way of that?" (3) "Why do you
  believe that to be true?" Then just listen and take notes.
- **blur's five rules of collaboration**: (1) strategy before development; (2)
  continuous reference to said strategy (otherwise judgments become subjective — "make
  it cool/relevant/innovative" all drift); (3) freedom to execute (because it's already
  boxed in, no surprises); (4) provide **fewer options of higher quality** (not "100
  sketches"); (5) we present our own work (least important of the five).
- No "convincing" is involved: he collects payment for the strategy work done up front,
  offers referrals, and takes a no-strings-attached posture — which makes clients *want*
  to stay because you've "looked into the soul of their company."
- He walks in deliberately **under-prepared** (little to no reading, no profiling) so he
  carries no assumptions — over-prepared facilitators go on a "conclusion mission" and
  find only what they already assumed ("if you go in saying you are the killer, you'll
  walk away concluding you're the killer — not a good detective").
- Strategy vs. design is **not zero-sum**: strategy rising does not mean design
  declining; there can be winners in both. He *chooses* to be the strategic design
  company that couples business understanding with great design.
- Everything centers on **the user — and the user is the customer's customer**, not the
  direct client. Understanding the end user's pain point (e.g., a $1 bar of soap as an
  "affordable luxury" for someone barely scraping by) is where the magic is found.
- Marketing your creative services: differentiate on a clear, case-study-backed
  strategic process, not on shiny visuals; let "the Behancers and the Fivers" fight over
  the clients who don't value it. Apply the client framework to your own studio.
- Redefines design (crediting Marty Neumeier): "a process where you invent a creative
  solution to a problem" — and he defines the problem as a **business** problem, so he
  becomes "the guy who wrote the brief," not the last to receive it.

**Mark Posh (context):**
- Disagrees that design has broadly gone downhill; design is still in high demand at a
  certain level. Sees high-quality people suffering but not everything declining.
- "Design thinking is always part of the process" — he can't separate design and
  strategy; a serious designer always includes strategic thinking.
- Cites Paul Rand as proof designers were always strategists (Rand told Steve Jobs what
  the logo should *mean*, not just execute it); the designer's job is to educate/guide
  the client through the process.
- His process always starts with a **questionnaire** (discovery → definition → design
  development); ideally in person because chemistry/connection matters.
- A 20-question questionnaire also serves as a **client-vetting/sales tool** — sending it
  and having prospects work through it drastically increases the chance they're serious.
  (Chris sharply challenges this as reducing strategy to a mere sales filter.)
- Recounts a soap-brand project where the buyer (a Jaguar-driving company owner) was not
  the user; the winning campaign spoke to the actual user (women for whom a $1 soap is an
  affordable luxury). Chris credits this as Mark doing real strategy — genuine surprise
  and discovery, not just vetting.

**Panel/audience (context — Bryce, Spencer, Craig, Jose referenced):**
- Bryce (context): businesses know they need design but not why, so they'll pick the
  design company that also knows strategy so they "design the right thing." Concludes he'd
  pick the strategy company — Chris: "like bringing a knife to a gunfight."
