## Key claims

_All dated 2017-05-09. Paraphrased unless quoted below. Attributed per speaker._

### Yo Santosa — GUEST (context, NOT persona; NOT Chris's biography)

- Life philosophy: "make money doing what I love"; quit a big branding agency after a week when they said she'd need a business degree to touch strategy.
- Spent ~5 years (2000–2006) at Yu + Co. doing motion-graphics main titles (*300*, *Ugly Betty*); learned storytelling and capturing "attitude/emotion" as gut instinct in LA's film industry.
- Founded Ferroconcrete off a single unsolicited pitch to **Pinkberry** (a West Hollywood mom-and-pop yogurt shop looking to franchise); won it, gave two weeks' notice, was debt-free in two months. Frames it explicitly as luck.
- Branding ≠ logo/graphic design: brands are **people/personalities**, and the designer is a **matchmaker** who makes the customer "fall in love" with the brand across accumulated touchpoints (online → store → product).
- "It's not about me, it's about solving a problem… communication" — her formative shift from designing for herself (the black-and-white Art Center aesthetic, hating a mandated purple) to designing for the audience; after that shift she started winning pitches and became art director.
- Pinkberry grew from one store to ~200 worldwide in three years; what set Ferroconcrete apart was "strategy and storytelling — distilling a brand's essence to give it its personality."
- **TBS rebrand** case study: the logo's semicircle read as a half-smile; iterating from literal gags down to a distilled animated "smile" character ("the stud") that embodied a comedy network; won the pitch despite being a small shop with no recent motion work. Stresses the **brand handoff** — a great client (TBS) instills the brand across departments and evolves it.
- Business lessons: take fewer, bigger, better-paying clients rather than every job; qualify prospects by budget first; don't burn bridges when declining (refer others). Pricing "is an art form."
- **Fruit** (family cookie business): launched as a physical store during a bad economy at $3.50/tart with a one-day shelf life — a "big mistake" made from a "brand perspective, not business." The pivot to online-only (month-long shelf life, national shipping, no storefront staff) tripled revenue yearly; later carried by Dean & DeLuca and Williams-Sonoma.
- **Commodity** (fragrance, co-founded with employees Owen and Song): "the Warby Parker of perfume" (Allure); tested on Kickstarter (nearly tripled its goal), then learned investors demand numbers/business plans, not brand decks; hired a "CFO for a day"; declined an investor who wanted them to move to Silicon Valley and cede everything but the brand, because being "in the trenches" learning the business was the point.
- Closing advice to young designers: start your studio/brand while young and family-free when risk is cheaper; "don't be afraid to fail and try again"; surround yourself with driven people who share your vision — "a company… is just an empty structure. It's the people that makes it a company."
