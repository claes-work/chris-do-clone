## Summary

A short instructional segment published on The Futur (@thefutur) in which **Nils Lindstrom**,
a type and lettering professor at Art Center College of Design, explains how to correctly add
weight to a letterform. Using the letter "N" as an example, he contrasts the wrong way
(mechanically adding a stroke in Adobe Illustrator) with the way a lettering artist does it by
hand. Chris Do is credited in the video title as the host/producer for The Futur but is **not
present in the transcript** — the captured captions are Nils speaking throughout. This is a
guest craft demonstration; it is filed as a context page about the guest.

## Key claims

**Nils Lindstrom (guest instructor — CONTEXT, not persona):**
- Adding a stroke to a character in Illustrator to make it heavier also changes the character's
  height, which is usually unintended. (paraphrase)
- When you increase a type's weight, you generally do **not** want to change its height. (paraphrase)
- Mechanically thickening a character modifies its "original DNA": the white space where the
  branch merges into the stem migrates upward and can nearly disappear. (paraphrase)
- Even in sans-serif type — often called "same weight" type — the weight is not actually uniform;
  the more weight you add, the more contrast there should be where the branch merges into the stem
  versus the vertical parts of the character. (paraphrase)
- This is why you should not use the computer to change a type's weight. (paraphrase)
- A lettering artist adds the same amount of weight while keeping the height unchanged, thins the
  contrast at the stem/branch junction proportionately as weight increases, and keeps the junction
  point in the exact same location rather than raising or lowering it. (paraphrase)
