## Summary

A one-on-one portfolio review, recorded at CSUN (Cal State Northridge) during The Futur's
"The Process" era (2017). Chris Do reviews the work of **Evan**, a 43-year-old design
student and career-changer: a former Frito-Lay sales rep who, after an on-the-job injury,
was retrained, took a graphic design class from a book, and — encouraged by his wife that
he was "not too old" — switched into design. Chris frames Evan's late start and life
experience as an **asset**, not a liability: age brings thoughtfulness, patience, research
depth, and the ability to set personal taste aside and serve the brief. He walks through
three pieces — a *True Romance* box set, a set of IKEA corporate-responsibility badges, and
Evan's self-designed wedding invitation — praising craft, comp presentation, image
sourcing, and range while giving targeted critique. Along the way Chris delivers a recurring
Futur lesson: **class is a job interview** — professors remember who "killed it" versus who
slacked, and will (or won't) recommend you accordingly. The video's throughline is the
"never too late / never too old to start in design" mindset.

## Key claims

_All dated 2017-05-20. Paraphrased unless quoted below. Attributed per speaker._

**Chris Do (persona):**
- A 43-year-old career-changer's age and life experience are a **strength** in design:
  a calm demeanor, a story, and lived experience translate into thoughtful work, patience,
  and the ability to "dig deep" and research — versus a 20-year-old who rushes to finish. (2017-05-20)
- Chris relates it to himself: even when he was in school he was "just rushing to get stuff
  done"; the depth that comes with maturity is visible in Evan's work. (2017-05-20)
- **Class is a job interview.** The level of detail and volume of work in a class project
  is apparent, and doing well in class matters beyond the grade. (2017-05-20)
- Professors remember who "killed it" and who was "slacking off," coming to class late and
  doing half the work; young people don't realize that later, when they need a job, they
  call those same professors for recommendations — and professors won't recommend someone
  who wasn't dependable. (2017-05-20)
- Craft praise on the *True Romance* box set: strong thinking, research, and detail; the
  illustrations (the pie disc his favorite) are good, but **better photography** of the
  physical piece would have taken it "to the next level." (2017-05-20)
- Push idea further: tie in **one more theme from the film** — e.g. low-pile red carpet or
  the diner's linoleum flooring as a backdrop — so "nothing is overlooked." (2017-05-20)
- Signal of a strong creative: **no shortage of ideas** — the ability to take an idea and
  keep expanding it; Chris bets Evan could produce "10 more things" (posters, etc.) if asked. (2017-05-20)
- On the IKEA badges: showing a **completely different genre and style** demonstrates depth
  across styles, and **the comps really sell the design.** (2017-05-20)
- Targeted critique on the weakest badge: the smokestack/factory imagery **fights the
  message** — a smokestack reads as destroying the environment, while "Labor" should evoke
  teamwork / constructing something together. (2017-05-20)
- On image sourcing: picking the product image from tractor companies (who shoot beautiful
  images to sell their tractors) shows you **know how to find the right images**; carefully
  picked images look "totally custom and tailored." You have to dig. (2017-05-20)
- On the wedding invitation: praises setting **personal preference aside to serve the
  brief** — Evan and his wife love modern/minimal/clean lines, but the venue and event
  demanded a different, appropriate style. "Listening to the script or the brief" and
  letting the space dictate what it needs to be is a professional strength. (2017-05-20)
- Working alone as a freelancer, you lose the **energy of other creatives** around you; the
  peer push of a studio/classroom raises everyone's game. (Chris affirms Evan's own point
  and hints he may have work/opportunities for him.) (2017-05-20)
- Aside on portfolios/self-promo: reviewees / students routinely fail to bring **business
  cards** ("nobody's bringing me cards, I don't understand it") — a missed networking basic. (2017-05-20)

**Evan (reviewee / CSUN design student) — context, NOT persona data:**
- Spent his 20s raising a family; became a **full-time single father** and put off school. (2017-05-20)
- Worked as a **Frito-Lay sales rep** ("slanging chips"); got injured, and because of the
  injury the company retrained him — he took a graphic design class off a list, learning
  Adobe Photoshop from a book with a helper on hand. (2017-05-20)
- His **wife told him he was "not too old,"** and he "took a chance" on switching to design;
  "it worked out." (2017-05-20)
- Is 43 years old; married ~20 years. (2017-05-20)
- Portfolio pieces: a *True Romance* box set (shot in his backyard); IKEA
  corporate-responsibility badges (Labor, Forestry, Environment); and the invitation for
  his own wedding (his wife a "harsh critic"). He likes to "show how I work" in his
  portfolio so a viewer understands his thinking. (2017-05-20)
- Marriage advice offered on a table card: "listen with the goal of hearing instead of
  replying." (2017-05-20)
- Career goal: wanted a job at a studio or firm; misses the peer energy he had in class
  now that he freelances alone at home. (2017-05-20)
