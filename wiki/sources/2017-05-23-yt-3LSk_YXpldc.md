## Summary (Pt.1)

A candid business/mindset **postmortem** in which Chris Do and the Blind team debrief a
lost proposal for a ~$1M project — the studio pitched, made the shortlist, but the job
(awarded value just under $900,000) went to a competitor. The video is framed as an
"after-action review" (a format Chris explicitly borrows from his military/Army
background): the team goes around the room to name what they did right and, more
importantly, what they did wrong, "because that's where we have room for improvement."

**Part 1 covers the setup and the "what we did right" list only.** Chris narrates the
stakes (a landmark client + budget that would have elevated Blind's portfolio and
represented ~20% of the year's Billings toward a stated $5M goal), then condenses the
team's "did right" findings into a pitching checklist: a well-designed proposal, a good
outside partner, a "B+" client call, a professional presentation, hitting every
deadline, a budget "in the zone," and being responsive throughout. The video ends on the
turn — "but we lost this job" — with the wrong/lessons analysis deferred to Pt.2.

## Key claims

Paraphrased unless quoted. All from this 2017-05-23 video; speaker is Chris Do unless
noted (attribution uncertain per auto-captions).

- **[2017-05-23] The stakes were high on three axes** (Chris, paraphrase): the client was
  huge, the budget was huge, and it was "a little bit of a new platform for us" — so
  winning would have both paid well and elevated Blind's portfolio to attract more
  big-name clients.
- **[2017-05-23] When a job comes in, Chris says he thinks about the money in terms of
  what it means to everyone else** (paraphrase): every job won means the whole company
  does a little better — enabling new hires, raises, and momentum.
- **[2017-05-23] The scale of the ask made him feel out of his league** (Chris,
  paraphrase): "I guess I felt like I was a little bit out of my league or that we were
  out of our league." Framed as an honest admission, not false modesty.
- **[2017-05-23] Project economics [self-reported]**: it was a ~$1M project; the winning
  studio's award was "just shy of $900,000" — cited to argue the budget "wasn't
  imagination." Turnaround was fast: ~a week to assemble the proposal, and the decision
  came "days later."
- **[2017-05-23] The loss was materially significant [self-reported]**: winning it
  "could have easily put us far ahead," representing ~20% of the year's Billings against
  a stated annual **goal of $5 million**.
- **[2017-05-23] Postmortem method — the after-action review** (Chris, paraphrase):
  explicitly modeled on military/Army practice ("what was the mission, did we accomplish
  it… three sustains"); go around the room, name what went right and wrong, and focus
  mostly on the wrong "because that's where we have room for improvement."

**Pitching lessons — the "what we did right" checklist** (Chris, condensed and
paraphrased; these double as reusable sales-clients principles):

- **Never underestimate a well-designed proposal.** The client may have seen prior work,
  but the proposal is the first look at the work you'd do *for them* — "make it count."
- **Partner with the best.** When you lack a resource in-house (development, a VFX
  specialist), team up with the best people on the planet rather than fake it; here they
  found exactly the right partner company.
- **The client call is where you close.** Self-graded a "B+": the clients threw
  unexpected curveballs and the team was "caught flat-footed." The call is the moment to
  listen for objections, build rapport, and address objections the instant you hear them.
- **Present like professionals.** Know the proposal and know the client inside and out.
- **Meet every deadline.** Missing a pitch deadline is "a huge red flag" — a deal breaker.
- **Land the budget "in the zone."** Know the client's price zone *before* writing the
  proposal by **price bracketing** — floating numbers to gauge comfort; here they were
  right in the zone.
- **Be responsive.** Answer questions and requests in a timely manner — earned trust
  makes a client "much more likely to hire us."
