# Amateur Mistakes That Experts Don't Make — Dealing w/ Defeat (pt. 2): Losing a $1 Million Proposal

★ L3-candidate — contains a distinct, reusable pitching framework ("call the domain experts" winning formula, throw-yourself-on-the-sword posture, "experts ask great questions") that belongs in `wiki/topics/sales-clients` and likely `persona/beliefs.md`.

## Summary

Part 2 of Chris Do's postmortem (an "after-action review") on why his agency Blind lost a large proposal — framed as ~$300K–$400K of real work that Blind intended to price at ~$1M. Continues from [[wiki/sources/2017-05-23-yt-3LSk_YXpldc]] (Pt1). Chris runs the debrief live with the Blind team, dissecting the loss to extract repeatable lessons. Core diagnosis: Blind lacked **domain expertise** in this field and did not do enough to compensate for it. Chris lays out a "winning formula" for pitching outside your domain — call the actual domain experts (vendors/specialists), interrogate them until you understand the approach, and become the expert of the *top-level conversation*. He contrasts amateur moves (assuming, playing slick, telling experts what you'll do) with expert moves (admitting ignorance transparently, asking smart questions, mirroring the standard of engagement they'd expect from a good partner). The team then "rewinds the tape" to score which questions they did and didn't ask on the actual call.

Speakers: Chris Do (subject — the lessons/formula) plus Blind team members (context). Some lines are unattributed team members; those are marked `attribution: uncertain`.

## Key claims

All dated 2017-05-27. Chris Do unless noted. Paraphrase unless quoted.

- **[Chris, self-reported]** Blind has ~20+ years of pitching experience and a high win rate; losing surprises Chris because he feels the current team "owns the pitch game." This was a big, significant project that would have pushed Blind forward.
- **[Chris]** You must reflect on losses specifically — "if you're not measuring the things that you're doing you're not able to improve," and you'll repeat the mistake. He references the military/Army "after-action review" model.
- **[Chris]** The single recurring problem: Blind did **not have domain expertise** here. Nothing in the portfolio suggested they'd done this kind of work before.
- **[Team, attribution: uncertain]** The client's stated reason for the loss: they wanted to work with someone they'd worked with before / someone who "can do it all in one place." A team member notes it was a client nobody at Blind had a relationship with, so there was no inside track to get — being considered at all, given how far out of range it was, *was* the inside track.
- **[Chris]** Blind has overcome the no-domain-expertise problem before — cites Xbox and architectural/real-estate clients (Hudson Pacific) where they lacked the specific domain background but still crossed the finish line.
- **[Chris]** The "winning formula" for every time you are NOT the domain expert (and assume you usually aren't): **call the domain experts.** Ascertain from them: what they'd need from you to start, what assets to deliver, how much it costs, and what questions *they* would ask this client.
- **[Chris]** "Throw yourself on the sword" — come in hat in hand and admit "we've never done anything like this," ask them to walk you through their approach. Don't try to be slick.
- **[Chris, self-reported]** He's done this since he was a college student — on a print job he'd tell the vendor "I'm a designer from school, tell me how your process works," and if he didn't get a good answer or feeling, he'd call another vendor. After talking to two or three domain experts you become an expert quickly.
- **[Chris]** Play (actually *be*) ignorant and transparent about it, because people often won't volunteer basics — they don't want to patronize/insult you, so they'll never tell you the thing you don't know unless you ask.
- **[Chris]** Build a generic toolbox of questions usable for almost any domain expert. Examples he'd ask: what would something like this cost / how many people / what issues usually come up / **where does this break (where does it fail)**; tell me about your process / how do you work; how should we be involved / what does your ideal partnership look like; what are the big milestones; what past examples/case studies are applicable; what terminology/acronyms do I need to be aware of.
- **[Chris]** Read the expert's engagement: if they don't ask *smart questions back* and aren't excited, that's a sign they don't want the job or aren't experts themselves — he'll bail. He probes to see if they'll arm him with what he doesn't know.
- **[Chris]** The mirror lesson: clients do the same to *us*. "Experts ask great questions." Asking great questions signals engagement and expertise; failing to ask them signals the opposite to the client.
- **[Team + Chris]** Rewinding the actual call: they DID ask about cost/resources, schedule, the open-ended "what do you need," a general sense of approach/milestones/iteration, case studies, and "what questions should we be asking." They did NOT ask about specific assets, and did NOT ask "where does it fail" — which was itself one of the client's questions to them.
- **[Chris]** Amateur tell on the call: the client asked how many days to facilitate; Blind said "one" — the standard in software is more like 10–12. That gap exposed they didn't really understand how the work gets done.
- **[Chris]** You can't put in the effort of a $200K job and expect to walk away with a $1M job. Blind planned to roughly double the price ("luxury tax") because they're good at managing projects and have client access — "we better behave like that," because the partners are doing all the work while Blind makes all the profit.
- **[Chris]** A client who won't "step up to the plate," get into the dirt, and partner isn't a good partner either — a $300–400K project demands mutual effort.
- **[Chris]** Biggest amateur mistake identified: at the very start they *told the experts* what Blind would do ("we'll handle the UX/UI design, you handle back-end"). Experts then defer to you because they don't want to step on your toes. Instead, open with "we don't know what we don't know — walk us through this," and ask the client for two hours of a specialist's time (worth it against a $400K job).
- **[Chris]** Possible tactic floated: ask the domain expert for a proposal they put together for another comparable project, to learn the standard structure.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (persona training data):

> "20 plus years of pitching and working in this business we still lose sometimes... it surprises me when we lose."

> "It's really important that you have to reflect especially on the losses so that you know how to do it better next time, because if you're not measuring the things that you're doing you're not able to improve the next time around and you're probably going to make the same mistake again."

> "This is always the problem and this is the number one thing that we're always going to encounter, which is we don't have — I'm going to say — do not have domain expertise."

> "I'm going to give you guys a winning formula you can follow for the rest of your days. Okay: every time we are not the domain experts — and I'm going to assume more often than not we are not the domain experts — what do we need to do? Call them. Call the domain experts."

> "This is what I call... throwing yourself on the sword. Okay, hat in hand you come out and say we've never done anything like this, we need to understand what it is that you do, how you would do this, what is your approach — because we don't know anything. We don't try to be slick, we don't try to be anything."

> "By the time you talk to two or three domain experts you become an expert really quick. And what we need to become is the expert of doing top-level conversations."

> "I'm going to say play ignorant — we're not playing, we are ignorant — and we're going to be transparent that we don't know, because oftentimes people don't want to patronize you... they would never tell you that because I don't want to make you sound stupid."

> "Where does this break? Where does it tend to break?... I'll use the broadest terms so that you can adapt it for anything — and I'm going to say fail."

> "If they don't ask... smart questions, and if they don't seem excited, you're going to bail on them. Yes. So guess what our clients are going to do to us? Same thing. If we don't ask smart questions... Experts ask great questions. By asking great questions you are engaged."

> "What we can't do is assume that we can put in the effort of a $200,000 job and walk away with a million dollar job... we're going to double up on the price on this thing... it's a luxury tax, it's because we're so smart, we're so good at managing projects and we have access to clients. Well, we better behave like that, because they're doing all the work and yet we're making all the profit."

> "So we told experts what we're going to do. So we know that that's not a good thing, right? And they're going to defer to us because they don't want to step on our toes."

> "We don't know what we don't know, we need you to walk us through and explain this, and you need to spare like two hours to sit here and talk to us — 'cause it's 2 hours worth a potential $400,000 job to you."

Team member (context, `attribution: uncertain`):

> "The reason why the client gave to us as why we didn't get the job was they wanted to work with somebody... they worked with before."

> "There's no way we can get more of an inside track because I called my sources... it was an [account] that nobody had worked with... The fact that we got considered for this, considering how far out of range it was for us, was the inside track — otherwise it would be like not even invited."
