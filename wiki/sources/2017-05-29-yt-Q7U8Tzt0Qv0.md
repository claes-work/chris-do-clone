# Trust Experts To Do Their Job — Dealing w/ Defeat (pt. 3): Losing a $1 Million Proposal

> ★ L3-candidate — introduces a distinct, quotable framework (the "executive
> producer" mental model + "you must ask or you will fail") not covered in Pt1/Pt2.

## Summary

Part 3 of Chris Do's team postmortem on losing a ~$1M proposal (Pt1
[[wiki/sources/2017-05-23-yt-3LSk_YXpldc]], Pt2
[[wiki/sources/2017-05-27-yt-VMirFYktjSA]]). This installment centers on one
lesson: **trust experts to do their job — and you must ask them to.** Chris walks
the team through what went wrong: this was the first proposal at this scale
(after 5–8 much smaller Blind proposals), which spiked his anxiety, and the team
failed to leverage the outside partner/experts they had access to. They never
asked the prospective client for two winning example proposals, never asked the
partner to write up the proposal or build the bid, and instead ground through the
bid themselves. Chris traces the root cause to the team being "too polite" —
sensitive to making others do "unnecessary work" — and offers a reframe: experts
do this work all the time, often for free unless booked, and asking is how you
win. He resolves an NDA worry (a signed partner working on the project is part of
the team, so sharing the RFP is fine) and closes with the **executive-producer
analogy**: in film the executive producer assembles the team, secures financing,
and takes the lion's share without directing, designing, or scoring — and that is
the role the studio should play here. Also reframes the fear of a partner
"stealing the client" and the guilt over repackaging someone else's bid.

## Key claims

All claims paraphrased unless in the Quotes section. Speaker attribution noted;
this is a team discussion led by Chris Do — only Chris-attributed material is
persona-eligible.

- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29 — self-reported]** This was the first major proposal
  Chris had done at this scale; he had done 5–8 proposals for Blind before but
  "nothing even close to this scale," which made his anxiety skyrocket and never
  really go away through the project. (Blind = his studio, [[wiki/entities/blind]].)
- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29]** The team never asked the client for two successful
  example proposals they had put together for similar past projects — which "would
  have helped immensely." Even losing the job, getting two winning proposals to
  study would have been a win.
- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29]** The team also never asked the outside partner/expert
  to write up the proposal or build the bid for the job.
- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29]** Root cause of not asking: the team is "very
  good-natured / polite" and over-sensitive to making people do "unnecessary
  work" or pitch for nothing — but it wasn't for nothing (they were out ~$600k,
  the partner out ~$400k). Politeness has to be overcome; **you must ask or you
  have a high probability of failing.**
- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29 — self-reported]** Lesson learned in 1996, early in his
  career: he used to prepare all the budgets and bids himself until someone told
  him to just call a freelance line producer, who will build a full multi-page bid
  for free — you only pay them if you book the job and they come in to execute it.
- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29 — self-reported]** Recent personal example: a New York
  guy doing a real estate proposal asked to hire Chris for information; Chris gave
  him a full keynote for free, unprompted-for-pay, "just because he asked" —
  because Chris knows the work can't really be done without them. But he wouldn't
  give it at random; **you have to ask.**
- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29]** Experts don't volunteer help because they may feel
  it's proprietary or insulting; when you ask, they know what's at stake. If they
  hesitate, don't accept a bare yes/no — ask *why* (risk? involvement? cost?) and
  solve for it (e.g., "we'll assume some of the risk," "I'll pay that").
- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29]** On the NDA concern: a non-disclosure agreement says
  you won't share with anybody not working on the project — a partner who has
  signed and is working on the project IS part of the team, so sharing the RFP
  with them is fine. (Team member raised the worry; Chris judged it overthinking.)
- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29]** Process reframe: don't do a single call with the
  client — do multiple calls, map out roles/responsibilities, find out the right
  questions to ask before doing the proposal. Doing this repeatedly makes the
  partner trust they won't "get ditched at the altar."
- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29]** On fear of the partner poaching the client: name it
  on the first call, ask for assurance ("we need to hear you won't poach our
  client"), and if you genuinely can't trust them, don't call them at all. Right
  now the studio has NO client on this — the worst outcome — so better to land the
  first job (even if later jobs get taken) and use it to onboard others.
- **[Chris Do, 2017-05-29]** The **executive-producer framework**: the highest-paid
  person on a film production is the executive producer, who options the script,
  finds actors, and gets financing — and does nothing else (no directing, set
  design, wardrobe, scoring). They take the lion's share because without them
  nothing happens. In this deal the studio is the executive producer; the client
  is the financing. Never feel guilty about it — that role is earned through
  relationships and reputation. Assemble the team, ask them what they need, get it
  done. Playing this way is "the major leagues"; not doing it is the minor leagues.
- **[team members, 2017-05-29 — context, attribution: uncertain]** Team admitted
  they didn't ask for example proposals because "it didn't occur to us" and out of
  guilt/conscience over "wholly taking somebody's bid, repackaging in our design,
  and getting the lion's share of the profit." Chris counters this with the
  executive-producer reframe.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (persona-eligible):

> "So the lesson to learn here is you must ask, or you're going to fail — you have
> a high probability of failing."

> "Even if we lose we win, because we got two proposals to study."

> "I never like to accept an answer for what it is. A yes or no answer doesn't do
> me a whole lot of good — I want to know why. What are you thinking, why are you
> hesitant?"

> "People don't volunteer stuff because they might feel like it's proprietary or
> it's insulting you. But when you ask, it's like, okay, we know what's at stake."

> "The most valuable person in terms of dollars on a film production is the
> executive producer. The executive producer options the script, finds the actors,
> gets the financing — and does nothing else... but they make the lion's share of
> the money, 'cause without them nothing happens."

> "In this case we are playing the executive producer. You guys need to remember
> that — anytime you forget, you tell yourself you're the executive producer of a
> feature film."

> "You just assemble the team, ask them what they need, and you get it done."

> "This is what I would consider us playing at the major leagues. If we're not
> doing this, we're playing at the minor leagues, or worse."

> "We're not asking them to do anything they haven't done 10,000 times already."

> "Never feel guilty for that."
