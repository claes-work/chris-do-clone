# Stop Selling. Start Closing. How To Win More Jobs Without Pitching

★ L3-candidate — This is a distinct, self-contained selling/closing framework ("Whisper vs. Scream" / "when you say it you're selling, when they say it you're closing") that belongs in `wiki/topics/sales-clients/`.

## Summary

Chris Do lays out his core reframe of the sales conversation: stop *selling* (pushing your solution) and start *closing* (getting the client to articulate the need themselves). The organizing metaphor is the **Whisper vs. the Scream** — when you scream (assert, persuade, tell them "this is great, you need this") people put up defenses and pull back; when you whisper (ask, understand, advise) people lean in. The mechanism is **consultative selling**: stay in the diagnostic phase as long as possible, ask "why" repeatedly, and understand the true nature of the client's problem before offering any solution. He illustrates with a doctor analogy (a doctor who diagnoses deeply gives a prescription that has real meaning) and connects the approach to Blair Enns' *Win Without Pitching* (try to kill the engagement three times to signal you don't need the work) and Bruce Lee's "be water" (show up empty, with no agenda). The payoff signal: you know you've done it right when the client starts saying the words you wanted to say. At that point you are no longer selling — you are closing.

Attribution note: The video is essentially a Chris Do monologue. An off-screen questioner poses one framing question mid-video ("what do I really mean when I say... what are some examples of how to whisper?") which Chris then answers; that prompt is transcribed in Chris's own voice (he appears to voice both the setup and the answer), so all substantive content is attributed to Chris. ## Key claims

All claims below are paraphrased from Chris Do, dated 2017-06-09 (source: this video), unless quoted in the next section.

- **Whisper vs. Scream (2017-06-09):** When you scream, people's defenses go up and they pull back; when you whisper, people lean in. People are accustomed to being sold to, so defenses rise fast the moment they sense a pitch.
- **Selling vs. closing (2017-06-09):** "When you say it, you're selling; when they say it, you're closing." Telling the client "this is good, this is great, you need this" is selling (the scream). Getting the client to voice the need is closing (the whisper).
- **Seek to understand first (2017-06-09):** A good salesperson first seeks to understand the true nature of the customer's problem and only then offers a solution. Chris does not enter a room assuming he has something the client needs.
- **Make it about the client (2017-06-09):** Don't make the conversation about you; ask about the client's challenges and goals. Asking questions lets the client look inward and discover what's really going on.
- **Stay in the diagnostic phase as long as possible (2017-06-09):** This is the central tactical rule. Analogy: a doctor who quickly prescribes ("take two pills") before hearing symptoms is worthless; a doctor who explores your stress, diet, family history, and work gives a prescription that carries real meaning because it's rooted in understanding you.
- **Keep asking "why" (2017-06-09):** Even when a client says "I need a new website" or "our branding doesn't look good," that's not enough to act on. Ask what's driving the decision, keep digging deeper, and you'll often find the real solution is something totally different from what they came in for.
- **Act as if you don't need the work (2017-06-09):** Don't show up with an agenda — "show up empty, be water" (Bruce Lee). Position yourself as an advisor, not a persuader.
- **Kill the engagement three times (2017-06-09, attributed to Blair Enns):** Chris cites Blair Enns' advice to try to kill the engagement three times — "this might not be a good fit," "the budget might not be right," "I'm not sure my schedule can accommodate this." Each attempt subconsciously signals to the client that you don't need the work.
- **Give up control to gain it (2017-06-09):** "When you give up control, that is when you gain it. When you stop trying to be smart is when you're smart. When you stop selling, you close."
- **The success signal (2017-06-09):** You know you've done it correctly when you start to hear the words you wanted to say coming out of the client's mouth (e.g., "We don't just need a website, we need to rethink our whole communication platform — can you help us?"). That is them saying it, and now you are closing, not selling.
- **ABC = Always Be Closing (2017-06-09):** Chris closes with the "ABC — Always Be Closing" tag (a nod to the *Glengarry Glen Ross* line, here reframed toward his closing-through-listening definition).

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (persona training material):

> "Stop selling, start closing. This is the idea of the Whisper versus the Scream. When you whisper people lean in, and when you scream people tend to pull back."

> "We're very accustomed to being sold to, so our defense goes up really fast."

> "When you say it you're selling — 'this is good, this is great, you need this.' When they say it you're closing. This is the whisper."

> "A good salesperson first seeks to understand the true nature of a customer's problem, and only then offers a solution."

> "I do not go into a room assuming I have something that you need. I want to understand what the problem is... then and only then can I provide something of value to you."

> "The thing that you want to do is stay in the diagnostic phase for as long as possible."

> "If you go to see a doctor and they're quick to prescribe a solution for you — take two pills and call me in the morning — but you haven't had a chance to even tell the doctor what the symptoms are..."

> "Now the solution, the prescription if you will, has real meaning to you because the doctor spent time to learn about you. So this is what we want to do in consultative selling: we want to ask lots of questions."

> "Even when the client comes to you and says 'I need a new website' or 'our branding doesn't look good,' that's not enough for you to go off and do your job."

> "If you keep asking why, and you keep digging deeper and deeper, you're going to start to find out perhaps the solution isn't what they came in for, but it's something totally different."

> "Another way of whispering is to act and talk as if you don't need the work. Don't show up with an agenda — show up empty, be water as they say, with Bruce Lee."

> "I'm not here to sell, I'm not here to persuade. All I want to do is advise you to the best of my ability to help you."

> "What Blair Enns says is to try to kill the engagement three times by saying: this might not be a good fit for me, the budget might not be right, and I'm not sure my schedule can accommodate what we're talking about. Each time you're subconsciously telling the client 'I don't need the work.'"

> "When you give up control that is when you gain it. When you stop trying to be smart is when you're smart. And when you stop selling, you close."

> "You guys know if you've done it correctly when you start to hear the words that you want to say coming out of their lips."

> "That is them saying it — and now you're not selling, you are closing."

> "ABC — always be closing."
