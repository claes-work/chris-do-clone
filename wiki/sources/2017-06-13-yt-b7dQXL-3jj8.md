# Surfacing Unspoken Objections — No time to whine about a loss — The Conclusion pt. 4

★ L3-candidate — "surface the unspoken objections" is a distinct, nameable sales move
(pre-empt the objections the client hasn't voiced yet) worth promoting into
`wiki/topics/sales-clients/` and `persona/beliefs.md`. Same for "diagnose don't
prescribe" and "be the least risky option."

## Summary (Pt 4 / conclusion)

The closing part of Chris Do's team postmortem of a lost ~$1M web project (Pt1
[[wiki/sources/2017-05-23-yt-3LSk_YXpldc]], Pt2 [[wiki/sources/2017-05-27-yt-VMirFYktjSA]],
Pt3 [[wiki/sources/2017-05-29-yt-Q7U8Tzt0Qv0]]). The team member who led the client call
(Ben / "Mr. Burns") recounts that he went in expecting to answer questions on the
proposal, but the client hadn't read it and instead pushed the team to pitch the whole
engagement live — for which he was unprepared, and the call fell flat ("neutral,"
no clear signal, but he sensed they didn't understand him).

Chris and the more experienced pitchers (Matthew) then coach the team on how to run such
a call. Core lessons: skip the fluff and get into the meeting; when a client hasn't read
the proposal, don't page-by-page it — reframe/recap the top three things you heard,
then jump around non-linearly to what matters most to them; and proactively "surface the
unspoken objections" (state and dissolve every concern before the client voices it),
modeled on the sales agent who pre-empted the team's own objections ("takes too long,
too expensive, doesn't work"). Additional coaching: stay present and listen intently
("listen to respond" is the trap), be willing to jump off the rehearsed script, buy your
partner time, and never knee-jerk answer a hard question — take a moment to think and ask
follow-up questions. Chris adds a new house rule (Blair Enns' "say what you think"),
insists the team be emotionally detached about the outcome, and works through how to
address specific objections that surfaced only in Scott's later client calls: the two-team
structure, the California-vs-East-Coast/time-zone concern (turn it into an advantage:
dedicated on-time team, embed a producer), unclear decision-maker (go around the room up
front, ask "who and how will you make your decision"), corporate CYA risk (be the
"least risky" choice), and the client's point-blank "what's wrong with our site?" that the
team failed to answer specifically. Chris closes: a "million dollar learning opportunity"
you only get in the field, not from a book.

## Key claims (dated 2017-06-13; paraphrased unless quoted; per-speaker)

**Ben / call lead ("Mr. Burns") — TEAM / context:**
- Went into the call carrying the weight of it; compares the pre-call feeling to gearing
  up as a former police officer / narcotics agent serving a search warrant. Says the
  money on the table got to him a little. (attribution: uncertain — the opening
  first-person narration is not explicitly named, but context points to the call lead.)
- Expected to field questions on the proposal; was NOT prepared to pitch the entire
  engagement live when the client hadn't read it. His instinct was to ask for questions;
  a backup agenda to present the pitch would have been better.
- Post-call read was "neutral" — no clear sign it went well or badly — but sensing the
  client didn't understand him told him he'd bombed it.

**Matthew — TEAM / context (experienced pitcher):**
- Get straight into the meeting; cut the fluffy conversation. Stay open on the agenda,
  feel out the room, keep the decision-maker engaged rather than rigidly time-boxing.
- When a client hasn't read the proposal, reframe/recap first: "here's what we heard last
  time — this, this and this — and here's how we addressed it," then jump straight into
  the relevant page. Summarize the problem so both sides are on the same page.

**Other team members — context:**
- The client gave no clear direction ("cool, tell us"), leaving the team stuck in
  "no man's land" unsure whether to start at page one.
- (Sales rule of thumb, one member) "Don't want the job" going into the pitch — a
  mentality check before entering.

**Chris Do — PERSONA:**
- Clients often don't read the proposal until the call; the team must be ready to tell the
  story quickly and be of the moment. (Chris admits he doesn't read his own proposals
  until he has to.)
- Demonstrate domain expertise not by claiming to be "really good," but by having an
  informed conversation and asking really great questions — that's how you know somebody
  knows something.
- Method for a cold-ish pitch call: quickly assess whether they read it / have questions;
  if not, don't shame them — recap the top three things you heard, then go non-linear
  (page-by-page is "deadly"), then surface the unspoken objections.
- "Surface the unspoken objections" = state and answer the concerns the client isn't
  ready to say out loud but has effectively already signaled; the top-tier sales agent did
  exactly this by naming the team's own objections (too slow, too expensive, doesn't work)
  before they could.
- Be present and transparent, in the moment; "listen intently, don't listen to respond."
  Be willing to jump off the rehearsed script at a moment's notice, because you can always
  tell when someone answers the wrong question.
- Buy your partner time; it's fine to say "I need a moment to think about that." An
  intelligent person is not the one who responds quickly. Ask follow-up questions — they
  often make the client answer their own (poorly-formed) question and lead you to the
  answer they want.
- Be emotionally detached about the outcome — passionate about the work, detached when
  presenting it. No zealots overselling.
- New house rule adopted on camera: Blair Enns' "say what you think" — practiced in small
  ways so it's automatic on game day.
- Address objections head-on then pivot to advantage — e.g. East-Coast/time-zone concern:
  "we'll work on your time and you won't feel the difference," dedicated team, later start
  = more done by their end of day; two-team concern should have been pre-solved in the
  deck with a partner commitment (fly out, embed a producer full-time). Should have padded
  the budget to cover partner travel. "It's not about the money, it's about making you
  feel comfortable."
- Identify the decision-maker: on a group call, frame up front, go around the room to hear
  who's listening for what; ask "who and how will you make your decision." Someone usually
  reveals the lead.
- Corporate buyers are in CYA mode — on a million-dollar project a bad hire is a
  career-ending mistake, so the vendor must be seen as the SAFEST / least-risky choice.
  The team's failure: "all we did was present risk" (two teams, one facilitator, etc.).
- When the client asks point-blank "what's wrong with our site?", answer specifically —
  experts can name the top three problems on sight. This is "diagnosing, not prescribing"
  (name what's wrong, don't hand over solutions) — and only when asked.
- Closing frame: this was a "million dollar learning opportunity," the kind of education
  you only get in the field when the stakes are highest — not from a book.

**Scott — context:**
- Two follow-up calls with the client surfaced that the engineering team's concern about
  the two-team structure was the real deal-blocker — a concern never voiced on the pitch
  call.
