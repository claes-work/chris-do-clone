# How Does Pitching Work When Trying To Get Work From Advertising Agencies?

★ L3-candidate — adds distinct, mechanically-detailed material on how the advertising/agency pitch system works (sales reps, competitive spec pitches, the economics) plus Chris Do's hard stance on refusing spec work, which likely goes beyond the current `wiki/topics/sales-clients` coverage.

## Summary

Chris Do explains, from [[../entities/blind]]'s experience as a motion/design studio, how "pitching" works when a studio wants to win work from advertising agencies. He defines a pitch as speculative (unpaid) work done in the hopes of getting a job — not even a client, just a single job — almost always in competition against other studios (typically at least three companies fighting for the same job). He walks through the two ways Blind procured commercial work: (1) via independent sales reps who hold relationships with agency producers/art directors and "slip you in" when they hear of a new campaign, and (2) less often, a direct inbound call from an agency. He stresses that commercial/advertising pitching is unpaid and brutal — Blind once spent nearly $30,000 pursuing a job they did not win. He frames spec work as an advertising-industry-specific norm driven by supply and demand being "out of whack" (more people willing to pitch than not), so playing the game requires learning to pitch. His counter-model: working with clients directly, where no one expects free work — Blind does not pitch for those clients, states its price, refuses spec work, and walks away if the client insists. He closes on "saying no is a very powerful thing."

## Key claims

- (Chris, 2017-06-18) A "pitch" refers to doing speculative work in the hopes of getting a job — not even a client, just a single job. (paraphrase)
- (Chris, 2017-06-18) Pitches are almost always competitive against other studios — usually at least three companies fighting for the same job. (paraphrase)
- (Chris, 2017-06-18) The old model — "just tell us some things, I'll present this thing to you" — used to work because there was less competition and more money; as competition rose and money fell, it became harder. (paraphrase)
- (Chris, 2017-06-18) Blind procured commercial work in two ways: almost always through independent sales reps (who don't work at Blind but hold relationships with agency producers and art directors and listen for rumblings of new campaigns), and occasionally via a direct inbound agency call. (paraphrase)
- (Chris, 2017-06-18) When an agency invites a pitch, the studio asks the budget and how many competitors there are, then decides whether to pitch at all. (paraphrase)
- (Chris, 2017-06-18) Advertising/commercial pitching is unpaid; Blind once spent almost $30,000 pursuing a job — and did not win it. (paraphrase; self-reported figure)
- (Chris, 2017-06-18) Spec/free work is very specific to advertising; elsewhere nobody expects you to work for free. (paraphrase)
- (Chris, 2017-06-18) Supply and demand are "out of whack" — more people are willing to pitch than aren't — so it's an industry trend, and to play the game you must learn to pitch. (paraphrase)
- (Chris, 2017-06-18) When working with clients directly, Blind does not pitch at all — clients come to them, Blind states its price, refuses speculative work, and walks away if the client insists. (paraphrase)
- (Chris, 2017-06-18) "Saying no is a very powerful thing." (verbatim — see quotes)
