## Summary

Coaching/interview episode of The Futur's "The Process." **Chris Do** introduces **Melinda Livsey** (spelled "Livesy" in the auto-captions) as the show's new co-host and a practicing graphic designer, then coaches her live on running her freelance creative business. Format is one-on-one diagnostic coaching: Melinda brings her real numbers and struggles; Chris interrogates them and prescribes solutions. The two also perform a role-play (Chris plays Melinda's role; Melinda plays a low-budget client, then plays Melinda-as-mother giving advice to a hypothetical daughter).

Melinda's background (context): ~9 years out of school, ~7 in-house at a large eyewear/action-sports company (marketing/advertising, then branding — product graphics, in-store, retail displays, packaging); studied graphic design + illustration at Cal State Fullerton; got her start via an AIGA portfolio review that led to an internship two months before graduating. Now solo/freelance.

Core arc: Melinda struggles to (1) win/qualify new business, (2) delegate, (3) stay focused, and (4) say no to underpriced work. Chris reframes each as a business-owner and mindset problem, culminating in the theme that money should have "no power over you."

## Key claims
*(All dated 2017-06-21. Paraphrased unless quoted. Attributed per speaker.)*

**Melinda's business numbers (context — Melinda-attributed, not persona data):**
- Melinda gets ~1 new-business inquiry per week on average (feast or famine; sometimes 2–3, sometimes zero for three weeks); ~90% of clients come from referrals, reaching her by text, email, and phone. [Melinda]
- Her average project is ~$800–1,000; branding/logo work sits at ~$5,000–6,000 but small projects ($200–800) drag the average down and eat most of her time. [Melinda]
- She needs to net $1,750 every two weeks after tax (~$3,500/month), which Chris converts to a needed gross of ~$7,000/month; she is a dual-income household so this is her contribution. [Melinda / Chris jointly]
- On branding jobs (~40 hrs each) she effectively earns ~$137.50/hr; on lower-tier work she works to roughly ~$100/hr; one production client pays $75/hr on-call. [Melinda]

**Chris's coaching claims (Chris-attributed — persona-relevant):**
- New business is the single most important activity; if you don't handle new biz you won't have a company for long. [Chris]
- 40 hours a week is a desk-job/full-time-employee cadence; a business owner realistically works far more because they do three jobs — the maker, the admin/client-relationship person, and sales/marketing. Chris claims he's essentially never worked a 40-hr week since starting his company; his hours run ~60–80+. [Chris]
- Working at half your normal rate (e.g. $75/hr production vs $137.50/hr branding) costs you and your company; billable-hour reality is worse once you count task-switching (re-finding files, re-entering the work). [Chris]
- Delegation always eats more time up front (explaining, building process, accepting the other person won't work at your level); every entrepreneur faces this dilemma and must accept it and optimize it anyway. [Chris]
- Build a stable of 2–3 go-to freelancers and give them steady work so they don't have to take other work and become unavailable when you need them. [Chris]
- Tip Chris hasn't done himself: record a video (voice + bullets) explaining a brief so it's waiting in the collaborator's inbox at the start of their day. [Chris]
- Focus comes from a daily "focus sheet" — a to-do list written for that single day (not the year); when off-task things appear, defer or delete them. Big goals chopped into medium, small, and hourly goals. [Chris]
- Qualify clients by talking about money "early and often"; don't build a bid/proposal before you know the budget and agree on scope. [Chris, citing Blair Enns]
- "Raise the gate" via your website: show the caliber of clients and glossy work (or, alternatively, publish process/pricing) so under-budget prospects self-select out. Like attracts like. [Chris]
- Money should have no power over you; releasing that attachment lets you say no to bad-fit, underpriced work. [Chris]

**Melinda's realizations (Melinda-attributed — context, QA signal):**
- She admits she "sandbagged" her 40-hr estimate and likely works more. [Melinda]
- She avoided asking about budget because she feared hearing "no," which she framed as "confrontation"; Chris pushes her to see that avoiding no means saying no to herself. [Melinda]
- Coached to the line: "If you say no to the smaller jobs, the ones that can't afford you, then you're making room for those that can." [Melinda, guided/scripted by Chris]

## Notable verbatim quotes

### Chris Do (persona voice — training data)

On the reality of self-employment:
> "It's a fallacy to think that people can go start their own business and work the same or less hours — that's not the case. Because now you're your own boss and you need to be doing the jobs of three people."

> "It's a lot of work, it's the grind life... when you're sleeping, dreaming and thinking about breakfast, I'm sitting there grinding, hustling, doing new business development, writing content. And it will separate the two of us really quickly."

On qualifying / the two-minute kiss-off (role-play, Chris speaking as the designer):
> "So for logo work we typically charge somewhere between five to eight thousand dollars — is that something that's within your budget?"

> "$200, yikes! Melinda I can't do work for $200... I can't sustain myself, you know we live in California... I'm trying to run a business. And so I do appreciate you calling me and unless you can significantly raise your budget... maybe there's somebody in my network that I can refer you to."

On not building bids blind (paraphrasing Blair Enns):
> "Blair Enns talks about this in the Win Without Pitching Manifesto. He says... I'm not in the bid building business, I'm in the creative service business. And I'm not going to build a bid unless I know the budget and that we have agreement on scope. There's no point."

On money mindset and his backstory:
> "I was not born in the United States, having escaped communism and coming to America. We didn't have much and we haven't had much... Don't waste, don't spend, just work hard and save your money."

> "I come from nothing, I will go back to nothing. I'm comfortable in nothingness... So the power that money has over me, it's whatever it is I give it. So I give it no power over me."

> "I am not a risk taker. I am not a courageous person. Courage is taking action when you know the odds are against you... I'm able to bet the farm on the business decisions we make at the office because in the back of my mind I have a secret backup plan. And the backup plan is I'm gonna go work for somebody else."

On saying no to bad work:
> "Why would you accept that? You're not half the person, you're not going to do half the work. Your expenses are not going to disappear by half. So just say no to that, and you're much better off working on developing your brand."

On raising the gate (Barneys analogy):
> "They haven't told me one thing with words but they told me everything with the brand experience... So the entire brand experience can be designed to filter out certain kinds of clients."

> "If you show a site with a hundred logos... it looks like they're worth $20 to me... I would show less work, I would spend more time developing case studies and showing each project in its best possible light so that it looks like it's really expensive and really thoughtful and intentional."

On focus:
> "During the day you're gonna get pulled a hundred different directions and everything is gonna seem equally important to you. And I fall victim to this all the time."

### Melinda Livsey (CONTEXT ONLY — NOT persona training data)

On why she took underpriced work:
> "Because I need the money."

On her fear of "no":
> "Anytime that there could be a no in there, I consider a confrontation."

The line Chris coached her to say (Melinda speaking, but scripted/guided by Chris):
> "If you say no to the smaller jobs, the ones that can't afford you, then you're making room for those that can."

On why the mindset shift is hard for her:
> "The need to make that certain amount, that dollar amount, seems more real than the belief that the larger jobs will come, because I don't see them currently."
