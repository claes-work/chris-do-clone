# Goal Setting and How To Achieve Your Goals (4 minutes)

★ L3-candidate — introduces a distinct goal-setting framework ("scaffolding" / the "magic stairway" + "chunking") worth promoting into `wiki/topics/mindset/`.

Solo Chris Do short (@thefutur) on how to set goals and follow through on them. All statements are attributed to Chris Do (persona-eligible).

## Summary

Chris Do argues (2017-06-24) that the most common goal-setting failure is scale: goals set too big feel intimidating and paralyzing (you don't know where to start), while goals set too small breed complacency once hit. His fix is to break a big goal into a descending ladder of smaller goals — monthly, weekly, daily, minute-by-minute. Goals must be clear, written down, quantifiable/measurable, and given a deadline. Seeing incremental progress is what keeps you moving. He introduces two named tools: "scaffolding" (a.k.a. the "magic stairway") — recursively decomposing a big goal into bite-sized steps — and "chunking" — grouping incoming work into mind-sized pieces, avoiding both over-chunking (biting the whole thing) and under-chunking (a million overwhelming pieces). He closes with a call to action to pick a set-aside goal and apply the principles.

## Key claims

- (2017-06-24, paraphrase) The most common goal-setting problem is scale: goals set too big feel intimidating so you don't know where to start; goals set too small get hit quickly and breed complacency.
- (2017-06-24, paraphrase) Big goals feel insurmountable and distant, so the key is to break a bigger goal into smaller pieces/steps — big goal → monthly → weekly → daily → minute-by-minute.
- (2017-06-24, paraphrase) Goals must be clear; clear goals produce clear results, fuzzy goals produce fuzzy results.
- (2017-06-24, paraphrase) A goal must be written down, quantifiable, objective, and measurable, and must have an assigned deadline.
- (2017-06-24, paraphrase) Regardless of whose theory you follow (he name-checks Tony Robbins and Jack Canfield), writing a goal down engages your neuromotor skills and makes achieving it "almost like magic."
- (2017-06-24, paraphrase) A key to achievement is seeing progress; with a very big goal you can't tell if you're getting closer, so translate it into a daily manageable action (e.g., "cut 200 calories every day" toward a six-pack).
- (2017-06-24, paraphrase) "Scaffolding" — the "magic stairway" — is his preferred tool: to get from point A to point B, break the goal into smaller bite-sized pieces, then break those pieces down again (example: "grow as an artist" → get better at Illustrator, Photoshop, InDesign, Premiere → for Illustrator: pen tool, building shapes quickly, drawing precisely, gradients, navigation/keyboard shortcuts).
- (2017-06-24, paraphrase) "Chunking" is power, but calibrate it: don't over-chunk (trying to bite the whole thing at once) and don't under-chunk (a million pieces that make the task overwhelming). Chunking means taking everything coming at you and grouping it into idealized sizes your mind can handle.
- (2017-06-24, paraphrase) Closing call to action: pick a goal you've set aside but really want, and apply these principles to build a clear plan to move forward.
