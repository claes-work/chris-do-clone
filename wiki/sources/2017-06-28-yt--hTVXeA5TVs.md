# Can You Charge To Diagnose A Creative Problem?

★ **L3-candidate** — introduces a distinct, named paid-discovery framework ("Embrace and Pivot", "diagnose don't prescribe", "come in empty", "be prepared to recommend a solution that is not you") that goes beyond generic pricing/sales-clients material.

## Summary

A coaching-style episode of The Futur in which Chris Do walks host **Melinda** (context speaker) through *why* and *how* a creative professional can charge clients for the diagnostic (discovery) phase of a project, rather than only for deliverables. Melinda raises the question after reading Blair Enns' *Win Without Pitching Manifesto*, which notes that doctors and lawyers charge for diagnosis and asks why creatives don't.

Chris reframes the problem through analogies to professions that get paid to diagnose (doctor, lawyer, mechanic, therapist, real-estate agent). His core argument: creatives can only charge for diagnosis once they stop assuming they already know the answer (a new logo/website), acquire real business/marketing/conversion skills, and run an actual diagnostic process. Much of the episode is live role-play: Melinda plays a plumber ("Joe Mo Plumbing, Anaheim") self-diagnosing that he "needs a new website," and Chris coaches her through repeated failed attempts ("Groundhog Day" / "that Tom Cruise movie") to stop the client, embrace-and-pivot, and drill into the real business problem with questions.

## Key claims

- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** The first thing creatives must change is *mindset*: you cannot enter a problem assuming you already know the answer. If you come in saying "you need a new logo," your diagnostic process has no value.
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** Reframe the engagement away from artifacts (logo/website) toward the real problem type: a communication problem, a customer-experience problem, or a business problem. Only then can you provide real diagnosis and charge for it.
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** Clients typically arrive **self-diagnosed** (and self-prescribed). You are not supposed to accept the self-diagnosis; doing so makes you an order-taker. Analogy: a patient walking in saying "I have stage-X cancer, operate now, no biopsy" would be seen as insane.
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** Adopt professional practice like doctors/lawyers/mechanics/real-estate agents: (1) don't assume the answer, (2) ask a series of questions to narrow the scope of exploration.
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** "Stop, drop and roll" — the first move when a client self-diagnoses is to **stop** them from moving forward while keeping them engaged, then drill into what is driving the decision (lots of "why" questions).
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** **"Embrace and Pivot"**: you cannot do a hard pivot to "let me talk about your business." First acknowledge/validate the client's stated need by reflecting it back ("you probably need a new website"), *then* pivot to "but sometimes when I ask a few questions we discover you need something totally different — may I ask a few?"
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** Speak in plain English, like a regular human being — avoid marketing-speak or being "too slick."
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** The reason Melinda is "stuck" is that charging for diagnosis requires a genuinely new skill set: understanding business problems, marketing problems, and conversion issues. Doing strategy superficially "just to get the sale" yields no real results.
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** A real diagnostic is *specific* (like a doctor running a defined battery of tests), not a vague pretense of running tests. You can't charge to prescribe if you don't have the tools/knowledge.
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** Chris says he can charge "10 to 20 times as much" because he has acquired and keeps working on these skills; you can't speak from a platform of knowledge you don't have.
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase)** To diagnose faithfully you must be **prepared to recommend a solution that is not you** — like doctors/mechanics who refer to a specialist when the problem is outside their ability. This shifts you from order-taker to consultant, advisor, potentially partner.
- **[2017-06-28] (Chris, paraphrase of the mechanic analogy)** A proper diagnostic process = appointment → intake conversation with narrowing questions → investigation (e.g., plugging into the car's diagnostic port) → a call back with the problem, the fix, an **estimate** (time and materials), upsells for other found issues, and negotiation — ending in a prescription for what to do. This is the template creatives should map onto.
- **[2017-06-28] (Melinda, context, paraphrase)** She read the *Win Without Pitching Manifesto* on Chris's recommendation; it observes doctors and lawyers charge for diagnosis and asks why creatives don't. Her blocker: she doesn't know what a creative "diagnosis" looks like, how to charge for it, or how to quantify its value. Her clients usually arrive already self-diagnosed.
