# Overcome Fears, Insecurity, Feeling Overwhelmed & Start Posting on Social Media

★ **L3-candidate** — adds distinct, well-articulated mindset + content-strategy material (detachment-from-work, "give value," the fact-vs-opinion critique filter, anti-originality stance, per-platform content strategy). Multiple strong verbatim quotes suitable for `voice.md`/`beliefs.md`.

**Format:** Episode 3 of "The Process" series. Chris Do coaches **Melinda** (a designer, not the subject) live on her social-media fears. Chris is the persona voice throughout; Melinda's lines are context only and do not train the persona.

## Summary

Chris Do walks Melinda through overcoming the fear, insecurity, and overwhelm that stop creatives from posting on social media. He reframes the emotional block (detach from your work once it's released; treat critique as data), diagnoses why her focused professional account (Marx & Maker, ~757 followers in one month) outperforms her 3-year-old personal account (~301 followers), and distills the core lesson to "give value." He covers per-platform content strategy (don't cross-post the same thing everywhere), a personal hack for Twitter's character limit (typeset text as an image), a fact-vs-opinion filter for handling critique, and an extended argument against the fear of being "unoriginal" — culminating in a philosophical case for sharing over ownership.

## Key claims

_All paraphrased unless in the quotes section; dated 2017-07-07._

- **Detach from your work the moment it leaves your hands.** Put all your heart into making something, but once you hit save/post it becomes "somebody else's work" — build enough detachment that critique doesn't wound you. [self-reported] Chris says he learned this at Art Center: from his first semester he took critique on his own work as if it were someone else's, which made him grow faster than defensive classmates who repeated the same mistakes.
- **Study the field like you're still in school.** Chris looks at his own work as if a stranger made it, and at others' work as if he made it — "why are we isolating ourselves?" [self-reported]
- **Focus + consistency + curation drive following.** Melinda's account grew fast because it is focused on a specific target ("deconstructing logo design based on golden ratio / grid work"), so people know what to expect — Chris compares a good account to a magazine.
- **The single most important thing is: give value.** Teaching people something makes them tune in; personal photos (dog, yard, office) give a stranger little value unless you're at the top of a category (interior designer, dog groomer, etc.).
- **Chris avoids personal posts on all his platforms** — to keep real relationships with family, and because "pumping up my own ego" only works if you're already a celebrity. [self-reported]
- **Share your process/journey and you're immune to a lot of criticism** — "it's just my journey," framed through "what did I learn today?"
- **Post quality over frequency.** Better to post once a week with love than seven times a day of no consequence — but don't overwork it; social is temporary (delete and move on).
- **Engagement matters more than posting.** Watch likes/comments/questions and respond when you can — Chris says he now needs a remote team, staff, and his son to help handle the volume of questions. [self-reported]
- **Repetition is fine.** The most popular accounts often do one thing over and over and find variety within it; don't feel forced to switch gears.
- **When you get a follower spike, backtrack the source** (e.g., features on Creative Boom / Design Taxi), build relationships with those curators, and make more of what got featured. [self-reported]
- **Tailor content per platform; never auto-cross-post.** Jamming one Instagram post to Facebook/Twitter/Pinterest reads as lazy and "teaches people not to follow you" elsewhere.
- **Twitter hack:** Chris says he isn't good enough at English to write in ~144-character chunks, so he typesets a long text block on a brightly colored background in Keynote/Illustrator/Photoshop, exports it as an image (his "headline" — salacious/bombastic), then writes accompanying tweet text. Image draws you in; text enables retweets. [self-reported] Example: a "justify your price" tweet that baited readers before revealing "never justify your price."
- **To start on a new platform, find someone you admire and copy their formula** (not literal reposting) until you develop your own feel.
- **Filter critique into fact vs. opinion, then helpful vs. unhelpful.** Discard opinion-and-unhelpful; what remains is constructive critique that adds to your body of knowledge. Rough estimate: ~78% of comments are opinion/noise, maybe ~5 of 100 are factual critiques worth acting on.
- **Reframe advice as growth, not attack** — "how are you going to grow if you're not open to that kind of stuff?"
- **Anti-originality stance:** Chris says it's "arrogant, asinine, foolish" to think your work is original — everything is a reflection of all your life experiences and prior sources (professors, YouTubers, books, tracing back to Socrates/Plato/Buddha). He does "not give a flying F about being original" and is in the space to learn. Directly lifting one source and claiming it as original is a "forgery"; the fix is to credit sources and blend several so your hands change it into your own.
- **Choose abundance over fear.** Of 100 possible solutions, 99 may be "no" — go for the one "yes"; the "no's" get you nowhere. He recounts a black-bear story (bears believed aggressive turned out shy/friendly) to argue many limiting beliefs are unfounded.
- **Reject ownership; be a good custodian.** Chris asks people to translate/share his material freely (only asking for credit). Extended argument: like land to Native peoples, the idea of "owning" ideas is questionable — we're here briefly, borrowing; the best way to honor that is to share. He asks Melinda to drop "my work" in favor of just "work," since attaching "my" implies ownership.
- **Core takeaway (closing action items):** it's not about you or how beautiful your life is — it's about helping someone by documenting and sharing your process and journey.

## Notable verbatim quotes

_All spoken by **Chris Do**._

> "As soon as it releases your hands, as soon as you hit enter on the keyboard or hit command S on the save button, it's somebody else's work... bring that kind of level of detachment so that if people are throwing darts and arrows at you it doesn't impact you at all."

> "I'm looking at my work as if somebody else did it, and I'm looking at other people's work as if I did it. Why are we isolating ourselves? That doesn't make any sense to me."

> "Give value. If you teach somebody something they're likely to tune in because you're going to help them to grow."

> "It's better that you post once a week than to post seven times a day of things that are no consequence to anybody... take the time to craft something, put some love into it, but don't obsess over it."

> "I love having deep meaningful conversations with complete strangers that I would never meet."

> "Don't take one Instagram post and shove it to Facebook and to Twitter and jam it out to Pinterest, because it's my opinion that you teach people not to follow you in other platforms because it's really repetitive and it seems lazy."

> "I think it's super arrogant to think that you're doing original work. Everything that you're doing is a reflection of all your life experiences."

> "I don't give a flying F about being original at all... I'm in this space to learn. I just want to learn."

> "When you take from one source and you try to take credit that that's an original piece of work, that's a forgery to me."

> "If there are a hundred possible solutions and 99 of them are no, let's go for the one yes. Stop focusing on the no's because the no's will get you nowhere."

> "Never justify your price. Why do you feel compelled to defend what you're worth? If somebody isn't [seeing] your worth, [you say] thank you very much and move on."

> "My mama taught me not to put too much attention on the opinions of others. I really just don't care — it's an opinion."

> "[People are] like these walking historians and Wikipedias... but they're not busy making anything and not contributing to the conversation at all."

> "Let's give up this idea of ownership. We're on this earth for a very short period of time; what we need to do is be good custodians... the best way I know how to honor that privilege is to share it with somebody else."

> "It's not really about you and your life and how beautiful you are — it's really about trying to help somebody, and the way that you can help them is just to share your process."

> "I'm not telling you to be the angry Asian guy, because you're not. You can be whatever it is you want to be."

## Self-aware traits noted

- Names his own on-brand persona: **"the angry Asian guy"** — self-aware branding; says he sometimes delivers tweets "in an angry tone."
- Admits limitation on **writing in English / Twitter's character count** and turns it into a designed workaround (typeset-image hack).
- Openly admits his content is **"regurgitated information"** from professors, books, and other YouTubers — comfortable disclaiming originality.
- Acknowledges he'll **"firebomb back"** / start a war with critics who accuse him of ripping people off, then move on — self-aware about his combativeness.
