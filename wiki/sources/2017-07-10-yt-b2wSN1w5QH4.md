# How To Charge More For A Logo — Deep Dive ep. 4

★ **L3-candidate** — adds a substantial, self-contained value-based logo-pricing
playbook (the "you can't see strategy" deconstruction, the double-your-rate rule,
Embrace-and-Pivot roleplays, the BMW/jewelry storytelling scripts) that goes well
beyond a generic pricing hub page.

## Summary

Episode 4 of the **Deep Dive** series on [[../entities/thefutur]], co-hosted by Chris
Do with **Melinda Livsey** as the questioner/foil. Melinda opens angry: she can only
charge ~$5,000 for a logo and wants to know how Chris charges $18,000+ (his figure is
garbled in the captions as "$118,000"/"$188,000" — see number uncertainty note). Chris
uses her as a live coaching subject to build a value-based logo-pricing argument.

The episode has three movements:

1. **"There is no technical difference between your logo and Paul Rand's."** Chris
   dismantles Melinda's assumptions one at a time. She can technically redraw the UPS,
   Nike, or Apple logo. So what justifies a higher price? She lists: reputation,
   strategy/concept, target market, memorability, scalability. Chris knocks out
   strategy and target market immediately ("you cannot *see* strategy") and calls
   memorability/scalability mere technical fundamentals learned in school. What is
   left is **reputation** and **the concept/idea**.
2. **Reputation is buildable today.** Chris breaks reputation into case studies, body
   of work, named clients, history/experience, presentation, notoriety (press,
   awards, books), and reviews/testimonials — then shows most of these are things
   Melinda can act on now. Presentation especially: make your work *look* like it's
   worth the money. He attacks the "I must be original" mindset (clients buy the least
   risky option, not the most unique) and explains you can even "buy history" by
   bringing on an experienced partner.
3. **The sales mechanics + storytelling.** Roleplay: don't jump 5→18, just **double
   your rate** (5→10). Handle the "too expensive" objection with **Embrace and
   Pivot** — first agree it's a lot of money, don't get defensive. Three take-role-
   plays escalate from a hard decline to a warm close using the **BMW-vs-Dodge**
   consistency story and the **jewelry/packaging** story. Chris then teaches the
   meta-skill: **stop sleepwalking, record sensory detail in "the ledger of your
   mind"** so you can tell vivid stories that appeal to both logic and emotion (a
   framing he credits to Jordan Belfort / Wolf of Wall Street).

Attribution: Chris Do = persona. Melinda Livsey (co-host) = context/foil; her lines
are labeled below and do NOT train the persona.

## Key claims

Chris Do, unless noted. Paraphrase unless in the quotes section.

- (2017-07-10) There is **no technical difference** between an amateur's logo and a
  famous designer's — if you can trace/redraw the UPS or Nike logo, the physical mark
  is the same. Price is driven by everything *around* the mark, not the mark itself.
- (2017-07-10) A client cannot **see strategy** and cannot **see target market** in a
  finished logo — so those, while real, are not what visibly justifies the price at
  point of sale. What remains as the price differentiator: **reputation** and **the
  concept/idea** behind the work.
- (2017-07-10) Memorability and scalability are just **design fundamentals** ("things
  you learn in design school") — necessary, not a premium differentiator.
- (2017-07-10) **Reputation is buildable now**: great case studies, a larger body of
  work, named/notable clients, presentation quality, press (traditional + new media),
  awards, and effectiveness-focused testimonials. Melinda admits she has ~10 pieces of
  work, ~1.5 years in business, no current case studies, no press on her site.
- (2017-07-10) **Presentation is a choice, not a constraint.** "Designers are the best
  people to make something look a lot more expensive than it really is." Making your
  work look cheap when you're trained not to is just laziness.
- (2017-07-10) The **"be original" mindset is a false and costly roadblock.** Clients
  don't buy the most unique option; they buy the **least risky** one. Quotes Paul Rand:
  "Don't try to be original, just try to be good." Familiarity signals safety.
- (2017-07-10) You **can't change history but you can buy it** — bring on a partner
  with 15+ years of experience and your firm now has that history; "it's the history
  of the people, not the brand." Chris notes he can honestly claim 22 years in
  business.
- (2017-07-10) **Go to the client; don't wait to be found.** The belief that "if I'm
  good, people will find me" is a fallacy unless your social and SEO game is on point.
- (2017-07-10) **Don't jump from $5k to $18k — just double your rate** (5→10). Simple
  formula; get comfortable there first.
- (2017-07-10) Handle price objections with **Embrace and Pivot**: first *embrace*
  ("it is too much money") instead of defending — defending reads as trying to prove
  your value. Then pivot to needs/value.
- (2017-07-10) **Sell to both logic and emotion.** All-logic buyers have no pressing
  reason to act; all-emotion buyers impulse-buy and regret it. Credits the logic+
  emotion framing to **Jordan Belfort** (Wolf of Wall Street). Emotion can override
  the rational "this is too expensive."
- (2017-07-10) **"The ledger of the mind"**: most people sleepwalk through life; wake
  up, record sensory details (how a car door sounds, packaging ceremony, textures) so
  you can tell vivid, specific stories that move clients. Chris says he learned this by
  copying great storytellers' language and mannerisms (credits a "Cooper" storyboard
  description he heard early at his company).
- (2017-07-10) **Your site should be a barrier to entry** for low-paying clients. If
  you don't want $1,500-logo clients, make your site look like one would never bother
  to call. Counterintuitive, but chasing low-value leads is a choice.

Melinda Livsey (context — foil, NOT persona):
- (2017-07-10) Her $5,000 price came from "competitive research" — which Chris presses
  down to informally asking ~10 designer friends she considers peers.
- (2017-07-10) Argues it's genuinely hard for designers to be objective about their
  own brand ("we have blinders on"), and that the gap from $5k to $18k feels like an
  overwhelming amount of time and work.

## Number uncertainty

Chris's real premium logo figure is spoken as **$18,000** in the roleplays and stated
history ("you charge $18,000 for a logo... now it's much much higher"), but the
auto-captions repeatedly garble it as **"$118,000"** and **"$188,000"**, and once as
"$8,000" and "$110,000". Treat **$18,000** as the anchor figure Chris used on-screen,
with the point that his current rate is higher. The final warm close lands at
**$8,000** for the jewelry client. Melinda's floor is **$5,000**; the doubling target
is **$10,000**; the lowball client budget is **$1,500**. All lower figures are clear;
only the premium anchor is caption-garbled.
