# Document and Show Your Creative Process — Here's How in 3 Minutes

## Summary

A ~3-minute short from The Futur in which the presenter argues that designers
should make their creative process **visible and transparent** — showing the
journey from client brief (point A) to finished product (point B), not just the
end deliverable. The core case: portfolios and case studies that lead only with
the final application leave the "how" a mystery; showing a consistent, well-
documented process reduces the client's perceived risk and makes them more
inclined to reach out.

The video is a single-speaker monologue with no on-screen introduction. The
delivery, vocabulary, and framing ("client brief," "wireframes," "concept
models," "creative recipe," clients choose the least risky option) are strongly
consistent with Chris Do's teaching style and recurring themes, so the material
is attributed to **Chris Do**. Attribution is marked `uncertain` only because
the speaker is not explicitly named in the transcript and The Futur does publish
content from other instructors.

## Key claims

- **[2017-07-13]** (paraphrase) Designers should make visible and transparent how
  they get from point A (the client brief / problem to solve) to point B (the end
  product), rather than leading with the finished deliverable.
- **[2017-07-13]** (paraphrase) A typical process runs roughly: client brief →
  wireframes → concept models → research → final application; the made things sit
  at the *end*, and you should not lead with them.
- **[2017-07-13]** (paraphrase) If you strip out steps one through four and leave
  only step five (the end product), it's a mystery how the result was achieved —
  who the work was for, what problems were solved, and what tailored innovative
  solutions were reached.
- **[2017-07-13]** (paraphrase) Talking about your process communicates your
  *why*, your motivation, the problems you solve, and the benefits you create for
  the customer — this is how you tell your brand story.
- **[2017-07-13]** (paraphrase) Process alone is not enough: if the end product is
  bad, the case study doesn't matter — a clear, interesting case study with a poor
  final result still fails.
- **[2017-07-13]** (paraphrase) Concrete example for web design work — a five-step
  formula such as: user profiles (stage one) → UX sketching → visual design /
  mockups (stage three, between wireframe and mockup) → and so on — beautifully
  documented (e.g., drawn on grid paper), showing you are a good documentarian of
  your own work.
- **[2017-07-13]** (paraphrase) Describe each step as part of your creative formula
  so prospects can understand your work "from afar" — before they even call or
  email — and know what they're getting into.
- **[2017-07-13]** (paraphrase) Clients don't choose the *best* option, they choose
  the *least risky* option; you reduce perceived risk by showing a consistently
  applied process that reads like a repeatable "creative recipe."
