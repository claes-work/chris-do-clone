**Chris's coaching claims (Chris-attributed — persona-relevant):**
- Anything is realistic and attainable if you're willing to put in the work, make sacrifices, and delay gratification. Most people want everything but are willing to give up nothing (their weekends, their evenings). [Chris]
- Chris's own goal: grow The Futur into a $100 million company ("it's going to happen"), which requires sacrifice and dedication. [Chris]
- Decompose big goals: divide the annual revenue goal into quarters, then months, so $100k → $25k/quarter → ~$8,500/month gross (he deliberately overshoots the monthly target so you don't fall short). Work in gross, not net. [Chris]
- Always estimate the gap slightly high so you don't come up short. [Chris]
- The fastest path to Melinda's goal is raising rates, not more volume: charge $8,000–$10,000 per logo instead of $5,000 and one logo a month roughly hits the monthly number. [Chris]
- Confidence to charge more comes from polishing your presentation and revisiting your own past work — distance lets you see it "with fresher eyes," almost as if done by someone else, and it rebuilds confidence. [Chris]
- Don't confuse a tactic with a goal. Press, awards, and case studies are tactics; the goal is the revenue number. Be clear about the ultimate goal first, then choose tactics to get there. [Chris]
- Prioritization exercise (framework): list every growth activity in a wide first column, then score each on impact and urgency (1–5 or 1–10) against your goal; circle the top three highest-scoring items and focus only on those. Add a fourth column for money/budget (relative $ signs, 1–5) — some high-impact items you can simply hire out (e.g. an SEO team) rather than doing yourself. This mirrors The Futur's "core kit" prioritization. [Chris]
- Score impact relative to the actual goal (revenue), not to vanity (being famous/liked). Behance = high impact (a 5); SEO = low impact for her right now (a 1–2), because organic searchers for "graphic designer in Orange County" are bargain hunters chasing $50 logos and know nothing about her — SEO won't help until she becomes a thought leader. [Chris]
- Write list items as concrete action items ("build a database of external resources"), not vague nouns ("collaboration"), so you can actually go do them. [Chris]
- Collaboration/finding partners is only high-impact if it produces work right away; if you're getting no work, a partner list gives you nothing yet (scored it a 2). [Chris]
- The focus sheet should line up with your goals so you move forward instead of being "busy and getting nowhere." Chop goals down to weekly/daily/hourly tasks so you always know what to do; new off-plan ideas get deferred ("not right now"). [Chris]
- Crossing items off a daily checklist is an addictive, consequence-free feeling of measurable progress — like fitness/sports people keeping a workout journal — and it compounds focus, productivity, and efficiency. [Chris]
- Recalibrate every ~3–4 weeks / end of month: review what happened, whether goals were hit, what went well, what to improve. New things flood in as you finish, so you constantly reshuffle. [Chris]
- Use the prioritization criteria as your "fake imaginary friend" — a decision-making partner. Chris recounts that collaborating with a friend in college got 2–3× more work done because they held each other accountable and made decisions faster. The same criteria that work for you also work for your clients. [Chris]
- When two items score equal on impact and urgency, rescore them more granularly to break the tie. [Chris]
- Every business needs three things: a strong identity, social media marketing, and a website. Covering two or three (not just one) means clients don't have to find other vendors. Build collaborator relationships (e.g. web developers) now so you're not scrambling when the work arrives. [Chris]
- Get known / go where the clients are: put work on Behance, get featured on blogs, enter design competitions, do self-promo/direct response, write micro-content on Medium/LinkedIn (break case studies into bite-sized pieces), set appointments (lunch once or twice a week), go on podcasts. Channel viewers who click through are mostly other designers, not clients. [Chris]
- Sell higher-value services by knowing more than the client: strategy/discovery (brand audit, social media scorecard), keynote deck design (The Futur charges ~$10,000, thinking of raising to $5k–$20k), catalogs/brochures, social banners/templates, email blast templates, and short animated videos. You can resell specialist partners' services (e.g. SEO partners) by leveraging relationships and knowledge. [Chris]
- List extra services on your site for SEO benefit, but don't build case studies around them (distracting from the primary task); introduce them situationally after discovery/identity work. [Chris]
