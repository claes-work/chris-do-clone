# Feeling Overwhelmed — Information Overload (The Process / Deep Dive ep. 6 w/ Melinda Livsey)

★ L3-candidate — introduces two distinct, reusable frameworks for coping with
information overload (the "learn with a purpose / shopping list vs. hoarding"
model and the "backpack" capacity metaphor) plus a focus/procrastination
diagnostic, none of which are yet captured in `wiki/topics/mindset`.

## Summary

Sixth episode of "The Process" (a.k.a. Deep Dive) coaching series in which Chris
Do coaches [[../entities/melinda-livsey]] (Futur instructor, here in the role of
coachee — context, not persona). Melinda opens by saying she is overwhelmed with
information and does not know what to do with it. Chris diagnoses the problem
through questioning: she has been consuming books (e.g. *Stop Stealing Sheep*,
Douglas Davis's *The Creative Strategy and Business of Design*, *101 Things I
Learned in Advertising School*), logo studies, articles, video tutorials, and
especially the Futur's own YouTube back-catalog, faster than she can apply any of
it.

Chris reframes the issue as a **consumption-without-application** problem and
offers several tools: go deep on one concept at a time rather than spreading
thin; read/watch **with a specific purpose ("shopping list")** instead of
**hoarding**; imagine two selves (one running the business, one learning) so the
business self sets the learning agenda; and picture a limited-capacity
**backpack** you can only fill with survival-essential items. He also surfaces
that Melinda has been using design study to **procrastinate** on the scarier
business/marketing skills she actually needs, tells her her design is already
"good enough," and urges her to redirect energy to onboarding clients and
marketing.

The back half turns therapeutic: Chris probes the inner "voice" telling Melinda
she is not good enough, gives her homework to trace that voice to a real person
(often a parent/mentor figure), and closes with reassurance that she is "good
enough" and does not need to earn self-love. Chris also candidly discusses his
own relationship to overwhelm, passion, and outworking naturally talented people
(Latin, drawing classes, Halo).

## Key claims

Attribution key: **Chris = persona** (subject); **Melinda = context** only.

- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) When facing information overload, go
  **deep** — master one concept at a time — rather than spreading yourself thin
  across many sources.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) Read to *understand and apply*: for every
  thing you learn, ask "where can I apply this today?" — anything applicable
  immediately makes you more valuable / makes you money.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) "Action beats intent" — a good idea stays
  mere intent until you apply it to something you are doing; only then does it
  become action, and action is where the goal is.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) You do not have to read a book A-to-Z;
  "mine" it for the content most applicable and impactful to what you do today,
  then start applying.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) Consume **with a purpose** — go in with a
  specific "shopping list" of questions/problems so your mind hunts for answers,
  instead of **hoarding** information indiscriminately. Set the questions
  *before* you consume.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) The "two people in one body" model: the
  self running the business tells the self doing the learning what to look for,
  so learning serves current business problems (e.g. better design, overcoming
  objections).
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) The **backpack metaphor**: early in your
  journey your capacity is limited, so be highly selective — only pack
  survival-and-thrive essentials (skip the "small TV" nice-to-haves). Over time
  the "magic backpack" grows and you get stronger, so later you can afford to
  hoard.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase, self-reported) Chris says he personally is
  far to the "information-overload" right end of the spectrum — an admitted
  hoarder with a near-infinite backpack — because everything he has been deeply
  passionate about and studied has come back to help him.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) Overload lives on a spectrum: far left =
  consume just to survive (fine); far right = unmanageable overload; each
  person's "sweet spot" is in between and differs by person.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) We gravitate to what we are already good
  at because it feels good and hurts less; you must make the "other voice" tell
  that self to shut up and do the scary, hated, procrastinated-on things — you
  hit diminishing returns doubling down on craft you have already mastered
  (most people can't even see the difference between two skilled designers).
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) Melinda's design is already "good enough";
  she should stop over-investing in design and shift energy to onboarding
  clients, marketing herself, and going where clients are.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) Pursue things you are **passionate** about
  regardless of whether you are good at them; do NOT get good at things you are
  not passionate about, because you will just get more of that thing (don't get
  good at sweeping floors you hate).
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase, self-reported) With passion, Chris
  believes he can outwork/out-hustle naturally talented people through discipline
  even if he starts behind (Halo analogy: he learned the maps and mechanics
  instead of relying on shortcuts/cheats and eventually beat friends who excelled
  quickly).
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase, self-reported) Chris does get overwhelmed
  and knows his limits — cited struggling with Latin and advanced calculus,
  dropping a community-college Latin class (didn't know what "conjugate" meant),
  and dropping VizCom 3 at Art Center (couldn't draw a box) — and generally
  doesn't pursue things long unless he believes he can do them really well /
  won't enter competitions expecting to lose.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) On self-worth: when someone says they are
  "trying to prove themselves to themselves," Chris is suspicious — alone, we
  don't judge ourselves. He assigns homework to trace the internal
  "you're-not-good-enough" voice to a real person (often a parent/mentor), then
  "break that down and destroy it." You do not need to earn the love of yourself.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) Sometimes all people need is to hear
  "you're good enough" from someone they respect; Chris positions himself
  self-deprecatingly as "just some wanker on the internet" happy to give that
  validation.
- **[Chris, 2017-07-26]** (paraphrase) Chris describes his own teaching niche:
  he is about **tactics and practical, actionable things you can do today**, not
  spiritual well-being / self-alignment — and tells viewers for whom that's a
  turnoff to go find their own mentor/channel that speaks their language.
- **[Melinda, 2017-07-26]** (context, paraphrase) She realizes she was using
  pursuing design skills to **procrastinate** on business skills, because she
  knew the design resources and it kept her "busy" avoiding the unknown of
  business.
- **[Melinda, 2017-07-26]** (context, paraphrase) She reflects that the coaching
  has changed her mindset, goals, and how she consumes information; reading the
  show's comments and re-engaging afterward makes the lessons "sink in."
- **[Melinda, 2017-07-26]** (context, paraphrase) Years ago she would have
  declined appearing on the show — she was a perfectionist trying to prove
  herself and only attempted things she knew she'd succeed at; growth came from
  sharing struggles vulnerably with others.
