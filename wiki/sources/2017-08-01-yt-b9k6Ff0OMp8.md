# How To Respond To A RFP (Request for Proposal)? What Should You Include In Your Proposal?

## Summary

★ **L3-candidate** — This is a canonical, compact teaching on two of Chris Do's most-referenced sales tactics: **price bracketing** (discovering a client's budget by naming two goal-post numbers) and **competitive positioning against a bigger and smaller competitor**. Worth promoting into `pricing/` and `sales-clients/` topic pages plus `beliefs.md`.

Chris Do lays out what an RFP is and what a proposal response should contain: a bio page of the key players, references / big clients / testimonials, contact information, the studio's process (framed as "the five steps"), and relevant case studies. His framing is competitive — you present not just "the best you," but "the best you in light of the competition you're going to be facing."

The core of the video is budget discovery via **price bracketing**. Rather than guessing, he asks the client directly, offering two numbers as goal posts (e.g., "Do you have between \$2,000 and \$8,000?"). If they balk, he asks for their price point, then brackets upward from that number (if they say \$5,000, he quotes "between 5 and 10" or "5 and 15") so the floor of the deal is the client's own stated number. He also endorses walking away or referring the client out when the project isn't a fit — a move that often makes the client want to keep talking.

The final segment is competitive positioning. Chris argues you are always up against both a cheaper, smaller competitor and a larger, more experienced agency, and the job is to "create some doubt in the client's mind" about both. Against the small shop: agree that if they want to save money they should go with the one-person shop — reverse psychology that surfaces the risk of the ball being dropped. Against the big agency: emphasize personal, high-touch service — "you're going to be working with me... you call me," backed by reputation, referenceable clients, and even offering his cell phone number and help "keeping the other guys honest" in evaluating competing bids. The through-line is carving out a defensible niche between the "basement" price and the "800lb gorilla."

- [2017-08-01] (paraphrase) If you want \$5,000 to design a website, bracket "between 5 and 10" or "5 and 15" — the least you'll get paid is \$5,000, and the client may negotiate you to something like \$3,800, which you can accept if it works.
- [2017-08-01] (paraphrase) Have an open conversation and don't be afraid to say you'll refer the client elsewhere if you can't do the project — often this makes the client want to keep talking and learn more about you.

- [2017-08-01] (paraphrase) To defeat both competitors you have to create doubt in the client's mind about each.
- [2017-08-01] (paraphrase) Against the small shop, use reverse psychology: agree that if they want to save money they should work with the one-person shop that lacks experience — pushing them toward it makes them not want to do it and casts doubt on the smaller shop dropping the ball.
