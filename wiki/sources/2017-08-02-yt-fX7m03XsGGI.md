# How To Build A Social Media Following — Get Started

## Summary

Short talk by Chris Do (delivered to a room of designers/creatives, apparently a workshop or live audience) answering an audience question: how do you turn an entire company of introverted designers into a "social media machine" when they don't want to self-promote. Chris's core reframe is that building a following is not self-promotion but **documenting your work** — he leans heavily on Austin Kleon's book *Show Your Work*, framing social media as keeping a public journal/mental scrapbook of what inspires you and what you're working on. The antidote to the introvert's discomfort is to become "a documentarian of the work you do" rather than a poser or a polished performer.

He gives a filter for what to share ("is this something somebody would share, or better yet, pay to learn?"), pushes "process not product," and argues that even non-creators can succeed by collecting and curating rather than creating. He tells two case-study stories: Jeanette Leal, a letterer with ~60k Instagram followers who got recruited by Nike a few years out of school by sharing her process; and "local_inspirations," a large-account curator (300k+ followers) whose reshare of one of Chris's videos caused measurable follower spikes and who, as a "side-hustle Instagrammer," earns close to his full-time graphic-design salary from social media ("making money while he sleeps").

The back half turns tactical: **speak natively to each platform** (don't replicate the same post everywhere — that disincentivizes cross-following); Instagram is visual, Facebook allows longer articles/multiple images, Twitter rewards wit or (his workaround) a strong image. He advises creating a personal brand guideline even if you're not a designer (he favors bold colors, bold typography, bold imagery), using relevant hashtags for discoverability, keeping personal/family content off business channels (put it on Facebook/Shutterfly), and running separate niche channels so streams don't mix. He also endorses **deleting old low-quality posts** to clean up a feed (you don't lose followers), and closes on his signature claim here: **quality beats frequency** — he says posting less drove a 400% average engagement uptick, doing more in two to three crafted posts than in thirty. Ends with a tool list.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona training material)

- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Building a following isn't self-promotion or posing — reframe it as keeping a public journal and becoming "a documentarian of the work you do." He trains and embeds this mindset into everyone at his company (Blind).
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Leans on Austin Kleon's *Show Your Work*: share bits and pieces — research, reference, sketches, plans, interviews, audio, photography, video, mood boards, journals, prototypes, demos, diagrams, inspiration, stories — that become a multimedia narrative you're building over time.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Sharing doesn't just build an audience; it helps you learn about yourself and document where you were in time/space, so you can recreate your process later.
- `[2017-08-02]` (exact quote) "Experts sometimes don't make the best teachers because they forget how it was done" — they forget the struggle; it becomes effortless. (Attributes the idea to someone he "often" cites; attribution of the underlying source is uncertain.)
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Filter for what to share: ask "is this something that somebody would share, or better yet [pay] to learn this thing?" A post that asks the audience to participate is even better.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) "Think process not product" — share the different stages of creation, not just finished work. Don't release only the most beautiful things.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Jeanette Leal, ~60k Instagram followers, ~2–3 years out of school, was recruited by Nike (Nike came to her) purely on the strength of sharing her lettering process — evidence the outcome is repeatable.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Collecting vs. creating: if you're not a creator, curate and show your collections — you can do very well as a curator.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) People in the social-media space are mostly not experts, so they're very open about sharing their process, tips, techniques, and tools.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Learn to speak natively to each platform; don't replicate posts across all channels — doing so removes the incentive to follow you on more than one. Give each platform a different take on what you do.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Platform differences: Facebook lets you write, include multiple images, and create articles; Instagram is about the visual (but a good written story still gets read — the data backs it up); Twitter rewards wit/humor/writing — since he's "not super witty," he competes by including a cool image.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Create a personal brand guideline even if you're not a designer; he personally likes bold colors, bold typography, bold imagery.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Use relevant hashtags — that's how images get linked so searchers land in the same feed (compares it to Amazon "you might also like").
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Keep personal content (kids, parents, grandparents, food, pets) on a personal Facebook/Shutterfly, off business channels; run separate niche channels (a business page for business, a camera page for camera content) so streams don't mix.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) If you think a post is hot but your followers don't, delete it — he's going back and deleting old "stupid"/junk posts to clean up his feed, and you don't lose followers by doing so.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) **Quality beats frequency**: he doesn't post often, posts in spurts, and saw a ~400% average engagement uptick by posting less, not more — doing more in two to three posts than in thirty. Take time to craft; write content, create value, then give it away. It's a conversation/dialogue — engage with your audience (anti-spam).
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Tool suggestions: BuzzSumo, Podtrac ("pod track"), Google Analytics, SEMrush, Crazy Egg, Hotjar.

### local_inspirations story (Chris Do reported, context)

- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase, [self-reported]) Chris normally gains ~2–3 Instagram followers/day and isn't very active; after "local_inspirations" (a 300k+ follower account) clipped and reshared one of his videos, he saw sudden spikes (~50 then ~120 new followers in a day), and each mention reliably produces a spike. He reached out and planned a podcast with the account owner.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase, [self-reported]) The account owner is a full-time graphic designer and side-hustle Instagrammer who told Chris he now makes close to as much money from social media as from his day job — "making money while he sleeps" — and posts very consistently.
- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Internal Blind competition: whoever organically grows their Instagram by 500 followers first gets a Disneyland ticket (or similar) bought by everyone else.

### Audience member (context, not persona)

- `[2017-08-02]` (paraphrase) Asked the seed question: wants to turn his whole company — "full of introverted designers and creative types" — into a social-media machine, but the team doesn't want to self-promote/pose; where do you start, and do they even have anything important to say?
