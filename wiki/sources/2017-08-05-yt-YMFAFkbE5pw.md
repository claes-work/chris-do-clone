## Summary

Episode 7 of The Futur's "The Process." **Chris Do** coaches **Melinda Livsey** (a Futur instructor / practicing brand designer — CONTEXT, not the persona) on how to "level up" her onboarding and sales process as she transitions from solopreneur toward hiring specialists (web, video/animation) and taking on larger multi-service projects (brand + print + web). Melinda's stated problem: her intake process is dialed for brand identity, but she "fumbles" and doesn't know what to ask when a project spans web too.

The episode is built around **two sales role-plays**, both using a fictional beverage-company client who saw Melinda's real "Clean Kanteen" project and wants a rebrand + new label + website (the client is "going nationwide in Whole Foods"):

- **Role-play 1 — Melinda = DESIGNER, Chris = CLIENT.** Melinda demonstrates her *current* process on a live call; Chris plays a probing client. Melinda struggles, gets tight/nervous "because of the cameras," fumbles defining scope and explaining deliverables (e.g. can't cleanly explain "secondary logo"). Chris repeatedly breaks scene to coach.
- **Role-play 2 — Chris = DESIGNER, Melinda = CLIENT.** After a role-swap, Chris demonstrates how *he* would run the same first call while pretending to have Melinda's constraints ("I'm going to pretend I don't know anything about digital"). This is the model demonstration: control the conversation, qualify budget by floating numbers, be honest about what's new (web), be enthusiastic, close by confirming the price and next steps.

Between and after the role-plays, Chris delivers the teaching: reading the client's face/body over the phone, projecting enthusiasm ("it's almost like I'm flirting with you"), the "smile so your tone changes" trick, never getting into the proposal-building business for free, the "I don't need this client" money-mindset (his box metaphor), knowing objections in advance, the objection bell curve across career stages, and — as the next level beyond this episode — charging for Discovery.

## Key claims
*(All dated `[2017-08-05]`. Paraphrased unless in quotes. Grouped by speaker.)*

### Chris Do (persona voice — training data)

Coaching commentary (Chris speaking as himself):
- `[2017-08-05]` The first client call is high-level, not a scoping session: it's about building rapport, earning trust, and demonstrating enthusiasm/passion — you do NOT do all the scoping on the first call. (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` Before you hang up, confirm the price range back to the client and get a yes/no — otherwise you walk away and build a full proposal (milestones, breakdown, combing the site) for a prospect who already privately decided it's too expensive but was too embarrassed to say so. "You don't want to be in the proposal building business because you get paid exactly zero dollars to build proposals." (quote/paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` Ask about budget in a way the client can decline without losing face or feeling embarrassed — like being in a fancy shop you can't afford, people won't blurt out "I can't afford this." (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` Over the phone you can't read faces, so warmth must come through voice: project enthusiasm and positivity. Chris frames this as "almost like I'm flirting with you" — meaning paying a compliment and making the person feel good, NOT sexual innuendo. (paraphrase + quote)
- `[2017-08-05]` One concrete technique: say things with a smile even though the client can't see you — your tone changes and it comes through. (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` You usually can't win on price ("most of the time we're the highest bid... I have to win it on something else" — charm, presence, engagement, empathy). (quote/paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` Weave back what you heard the client say (their Whole Foods rollout, that you're a fan/shop there) — it subliminally signals "I heard you, I believe what you believe," which garners trust. (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` Know the objections before you get on the call, and know how you'll respond; over years you've heard every objection. (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` Money-mindset: tell yourself "I don't need this client." Put your emotional attachment/expectations "in a box"; put the things you're confident about in one box and the things you're not confident about in another. Detaching from needing the win removes the anxiety that makes your face/tone go tight. (paraphrase; box metaphor)
- `[2017-08-05]` Believe you bring so much value that a prospect choosing a cheaper vendor "made a big mistake" — but you have to earn that real self-confidence by doing the work (reading, watching, applying), "you can't hire someone else to do your push-ups for you." (paraphrase + quote)
- `[2017-08-05]` Seeing yourself in the third person — and reading the room — is a valuable, trainable skill (he ties it to a golf-lesson analogy). (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` You must know your own deliverables and process cold, so describing them is "one flow," not stumbling or reading a script — otherwise you're not ready for the conversation and the client suspects you invented it to jack up the price. (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` The next level (flagged as beyond this episode): **charge for Discovery.** "If you're able to help the client solve a problem through talking you should charge money for it" — call it strategy, discovery, or a brand audit; start with a nominal fee (~$500). (quote/paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` Real Discovery is helping the client realize something they didn't know — NOT asking their favorite colors or brands they love. (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` Objection bell curve across a career: cheap low-end clients throw many objections ("cheap wankers"); the mid-range sweet spot has very few objections and good money; six-figure engagements bring a whole new set of objections. Stay in the middle and it goes "like Swiss ball bearings." (paraphrase + quote)
- `[2017-08-05]` [self-reported] Chris used to charge $5,000 for websites; a later website came in around $80,000–$100,000 — "you can go from 5,000 to 80 really fast if you learn what to do." (quote/paraphrase, self-reported financials)
- `[2017-08-05]` Reading a client physically: he watches for a facial tick, clenched jaw, fluttering eyes — the same signal can mean different things (searching for words vs. lying); he uses it to sense discomfort with a price the client verbally accepted. (paraphrase)

Chris as the DESIGNER in Role-play 2 (his demonstrated technique — persona-relevant; prices are illustrative of the method, not a current rate card):
- `[2017-08-05]` Open by thanking the client for calling and asking what they saw / what they want; then ask the expansion question: "Beyond [the makeover], if we hit it out of the ballpark, what else could we do?" — surfacing future products, marketing, advertising. (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` Be openly honest that web is a new service: "I need to understand a little bit more about what that's about... I'm going to have to do some auditing... I obviously can't do it on this first call, so what I like to do is just kind of float some numbers by you to see what you're comfortable spending." (quote)
- `[2017-08-05]` Bid the uncertain (web) as a range and on the high side: "I'm going to bid on the high side because I don't want to go out of business because I underbid the job — it's not fair to you, it wouldn't be fair to me either." (quote)
- `[2017-08-05]` Ask the decision question directly: "You're obviously considering multiple vendors — how will you make the decision as to who you work with?" (quote)
- `[2017-08-05]` Close the call with the process and terms: proposal by end of week → client signs → 50% deposit to start → "we're off to the races." (paraphrase + quote)
- `[2017-08-05]` Don't over-sell: "I could have sold a lot more to you, but you were so agreeable... I'm not going to offer up things" that aren't yet in the client's wheelhouse (e.g. full strategy/Discovery). (quote/paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` On the first call, assess whether they can afford you before deep scoping — "if we're not in the same ballpark I can't work with you." (paraphrase)

### Melinda Livsey (CONTEXT ONLY — not persona training data)

- `[2017-08-05]` Her current process is tuned for one-person brand-identity work; as she grows and brings in specialists (web, video/animation) she doesn't know how to onboard those collaborators or ask the right scoping questions for bigger multi-service projects. (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` She "fumbled" on a real inquiry needing branding + a website — didn't know the questions to ask or how to adapt her narrow brand-focused intake. (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` As the DESIGNER in Role-play 1, her demonstrated pricing (her own process, quoted while playing herself): branding ~$8,000–$10,000; print starting at ~$5,000 depending on bottle/label count and complexity; web starting at ~$15,000. (paraphrase — Melinda's numbers, context)
- `[2017-08-05]` Self-critique: on camera she flutters her eyes searching for words, felt nervous because she'd never landed a project like this and had "nothing to reference or pull for," and found it hard to view herself in the third person in the moment. (paraphrase)
- `[2017-08-05]` A real client once told her they chose her because they "felt validated" and she was "so enthusiastic about our project" (Melinda offering this as confirmation of Chris's enthusiasm point). (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes
*(Auto-captions, lightly cleaned for readability; no speaker labels in source — attribution inferred from context. Chris quotes = persona training data; Melinda quotes = context only.)*

### Chris Do (persona voice — training data)

On what the first call is:
> "First call is high level. First call is about building rapport, earning trust, demonstrating enthusiasm and passion, and that you're not a BS-er and you know what you're talking about."

On never building free proposals:
> "You don't want to be in the proposal building business, because you get paid exactly zero dollars to build proposals... I want to be very clear about that: if we go away and come back with a bid for these things in this price range, are you okay with that? And you have to ask it in a way that I can say no and not lose face and feel embarrassed."

On enthusiasm / "flirting":
> "It's almost like I'm flirting with you. I want you to feel good... A lot of people assume flirting has some kind of sexual innuendo, and it's not — it's about paying a compliment to somebody, making them feel good about themselves."

> "Some of the clients I flirt with are men. I just want them to feel good about the process of working with us... I want them to imagine working with us, and with me and the team, it's going to be better than everybody else — cuz most of the time we're the highest bid. I already know that going in, so I'm not going to win it on price, I have to win it on something else."

The smile trick:
> "One simple trick is to say things with a smile, even though they can't see you. If you say things with a smile, it comes out a little bit differently... your tone changes."

On trust:
> "The more you're able to weave in what you heard into the conversation, subliminally you're sending a signal to that person that 'I heard you, man, and I believe what you believe, I feel what you feel, we have the same values.' That garners trust — t-r-u-s-t — it garners trust. You need more of it and this is how you do it."

The money-mindset / not needing the client:
> "In my life, I'm happy where I'm at right now. I don't need this client, and whatever they have to offer me would be nice, but it's not something I need right now. You have to take away all your expectations and put them into another box."

> "I don't need this client. I don't care if I have this client, and if they pick somebody else they made a big mistake in their life. That's how I feel... So go ahead, please, work with the other person, pay less money, try to take a shortcut — and I truly believe this, if it doesn't work out it's because you didn't pick the right partner."

> "You can't hire someone else to do your push-ups for you. You've got to do the work yourself, and when you can do that, that belief, that self-confidence, it will be there, it will be real."

On knowing objections and bidding high on the unknown (Chris as designer, RP2):
> "I'm going to bid on the high side, because I don't want to go out of business because I underbid the job. It's not fair to you, it wouldn't be fair to me either. So we'll do a little discovery, we'll figure it out, and then we'll bid it correctly once we know what we're building."

> "What I like to do is just kind of float some numbers by you to see what you're comfortable spending, and then I sit down with my developer and we'll figure out what we should do and how we should approach it."

On not over-selling:
> "I could have sold a lot more to you, but you were so agreeable... typically here I'd get into whole strategy and discovery, but that's not part of your wheelhouse yet, so I'm not going to offer up things."

Charging for Discovery (the "next level"):
> "If you're able to help the client solve a problem through talking, you should charge money for it — whatever you want to call it: strategy, discovery, a brand audit call... The first thing you want to do is validate that your process is worth money by charging for it. Let's start with some nominal fee, let's say $500."

> "People, don't mistake this — asking questions like what your favorite colors are and the brands that you love, that is not discovery at all. Discovery is about helping the client realize something they didn't know."

The objection bell curve:
> "At the beginning of your career, for lower-level clients, they have a lot of objections because they're cheap wankers... Somewhere in the middle, where you're heading, very few objections... When you get into the six-figure engagement, you've got a whole bunch of new objections. So as long as you're in the middle of that bell curve, life is good."

Self-reported pricing trajectory:
> "It happened for me. I was charging $5,000 websites, it's horrible. I think the next website we got was 100 grand or 80 grand or something like that. So you can go from 5,000 to 80 really fast if you learn what to do."

On reading the room / third person:
> "Seeing yourself in the third person is a very valuable skill... not only seeing yourself, but reading the situation in the room. I'm thinking about what you're saying, but I'm also reading your face the entire time."

On knowing your own process cold:
> "Notice when I talked about the deliverables and how I do what I do, it's just one flow. I'm not stumbling through it, I'm not reading a script. If I don't know my own process and what I'm going to deliver, then I'm not ready for this kind of conversation."

### Melinda Livsey (CONTEXT ONLY — not persona training data)

Her stated problem:
> "My current process is really good for branding and me being a one-man show, but as I grow and want to bring in other creatives with specialties — say web design or maybe even video animation — I don't know how to bring those people in with my current process."

Her self-critique after Role-play 1:
> "Fluttering my eyes, definitely searching for what I want to say... I felt nervous because I haven't had a project like this that has panned out. Usually when I approach something I approach it with my previous knowledge and experience — and I don't have that... so I don't have anything to reference or pull for."

Confirming the enthusiasm point (a real client to her):
> "One client had said to me, 'We really want to go with you because we felt validated and you're so enthusiastic about our project.'"
