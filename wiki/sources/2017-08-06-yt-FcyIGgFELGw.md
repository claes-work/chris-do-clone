# How To Respond When Clients Say You're Too Expensive — Roleplay

★ **L3-candidate.** Reason: This is a dense, self-contained objection-handling script — a
live role-play where Chris Do voices the designer AND narrates the tactics ("here's why I do
this"). It contains reusable pricing-negotiation playbook material (anchoring, the walk-away,
reframing "future work" bribes, holding a floor price, high-anchor stress-testing, the
insurance/gambling analogies) that belongs in `wiki/topics/pricing` and `persona/beliefs.md`
+ `persona/voice.md`. Do not promote from this L2 page — flag only.

## Summary

A recorded live role-play. Chris Do sets the scenario: a client wants a logo, the designer
wants to charge ~$500 (later escalated), and the client will resist. Chris instructs a Futur
team member to **play the client** ("be the client... I don't want you to be a sympathetic
creative person") and Chris himself **plays the designer** — the client addresses the
designer as "Sheri"/"Sher" (character name; the client persona is "Mr John Smith"). Between
exchanges Chris breaks character to explain the negotiation logic to the audience.

The role-play walks through a full "too expensive" objection sequence: the designer anchors at
$1,000–$2,000 against the client's $200 reference point; converts the "another designer only
charged $200" objection into a cost-of-missed-deadlines question; ties price to the client's
own hourly value; refuses to "sell"; delivers a calm walk-away ("if that's not a good fit, I
totally understand... but if things change, reach out"); dismantles the "give us a discount now
and we'll bring you future work" bribe by mirroring it back; asks the client's maximum budget;
and concedes to a one-time $750 while explicitly stating future work returns to full price.
Chris then debriefs: never negotiate at your true floor (you'll get dragged below it), and
anchoring high on purpose is fine even if it provokes a "that's crazy" reaction — he closes by
demonstrating a $5,000–$10,000 anchor defended with insurance/gambling analogies.

## Key claims

### Chris Do — coaching / negotiation principles (out of character)

- [2017-08-06] For a role-play, tell the client-player to be adversarial, not a "sympathetic
  creative person," so the practice is realistic. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] Never negotiate starting from your true final price — if $500 is already your
  floor, opening there gets you "dragged down" to $400 or $350. Hold the floor by anchoring
  above it. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] After the client contemplated $750, the designer can still tell them to go get a
  cheaper $500 option — that concession leaves the designer "room to go a little bit lower" but
  Chris refuses to negotiate at his real floor. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] Anchoring far above the client's budget deliberately provokes an "allergic," "this
  is crazy" reaction — and that's acceptable; you defend the anchor rather than retreat from it.
  (paraphrase)

### Chris Do — designer lines (in character)

- [2017-08-06] Price the logo at $1,000–$2,000, framed as based on analyzing the client's
  business, communications, and marketing goals. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] When the client cites a $200 competitor, congratulate them ("you got a really good
  deal"), then surface the hidden cost: the cheap designer missed deadlines — "how much did that
  cost you in terms of opportunity when a deadline was missed?" (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] Tie price to the client's own worth: a successful entrepreneur's time is worth "in
  excess of $500 an hour," so cheap work that consumes their management time is expensive.
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] Refuse to "sell" — reframe the conversation as the client seeking a better solution
  than they currently get (design quality, professionalism, customer experience, doing what you
  say you'll do). (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] "What my clients come to me for isn't necessarily for the logo" — the logo is a
  commodity available from $5 on Fiverr up to an international branding firm; the value is the
  solution and experience. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] Deliver a calm walk-away: if the price isn't a fit, "I totally understand, use the
  options available to you" — but if you get frustrated, get subpar work, or miss deadlines that
  cost real money, reach out. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] Dismantle the "give us a discount now, more business later" bribe by mirroring it:
  ask whether the client, as a vendor, would accept "half of what you're worth" from a customer
  who promises to maybe come back. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] Repeat business is earned by delivering, not discounted for in advance — "most of my
  clients are repeat customers because I give them what they need." (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] Ask the client directly for their maximum budget before deciding whether to concede.
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] When conceding to a middle number ($750), make it explicitly one-time and state that
  future work returns to normal pricing of $1,000–$2,000. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] Defend a high anchor with risk analogies: buying design is like insurance — you
  don't buy a cheap stroller, put family on a discount bus, or use a discount laser-eye surgeon;
  "you only have two eyes." The price is "crazy until you compare it to people who charge a lot
  more for the same work — I think I'm a bargain." (paraphrase)

### Client ("Mr John Smith") — CONTEXT ONLY (played by Futur team member, excluded from persona)

- [2017-08-06] Objects that the price is "crazy"; another graphic designer charged only $200.
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] Offers future business in exchange for a lower price now. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-06] States a maximum budget of "around 500, 600." (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do (coaching, out of character):**
- "I don't want to negotiate at my final price... I don't want to negotiate at $500 because I'm
  gonna get dragged down. I'd have to then meet him at 400 or 350 — there's no way I'm going to do
  that."
- "So you've now just contemplated spending $750 and you just told me to go out and get $500... so
  I still even have room to go a little bit lower."
- "Give me a crazy reaction — I charge between five to $10,000 to do a logo. Give me a crazy
  reaction."

**Chris Do (designer, in character):**
- "Wow, you got a really good deal, Mr John Smith... okay, so you're okay with missing deadlines?
  How much did that cost you in terms of opportunity when a deadline was missed? Have you thought
  about that?"
- "You're a successful entrepreneur — how much is your time worth?... I have to imagine it's
  probably costing you in excess of $500 an hour."
- "I'm not trying to sell you anything, Mr John Smith. I assume that you and I are talking because
  you're looking for a better solution than what you are currently getting."
- "What my clients come to me for isn't necessarily for the logo... you can pay any kind of price
  from $5 on a site like Fiverr on up to a couple hundred if you hire an international branding
  firm."
- "If that's not a good fit for you, I totally understand — use the options that you have available
  to you... but if things should change on your end, if you should get frustrated, if you should
  start to feel like you're spending too much time managing people or getting subpar work or
  missing deadlines that cost you real money, please do reach out. I will be here."
- "How many times have you gone in to buy something... when you sell something to one of your
  clients where somebody says 'I'll give you half of what you're worth and if I like it I'll come
  back and give you something more, maybe' — would you do that deal?"
- "Most of my clients are repeat customers of mine because I give them what they need... and that
  should just be part of a working relationship."
- "What's the maximum amount of money that you're willing to spend on this?"
- "I'm willing to do it for 750, but I also want to be very clear... should you want to work with
  me in the future, I have to charge you where I normally charge, which is probably somewhere
  between a thousand to $2,000."
- "Are you a gambler?... that's why I pay for certain kind of insurances. You don't want to buy a
  cheap stroller for your child... you don't want to put your family on a discount bus, you don't
  want to go to a discount laser-eye surgeon... you only have two eyes."
- "My price is crazy — crazy until you start to compare it to people who charge a lot more than me
  for the same kind of work. I think I'm a bargain."

**Client "Mr John Smith" (CONTEXT — Futur team member, not persona):**
- "That's just crazy... last month we did some work with another graphic designer and he charged
  us like $200."
- "If you help us this time, perhaps we can have more business for you later if you give us a
  better price than what you're asking now. How does that sound?"
