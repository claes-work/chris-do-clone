# How To Get Work On Behance: What you need to do — Ep.8

★ **L3-candidate** — Reason: concrete platform-growth case study. A real designer (Melinda) updates her Behance portfolio and reports measurable job leads within ~2.5 weeks, and Chris Do walks through the ROI math plus the underlying portfolio strategy (build out brand applications, repurpose into micro-content). Strong raw material for a `content-strategy`/portfolio topic page.

## Summary

Episode 8 of "The Process" (a.k.a. Grow-Your-Business), a mentorship series in which Chris Do coaches designer Melinda on building her business. This is a mid-term recap/check-in. Melinda reports that after updating her previously-neglected Behance portfolio (~2.5 weeks earlier), she received 4–5 new client inquiries — two directly from Behance, others from Instagram, plus invitations to write for blogs, appear on podcasts and webinars.

Chris uses her results to teach a portfolio-ROI lesson: she spent roughly 8–16 hours per project (three projects) building out brand *applications* (stationery, packaging, letterhead, mockups, photography) so each project shows the full scope of a brand rather than 2–3 images. He frames "~3 days of work per job lead" as an extraordinary return, and notes the updated work can be repurposed into micro-content, website updates, capabilities decks, and portfolio pieces.

The conversation then covers: competing with yourself vs. others; the negotiation principle that whoever cares less holds the upper hand; sitting in on discovery meetings with strategist/consultant friends; Chris's "three golden questions" framework for discovery; learning the "language of business" as a bilingual skill (referencing Todd McFarlane); getting comfortable talking about money up-front and being rejected; and a debate about the "nobody cares about your dog" social-media advice from an earlier episode, after a client hired Melinda partly because of a dog photo.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (SUBJECT — persona data)

- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Updating your Behance portfolio works, and fast — even abnormally-good results like Melinda's (bites within ~2.5 weeks) show the platform delivers when you post updated work.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Don't just show 2–3 images of a brand identity; build out the *applications* — stationery, packaging, letterhead, product mockups, photography — so the client can sense the whole brand. This also triggers the client's own needs ("my stuff doesn't look this good in all applications").
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Most designers underestimate the time they spend, so double their estimate when reasoning about effort. (He doubles Melinda's 8 hrs/project to ~16.)
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) The ROI math: ~three days of focused work producing one new job lead is an incredible return — there are very few things in life where 3 days of focused effort makes someone call you with new business.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Updated application work compounds: you can cut it into micro-content, update your website, capabilities deck, and portfolio — so the hours go much further than the immediate leads.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) If you have no job leads, maybe you haven't put in the necessary work — the person to blame is the one in the mirror.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Break the habit of giving unsolicited advice; instead ask "why" questions to discover the real issue and help the other person discover it themselves.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) We procrastinate on the challenging, important task (onboarding a big client, writing a proposal) and do easy busywork (checking Instagram) instead; that's human — acknowledge it and decide consciously.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) The world usually doesn't change after you post a few new projects — *you* change: you listen and look for different things. Cites the "reticular" idea that once you focus on buying a hose, hose ads appear everywhere, because the brain filters for what you concentrate on.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) His three golden questions for discovery: (1) What are you trying to achieve? (2) What's getting in your way? — then listen intently and follow up until both you and, more importantly, the client have a clear picture.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Human nature seeks pleasure and minimizes pain — apply that as the general framework for what motivates any person, group, or company (B2C or B2B). A client wouldn't be talking to you if they'd already achieved their goal, so his questions generally warrant a "no, I'm not getting that" answer.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) You don't need domain expertise (e.g. B2B, or Tetris) to facilitate strategy — apply the same framework: figure out what they want and what's blocking it. He demonstrates by "solving" a made-up Tetris goal.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Learn the "language of business" — being conversant means you enter meetings at least at a junior-high vocabulary level, not first grade. It's far simpler than learning an actual foreign language (he's studied French, Latin, Mandarin); there aren't that many terms or concepts.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Attributes to comic artist Todd McFarlane the idea of being "bilingual" — speaking both the language of art and the language of business — as what makes an artist effective in business meetings. Chris says he'll "steal that."
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Talking about money up-front opens you to rejection (like asking someone out) — just because you haven't heard "no" yet doesn't mean you've become comfortable with it. As soon as you raise something objectionable, you open yourself to rejection.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) He pushes hard on acknowledging progress because people don't take enough credit for what they've done, which keeps them stuck in doubt and self-criticism; his job is to force acknowledgment of wins, not just fix mistakes.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Putting yourself out there publicly (a portfolio, a show, social) requires being vulnerable and criticized, which builds resilience and toughness over time.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) On the dog-photo debate: he stands by "nobody cares about your dog/cat/food/kids photos" unless it's your profession. One client hiring because of a dog picture is one testimonial, not a repeatable plan.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) If a dog photo is the *only* thing a prospect can relate to about you, you're not doing a good enough job relating elsewhere. He (Chris) has dozens of articles, 100+ posts, and ~300 YouTube videos so there's "no secret" about his beliefs and values — he wants to attract people who think like him. The dog photo belongs on the "About" page, not as the lead content.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase, [self-reported]) He manages limited time and focuses on the account that "puts food on the table" — the one that pays for his kids' food and college tuition — rather than spreading across many personal accounts.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) On spotting Melinda: he reached out because he saw how she took a simple assignment/exercise and went "all in" with detail and an eye for design; he's good at picking potential winners (sometimes he misreads a situation).
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) Closing line: "You are not defined by your past; the future is what you make of it."

### Melinda (mentee — CONTEXT, not persona data)

- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) She was on Behance before but inactive with outdated work; after updating she got a few inquiries within ~2.5 weeks — two leads from Behance, others from Instagram.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) She updated three projects' presentations, adding more brand applications (print, stationery, packaging), spending roughly 8+ hours each.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) She's practicing asking "why" instead of giving advice, competing with herself (noticed while golfing with her dad), and talking about money on initial calls.
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) She sat in on two discovery meetings — one with a strategist friend, one with a consultant assembling a specialist team — and realized how many questions she wouldn't have known to ask (she's more familiar with B2C than B2B).
- `[2017-08-09]` (paraphrase) A client hired her partly because of her dog picture ("you're the dog lover"), which she read as the client wanting to relate on a personal level and build trust — though she declines to prescribe it as a tactic.
