## Summary

An episode of The Futur's "My First Portfolio" series. Chris Do reviews Greg Gunn's graduating portfolio from Otis College of Art and Design (Greg graduated 2006; Chris taught at Otis for several years, and the two met there around 2004). Chris and Greg pretend Greg is a fresh grad presenting his book for the first time, then go through the ~11-year-old student animation and motion-graphics work piece by piece.

The bulk of the runtime is Greg narrating his student projects (a National Geographic network ID with a hand-shot ink/liquid texture, a rhythm-synced 3D cube animation, a motion-graphics end tag, holiday character shorts from the studio he co-founded, and X Games pitch storyboards from a "pond scum" intern stint at Motion Theory). Interwoven throughout is Chris's live critique, in which he teaches transferable design principles using Greg's early work as the example.

The stated purpose of the series (per Chris's closing): to humble viewers and show that accomplished designers did not start out great — there are "many steps in between where you're at today versus where you started."

## Key claims (dated `[2017-08-16]`)

### Chris Do (persona-relevant — design teaching)

- `[2017-08-16]` **Portfolio-critique method:** When reviewing weak work, don't dismiss the person — "take a step back, look at this piece by piece." Start from the communication goal ("what do we need to communicate here?"), then diagnose specifics like legibility and typeface/font rationale, and ask the designer to explain the thinking and how it connects to the brand.
- `[2017-08-16]` **Clarity is the job:** "As designers we're fighting confusion." The viewer must be able to tell what they're looking at; control how bright the background is and how big elements are in the frame to make an image read.
- `[2017-08-16]` **The "turn off all your layers" debugging technique:** Younger/in-school designers tend to keep layering (gradients, overlays, more and more elements) out of "inexperience and insecurity" until a file becomes too dense. Fix: turn off every layer, turn them back on one at a time, and when it starts to feel too complicated or "not resolved," the break is in one of the last two layers you turned on. Then change one variable — scale, contrast, color, texture, or opacity — until it works.
- `[2017-08-16]` **"You don't need to put all the sauce on it" (high-quality-ingredients metaphor):** A strong idea doesn't need everything piled on. The best restaurants serve one piece of salmon or a salad with three ingredients done to perfection — start with high-quality ingredients (here, the concept), prepare simply, and let the content come through. Over-seasoning ruins the plate.
- `[2017-08-16]` **On reverse-engineering a good art-direction save:** Reviewing how Greg's art director improved an X Games comp, Chris models how a strong AD synthesizes a junior's scattered ideas into a legible style — keeping the high-contrast idea and the "what if you couldn't see parts of the body" negative-shape idea, stripping hand-brushed texture, introducing a halftone, and building a multi-layered comp with three clear "reads" (primary subject, foreground element, screened-back background) without overwhelming the viewer.
- `[2017-08-16]` **Purpose of the "My First Portfolio" series:** "You don't come out [of school] great... this is where we started." The point is to humble people and show there are many steps between where you start and where you end up. (Notes Greg has since worked on beautiful campaigns with major artists and brands.)
- `[2017-08-16]` **Community/engagement practice (channel operations):** The Futur team is "on top of" comments and responsive for the 48 hours after a video is released; less responsive after. Framed as a reward for viewers who engage early.

### Greg Gunn (CONTEXT — not persona data)

- `[2017-08-16]` Graduated Otis College of Art and Design in 2006; primarily did animation, some design. All student work was 4×3 SD because HD "wasn't a thing / wasn't a format" yet.
- `[2017-08-16]` His school strategy: pick one or two projects per semester to "put everything into" and coast the rest to a C-minus pass with minimal effort.
- `[2017-08-16]` For a National Geographic network-ID assignment he designed and animated everything himself (100%), and physically shot the liquid/ink texture — black ink on acrylic/lucite over white, lit in his apartment bathroom, blown with a straw for movement, shot on a rented mini-DV tape cam and captured off tape.
- `[2017-08-16]` Started an animation studio (heavy on characters) with two friends right out of school; they made personal holiday shorts and emailed them to everyone they knew, including former teachers.
- `[2017-08-16]` As a junior he interned ("pond scum" / "lowly intern") at **Motion Theory**, doing X Games pitch storyboards and a logo brand-refresh; recalls getting excellent-but-unremembered guidance from the creative director. The company did not win the pitch.
- `[2017-08-16]` Self-assessment of his intern X Games logo, looking back: "it's terrible" — poor legibility, antiquated/gothic typeface that felt off-brand for X Games.
- `[2017-08-16]` Chris did not teach Greg as a sophomore (Chris wasn't accepting sophomores at the time); Greg audited/hung around Chris's open classroom before taking his class later, reasoning "this guy's smart, gives people a hard time, I could probably learn something."

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do:

- "As designers we're fighting confusion."
- "The tendency for a younger designer, a person who's straight out of school or still in school, is to layer things... it comes from a place of inexperience and insecurity — I don't know what else to do, so I'm just going to keep putting more and more stuff on it."
- "Turn off all your layers, and then turn one layer on at a time, and as soon as it feels too complicated or you get that weird feeling it's not resolved, somewhere you broke it in one of those two layers."
- "If you have a really strong idea, you don't need to put all the sauce on it."
- "We go to the best restaurants... we say, wow, that one piece of salmon, or that one salad with these three ingredients done to perfection. It starts with having high-quality ingredients — in this case the concept — preparing simply and letting the content come through."
- "You don't come out [of school] great... this is where we started, and for you guys to say, if you want to get to where he's at, you have to take that first step, and there's many steps in between where you're at today versus where you started."

Greg Gunn (CONTEXT):

- "The way I'd approached school was I picked one or two projects for that semester that I wanted to put everything into, and the rest of my classes I just wanted to pass — C-minus pass, minimal amount of effort."
- "It's terrible... I can barely read it, and the typeface feels very antiquated and gothic, and to me that totally contrasts what X Games is."
