# Meet Storyboard Artist & Illustrator Tristyn Pease — 4 mins

> **CONTEXT PAGE** — profiles Tristyn Pease, not the subject; persona data only if Chris Do speaks.

## Summary

A ~4-minute studio profile of storyboard artist and illustrator **Tristyn Pease**, filmed
on a job at The Futur / Blind. Tristyn explains how she is represented by a creative
staffing agency ("frameworks"/Framers, likely Framestore or a similar rep — spelling
uncertain from captions) that she found via a legitimate Craigslist ad. The agency
markets her to clients, negotiates her rate, and takes a 20% cut. She walks through her
day rate, her mobile working setup (MacBook Pro + Wacom tablet), how an artist builds a
portfolio and moves up rates over time, and shows recent illustration work (including a
national-parks-poster-style piece for a client that fell through). The interviewer
(Chris Do, host of The Futur) reacts, asks about rates and the agency model, and offers
a brief aside on the "creative and the bad cop" negotiating dynamic. It closes with
Tristyn's tongue-in-cheek advice that being "boring" (drama-free, headphones on, gets the
work done) is what keeps clients hiring you.

## Key claims

### Tristyn Pease (subject of the profile — CONTEXT)
- [2017-08-22] She found her agency representation by answering Craigslist ads — most were "dodgy," but one legitimate one was for the agency she now works through. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-22] The agency represents her like an actor's agent: they hustle, market, and negotiate on her behalf so she doesn't have to do her own sales/marketing. Clients who know the agency ask if a specific artist is available, and she gets referred in. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-22] The agency takes a 20% cut of what she makes. (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- [2017-08-22] Her billed rate is $800/day; she takes home $600 after the agency's cut. (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- [2017-08-22] When she first started with the agency she "did not make a lot" — new talent is advertised to lower-budget clients, and rates get bumped up as the roster of happy clients grows and bigger-budget projects come in. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-22] Once an artist is booked heavily, they (or their agent) can be braver about raising rates; she lets her agent ("Jen") set and defend the price. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-22] Her setup is fully portable: a MacBook Pro and a Wacom tablet she draws directly on; she only needs "two outlets and a chair" to work anywhere. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-22] One shown piece was styled to match vintage national-parks posters (graphic style) for a client that later backed out; unmade client work can be posted to her own Instagram once released. (paraphrase)
- [2017-08-22] Her self-described edge: being "boring" — drama-free, headphones on, reliably getting the work done — is what makes clients want to keep working with her. (paraphrase)

### Chris Do (interviewer/host — persona-relevant, attribution: uncertain)
- [2017-08-22] Offered a negotiating aside: it is useful to have another person play the "bad cop" so the creative can stay the creative — "it has to be the creative and the bad cop." He said when he hired an assistant he'd designate them as the project manager so he could tell clients his hands were tied.
