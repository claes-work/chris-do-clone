Series premiere of a coaching format in which Chris Do live-coaches Rebecca Hyneman, a
47-year-old creative director launching her own studio ("Craftwell"). Rebecca has an

The episode is business-development-heavy. Chris's central lesson is **selling through
curiosity**: stop pitching decks and asking for time/money, and instead become intensely
curious about people, dig for their real problems, and let compatible needs surface. He

actionable homework: go talk to strangers everywhere to build listening/rapport skills.

### Chris Do (coach) — persona data

- [2017-09-09] The best way to reach the point of asking for a sale is **through curiosity**
  rather than pitching; spend time finding out someone's challenges, then craft something
  tailored to them. (paraphrase; references a prior episode with Eli Cohen on "selling
  through curiosity")
- [2017-09-09] When prospecting, don't ask the client "how can I help you" — don't make them
  figure out your solution; just dig to understand what their world is like and surface a
  problem. (paraphrase)
- [2017-09-09] **Kill the assumptions.** Don't assume you and the client mean the same thing;
  start clean and keep asking "tell me more about that / what's motivating this?" (paraphrase)
- [2017-09-09] Sending a capabilities deck or cold email unsolicited asks for two big things —
  the prospect's **time** (to read it) and their **money** (to hire you) — without first
  creating any value. Chris deletes ~97 of 100 such emails. (paraphrase)
- [2017-09-09] Financial literacy for a studio owner: **your take-home salary is not profit.**
  Revenue − cost of goods sold (what you pay others to make the work) − your own salary −
  overhead = net profit. A business must be profitable on top of paying you a normal wage to
  do your job. (paraphrase)
- [2017-09-09] The CEO's job is vision and big strategic decisions, not doing the production
  work; if the CEO disappeared, production/marketing/sales would still run. (paraphrase)
- [2017-09-09] Behance is a legitimate lead source — Chris's friend (owner of "Farm Design")
  gets most of his job leads there and staffs six or seven full-time designers; in a crowded
  market you win by making the work, its presentation, and its photography **exceptional**,
  and by getting titles/tags/descriptions right so search can find you. (paraphrase)
- [2017-09-09] Keep a separate brand (personal name) from the new studio brand until you can
  show work actually done under the new business — presenting agency work as your studio's is
