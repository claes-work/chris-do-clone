# Typography Critique — "Basic Typography" Poster Review (5 mins)

★ **L3-candidate** — Compact, self-contained live typography-critique that demonstrates Chris Do's teaching method end to end: turn off all layers, add elements back one at a time, establish a hero, enforce one axis/grid, and set primary/secondary/tertiary reads via contrast. Good raw material for a design-craft topic page on live layout critique and visual hierarchy.

## Summary

A short live design critique in which Chris Do reviews a student/instructor-made "Basic Typography" class-demo poster. The poster was created by a graphic design instructor who teaches at a community college; Chris and co-host Molly critique it together, with the instructor present. Chris diagnoses the layout as "80% there" but cluttered by too many typefaces and elements competing for attention.

He walks through his method aloud: hide small unclear elements ("either show more of it or get rid of it"), turn off ALL layers and add them back one at a time, re-checking the composition after every change ("every time we make a change we have to look at it"). He simplifies the type, aligns everything to a single axis/grid, and elevates the illustrative letterform (the interplay between the serif "A" and a fluid, photographic "F") to be the visual hero. He then defines a deliberate reading order — primary, secondary, tertiary reads — arguing that the original crowded layout had everything "fighting for attention at the same time." His summary principle: contrast is the most important thing to play with in design fundamentals, and the tension between the sharp serif A and the fluid photographic F is what makes the piece work.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (critic) — [2017-09-11]
- When a layout isn't working, the first move is to turn off all layers, then add elements back one at a time to see what each contributes. (paraphrase)
- Small, unidentifiable elements should either be shown more fully so the viewer can read them, or removed entirely. (paraphrase)
- After every change to a composition you must stop and look at it again to judge whether it improved or worsened. (paraphrase)
- Too many different typefaces is a core problem; the layout "just needs to be simplified." (paraphrase)
- Keep everything anchored to one axis / one grid so a single element can stand out as the hero. (paraphrase)
- A design should have a deliberate reading order — a primary read, a secondary read, and a tertiary read — rather than multiple elements competing for attention simultaneously. (paraphrase)
- Contrast is the most important thing to play with in terms of design fundamentals and principles; increase contrast wherever you can. (paraphrase)
- Contrast of form (sharp serif letterform vs. fluid letterform) and of medium (a photograph vs. a vector piece of art) is what makes the hero letterform work. (paraphrase)

### Molly (co-host) — [2017-09-11] — CONTEXT, not persona
- The relation between the "basic typography" line and the instructor credit "just isn't working." (paraphrase)
- Agreed the poster felt "80% there" with something throwing both her and Chris off. (paraphrase)

### Instructor (poster author, guest) — [2017-09-11] — CONTEXT, not persona
- Currently teaches a graphic design course at a community college; the poster was a class demo. (paraphrase)
- Gave live direction/feedback during the critique (e.g., agreeing a type element needed to move down/farther). (paraphrase)
