## Summary

Episode 2 of a coaching mini-series on The Futur (@thefutur). **Chris Do** coaches an early-stage designer/agency founder — introduced as **Rebecca** — who describes herself as an introvert and is nervous about networking. The whole episode answers her recurring question: *"How do I bring in clients / get that first meeting?"*

Rebecca runs small web/branding projects (average ~$7,000) and has exhausted her LinkedIn contacts. Chris walks her through a low-pressure, introvert-friendly business-development method built entirely on relationships she already has and things she already loves, rather than cold networking events. He probes her network (her accountant "Dennis," her husband at Disney, her mommy-and-me circle) and her hobbies (yoga, cooking, hiking) to surface warm channels and realistically-sized prospects (local yoga studios, bike/supply shops, small food brands) rather than dream clients like Lululemon.

He then contrasts two ways he personally wins business: (1) the slow, organic, in-person "ambassador" route (his Tender Greens story — becoming a regular, helping the line, eventually being introduced up the chain to the chef and marketing director, though the job ultimately went to Pentagram), and (2) his current method — inbound: creating so much content (posts, live streams, tweets) that people who've known him for years, plus fans of the show and in-house marketing directors, come to him. He closes on patience and non-desperation ("my urgency isn't their problem") and reframes networking as "be out there to learn, to share, to create value" — otherwise everything you say sounds like an advertisement.

## Key claims
*(All dated 2017-09-12. Paraphrased unless quoted. Attributed per speaker.)*

**Chris Do's coaching claims (Chris-attributed — persona-relevant):**
- For an introvert launching a business, walking into a studio/shop cold as an unknown is unlikely to work — start instead from warm relationships and your own passions. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- Business development should begin with **low-hanging fruit**: people you already know, and the activities, hobbies, and interests you're passionate about. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- Mine your existing network for introductions — e.g. ask your accountant directly: "I'm looking for clients, I do killer websites, do you know anyone who could use this? If so, please connect us." [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- A spouse recommending you into their employer is a difficult channel — there's a perception the only reason for the conversation is the marital connection, so it's hard to get through that door (depends on corporate culture). [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- **Don't chase the big brand; chase its suppliers and the small mom-and-pop shops.** Everyone wants Lululemon, but small local yoga/fitness/food businesses that can afford ~$7,000 desperately need help — and you make a bigger difference at a small company than at a big one. Scaffold up: little yoga studios first, Lululemon later. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- On the money/fun spectrum: the money tends to be on the "boring" side; the low budgets tend to be on the "fun" side (entertainment IP like Marvel/Disney/Star Wars/Nike acts as if you should pay them for the privilege). [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- In the **launch phase only**, you can do things Chris would normally frown upon — breakeven or even at-a-loss work — to build a client base and portfolio, but go in "with very sober eyes": under-charged clients almost never come back at a higher rate (it's "one and done"), so only take such work if it's good enough to leverage into the next job. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- Restarting a business means adopting a young/scrappy mentality again — prior history doesn't carry over; "we're starting over." [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- **Build spec projects** for the industry you want to move into (e.g. athletic/sports), treating it like being a student again, to show work you don't yet have real clients for. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- The "magic bullet" for getting clients doesn't exist: you build rapport, get to know people's problems, and become passionate about the work — starting from your own passions. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- Chris is a **creature of habit** — same place, same order, day in and day out — and the advantage is that the people get to know you and you get to know them; bouncing around means nobody knows you. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- The **"ambassador" pattern**: by becoming a helpful regular (teaching confused customers how to order at Tender Greens), he built a relationship over weeks/months before ever mentioning he's a brand consultant — which led to introductions up the chain (manager → chef Jason → marketing director "Christy/Christine"). The job ultimately went to Pentagram, but the relationship remains and "you just never know." [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- **Patience and non-desperation:** "My urgency isn't their problem." He once waited nine months for a friend to call; he can wait it out because he has other things to do. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase / quote below)
- **Today his business is entirely inbound:** the sheer volume of content he shares (Twitter, Facebook, live streams about branding) brings people out of the woodwork — people he's known 10–20 years, fans of the show, in-house marketing directors, agency design directors — asking for meetings. You have to "put yourself out there in order to bring them in." [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- Company history: Blind has been in business since **1995**, but the name "Blind" doesn't have brand pull that generates work on its own. Chris has only done business development himself in earnest for the **last ~2–3 years**; before that it was sales reps, in a market with an abundance of work. When the work dried up he chose to change rather than let the company die (iceberg/steer-the-boat analogy). [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- Chris frames his own advantage as a ~3-year head start, and Rebecca's advantage as a shortcut — he'll tell her what actually worked. He disavows his own early method: "I sat in my room and work came in" — that was a bad plan; it's better to get out, meet people, build personal relationships. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- Jobs (and clients) come through someone you know, not through sending out résumés — "no one really cares" about the résumé. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- **Reframe of networking:** forget about trying to get new business — just be out there to learn, to share, and to create value; otherwise everything you say sounds like a promotion / an advertisement. [2017-09-12] (paraphrase)
- Concrete outreach script for a small brand you genuinely love: "I love your product / your spices are amazing. I couldn't help but notice the site was broken — do you think this is affecting your sales? Is this something you need to work on?" [2017-09-12] (paraphrase / quote below)

## Notable verbatim quotes

### Chris Do (persona voice — training data)

On the introvert's starting point:
> "Start with the low-hanging fruit, start with people you know, start with activities, hobbies and interest that you're very passionate about. Just start there."

The reframe of why to network at all:
> "Forget about trying to get new business, just be out there to learn, to share, to create value. That's the most important thing I can tell you — otherwise everything you say sounds like a promotion, it sounds like an advertisement."

On going after the small clients, not the dream brand:
> "Everybody looks at the big brand but they never look at the suppliers and the small mom-and-pop chains that are just right within your grasp that desperately need help... and you would make a big difference at a smaller company than you would at a big company."

> "First we'll do the little yoga studios and the next thing will be working for Lululemon as a client."

On being a creature of habit / the ambassador route:
> "I'm a creature of habit. I go to the same place, I order the same thing day in and day out... there's an advantage to that because you get to know the people and they get to know you. If you're bouncing around trying lots of different things, nobody gets to know you and you don't get to know them."

The outreach opener (small brand you love):
> "Hey, I love your product, the spices that you make [are] amazing. I can't help but notice that when I went to the site some of it was broken — do you think this is affecting your sales? Is this something that you think you need to work on?"

On patience and non-desperation:
> "My urgency isn't their problem. Their urgency is my problem — that's what I'm waiting for."

> "I waited nine months for my friend to call me. I can wait this out. I've got other things to do."

On how business comes to him now (inbound):
> "The business comes to me today, and the way it comes to me is because I'm creating so much inbound content... people that I've known for 10, 15, 20 years are coming out of the woodwork and saying 'I love what you do, I think we need help, can we have a meeting.'"

> "You have to put yourself out there in order to bring them in."

Disavowing his own early method:
> "That was a bad plan, because I didn't do anything — I sat in my room and work came in. So it's better to get out there, start meeting people in the community... getting personal relationships, getting to know people."

On launch-phase discounted/spec work, with sober eyes:
> "In the launching phases of your company I believe you can do things that I would normally frown upon, which is to do work that is breakeven or even at a loss to help build up your client base and to help build up a portfolio — but go into this with very sober eyes."

> "It's very unlikely that they'll come back and hire you at a much higher rate. This is a one and done... So you have to think about: will this work be good enough for me to leverage to get my next job? If it's not, don't do it."

On restarting scrappy:
> "We have to have that mentality like we're a young, scrappy firm again, and we need to do what we need to do to get the next leap for us."

### Rebecca (guest — CONTEXT ONLY, NOT persona training data)

On her core problem:
> "How do I get, how do I bring in clients?... I am thinking about doing networking but I really don't like networking, that makes me nervous."

On thinking too big:
> "I always keep thinking so large... I guess I never just thought little."
