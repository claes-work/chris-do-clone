# Graphic Design Typography Critique for Multi-page Gourmet Goods Brochure

## Summary

A live design critique from The Futur in which Chris Do reviews a multi-page
brochure/booklet for a gourmet edible-goods brand ("H&M company" / confectionery),
designed by Eddie, a self-taught graphic designer from Indonesia with a one-year visual
communication short course and his own studio. Co-host Molly participates, giving her
impressions. Chris works live in Photoshop, moving elements around to demonstrate fixes.
The critique covers typeface pairing (Didot + Baskerville serifs, plus a sans), color
palette (gold / salmon / navy), contrast and scale in the logo lockup, negative/white
space as a marker of luxury, tying the front and back covers together as a
three-dimensional object, breaking the gutter, alignment, the rule of thirds, and using
gold sparingly. Chris is broadly complimentary of Eddie's instincts and best pages while
proposing concrete refinements.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (critic)

- [2017-10-08] Didot and Baskerville are both serif typefaces that pair well together;
  combined with a sans-serif they form good typeface choices for this brochure.
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] An old-fashioned / old-timey logo treatment feels appropriate and relevant
  for food products. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] He dislikes the script/package typeface used for one word; he'd prefer it
  hand-lettered with the flourishes worked in, or more customization added to make it
  work. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Gold, salmon, and navy/royal blue are very nice colors together —
  especially if the gold were a metallic that would "pop." (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Blue product on a blue background is hard to read; staging a white product
  behind, or mixing white and blue products, helps separate them. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Contrast principle: when two type elements sit together, one should be
  bigger and the other smaller (e.g. enlarge "gourmet," shrink "H company"). (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Endorses the designer's instinct to use a light ultra-condensed weight
  because the heavier option "felt a little heavy for a food product." (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Packing too much in makes a layout feel like a catalogue rather than
  gourmet; give it breathing space — "white space is your best friend," citing Apple's
  very minimal photography as what "screams luxury." (paraphrase; quote below)
- [2017-10-08] Tie things together / echo and repeat elements so they relate — e.g.
  stretching one element so it overlaps and its shape is echoed elsewhere. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Don't put the logo in the upper-right corner; moving it there throws the
  composition off. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Treat a booklet/catalog/brochure as a three-dimensional object — the front
  and back covers should relate; repeat grid lines/elements across front and back so they
  tie in, which the client feels subconsciously and which gives you something to talk
  about when presenting. (paraphrase; quote below)
- [2017-10-08] Organic, floating elements (e.g. beans) are nice against blocky, rigid
  layouts; shrink them to open up negative space. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Question whether every image is needed (e.g. the image underneath the
  macaroons) — cut what isn't necessary. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] A dark overlay on a clean marble-slab image makes it feel "smoky and a
  little dirty"; consider making it more opaque or filling the page instead. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Let images either stop right at the gutter or run well over it, so the
  reader appreciates that the image slid across the spread. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Alignment: elements should relate to something — push to the right or left,
  don't leave them floating unrelated. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Rule of thirds: dividing a page into thirds is a dynamic way to break down
  a layout; the page with the most negative space is the most successful. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Repeat distinctive elements (e.g. diagonal elements, a gold bar) elsewhere
  to tie the composition together. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-08] Use gold very sparingly, tastefully, and with intention — over-using it
  makes everything gaudy. (paraphrase; quote below)

## Notable verbatim quotes
