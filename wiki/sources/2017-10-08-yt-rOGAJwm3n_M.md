## Summary

Debut of The Futur's **"Pitch This!"** segment. **Chris Do** and co-host **Ben Burns** set up a recurring format: Ben is shown a design frame, gets a minute to prepare, pitches it to Chris as if Chris were the client; Chris critiques Ben's *presentation* (not the design), then re-pitches the same frame himself so viewers can see the difference in how a formally-trained art director talks about work.

The frame is real client work: a branding direction ("the new nautical") for a property at **450 Alaskan** in Seattle — Pioneer Square, an industrial waterfront location. The concept mixes historical/nautical typography and maps with vibrant modern color, minimal layouts, and a "salty" older-gentleman hero persona.

Ben pitches first (calls it "the new nautical," praises the salty hero, uses design-speak). Two crew members (**Molly**, **Aaron/Erin**, plus off-camera chat) react. Chris then critiques: Ben started strong by giving a theme/"lens," but ran out of ways to talk about it, sounded "pitchy" (like selling), and used the word "salty," which carries negative connotations to a client. Chris then delivers his own model pitch of the same frame, demonstrating: anchoring type/pattern to concrete client-known references (working harbor vs. yacht club, container-ship architecture, sail-mast reversed type, herringbone/topographic map motifs), describing texture/feel, and surfacing the mood-board images deliberately so the client gives usable feedback.

The framing thesis: the old belief "the work should speak for itself" is a lazy cop-out; language is a designer's power, and a great designer who can't present will hit a ceiling in both career and client caliber.

## Notable verbatim quotes

### Chris Do (persona voice — training data)

On the old "work speaks for itself" belief and language as power:
> "I used to feel like if I did all the work and the work was good it should speak for itself... I thought that if you explained it with all that art speak [it] is a bunch of BS and nobody believes that anyways and it's because you were lazy. And now that I'm a little older, a little bit more mature, I realized the power of language and how specific you can describe something and to kind of paint in the gaps, to fill in the imagination — that's your power as a presenter, as an art director."

On the ceiling for designers who can't present:
> "If you're a great designer and you can't present the work, you can't articulate the ideas and the thinking behind it, you're only gonna go so high in the corporate chain, you're going to only attract a certain kind of client."

Giving the client a lens:
> "Ben started out by calling this the new nautical and I like that he gave this a theme for us to bite into, so he's giving me — [the] client — a lens to look at this work."

On being "pitchy":
> "What I heard Ben say, it sounded a little pitchy to me, like he was trying to sell me. I like a more straightforward approach and just kind of say it as it is and fill in the gaps — so what aren't we seeing, what aren't we able to connect?"

The core technique — anchor the unknown to the known:
> "What we want to do is we want to take something we don't know and attach it to something we do know. So when you talk about type and you use another type term, that's not going to help people. But if you talk about design and you connect it to something that they know... then we have this shared visual language."

> "You have to remember the person sitting on the opposite [side] of the table is not an artist, is not a designer, though they have creative tendencies and they appreciate good design. Using very specific design language in some instances, where it's not telling the larger story, [is] not necessarily a good thing... your eyes might roll over."

Model pitch — grounding in concrete references:
> "When I think about the harbor in the bay I think of two things: one, our sailboats, and I think about container ships. This is a working harbor, this is not some yacht club, and I think it's reflective of the culture and the people we're trying to attract."

> "The man is our typical client. He's sophisticated, he has a world view, he's seen things in his life, and he doesn't care about the glossy trappings of a glamorous life. This is truly the Seattle market."

> "The inspiration for that was from looking at sailboats... because they're translucent you see it flipped backwards, and I think we can introduce those kinds of elements as kind of a nod to sailboats without it being too on the nose."

On describing the other senses:
> "The only thing that I felt was missing... is literally telling people how something feels like in their hand, so it's kind of missing that texture... describing the other senses, what you hear, what you might touch, what it feels like — those are all good things."

On surfacing mood boards deliberately to get feedback:
> "We put a lot of time and energy into selecting these images so I need to be able to pull them out, I need to surface them, I need to highlight them and give the client something to think about... and I want to be very deliberate and intentional not only in what we put on the page but how we talk about it, because I want that feedback."

On word choice ("salty"):
> "Salty [does] have [a] negative [connotation]... it's not a good word, guys, it's not a good word. So that's something you want to think about, because this is the client."

### Ben Burns (CONTEXT ONLY — co-host being coached, NOT persona training data)

Ben's pitch of the frame:
> "Welcome to the new nautical. With vibrant colors and the use of historical typography — these geometric sans serifs — we've combined two contrasting ideas... and I think our hero really kind of sums it up. It's this older gentleman, he looks a little bit like he's salty, you know, that he can tell these stories of life on the water but he's not faded into obscurity yet."

On the difficulty of learning visual vocabulary:
> "Learning the visual vocabulary... if you don't go through a fine arts program it's very difficult to pick up kind of organically, because these are words that you rarely use. And so as I watched Chris pitch ideas I've just [gone] like, wow, I wish I could do that."
