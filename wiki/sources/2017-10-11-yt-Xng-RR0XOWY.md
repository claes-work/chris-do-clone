# How To Brainstorm Using Google Search & Stock Photo Sites

## Summary

Chris Do demonstrates a live "art of search" ideation method, using Google and
Shutterstock as a free, always-on creative partner to brainstorm imagery for a
YouTube thumbnail on the theme "finding your passion." Co-hosts drive the keyboard
while Chris narrates the process: start from the dictionary definition of the target
word, go broad before narrowing, apply a "three flicks" rule to judge whether the
search term is right, add abstraction words ("concept," "idea," "metaphor") to escape
cliché, mine stock-site metadata/keywords for better search terms, collect anything
inspiring without judging, and chase related-image and same-artist rabbit holes.
The underlying craft lesson: creativity is connecting two disparate/unrelated ideas
into a common thread, and search-driven references feed that.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2017-10-11] To survive and thrive in the knowledge economy you have to learn how to
  learn; nearly all the world's information is at your fingertips and the skill is
  unlocking it. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Students get "creatively constipated" and stuck; the usual cause is weak
  search — they type the most basic terms; three or four better searches later, they
  find exactly what they need. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Type search terms slowly so Google's predictive/autocomplete text reveals
  what most people actually look for. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Before searching images, look up the dictionary definition of the word;
  often we don't understand the meaning of words we're designing around. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Rule of thumb: "three flicks of the screen" — if you don't find what
  you're looking for within about three scrolls of image results, you've typed the
  wrong word. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Start broad, then narrow; don't box yourself in early by typing four or
  five words, which collapses results to one thing. Broad-to-specific is the journey.
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] To escape cliché, add an abstraction word to the noun — try "passion
  concept," "passion idea," "passion metaphor"; this is often "the secret sauce."
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Google image results are messy because people upload with junk filenames
  (e.g. "1-44-2a7b") and poor SEO, so Google's crawlers guess; stock sites like
  Shutterstock are more curated because contributors are paid/incentivized to meta-tag
  and think about how buyers search. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Read the keywords/metatags stock contributors attach to an image —
  the word you think you want often isn't the word everyone uses, and those other words
  can be more powerful (e.g. discovering "abstract passion"). (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Don't judge too quickly; people are too critical too fast. If something
  inspires you or is "1/8 of an idea," grab and save it into a bucket — it costs nothing
  and you never know where it leads. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Creativity is the ability to connect two disparate/unrelated ideas and
  find a common thread; images that combine two things are consistently appealing.
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Use stock-site "related images" and same-artist browsing as additional
  entry points to chase infinite rabbit holes for inspiration. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Search is a creative partner that works 24 hours a day, never takes a
  vacation, has no workers' comp, and costs nothing. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-11] Borrow techniques you see (e.g. a silhouette deteriorating into petals/
  hearts, reflections in paint) and remap them onto your own subject. (paraphrase)
