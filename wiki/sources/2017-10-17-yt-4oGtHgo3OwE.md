## Summary

Chris Do lays out what he believes makes creative people *want* to work at a company
and give their best. His model rests on four pillars: (1) a fun, psychologically safe
place where mistakes and creative self-expression are welcome; (2) autonomy —
self-directed people given goals rather than prescribed methods; (3) a culture that
treats mistakes/failure as healthy and necessary for learning and growth, encouraged
top-down and company-wide; and (4) protecting people's time by managing workload so
they work a normal eight-hour day and avoid late nights and weekends. He frames the
underlying leadership realization: projects and money come and go, but you have to take
care of your people, because neglected people burn out, become disgruntled, and stop
giving their best.

### Chris Do

- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) The first priority as a leader is to create a fun place
  people *want* to be, where it feels safe to make mistakes, explore your creative
  self, and express yourself how you want.

  self-directed; he gives people goals and lets them figure out how to get there.

  you're not making mistakes, you're only doing things you've already done many times.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) Learning new things and taking on new challenges requires
  getting into uncomfortable areas — this is the real meaning of "going out of your
  comfort zone" / "thinking outside the box."
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) The liberty to make mistakes must be encouraged top-down
  and be a company-wide value, not left to individuals.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) The company manages workload so people can put in an
  eight-hour day and go home on time.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) Their point of pride is measured like an industrial
  safety sign: instead of "days without an accident," their version is "days without
  working late or working on the weekend."
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) A realization he had as a business owner: projects and
  money come and go, but you must take care of your people.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) If you don't take care of your people, they burn out,
  become disgruntled, and won't give the best of themselves.
