## Summary

Chris Do and Ben Burns do a "status check-in" on Ben's progress after ~6 months at
Blind/The Futur, using recent live client calls (including a lost million-dollar tech
job) as teaching material. Chris coaches Ben on how he personally runs sales calls:
managing the energy in the room, preparing very little and "showing up empty" (present,
without attachment or expectation), handling loaded/"shotgun" questions by *unpacking*
them and reflecting them back, and staying non-adversarial with prospects. A large
middle section addresses Ben's admitted pressure and impostor syndrome — Chris reframes
Ben's strengths (digital experience, full sales-cycle onboarding, research, fearlessness)
and argues everyone should "double down on who they are" rather than imitate others
(including imitating Chris). The final third is a blunt money-mindset segment: Chris
distinguishes "real business people" (enterprising firms that treat money as a tool and
value ideas) from small-business "hobbyists built around a trade" who cling to every
dollar, undervalue ideas, and micromanage — the latter being clients he chooses not to
work with.

### Chris Do (SUBJECT — persona training data)

- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) On calls he is "just managing energy" — when he hears
  negative energy he turns it into positive energy as quickly as he can, without being a
  "snake oil salesman"; the goal is to go with the flow.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) He preps very little for calls (doodled on a magazine
  before one call) — the strength is being completely open and present, "zoned in," a
  "Zen moment," trusting his training and experience rather than scripts.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) His first ~1.5–2 years of calls he was "a nervous wreck"
  with scripts and questions; there is no shortcut, but you work toward trusting your
  preparation and trusting that the other people are good people too — and accepting
  "it's okay for it not to work out."
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) Clear roles and responsibilities before a team call are
  huge — the "boat" metaphor: everyone must "earn their weight or get off the boat";

  Chris's role is the "smoother" / objection handler ("embrace and pivot").
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) Technique — **unpacking** a loaded question: take the
  question apart, repeat it back to the client so they feel validated and heard, then
  ask which sub-question matters most (or answer the truest one). He learned validation
  from his therapist: reflecting it back makes people feel important; failing to validate
  turns the energy negative.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) Contrasts unpacking with the "politician" technique
  (talk until you answer the question *you* wanted) — he dislikes the politician move
  because smart people notice they were never answered and feel manipulated.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) **"Show up empty"** — not unprepared/lazy ("level four"),
  but having done the brief/homework and then purging expectations, attachments, and
  bad energy (a prior fight, the bills, the next client) so you're wholly present.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) He "plays a game" of noticing who in the room is actually
  listening to the client — you can tell by whether answers match what was asked (the
  "what's your favorite color" test).
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) Never treats leads adversarially — not us-vs-them; both
  parties want the client's business to succeed, and sharing that motivation is powerful.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) Abundance vs scarcity — there are as many billion-dollar
  clients with six-figure identity jobs as there are $5,000 clients; more and bigger
  jobs always exist, partly a function of living in a high-commerce city (Paris, London,
  New York, LA — "looking for the lion in the desert" is hard). Don't negotiate against
  yourself out of perceived scarcity.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) On a million-dollar tech job he told the team "chill the
  eff out... just another million-dollar job" — acting weird / not being yourself puts
  you at an immediate disadvantage. They lost that job, but not because they weren't
  themselves.
- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) Money mindset — "if they go with somebody else it's their

- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) Impostor syndrome — he once felt he had to be better than
  every specialist he hired (illustrators, type experts) and tried to insert himself

- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) Growing up he had an identity crisis wanting to be

- `[2017-10-17]` (paraphrase) **Money segment (blunt):** small business owners who think
