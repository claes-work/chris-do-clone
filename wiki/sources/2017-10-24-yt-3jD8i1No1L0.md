## Summary

A ~40-minute Futur live stream on **creative/art direction and communication** —
"how you deal with direction, how you give it, how you receive it." Molly Zrelak
opens with a personal story: Chris (by email, in his absence) assigned her to make
micro-content celebrating typefaces (Helvetica), asking for personality/point of
view; she ran straight into making without asking clarifying questions, cycled
through weeks of unsatisfying revisions, and ended in a frustrating 2.5-hour work
session. Matthew Encina uses this as a teaching case for the rest of the show.

Matthew's throughline: the failure was a **communication/alignment** failure, not a
skill failure. His prescription is front-loading the process with questions and
cheap checkpoints (echoing an ~80/20 diagnose-vs-make ratio the crew attribute to a
prior Chris episode). He walks through: asking intent-level questions instead of
tactical ones; recapping any brief into a **one-page document** sent back for
confirmation before making anything; his Blind **discovery-session** document and how
he distills it into a one-page **creative brief / "sandbox"** per designer. He then
covers being a good director more broadly: handling peers who go rogue, scope creep,
inviting the client into 50/50 decisions, the designer→art-director→creative-director
path (and that not everyone should or must climb it), articulating your own thought
process when giving feedback, taking 100% accountability, non-intimidating body
language ("hovering art directors"), defaulting to objective goals/parameters, and
firing designers who can't take direction.

Persona value for the Chris Do clone: **none directly.** Retain only as (a) context on
The Futur's teaching bench and Matthew Encina's method, and (b) weak, secondhand
leads on Chris's frameworks that must be confirmed from primary Chris sources before

### Matthew Encina (context — creative direction & communication)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) The Helvetica project failed because Molly heard an
  instruction, ran off and made things in broad strokes without asking questions or
  showing her research — instead she should have narrowed with questions first, then
  done focused research, then checked alignment at each step, so each pass gets more
  narrow. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) Ask **intent-level** questions, not tactical ones: "what's
  the goal / the point / your point of view / your relationship with this?" — not
  "what size/weight should I use?" Tactical granular questions are easy yes/no items;
  intent is what you must understand thoroughly first. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) After any briefing/brain-dump, go away and **recap it into a
  one-page document** — "here's what I heard, here's the goal, the intent, the
  creative parameters, and my first step" — and send it back for confirmation before
  making anything, because making is where hours get wasted; the recap costs ~10
  minutes. He does this both with clients and with his own designers. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) At Blind he runs multi-hour (e.g. two ~8–10h days)
  **discovery sessions** capturing users, psychographics, business goals, features,
  and brand, then distills the whole thing into a **one-page creative brief per
  designer** — a "sandbox" of specific, plain-language creative direction ("use
  Japanese manga aesthetic: illustrations, ink, halftone, quirky characters; not
  boxy/corporate; use photos of the community"). It must be explainable "to my mom."
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) Specificity beats "colorful, vibrant, fun" — powerful words
  that mean nothing until interrogated ("vibrant — do we mean neon? saturated?
  rainbow?"). Vague direction leaves room for interpretation you may not want.
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) A tight brief lets designers *contribute* within the box:
  they check alignment and add ideas (e.g. proposing anime references), then teams
  build style-scapes / curated mood boards to present a few directions to the client —
  taking baby steps and checking in rather than going too far before alignment.
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) On a **peer** going off-direction: it's not your job to give
  a peer feedback if you're not their supervisor — raise it with the creative
  director instead, or approach the peer with curiosity, not correction. Different
  interpretations of one brief are a *feature* of a robust creative team. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) On **scope creep** (assuming scope/revisions were set
  airtight up front): stay unemotional and objective, hold up the agreed goals sheet,
  and ask whether the new direction serves the original goals; "anything's possible
  with time and money." Keep the client bought-in via a 50/50 relationship — involve
  them in every decision so they can't disown it later. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) He gives clients recommendations ("I recommend A based on
  B and C") but lets them decide since they pay; agrees with his own pick ~65% of the
  time, is fine with the other ~35%, no emotional attachment to a favorite direction.
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) **Not everyone should become a creative director** — it's a
  mindset change. Makers need long uninterrupted time to saturate/synthesize;
  managers bounce between clients/teams keeping everyone aligned and make nothing
  themselves. The designer→art-director→creative-director ladder is not required for
  success. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) At Blind's structure: an **art director** owns one project/
  team (the CD's "right-hand man"); a **creative director** runs multiple teams/
  projects. Titles differ by industry (in illustration/animation "art director" is
  top-level). (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) Giving good feedback = **articulating your own thought
  process**: start with the feeling ("this doesn't feel right"), dig into why ("it's
  too close together, needs negative space"), propose a direction to try, and get
  buy-in ("is that cool?") rather than barking orders — a skill built through
  practice. Being self-aware/transparent about your thinking is the core. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) **Body language** matters: don't "hover" behind a designer
  (references the "Hovering Art Directors" site); pull up a chair beside or even
  kneel below them so it's a conversation, not an attack from behind. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) Default to **objectivity**: "Design is not art — it's solving
  a problem." Review work against the stated goals and parameters, so critique isn't
  subjective and no one gets defensive. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) Designers who genuinely can't take direction get let go /
  not re-booked ("we just fire them"); he softens it by taking the blame himself, but
  you need team players. Work getting killed after weeks isn't personal — "you're
  getting paid to do a task," credit/awards are just nice-to-have. (paraphrase)
- [2017-10-24] (Matthew) His own transition to director came when he stopped
  insisting on doing everything himself: on a 9-day, 30-second spot with two others he
  learned to divide/conquer to people's strengths — the biggest hurdle was accepting
  "I can't do everything myself." (paraphrase)
