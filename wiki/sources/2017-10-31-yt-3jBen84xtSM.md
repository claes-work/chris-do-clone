## Summary

An exercise-format episode of The Futur in which the team practices pitching design
work aloud. A self-taught designer on the team ("Ben") frames the motivation: design
schools teach a "visual vocabulary" by osmosis — words and ways of describing work —
that self-taught designers don't naturally pick up. To practice, the team pulls up a
set of found style frames (Russian promo frames for a "welcome 2013" tech forum/exhibit,
with unreadable text) and two people pitch them in turn as if presenting to a client.

Chris Do goes first, describing the frames in visual terms (geometric shapes used
unconventionally, clean modern typography, "simple shapes pulled together in a complex
way," thin lines with dots reading as technology). Matthew then delivers a more
polished, story-driven pitch — establishing the sequence up front, walking the client
frame by frame, building from "simple" to a "grand finale" celebration, using warm,
tactile texture language (marble, cylinders) as a deliberate contrast to the "cold and
digital" clichés of tech.

Chris closes with the teachable principle: before describing any pitch, give people the
context and a frame of reference so they know how to evaluate what they're seeing —
then walk them through the story, spend time on the crescendo, and skip the repetitive
frames.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona)

- `[2017-10-31]` (paraphrase) The most important thing in a pitch is to set context and
  a frame of reference BEFORE getting into details — otherwise the audience is left
  wondering "wait, what am I looking at?" as you flip through slides.
- `[2017-10-31]` (paraphrase) A good pitch establishes the overall piece and how it
  unfolds over time up front, then takes the client "frame by frame" as the story develops.
- `[2017-10-31]` (paraphrase) You should spend time on the crescendo/ending — the moment
  that matters — and deliberately "blow through" the repetitive frames that are "just
  more of the same," rather than dwelling on them.
- `[2017-10-31]` (paraphrase) When pitching found frames with no given context, invent a
  reasonable frame ("this is probably some kind of promo announcing a technology event")
  so both pitcher and audience can talk about it "in the same way."
- `[2017-10-31]` (paraphrase) Describe work in visual terms — geometric shapes used
  unconventionally, clean/modern typography, "simple shapes pulled together in a complex
  way" — and translate visuals into feeling and meaning (thin lines + dots read as
  "technology," basic shapes read as "basic principles").

### Ben (context — self-taught designer, uncertain exact identity)

- `[2017-10-31]` (paraphrase) Design schools teach a "visual vocabulary" largely by
  osmosis — students absorb words and ways of describing work by watching teachers
  critique. Self-taught designers aren't exposed to this and struggle to talk about design.
- `[2017-10-31]` (paraphrase) The inspiration for the exercise was hearing Chris and
  Matthew pitch work in person and noticing how differently — and how fluently — they
  described it.

### Matthew (context — presenter, uncertain exact identity)

- `[2017-10-31]` (paraphrase) Open a pitch by telling the client what they're about to
  see — "a set of boards to show you the sequence of how we saw this spot unfolding over
  time" — before showing anything.
- `[2017-10-31]` (paraphrase) Pitch the story/concept and let the visuals support it:
  connected nodes and lines = "the age of information," warm tactile textures (marble,
  cylinders) deliberately counter the "cold and digital" clichés of tech.
- `[2017-10-31]` (paraphrase) Structure the narrative as a build: start simple, pull
  information together, then "blow out" into a bigger celebratory experience / "grand finale."
