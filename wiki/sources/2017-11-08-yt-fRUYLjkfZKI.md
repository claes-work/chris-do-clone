# Get Out Of Your Comfort Zone — publicly commit to it (ep. 1/4)

## Summary

Coaching/catch-up episode of The Futur hosted by Chris Do, part 1 of 4. Chris brings
back two mentees he had previously coached on the show — Melinda and Rebecca (first
names only; family names not public → REDACTED) — to review what advice has been
hardest to implement and what has helped most.

Melinda's struggle: turning learned sales/strategy skills into results is bottlenecked
by lead generation and by the chicken-and-egg problem of needing paying clients to
practice a strategy service (the Futur's "CORE" strategic framework) that she doesn't
yet feel confident selling. Her workaround was leaning on the paid "Pro Group"
community of CORE practitioners and building case studies with friends/clients.

Rebecca's struggle: getting out and talking to people (small talk / positioning).
She has been deliberately striking up conversations with strangers (grocery line,
parking attendant, restaurant queues); her husband noticed unprompted that she is
friendlier, and she took a nervous sales call herself instead of delegating it.

Chris's throughline is the episode's thesis: **publicly committing to a goal creates
accountability that forces you out of your comfort zone**. He models it with his own
Adobe MAX story — he posted his goal on social media that morning ("day 2 is to get a
brand partnership deal"), which made going home empty-handed impossible, so he pushed
through the discomfort to pitch a vendor. He frames discomfort as skill that
compounds: the 1st conversation is hard, the 12th less so, and the difficulty ladder
just escalates to higher-stakes people over time. He also discloses he is himself an
introvert who still struggles with this after 10 years.

## Key claims (grouped by speaker)

### Chris Do (persona)

- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) Publicly committing to a goal (e.g. posting it on social
  media) creates accountability that makes it psychologically impossible to quit,
  because you'd disappoint not just yourself but the community watching you.
- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) Getting comfortable talking to people is a skill that
  compounds: the first conversation is difficult, the 12th is less difficult, and
  eventually it stops being "a thing" — but the challenge never ends, it just levels up.
- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) The difficulty ladder of conversation escalates:
  inconsequential strangers (nothing to gain or lose) → a client you'd like → your
  dream client → your dream client's boss → the president of a country.
- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) Chris identifies as an introvert; he believes much of the
  creative community are introverts, which is why "we run towards the computer" — it's
  controllable, safe, and you don't have to talk to anyone.
- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) He still struggles with this himself after ~10 years of
  working on it; he is not performing the struggle — the accountability is what keeps
  him practicing and reporting back to his audience.
- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) Learning a strategy service (CORE) is a chicken-and-egg
  problem: you don't feel confident to do it so nobody hires you, and you can't
  practice if nobody hires you. The way out is practicing inside the Pro Group
  community of practitioners and building case studies.
- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) With unqualified leads, get to the money/budget
  conversation fast — don't waste their time or yours. Chris's recommended opener:
  greet them, then ask their timeline and budget up front, explaining "I get a lot of
  inquiries that aren't a good fit, and the best way I know is to bring up money right
  away."
- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) People hire who they know, so referrals will always be
  your best source of leads; putting yourself out there publicly catches new leads who
  could never have found you the old way — including many unqualified ones, which is
  exactly why you must practice saying no fast.

### Melinda (mentee — CONTEXT, not persona)

- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) The hardest advice to implement was lead generation, plus
  needing to hear strategy/selling concepts more than once — she had to learn from
  several Pro Group peers, not just Chris, to truly understand.
- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) Being on the show increased her leads (notably via
  Behance and referrals), but not enough qualified leads to practice selling strategy;
  many inquiries have tiny budgets (e.g. $500 for a logo + packaging) or impossibly
  short timelines.
- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) She now brings up money ~15 minutes into a call, down
  from 45 minutes or "three days later," and is reframing rushed packaging requests by
  asking why the client is in their position and whether they have a style guide/roadmap.

### Rebecca (mentee — CONTEXT, not persona)

- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) Her hardest thing was getting out and doing small talk /
  positioning; it was hard at first but has gotten easier and now has "its own
  momentum" driven by her own inner voice rather than external pressure.
- `[2017-11-08]` (paraphrase) Her husband noticed unprompted that she is friendlier;
  she now consciously greets and chats with strangers, and took a nervous prospect call
  herself instead of delegating it.
