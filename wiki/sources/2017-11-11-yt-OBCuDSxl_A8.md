# How To Talk About Money With Clients Ep.2/4

> Captions present (auto/uploaded English track available). Transcript ingested from raw file.

## Summary

Part 2 of The Futur's 4-part series on talking about money with clients. Chris Do
coaches a small group of designers (Rebecca, Melinda, and other participants) through
two intertwined problems: (1) how to handle an inbound inquiry from an unqualified,
budget-vague prospect without wasting time or feeling like you're being rude, and
(2) how to generate qualified leads in the first place so you're not desperate for
any single job.

The first half is a live role-play. Chris frames the salesperson analogy: when you
walk into an electronics store to buy a $2,000 camera lens but only have $400, you
just want the price — you don't want a discovery conversation. He then role-plays
receiving a cold inbound call, opening by getting the caller's name, thanking them,
and asking for the budget up front to avoid wasting both parties' time. He surfaces
the client's constraints (budget $500, scope: logo + packaging, timeline: next
Friday) and delivers his signature constraint framework: you can usually satisfy two
of the three variables (budget, scope, timeline) but not all three. When the fit is
poor he refers the lead out to his network via an intro email rather than chasing it.

The second half reframes the underlying anxiety ("I don't want to lose the job") —
you can't lose something you never had — and pivots to lead generation. Chris uses
the "priming the pump" metaphor (from Darren Hardy's *The Compound Effect*) and a
locomotive analogy to explain that consistent marketing takes huge upfront effort
with no visible result, then compounds. He gives platform-specific advice
(Facebook = older/moneyed audience, Instagram = beautiful visuals, Twitter =
conversational/customer service, Pinterest = archival + buyer's mindset), stresses
meta-tagging/SEO of portfolio work on Behance and Pinterest, and explains how
influential bloggers and platform editors amplify good, findable work. Closing
mindset beat: it's supposed to be hard — the difficulty is what makes success
valuable and keeps competition out.
