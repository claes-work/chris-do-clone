# The Best Logos Ever Designed Are Simple Not Interesting & Not Overworked

## Summary

Chris Do (host, The Futur) and co-host Molly critique a set of logos live, using the
exercise to argue a core design principle: the best logos are simple, restrained, and
built on a well-chosen typeface with minimal modification, not "decorative,"
"interesting," or overworked. Chris praises a "City Steel" mark (sans-serif with a
slab-serif quality, an overlapped dimensional "C", orange + desaturated slate-blue
palette) and a wordmark set in a Futura-black-style typeface with slight modifications
that make its letters "feel like a community."

The central tension: Molly pushes back that simple type-only marks look "boring" on
their own. Chris agrees they can look boring in isolation but argues that (1) a good
logo *can* be boring, and (2) you must judge the mark alone, separately from its
application. Molly then clarifies her own point — luxury fashion marks (Louis Vuitton,
Dior) look plain as bare letters but read as powerful because our minds recall the
art-directed campaigns, products, and full-page magazine ads they live on.

Chris invokes Dieter Rams and Braun as the benchmark of simplicity and restraint, and
compares timeless furniture (Saarinen tulip chair, Saarinen table) that still looks
current after ~50 years to overworked "funky" design that dates quickly (his metaphor:
flared bell-bottoms and pigtails). He frames the recurring battle with young designers
who resist restrained, typographic solutions because "you haven't done anything" — and
positions the designer's value as good judgment and taste that delivers something
lasting, not the latest trend.

  "amazing," "dynamic," and "the latest coolest thing" are qualities that age quickly.
