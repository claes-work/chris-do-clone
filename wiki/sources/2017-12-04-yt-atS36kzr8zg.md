## Key claims

All dated 2017-12-04. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Attributed to **Chris Do** unless noted.

- Chris (paraphrase): The way to fix a flat, monotonous layout is to break up uniformity — when every group of type occupies roughly the same amount of space/mass, the eye reads it all as "the same" and disengages; introduce contrast and variation to create hierarchy.
- Chris (paraphrase): Look at type in terms of "volume and mass" — treat blocks of text as groups and evaluate how much visual space each group takes relative to the others.
- Chris (paraphrase): Things generally "look better smaller" because reducing an element's size increases the surrounding negative space. Analogy: a small room feels cramped with large furniture and better with small furniture — scale elements to leave breathing room.
- Chris (paraphrase): Justified type with lots of negative space reads well, but redundant/repeated words (e.g. "the... the...") in a short list are a problem; find a clever way to reduce redundancy rather than repeat.
- Chris (paraphrase): A print technique — printing on vellum, with the red on the front and the black mirrored on the back — lets the two overlapping words be read against each other (a physical overprint/registration trick), not just a translucent blend.
- Chris (paraphrase): With enough experience ("10,000 hours of typography and layout") you develop intuition for exactly where elements belong; much of what he's doing is intuitive because he's done it "a million times."
- Chris (paraphrase): Spacing carries meaning — if the gap between an element and a block is even/equal it can feel wrong or ambiguous (is this idea attached or separate?); halving the distance toward a one-third / two-thirds relationship (referencing what "Eddie was doing") often feels better than equal spacing.
- Chris (paraphrase): Avoid italicizing an entire paragraph — italics signal emphasis, so emphasizing everything destroys the emphasis and forces other fixes (like adding a second color) to restore hierarchy; a regular weight would have preserved alignment better.
- Chris (paraphrase): Beyond the usual vertical/left-justified alignment, designers should exploit other axes — the baseline as a principal axis, a horizontal axis, and even rotating type to create a new working axis. Rotation works better when a list is longer.
- Chris (paraphrase): You can anchor elements to each other by adding a separate design element (e.g. a drawn shape tying a baseline to another line) rather than only moving the whole block of type around.
- Chris (paraphrase): Not everything on a page must be purely functional — a design also has to catch and hold interest (e.g. a billboard glimpsed in passing), but he prefers to make elements functional where possible.
- Chris (paraphrase): The goal in placement is to find the point where an element is either too far out (and the composition "breaks") or too close (and it "doesn't do anything") — you tune to the sweet spot between.
- Molly (paraphrase): Her fix suggestion for the flat layout was "contrast" / more variation, because right now everything sits at the same level and she doesn't want to read the two lines.
- Molly (paraphrase, prompted repeatedly): The recurring fix when an element gets too big/heavy is to "make it smaller."
