# F1 Formula 1 Logo Review Critique

> Auto-captions available; cleaned/punctuated by curator. Solo Chris Do narration.

## Summary

Chris Do reviews the November 2017 Formula One rebrand — the new F1 logo designed by
Wieden+Kennedy, replacing the 1994 Carter Wong mark. He frames the critique as a response
to designers loudly panning the new logo ("under protests"). His core argument: judge a
logo by its **system and business fit**, not by its standalone aesthetics. He walks through
the identity system (three custom typefaces — F1 Regular, F1 Turbo, F1 Torque — flexible
repeatable marks, application on merchandise/uniforms/champagne bottles/cars), quotes the
brief ("reposition Formula One as a forward-facing entertainment brand which works across a
multitude of channels"), and cites industry voices (Michael Beirut's diving-vs-swimming
analogy, Ross Brawn on flexibility, Carter Wong's classy public response to being replaced).
He closes with his signature "the logo is the tip of a giant beast called the business"
argument and warns designers that fixating on aesthetics leaves them "fighting for the table
scraps."

## Key claims

### Chris Do (narrator)

- [2017-12-15] The 1994 F1 logo was designed by Carter Wong; it has a dated 80s-racing feel
  and was applied mostly as a mark superimposed on images, not as a full identity system.
  (paraphrase)
- [2017-12-15] The new F1 logo was designed by Wieden+Kennedy — the agency behind Nike's
  "Just do it" and the award-winning Old Spice "smell like a man, man" campaign. (paraphrase;
  note: he adds "there's some debate about who came up with what idea" re Nike tagline)
- [2017-12-15] A mark must be conceived within an entire system: how it's applied, how
  flexible it is, and what components can be deconstructed from it and reused elsewhere — so
  it becomes "a very rich tapestry of design." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2017-12-15] A logo is the beginning of a conversation, not a closed one — designers
  explore many options (e.g., negative-shape explorations) and arrive at a solution
  together with the client. (paraphrase)
- [2017-12-15] Logo size/treatment should be designed for the screen space or printed
  surface area available (illustrated by the "one small repeated logo vs. one big logo"
  system). (paraphrase)
- [2017-12-15] The mark's genius is that it can recede — shapes and lines indicate speed,
  power and torque without shouting; it fits alongside sponsor logos rather than competing
  with them. (paraphrase, echoing the designers' intent)
- [2017-12-15] If the original design team (Carter Wong / W+K community) isn't upset, angry
  designers should ask themselves why they are. (paraphrase)
- [2017-12-15] ★ The logo is just the tip that "sits on a giant beast called the business";
  the business has a vision it needs to move forward, and designers who spend all their
  energy arguing over whether a logo is good will be "fighting for the table scraps" and lose
  the war within the world of business. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2017-12-15] Today every brand needs to think of itself as being in the entertainment
  space / as a media company. (paraphrase)

### Cited third parties (CONTEXT — not Chris; do not train persona)

- [2017-12-15] Michael Beirut (Pentagram), via Twitter: "most people comment on the logo
  launches as if they're judging a diving competition when they should be judging a swimming
  competition" — i.e., branding is a long-distance effort. (quote, attributed to Beirut)
- [2017-12-15] Ross Brawn (F1 managing director): the new logo is more flexible for
  merchandising and digital use; "the old logo was neither iconic or memorable." (quote,
  attributed to Brawn)
- [2017-12-15] The F1 design director (transcript renders name as "Turley") on the identity:

- [2017-12-15] Carter Wong (original 1994 designers) publicly responded with class after
