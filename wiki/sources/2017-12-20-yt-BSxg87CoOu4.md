## Summary

A Futur teaching episode presented by Ben Burns laying out a step-by-step methodology
for presenting logo/identity designs to clients so as to sell a single concept and
control revisions. Ben frames logo presentation as "a science, not an art" and argues
the presentation is the "packaging" for the product (the logo). He covers prerequisites
(solid discovery, style-scapes, best work, right tools — Photoshop + Keynote), then a
7-step presentation build: (1) start at the beginning by re-stating the client's pain
point/objective, (2) recap the collaborative steps and discovery words taken so far,
(3) display work — exactly three closely-related concepts, each on a fixed 5-slide
sequence (isolated logo on white → one-color split screen → standard mock-ups → small-
format mock-ups → large-format mock-ups → close on isolated logo on white), plus a
hidden "all options compared" slide. A mid-episode segment with Emily demonstrates
building mock-ups from Storyblocks stock via Photoshop smart objects. The close covers
asking targeted questions that steer clients away from personal preference, not
expecting feedback or creative direction, and setting timelines/next steps. Sponsored
by Storyblocks.

## Key claims

All claims below are attributed to **Ben Burns** (presenter) as of 2017-12-20 and are
paraphrased unless quoted. The single Chris-Do-attributed item is marked.

- **[Ben Burns]** Presenting logos is a science, not an art — a repeatable methodology
  honed by mistakes lets you sell logo designs more easily. If the logo is the product,
  the presentation is the packaging.
- **[Ben Burns]** Stop sending logo designs over email; identity work should be
  presented in person or over the phone. Emailing individual JPGs (he once sent 15
  options) is a mistake that invites the client to act as art director.
- **[Ben Burns]** Don't treat clients like designers/creative directors even when they
  are — creative direction is the designer's job, which is what they hired you for.
- **[Ben Burns]** The real purpose of a strong first presentation is to manage and
  control revisions (avoid scope creep / version 16, 17, 18) by selling the client on a
  single concept up front.
- **[Ben Burns]** Prerequisites before presenting: (1) a solid discovery phase that
  organizes the client's ideas so both start "on the same page"; (2) style-scapes
  ("mood boards on crack") to set a visual compass direction; (3) genuinely incredible
  best work; (4) the right tools — Photoshop for mock-ups, Keynote (preferred over
  PowerPoint) for the deck.
- **[Chris Do — referenced by Ben]** Chris Do calls the multi-step creative journey
  "scaffolding" — building toward the final product in sequential steps. (Ben notes his
  own process winds around more than a clean linear path.)
- **[Ben Burns]** Step 1 — Start at the beginning: re-state why the client wants a new
  identity, inserting their pain point (or objective), to put them back into the buying
  mentality they had at the start of the engagement.
- **[Ben Burns]** Step 2 — Recap the collaborative steps and the discovery words the
  client themselves chose; it's hard for people to disagree with themselves, so
  reminding them of their own decisions makes the results hard to refute. Spend no more
  than ~5 minutes; it's a "palate cleanser."
- **[Ben Burns]** Step 3 — Present exactly **three** logo concepts. Three lets a client
  say no to one without being forced to say yes to one, while still choosing between two.
  The three must be closely related because the style-scape already set direction.
- **[Ben Burns]** Attach a story/thought to each concept — narrative raises perceived
  value (cites an eBay study where a story-driven description sold an item for a much
  higher price than spec-only). Ask whether each story is better told visually, e.g. by
  showing the relationship between a designed element and its inspiration source.
- **[Ben Burns]** Each of the three versions uses the same 5-slide sequence: (1)
  isolated logo on white with heavy negative space; (2) one-color logo on a split
  dark/light screen to prove it works without color; (3) standard mock-ups (business
  card, stationery, envelope) — every template/stock photo must be on-brand; (4)
  small-format mock-ups (pens, pins, wax seals, cufflinks); (5) large-format mock-ups
  (signage, wayfinding, billboards) — putting a small-business owner's logo on a
  building is an ego boost. Then close each version on the isolated-logo-on-white slide
  again as "closure." Total: 5 slides × 3 versions = 15 slides.
- **[Ben Burns]** Mock-up ideas double as upsell opportunities — roughly "seven times
  out of ten" the client asks to actually produce items they see, letting you sell
  additional services.
- **[Ben Burns]** Include a single "all options side by side" comparison slide, kept
  hidden but available, because clients always ask for it even though comparing apples
  to apples is not ideal.
- **[Ben Burns]** After the "thank you" slide, ask targeted questions that steer clients
  away from personal preference. Openers: "Did we miss the mark completely?" (breaks the
  awkward post-presentation silence); referencing a named user persona (e.g. "Joe Dirt")
  to trigger empathy instead of "what do you like?"; "Did we take a step in the right
  direction to [pain point / grow revenue]?"; "Is there one direction we can cross off
  the board right now?" (surfaces the least-liked option without forcing a favorite).
  Avoid open-ended "what did you think / did you like these?" — that's the danger zone.
- **[Ben Burns]** Step 6 — Don't expect feedback or creative direction in the room; give
  the client time to digest and share internally.
- **[Ben Burns]** Final step — Set timelines and expectations; never leave a meeting
  without a due date for feedback and clear next steps, or clients ghost.
- **[Emily, Futur team]** For mock-ups: source high-res stock (Storyblocks), pick images
  that match the brand (a rustic scene won't suit a slick brand like Apple), and build
  Photoshop smart objects so images can be swapped without redoing the work.
