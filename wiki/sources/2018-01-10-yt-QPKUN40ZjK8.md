# Double Your Revenue! Business Strategy On How to Grow Your Design Agency

## Summary

Chris Do runs a live business-coaching / "business therapy" session with two partners
of a very small video-production agency — referred to as Jung and Mark (company nickname
"Access of Evil"). The agency is a two-person partnership plus a couple of interns, doing
~$200k/year, and the partners want to double revenue to ~$400k. Rather than prescribe a
generic plan, Chris insists on a dialogue so the client arrives at his own answers.

He diagnoses the real bottleneck as a **sales-pipeline problem**: nearly all revenue comes
from existing clients, and the partners have never asked those clients for referrals. Chris
frames a growth strategy as picking fruit at three heights — **low-hanging fruit** (referrals
from existing happy clients), mid-hanging, and out-of-reach — and stresses it is "all of the
above," not either/or. Most of the session is a concrete referral + cold-outreach playbook:
a spoken script for asking clients for referrals, capturing referral contact info on the spot
(so the client doesn't forget), succinctly explaining your expanded services, and writing a
short personalized outreach email. He closes with a Simon Sinek anecdote about **the order in
which you say things** — lead with the ask, then the pleasantries, so the warmth doesn't feel
like manipulation.

Single primary teacher (Chris Do). The two coached partners speak as clients (CONTEXT).

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2018-01-10] Before coaching anyone, define terms and current state — company size, structure,
  current revenue, target revenue — instead of writing solutions that work for you, not them.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] 200% growth (doubling revenue) is very ambitious and requires becoming a
  fundamentally different, more capable person — "you have to be twice the human you are today."
  (quote/paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] Warns against chasing an arbitrary big number ("I want to do 10 million") without
  asking who you must *become* to be worth that much money. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] Hiring more people does not necessarily increase revenue — it increases expenses;
  so headcount is not where to look for growth. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] Good coaching / facilitation is a dialogue — "more like business therapy" — you
  must get the person to find the answer themselves so the solution actually fits them.
  (paraphrase; quote "business therapy")
- [2018-01-10] If revenue is coming only from existing clients and they are not referring you,
  that is a big problem — and the fix costs nothing but asking. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] Work the "lowest hanging fruit" first: squeeze more out of people you already know,
  because onboarding new clients is very hard. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] Two mindset blocks to destroy: (1) the fear that asking for referrals damages the
  relationship — asking actually strengthens it; (2) doubt that asking is worthwhile — just try
  and see. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] Most people are not thinking about you — they are busy running their business and
  balancing life; the assumption that they don't want to refer you is unfounded. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] Referral ask script: tell the client you're growing and have hired help, remind
  them of the good work, and ask "do you know anybody that could use our services?" (paraphrase/quote)
- [2018-01-10] When asking, help the client understand what you do — briefly name your full range
  of services (e.g. shooting, editing, color correction, events, product launches) — succinctly,
  without pitching or selling. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] Take the referral's contact info *on the spot*, because the client will return to
  their own busy life and forget — "take control of that relationship" rather than wait in the dark.
  (paraphrase; quote fragments)
- [2018-01-10] Cold outreach email tips: craft the subject line, research "how to write marketing
  emails / the best subject lines," and don't make it read like marketing up front. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] Open the email with a simple story referencing the mutual connection ("I was talking
  to Bob, he recommended I reach out…"), state you'd love to discuss their sales/marketing efforts,
  and ask for a coffee. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-10] Keep outreach personal and very short — a recognizable template gets sent to archives;
  don't tell your whole life story, "just get to the ask and then do the other stuff later." (quote)
- [2018-01-10] Cites Simon Sinek (author of *Start With Why*): the *order* in which you say things
  matters. Leading with pleasantries before an ask negates the pleasantries; flipping the order —
  ask first, small talk after — makes the warmth feel genuine rather than manipulative. (paraphrase,
  attributing framing to Sinek)
