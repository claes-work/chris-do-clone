# Why Some Designers Are More Valuable Than Others

★ **L3-candidate** — This is a landmark positioning/value framework talk. It introduces two of Chris Do's most repeated frameworks: (1) the **value scale of questions** (low-value quantitative/fact questions → mid-value qualitative questions → high-value purpose questions), and (2) **Discovery as a paid, question-based service** that peels back symptoms to reach cause/purpose (the "onion"). Both are central to persona/beliefs and worth promoting into `wiki/topics/` at synthesis.

## Summary

A single-speaker lecture by Chris Do (monologue; no other speakers) arguing that a designer's value has nothing to do with experience, portfolio, awards, education, or studio size. Instead, value comes from the ability to **help others find their meaning and purpose**. He anchors this in Maslow's hierarchy of needs — self-actualization (creativity, meaning, purpose) sits at the top — and in the premise that "the most difficult thing in life is to know yourself," so helping someone know themselves is inherently valuable.

The mechanism for delivering that value is **asking questions**. Chris introduces a "value scale" of questions: fact-based quantitative questions (what does it look like, when is it due, what's the budget) are low-value and turn the designer into an order-taker / "hands for hire." Qualitative, open-ended questions (how did you come up with this idea, how will you judge success) are higher-value and invite the client to collaborate. The highest-value questions get to purpose — "why is this important," "why do you exist" (beyond money) — and cause clients to reflect. He frames a company's stated needs as **symptoms** on the outside of a circle; persistent purpose-driven questioning finds the **cause** at the core, "where the true solutions live." He names this practice **Discovery** — a paid framework of questions designed to surface deeper meaning — and closes with a sample purpose statement produced from one, then asks the audience what such an outcome is worth.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) The most difficult thing in life is to know yourself; the easiest thing is to give advice. (Frames this as a response someone gave when asked.)
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) What makes a designer more valuable is NOT experience, portfolio, awards, education, staff, or studio size — those are the answers he usually gets when he asks the question, and they're wrong.
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) A designer's real value is their ability to help others find their meaning and purpose — because if knowing yourself is the hardest thing, helping someone know themselves is very valuable.
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) This help can take any form: podcasts, video/animation, storytelling, an identity system (branding), a website, or social media — all are ways to help someone know and tell their own story.
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) You help someone know themselves through asking questions.
- `[2018-01-18]` (quote) "Your value is determined by the quality of the questions that you ask." (Restated: the questions you ask demonstrate your knowledge, experience, confidence, and therefore your expertise.)
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) Quantitative, fact-based questions (what does it look like, when is it due, what's the budget) are important for getting the job done but are the lower-value questions — they lead to checklists and can be pedantic. These are the questions he asked early in his own career when he just wanted the facts.
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) Qualitative questions (how did you come up with this idea, describe how this looks in your mind, how will you judge if this is successful) are higher-value, open-ended, and invite the client to collaborate and participate.
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) Detailed/fact questions make you an order-taker — like taking a fast-food order — and cause the client to see you as "hands for creative work" (someone they sit next to and direct: what shade of red, 68 or 72 point type).
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) The highest-value questions get to purpose and make people reflective: "why is this important," "why do you believe that to be true," and the mother of all questions, beyond money — "why do you exist / why does this company exist."
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) Companies lose their way when founders are no longer connected to running the company; they become profit-driven and beholden to shareholders. That's why firms bring founders back or pay large management consultancies to remind them who they are and return them to their roots/purpose.
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) On the outside of the circle are the **symptoms** — what clients tell you they need ("I need a new website"). Persistent purpose-driven questioning finds the **cause** at the core, which is where the true solutions live. (e.g., "why do you need a new website?" → "customers are complaining" → keep diving to the core.)
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) The "onion" analogy: at the center is the core purpose/meaning; you keep peeling back layers to reach it.
- `[2018-01-18]` (paraphrase) This practice is called **Discovery**, and they charge money to do it — a framework of questions designed to surface deeper meaning.
- `[2018-01-18]` (quote) Sample Discovery output: "we help the client figure out that they exist to create a safe and supportive environment for individuals who have felt ostracized by society so that they can form meaningful connections."
