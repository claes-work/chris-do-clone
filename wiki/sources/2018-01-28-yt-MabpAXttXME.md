# How To Hire Freelancers & Grow Your Business Pro Call

## Summary

A short "Pro Call" excerpt in which Chris Do coaches a caller (a designer/business
owner, unnamed) on how to hire freelancers profitably and grow beyond doing all the
work themselves. Chris breaks down realistic freelance day rates versus billable client
rates, explains why the margin between the two is what makes an agency viable, and gives
a practical method for calibrating expectations, trialing freelancers before committing
to project rates, and sourcing talent by cold-messaging designers on social media whose
follower/view ratio suggests they are still hungry for work. He closes by urging the
caller to build a standing database of freelancers so that no single unavailable person
becomes a bottleneck.

The coachee is not named in the transcript; treat their statements as CONTEXT only.
Only Chris Do's coaching and framing feed the persona.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (SUBJECT — persona-eligible)

- [2018-01-28] When bringing in a freelance graphic designer, Chris typically pays
  roughly **$400–$600 per day** for an eight-hour day; $600 / 8 ≈ **$75/hour at the
  high end** — and that high end is someone with a couple years' experience who works
  fast and can be trusted to deliver. (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- [2018-01-28] A hustling freelancer winning their own work may charge **$125–$150/hour**,
  but you cannot pay the people working *for* you at that level — otherwise there is no
  margin left for profit; the gap between billable rate and pay rate is the point.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-28] Per-project (fixed) rates are good once you've worked with someone and
  trust they can deliver, because you control the expense and don't worry about hours;
  but Chris has been burned when he project-rated someone who turned out terrible in the
  first two days, forcing him to break an agreement — which he dislikes doing.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-28] Work with a freelancer on a plain freelance basis first to learn how they
  work *before* moving them to project rates. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-28] When hiring (freelance or staff), calibrate your expectations against what
  the person can realistically produce — e.g., for a logo brief given in the morning,
  ask what to expect by end of day, agree on it, then proceed. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-28] Use a "half-point check-in": have the freelancer send work partway so you
  can catch a wrong direction early. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-28] Chris cites **20-plus years** of working with freelancers: most worked out
  well, some were total disasters. (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- [2018-01-28] Sourcing method: follow designers whose work you like on social; there's a
  "sweet spot" ratio — someone with ~2M views and ~200k followers is probably hireable
  (gut guess), while someone with a few hundred thousand views and only several hundred
  to a thousand followers is likely still looking for work. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-28] Reach out via both website contact forms and DMs, because many creatives
  (himself included) don't check their email inboxes. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-28] Cold-outreach script: praise a specific piece of their work, say you have
  work that needs their talent, and ask how they work, their prices, and where they're
  located; genuine job-seekers will be thrilled to hear from you. (paraphrase)
- [2018-01-28] Build up a **database of freelancers** you want to work with, because your
  first choice may not be available — this is why The Futur maintains a directory of
  freelancers across disciplines. (paraphrase)

### Coachee (CONTEXT — not persona-eligible, attribution uncertain / unnamed)
