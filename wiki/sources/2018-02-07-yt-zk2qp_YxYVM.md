## Summary

A graphic-design portfolio review on The Futur's YouTube channel (Part 2 of a
two-part series). An instructor (not Chris Do) walks the student, Emily — a former
intern/freelancer at Blind/The Futur who worked during design school — through her
portfolio chronologically, from her oldest piece to recent professional work. The
conversation traces her growth from "make it because it looks cool" arbitrary
design toward strategy-driven, client-centered design.

Projects discussed:
- **Oldest piece (~2013):** a packaging/branding class project — a made-up vodka
  brand in a red-and-white Russian-constructivist style. Emily says the design was
  purely aesthetic with no underlying meaning; no longer in her portfolio.
- **Group project — surfboard promo:** a stop-motion-style promotional video using
  a handmade miniature set and sculpted clay surfer character; Emily helped paint
  sculptures and did some filming, and learned Premiere Pro and miniature-shooting
  technique (shallow depth of field, tilt-shift effect).
- **First professional branding project (early 2016, "Brainy"/client work at
  Blind):** logo explorations, style scapes, a responsive website carousel, and
  print/collateral mock-ups. Emily describes learning to design for a real client
  and real code constraints, and struggling to use the strategy deck.
- **A second client branding project ("Tango", an IT manager brand):** logo with a
  baseline bar signifying "bottom line," signage, poster, and website mock-ups —
  Emily's first in-depth website design.

The instructor draws out lessons about strategy-to-design translation, honesty
about skill gaps, seeking help, and balancing form with function.

## Key claims (grouped by speaker; all `[2018-02-07]`)

### Reviewer — instructor, NOT Chris Do (likely Greg Gunn; attribution: uncertain)
- `[2018-02-07]` Beginning designers naturally make arbitrary, "that looks cool"
  decisions with no underlying meaning; this is where everyone starts.
- `[2018-02-07]` A design with a connectable story communicates; without one, a
  viewer can only react subjectively ("I like it / I don't"), which means little.
- `[2018-02-07]` Shooting miniatures nearly always yields shallow depth of field;
  tilt-shift photography exploits this to make real scenes look miniature — the
  effect to avoid when photographing something small.
- `[2018-02-07]` When stuck searching for reference imagery, change the search:
  use a thesaurus / different search strings, or ask a teammate for ideas.
- `[2018-02-07]` A good designer honestly admits what they don't know and says "I
  know someone who can help" rather than bluffing — people respect honesty. (Note:
  reviewer frames this as his own practice — "I'll try to find people smarter and
  more experienced… reverse-engineer their process… ask for a consultation.")
- `[2018-02-07]` The process should funnel from broad (no strategy) to specific, so
  that by the time you design, the direction is clear.
- `[2018-02-07]` Strong concept/strategy makes good design easier and gives you a
  metric to measure against ("does it solve the problem I promised?"), saving you
  from getting stuck.

### Emily — student (CONTEXT, not the subject)
- `[2018-02-07]` Her oldest piece's decisions were "totally arbitrary… oh that's
  cool, I'm gonna do that"; she has since outgrown the vector-constructivism style.
- `[2018-02-07]` She freelanced at Blind/The Futur during design school, stacking
  classes at the start or end of the week to work on projects mid-week.
- `[2018-02-07]` The biggest shift from school to real work: the technical/
  application layer — designs must be responsive, code-able, on-brand, consistent,
  and above all "it has to work."
- `[2018-02-07]` On her first branding project she didn't know how to use the
  strategy deck Chris gave her — she read it but couldn't translate it into design,
  and restarted from scratch at each phase.
- `[2018-02-07]` Now she designs by asking whether the work solves the customer's
  pain point, serves their goals, and helps their business — not just how it looks.
- `[2018-02-07]` When stuck finding style-scape reference photos she has "no
  solution" except to keep looking / dig deeper.
