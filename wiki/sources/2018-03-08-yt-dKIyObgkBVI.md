## Summary

Episode 10 of a recurring role-play/coaching series. Chris Do coaches Melinda, a designer
who has moved from being an "order-taker" to a "trusted advisor," on how to sell the
*thinking* (strategy) half of design work — the high-margin part — over the phone with no
website, no deck, and no visuals. He argues that thinking, unlike making, needs no
mock-ups: you demonstrate its value purely by talking, listening, and telling stories.

They run a live sales role-play: Melinda plays a startup founder with a finished app
framework/UX who needs branding and a website; Chris plays the design-firm principal. Chris
demonstrates his sequence — thank the caller, ask why they reached out (referral discovery),
frame the engagement as a repeatable "framework," describe a discovery process centered on
the client's *customers* (pain points, jobs-to-be-done, psychographics not just
demographics), quote strategy at $5K–$10K up front and separate from deliverables, then
soft-quote deliverable ranges (website $20K–$30K incl. dev, logo $10K, collateral ~$10K),
and close by offering to send a deck/proposal only as backup.

A meta-discussion follows: Melinda closes everything in one call; Chris normally splits
qualifying (call 1) and closing (call 2). They reconcile the two approaches. Chris warns
against "going past the sale" — once the client says yes, stop sending things that could
confuse them. He also stresses describing the process in "bite-sized pieces" (the
block-of-marble / chisel metaphor) — expand only as the client asks for more.

Chris then delivers the standout **intangibles demo**: he "orders lunch" for Melinda twice.
First as an order-taker (Caesar salad, bread, soup — direction received, no dialogue), then
as a strategist who asks about dietary preferences, past peak experiences (Club 33 at
Disneyland), and quantifies that 40% of the value was the *experience*, not the food —
ending by proposing they actually go to Disneyland. Point: an order-taker delivers the
plate; a strategist surfaces what the client really wants, makes them feel understood, and
can bill $10K for that realization. Done well, this leaves you with almost no competition.

Coaching business advice: Melinda is at capacity ("stretched," working late) — time to hire
a *roster* of semi-available freelancers who know her process, not full-timers. And keep
doubling the strategy rate: $5K → $10K → $20K. Strategy price also acts as a filter — a
client who nickel-and-dimes design will never pay for discovery, because they don't value
thinking.

  it's your best opportunity to establish who you are and what you'll do for the client.
