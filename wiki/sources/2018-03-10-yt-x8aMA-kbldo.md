## Summary

A single-narrator lesson on why you should refuse low-paying / lowball work.
Kier McLaren illustrates the principle with two stories from his own career:

1. **The sporting-goods / firearms store** (family business, "in business since
   1890s"). The owner, **Meyer**, is offered a chance to undercut on a rare,
   hard-to-get Smith & Wesson revolver during wartime demand. Rather than argue
   with a customer who claims he can buy cheaper elsewhere, Meyer hands the
   customer ~$500 cash to bring back three units at the cheaper price — knowing
   the competitor is sold out. The customer returns empty-handed; Meyer's punch
   line: *"If I was sold out I'd sell them for $120 too."* Moral: if a customer
   says they can get it cheaper elsewhere, let them go — either they're not
   telling the truth or they're not the kind of customer you want.

2. **The motion-picture camera rental company** Kier worked for (supplied 35mm
   film cameras/gear to the film, advertising and TV industry). They had enough
   gear for four simultaneous shows. After renting three sets they'd panic and
   discount the fourth to a lowball client — then a full-price client would call
   and they'd have no gear left. Over three years they saw they *always* had four
   shows, so the "risk takers" started refusing the lowball fourth job, and a
   full-price client reliably showed up at the end. Moral: know your business,
   have the courage to stand by what you're worth, and don't panic-discount.

Kier repeatedly qualifies: if you genuinely need cash flow to make rent/payroll
on Friday, take the money — he's not a fool about that. But if you have an overall
vision of what your business is worth, don't take lowball jobs, especially selling
intellectual/creative work (not physical goods). Taking cheap work ties up your
staff so you can't serve the high-value client (e.g. "General Motors") when they
call. Fear is framed as the biggest issue in business and personal life; a future
episode is promised on overcoming it.
