## Summary

Role Play episode 11. Chris Do sets up a scenario: a client comes in who "knows exactly what she wants" — a logo, already sketched, done fast and cheap — and tries to dictate how the designer works. Chris plays the design-studio side and models how to hold the line without arguing. He reframes the engagement away from "logo execution" toward strategy, brand and customer-acquisition work; when the client insists on just a cheap logo, he calmly prices the real engagement at "north of $20–30K," tells her that if all she wants is her sketch executed she should hire a young graduate or a design-broker site for a few thousand dollars, and offers to refer her out. He uses a nuts-and-bolts manufacturing analogy to flip the "client dictating the process" objection back on her, then closes by putting the deal on pause rather than caving.

In the debrief, Chris teaches the underlying method: **embrace first, then pivot to logic**; take a position only to break down the other person's position ("I'm not here to argue with you"); give the client little to fight against; get people to **double down on their own thinking** by asking "why do you believe that?"; and only chase openings when the client changes their argument. A long tangent follows on Chris's own learning posture — he welcomes being challenged (by his wife, kids, coaches), is a "constant improver," and reflects that the more informed two people are, the less they debate and the more they share and "steal from each other in a positive way."

### Chris Do (persona)

- `[2018-03-16]` Open the conversation from a point of friction and don't over-help, so the real client pain points surface. (paraphrase — his role-play setup instruction)
- `[2018-03-16]` Position yourself not as a logo executor but as someone who runs strategy first: "I'd love to make a logo for you, however I don't do it the way we used to do just a few years back — we go through strategy and then find insights into who your customers are and how to help your business." (quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` Clients often self-diagnose and prescribe a solution; that's like walking into a doctor and demanding penicillin for a headache — "the solution may not be connected to the problem, and that's why we do what we do." (paraphrase + partial quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` Design is "fairly subjective"; strategy exists so the client isn't left questioning whether the final decision was right. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-16]` Price the real engagement honestly and let it filter: "probably even to enter into a conversation with me about doing logos... we're gonna be north of 20 to 30 thousand dollars easily." (quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` If the client just wants a sketch executed cheaply, tell them the truth: "you could probably just hire some young graduate or maybe go onto one of these sites that broker design services... it probably costs you a few thousand dollars at most." Be willing to refer them out. (paraphrase + partial quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` Refuse to strip out the value: "would you really want to pay for all that stuff only for me to make the logo?... that seems like overkill." (quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` Name the mismatch directly: the client wants fast, cheap, done yesterday — "I don't do that, I really don't." (paraphrase + quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` "Measure twice, cut once" — clients who make decisions without parameters change their mind tomorrow and get stuck; constant changes signal indecisiveness to their own consumers. (paraphrase + quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` Self-definition of the business: "I'm not in the logo building business, I'm in the brand building business... the client customer acquisition building business — that's what I do." (quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` Use the client's own domain to reframe: asks the nuts-and-bolts manufacturer whether she'd let a customer dictate her manufacturing process — "we're experts for a reason, we have a very clear process that gives very predictable results, and you want to dictate how we make it." (paraphrase + partial quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` Close without caving: "you deserve to have a design firm to do exactly what you want, and I also want to work with clients who want the kinds of things that I can bring to the table." Then pause and circle back if it makes sense. (quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` Method — embrace, then pivot: "the embracing part is taking care of the emotions... we can't come in to convince someone who's irrational at the moment; you have to embrace them where they're at, take care of the emotion first, then pivot to logic." (paraphrase + quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` Not everything is emotional — "I need it done in two weeks for $2000" is a quantifiable, logical need, not qualitative; don't force emotion labels onto it. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-16]` The embrace is just to listen and say it back: "I understand — here are your options." Then have them **double down on their thinking**. (quote + paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-16]` Getting people to double down exposes weak assumptions: ask a client praising a new business coach "what have they done, where did they go to school, what have they achieved?" and they start doubting. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-16]` "All you have to do is ask them why do you believe that, and it usually is enough to mess most people up, because most people are not that thoughtful and intentional — they're mostly reacting to life." (quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` "You're disagreeing without being disagreeable... I can't get into a fight with you if I don't take a position — my position is to break down your position." (quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` Most people want to be at the center of the conversation, so asking about their thinking plays into their natural desire to have energy focused on them. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-16]` A difficult client who is "single-track" and just repeats the same objection gives you nothing to work with; you find the opening only when they change the argument. If there's no progress, end it: "I don't feel we're making progress, we're not going to see eye to eye, it's not a good fit — then I'd get rid of them." (paraphrase + quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` "The bigger your emotional reaction to not wanting to do something, the truer it is — you're not ready to face that part of your life." (quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` On his own posture: he welcomes being challenged (wife, children, parents, business coach) and encourages it because "I want to learn if I'm doing something dumb"; calls himself "a constant improver." (paraphrase + quote)
- `[2018-03-16]` "The more informed you are, the less likely we're going to debate... it's only the ignorant people you can debate with, and they're easy to defeat." The more you learn, the more you feel you don't know. (quote + paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-16]` Between two informed people, "instead of debating we're stealing from each other in a positive way — I steal your idea, you steal my idea, and we both grow." When meeting other mentors/coaches, "I just subjugate the idea that I have to prove myself to be right because I just want to learn." (quote)
