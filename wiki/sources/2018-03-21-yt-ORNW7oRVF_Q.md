### Charli Marie (GUEST — CONTEXT ONLY, does not train persona)

- The most important thing to learn after design school is to **identify problems and find
  solutions**, rather than waiting for clients (or teammates) to dictate what to do. School
  trains you to chase grades; work requires independent thinking.
- When a teammate brings a request ("I need an image / a webpage with this content"), first
  **pause and clarify**: what is this for, why do we need it, do we already have something
  we can adapt? Understand the whole situation before producing.
- Favor the **smallest solution** that solves the problem (e.g., tweaking a landing page or
  adding info where users already look) instead of building a full new solution.
- This is not laziness or work-avoidance: companies always have more work than can be done,
  so the goal is to spend time on the **highest-impact, highest-return** projects. Solving
  problems with minimal solutions frees time for what matters most.
- Staying engaged in-house requires working for the **right company** — one willing to keep
  improving (user testing, data analysis, iterating on the website) rather than sticking to
  old ways for convenience. In-house lets you revisit and improve your past work, which
  agency/vendor designers rarely get to do.
- Iteration should be **optimization, not change for its own sake**; brand consistency is
  maintained by improving the same brand's execution and user experience over time.
- Protect yourself from being replaced by a cheaper version by **demonstrating value beyond
  graphic design** — applying design thinking across the company and influencing the brand
  and team strategically.
- She has personally **not experienced gender discrimination**; credits it partly to
  working for a **smaller company** with a more selective hiring process. Larger companies
  hiring fast are likelier to admit "bad apples" and breed an unwelcoming culture.
- When choosing an employer, vet the **team and people**, not just what the company does —
  follow potential colleagues on social media, understand who you'll spend your days with
  (often more time than with your partner).
- **Don't be afraid to leave** a company where you can't improve or produce results — but
  don't quit rashly without financial support. Distinguishes "golden handcuffs" from
  "bronze handcuffs"; if escaping means waking at 5 a.m. to build your portfolio and find a
  better job, do it — the temporary pain is worth it.
