## Summary

Episode 2 of The Futur's "Young Guns" series. Chris Do and co-host Emily review the online presence of five young/early-career designers and independently score each 1–5 across four fixed categories, without revealing scores until a later episode. For each designer they walk the live website / Behance / Instagram on-screen and critique it in real time — brand naming, site UX, project presentation, about-page copy, and social-media engagement math. The through-line is how a hiring client would find and evaluate you: consistency, clarity of service, craft of presentation, and personality. Recurring lessons: don't dilute a professional feed with unrelated personal/photography posts; write short, punchy about-copy; make process visible and well-photographed; be suspicious of engagement under ~10% (signals bought/for-follow audiences); and boosting posts inflates reach that collapses once you stop paying.

## Chris Do's four-category review rubric (the method)

Scored 1 (lowest) to 5 (highest), each host scoring privately:

1. **Social-media marketing** — Are you putting your name out consistently? Good engagement? High-quality images? A theme / editorial point of view?
2. **Presentation & quality of the work** — Creative, professional, complete work; good lighting, good mock-ups; does it feel finished.
3. **Clarity of service** — Did you describe what you do? Focus (laser vs. scattershot) plus evidence you actually do that thing.
4. **Personality of the person** — Your story / bio, a fun and professionally-shot headshot, activities, interests, hobbies outside work.

## Key claims (grouped by speaker)

### Chris Do (SUBJECT → persona)
- `[2018-03-24]` A portfolio should be judged the way a hiring client evaluates you: *how did I find you, is the work consistent, is it clear what you do, is the craft/presentation high* — if yes, "there's a good chance I'm going to add you to the list of people to call." (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` Engagement below ~10% of your following is a red flag — it suggests follow-for-follow or bought followers ("you didn't earn the followers"). (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` A sudden engagement spike that "fell right off the map" is suspicious and usually means a **boosted/sponsored post**; once you stop paying, the audience drops off. Boosting is fine, but you must then earn those new eyeballs by engaging back. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` When you get an anomaly of outsized attention, study it and deliberately replicate/duplicate what worked ("social listening"). (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` **Super stacking**: align as many of your hobbies, skills and interests as possible. A designer who is *also* a strong photographer should integrate original photography into the design work — then you do what few designers can (great photos) and what few photographers can (typography/design), instead of competing separately in two crowded fields. (paraphrase; concept named on-camera)
- `[2018-03-24]` Keep your professional feed on-message: "inform or inspire." Cycle out unrelated personal/photography posts if you don't want to be hired for that; put personal life in ephemeral Instagram Stories instead. He did this himself — posted "pictures of me doing dumb things" for an initial following, then removed it. (paraphrase + partial verbatim)
- `[2018-03-24]` The **Peter McKinnon model**: early vlogs got no traction; value/tutorial videos ("camera hacks," "how to take better photos") built a massive following; only *after* the following did personal/vlog content start to matter. Build the audience on craft first, then earn the right to personal stories. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` About-page copy should be short and punchy — "give me the pertinent details and the need-to-know bits." Most designers aren't copywriters, and writing more can hurt you. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` If a menu link is dead, don't leave it grayed-out to send visitors nowhere — remove it entirely. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` If you invest effort in good mock-ups, let viewers zoom in; not being able to enlarge a detailed image on a medium-res monitor is a UX failure. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` **Branding ≠ visual identity system.** People conflate them; branding includes messaging, tone and voice/personality, not just the identity pieces. Wants to see more than identity when a project is labeled "branding." (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` Endorses inventing a **fictitious company name** early (crediting Eli Altman's Futur podcast episode on naming): it survives the company outgrowing you or being sold, so your personal name isn't forever attached to something you've left. If you brand as a company, keep the language consistent ("about us," "founded by…") to sustain the illusion of being bigger — not deceptive. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` A good portfolio can be sequenced like a "seven-course meal" — as long as the work is compelling, a linear next-next flow that keeps viewers "going down the rabbit hole" is acceptable; there's a benefit to "not letting people think and just letting them see the work." (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` Don't show old-vs-new logo side by side when the difference is subtle — to the untrained eye they look the same and it undercuts the work; just present the new logo with "updated." (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` Craftsmanship of process photos matters: shoot near a window in daylight for contrast and correct color temperature; dark, vignetted night shots read as amateur. Bold, legible sketches (praised in one feed) beat faint ones you can't see. "This is the window into your world." (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` Deleting old off-brand posts "is not gonna affect your numbers" — you can delete and rebuild a feed intentionally, planning posts in sets of at least three (or six/nine) so the grid reads coherently. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` Contrast/value critique as craft standard: logo elements too close in value "all blends together… you want to make sure there's great contrast." (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` Basing a whole portfolio on a single project "is putting a lot of weight on one project"; a Behance-only presence with no website is the weakest position for getting hired. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-03-24]` Specialists vs. generalists framing: he sorts the five into logo-design specialists (Connor, Sean) vs. "gentleman's graphic designers" (Arun, Spencer, Sherif). Clarity/specialization scores higher. (paraphrase)
