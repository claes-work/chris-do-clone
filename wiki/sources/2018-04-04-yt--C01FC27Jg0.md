## Summary

A live sales role-play from The Futur, hosted by **Chris Do**. Chris plays a design-firm
principal fielding a cold inbound from **"Sarah"** (a client character, voiced by
another person — a co-host/audience member), who wants a branding project on a **£2,000**
budget. Rather than accept, discount, or flatly refuse, Chris demonstrates his signature
"price buyer" handling: he stays warm and on the client's side, uses a **take-away** posture
("I'm not sure I'm the guy to do it for you… you can probably get it done for less"),
runs **discovery** to surface the real business problem (low sales, wrong target audience),
quantifies the **cost of inaction** ("millions"), and reframes the budget as
**disproportionate** to the stakes via a Jim Rohn "major effort / minor goal" analogy and
an **"insurance policy" percentage** device. This resets the anchor from £2,000 up to a
**£20,000 starting range**, closes toward due diligence and contracts, and ends with a
debrief on the emotional posture (being on her side, matching energy, teaching + making
her laugh to win). Core persona material for pricing/negotiation.

## Key claims

_All dated 2018-04-04. Paraphrased unless quoted below. Chris speaks as himself (his own
sales philosophy) in the intro and debrief, and voices the **designer** character during
the role-play; **"Sarah"** is the client character voiced by another participant._

- **(Chris, own belief — intro):** Give him a room with a live client — someone with a
  real problem and real money who will pick somebody — and he will close a good percentage
  of those jobs.
- **(Chris as designer — take-away opening):** Instead of refusing or discounting, open by
  affirming you understand what the client wants but expressing doubt you're the right fit
  at that price; point out they could likely get it done for less (even naming a cheaper
  provider). Removing yourself makes the client want you.
- **(Chris as designer — anchor reset):** State your own normal starting range plainly
  ("usually we start somewhere between fifty [thousand] to twenty thousand"), so the
  client's £2,000 is measured against your real floor, not the reverse.
- **(Chris as designer — leave the door open):** Tell the client it sounds like they don't
  need you and can find someone else; but if they don't like what others do, they can call
  back — "the price is still the same," and then you can help. Never chase; never drop the
  price to keep them.
- **(Chris as designer — discovery):** Ask what's motivating the conversation / what
  they're trying to get done. Surface the real problem beneath the branding request: not
  enough sales, not reaching the right (younger, millennial) target audience.
- **(Chris as designer — cost of inaction):** Get the client to quantify what the unsolved
  problem costs — here, "millions," every single day it goes unfixed. Anchor the decision
  to the size of the problem, not the size of the deliverable.
- **(Chris as designer — proportionality via Jim Rohn):** Uses Jim Rohn's "major effort /
  minor goal" idea: you wouldn't spend a million dollars to buy a suit, and you shouldn't
  make a minor effort toward a major goal. A £2,000 spend is disproportionate to a
  multi-million-dollar problem — and get the client to agree it's disproportionate.
- **(Chris as designer — the "insurance policy" reframe):** Don't spend a million to fix a
  million-dollar problem (you'd only break even); instead ask what *percentage* of the loss
  they'd reasonably spend as an "insurance policy" to stop it. The client offers 30%; Chris
  reframes even that down ("30% of a million is $300k… I can help you for a lot less than
  that"), so any credible fee now sits comfortably below the client's own stated ceiling.
- **(Chris as designer — reset + close):** Land the real starting point ("at least we can
  get started in the $20,000 / £20,000 range… seems pretty reasonable"), then close toward
  process: encourage due diligence (talk to multiple firms), and if it's a fit, move to
  contracts and schedules. Frame the work as not just fixing the problem but "plugging the
  hole in the boat" and growing the business.
- **(Chris as designer — graceful exit):** Even at the close, keep the take-away warmth:
  "I wish you well… if you need my help assessing other firms I'd be glad to help you."
- **(Debrief — observer + Chris):** The move worked because Chris stayed *on the client's
  side* and clearly liked her idea, while making it her responsibility to "stand up to her
  side of the bargain"; if she can't, she's free to leave. Chris frames his method
  emotionally: match the client's energy, flirt/tease a little, and "if I can make you
  laugh and teach you something, I'm gonna win."

## Notable verbatim quotes

_Generous quote bank of Chris's negotiation lines. Chris voices the **designer** character
throughout the role-play (still persona/voice data — his own scripting) unless noted.
Where a line is spoken by the client character it is attributed to **"Sarah"**._

**Chris (own belief, intro):**
> "Just give me the room with a live client — meaning they have a real problem they need
> to solve and they have real money and they're gonna pick somebody — and I will close a
> good percentage of those jobs."

**Chris as the designer (take-away opening):**
> "Here's the thing: I think I know what you want… I'm just not sure I'm the guy to do it
> for you. And I think you can get what you want done for that money, for maybe even less."

> "There's this girl I know in Edinburgh that could probably do it for a lot less. However,
> I'm just conflicted, 'cause I don't know how to do that for that budget. Usually we start
> somewhere between fifty to twenty thousand dollars or pounds, so I can't reconcile this
> in my mind."

> "I don't want to say no to you, because I like your vibe, I like your vision, I like your
> company — but I'm not sure this is gonna work. Is there anything else that I'm missing
> here, Sarah? Maybe there's more budget somewhere else if you want to work with me. But
> otherwise I just can't take it on. What do you think?"

**Chris as the designer (leave the door open):**
> "It sounds like you can't do it — you probably don't need me. I'm sure if you call a few
> people you can find somebody else to do it for you. It's just not gonna be me, sadly. If
> you should talk to other people [and] you don't like what they do, feel free to give me a
> ring — the price is still the same, but then I can help you."

**Chris as the designer (discovery):**
> "Just out of curiosity, what's motivating this conversation today? What are you trying to
> get done?"

**Chris as the designer (cost of inaction):**
> "Here's what I heard you say: you're not getting enough sales, you're not connecting with
> the right audience. Those are big problems to solve. Like, if you don't fix this problem,
> what's it gonna cost you?"

> "So it's gonna cost you millions if I don't fix this problem, yet all you want to spend —
> what I heard — was two thousand pounds to fix the problem."

**Chris as the designer (Jim Rohn / proportionality):**
> "I've been reading this book… this author's name is Jim Rohn. He talks about the minors
> and the majors… oftentimes in life we get these things where we make a major effort into
> a minor goal — you want to spend millions of dollars to buy a suit, that doesn't make
> sense. Conversely, you don't want to make a minor effort for a major goal."

> "So do you stand to lose millions of dollars of business — and every single day it's
> gonna cost you money — does 2,000 pounds seem an appropriate amount of money to spend?
> What do you think?"

> "It sounds disproportionate to me too."

**Chris as the designer (the "insurance policy" reframe):**
> "Now, you wouldn't want to spend a million dollars to fix a million-dollar problem,
> because then the best you would get is you would break even. But some percentage that
> seems reasonable — and I'm a business guy, I want to be reasonable too — so if you could
> buy an insurance policy to stop this from happening, how much percentage would you spend?"

> "Thirty percent sounds very generous — nah, I don't think we need to spend that much,
> because 30 percent of a million dollars… my math is really not that good, I think it's
> three hundred thousand. Here's the great news: I can help you for a lot less than that."

**Chris as the designer (reset + close):**
> "I don't know all the parts and pieces that need to be fixed, but I know at least we can
> get started in the twenty thousand dollar, 20,000 pound range — that seems pretty
> reasonable, doesn't it?"

> "Before we leave — and I think you do need to do your due diligence — I think you owe it
> to yourself to talk to multiple design firms to figure out who's a good fit for you. If
> you should decide that we're a good fit, we can get into contracts and then figure out
> schedules and all that kind of stuff."

> "So not only are we gonna fix the problem, we're gonna plug the hole in the boat — we're
> gonna actually grow and expand your business. This is what I help my clients do."

> "I wish you well. Let me know, I'll be here. And if you need my help in assessing other
> firms, I'd be glad to help you."

**Sarah (client character, voiced by another participant):**
> "Hi Chris, how's it going? Um, we have to do some branding… the budget is 2,000 pounds."
> _(attribution: uncertain — client character, not Chris)_

> "We're not getting high enough sales, we're not reaching quite the right target audience;
> we want to sort of fit in with a younger millennial vibe." _(client character, not Chris)_

**Chris (own reflection, debrief on the emotional posture):**
> "I don't care about you — and I match her energy, and I flirt with you a little bit. I
> mean, if I can make you laugh and teach you something, I'm gonna win."

**Observer (debrief, another participant — paraphrased context, not Chris):**
> "It felt like you were on her side and you want to help her, but if she can't stand up to
> her side of the deal, she's free to go somewhere else… you like her idea and you'll do it
> if she can stand up to her side of the bargain." _(attribution: observer, not Chris)_
