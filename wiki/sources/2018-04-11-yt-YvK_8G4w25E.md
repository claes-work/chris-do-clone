# Framing Your Shot – Composition and Storytelling

Futur YouTube tutorial (@thefutur), published 2018-04-11. A short craft segment on how
lens choice, camera angle, and framing change the meaning and emotion of an otherwise
identical scene — pitched at students assembling "style frames" for motion/design work.

## Summary

An unnamed Futur instructor demonstrates composition and visual storytelling by rendering
a single 3D scene of runners at the start of a race, then showing four different
camera/lens treatments of the exact same scene, unchanged. He argues that moving the
camera and swapping the lens — without moving the subjects — drastically changes what the
viewer focuses on and feels. Students Molly and Erica confirm the difference; the
instructor closes by urging home viewers to recompose and reframe any shot that feels
boring or unfocused, thinking about the mood and emotion they want to convey.

The four treatments demonstrated on the same runners scene:
1. **35mm profile, knee-height, slightly angled up** — generic "vanilla" establishing shot to set the scene / lay of the land.
2. **50mm focused on one runner** — makes that runner the hero; others fall smaller and out of focus, becoming secondary characters and controlling the viewer's eye.
3. **18mm ultra-wide, camera on the floor angled up** — shallow depth of field on the runners' feet/legs; makes the shot about the detail and speed of the start rather than any character.
4. **100mm overhead pointing straight down** — a wide overhead about the runners as a whole: who's in the lead and the pace of the pack.
