# Want To Raise Your Value? Solve Bigger Problems.

Speaker: Chris Do (solo talk / stage excerpt, first person throughout). Single speaker — all lines are Chris Do; no guest present.

## Summary

A short excerpt in which Chris Do argues that the label you give yourself constrains the size of problem you're allowed to solve — and therefore how much you can charge. Calling yourself a "graphic designer" boxes you in: a graphic designer solves "graphic problems," and people no longer pay much for graphic problems, leaving you low on the decision-making chain as an order-taker. Chris says he stopped calling himself a graphic designer and reframes himself as a "business designer" (sometimes "experience designer," or just "a designer" who invents solutions), which lets him talk to the C-suite. He ties the self-label directly to the "law of the instrument": if your only tool is a hammer, every problem looks like a nail, so a graphic designer routes every client problem (retention, delivery) back to logos and menus. Redefining what you do and how you label yourself is presented as the path to 10x–100x value.

Distinct from the related "Why Some Designers Are More Valuable" page (2018-01-18): that page frames value around *where you sit in the decision-making chain / who you talk to*; this excerpt zeroes in specifically on the **self-applied label** as the lever — the mechanism by which the label both summarizes you and traps you (the "little box"), and the "law of the instrument" as why a narrow label narrows the problems you can even see.

## Key claims

All claims are Chris Do, dated `[2018-04-18]`.

- [2018-04-18] Labels matter "a lot" because they let people quickly summarize who you are — but they also put you in "a little box." (paraphrase)
- [2018-04-18] A graphic designer solves graphic problems; a fashion designer, fashion problems; an interior designer, interior problems — the label defines the problem class. (paraphrase)
- [2018-04-18] People don't pay a lot of money anymore for "graphic problems," which leaves the graphic designer low on the decision-making chain and reduced to an order-taker. (paraphrase)
- [2018-04-18] As an order-taker you watch clients make changes that cost them money and hurt the product, but you can't influence it because you're not at a place where you speak to the C-suite (CEO, CMO, CRO). (paraphrase)
- [2018-04-18] Chris stopped calling himself a graphic designer; he now thinks of himself as a "business designer," sometimes an "experience designer," or maybe just "a designer" who invents solutions for things. (paraphrase)
- [2018-04-18] Law of the instrument applied to designers: if your only tool is a hammer, every problem looks like a nail — so all client problems (poor customer retention, a food-delivery service) get routed back to the things you know how to solve (a new logo, a new menu). (paraphrase)
- [2018-04-18] Redefining what you do and how you label yourself starts to make you "a different person" — and this is how you reach 10x–100x in value. (paraphrase)
