## Summary

A first-person reflection by Keir McLaren on his own upbringing and worldview: a
childhood with little support and much resistance, a family that "from the outside
looked pretty good" (Ivy League father, country club) but which he rejected from the
inside as "a life about things… that weren't really that important." He describes never
having "an allegiance to the system," disbelieving that government/adults/police tell
the truth (reinforced by work in the inner city), and the conviction that there is "no
safe space" — which forces a person to create a world for themselves. He frames Los
Angeles as a place of people who took enormous risks ("rolled the dice"), and closes
with an anecdote about a homeless, alcoholic man he was driving to a job interview whom
he confronted for "living my life the way my daddy said" — the man later held that job
for five years. The throughline is Keir's theme: going against your upbringing and the
rules to "become who we really need to be."

Mid-monologue, Keir inserts one characterization of **Chris Do** (from their working
relationship): the worst thing you can say to Chris is "this is how the other guy does
it" — Chris is not interested in how the other guy does it, only in the best way to do
it, and is open to a better way when one exists.
