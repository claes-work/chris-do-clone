## Summary

Kier McLaren delivers a solo monologue on self-inquiry: the answers a person needs are
already inside them, and society trains people to look outward instead of inward. He
argues the first step is recognizing you have a problem — doubts and instincts are your
"soul delivering you information," and should not be ignored. He offers a concrete
tool: the inner-child written dialogue — write a question to your younger self with your
dominant hand, then answer with your non-dominant hand without thinking, to surface
buried beliefs (e.g., inherited fears about needing a paycheck, not being good enough).
He illustrates the theme with The Wizard of Oz and Joseph Campbell's hero's journey:
the Lion, Tin Man, and Scarecrow each already possessed what they sought. Kier closes
with a diagnostic: an emotional reaction wildly out of proportion to reality is the
clearest signal that something big is going on inside you. He is explicit that he has no
answer for anyone else — only "good questions" — and that copying others (e.g., a great
cinematographer) just makes you a redundant copy; the world needs you, not another him.
