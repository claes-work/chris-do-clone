## Key claims

### Chris Do (host, subject) — [2018-05-16]

- The challenge constraint: pick a retail product under $10; you may change colors, logo,
  and graphics, but must keep all the original typography (the existing copy) in the
  redesign. [2018-05-16]
- Designers get one week to complete the design work once a selection is locked; the
  selection cannot be changed after it is made. [2018-05-16]
- Reads Connor's strategy approvingly: picking a product with few elements is a smart
  move, and Connor carries "big expectations" from winning the first challenge.
  [2018-05-16]

  flagging typographic density as a difficulty factor in the choice. [2018-05-16]
- Announces that in the next episode he will critique all designs unsparingly and invites
  the audience to "play along" and judge whether they agree or disagree. [2018-05-16]

### Young-Guns contestants (CONTEXT — not persona) — [2018-05-16]

- Arun picked a Khadi/Cadi natural rose-water soap, positioned as a premium Indian
  product (~70 rupees, above an average soap). [2018-05-16]
- Sean picked Sensodyne toothpaste — small, box-shaped, and (in his view) improvable
  design. [2018-05-16]
- Spencer picked Gold Bond lotion, arguing the green/red palette and busy hierarchy
  don't represent the product well. [2018-05-16]
- Connor picked a cough sweet (Minotaur/bull mascot), planning a bold logo and a redrawn
  mascot illustration. [2018-05-16]
- Sherif picked Clotrimazole (broad-spectrum antifungal), citing a busy layout, too many
  colors, background text, and poorly set type with no weight/size differentiation.
  [2018-05-16]
- Recurring designer process pattern (contestant methodology, context): start with paper
  sketching to get ideas/bad ideas out before committing time in Illustrator; for
  type-led or word-mark-led designs, some skip the sketchbook and lay out typefaces
  directly in Illustrator; build a foldable die-line by laying out the six faces of the
  box; construct physical mockups (trace/measure a new bottle shape, print, cut, paste,
  photograph). One designer argues a serif on a black background reads "classier" /
  "more premium" and helps justify a higher price range. [2018-05-16]
