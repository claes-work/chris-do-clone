## Summary

Chris Do interviews Karin Fong about a ~30-year career in main-title and motion design.
The two open by establishing that they met ~20 years ago at **R/GA (Robert Greenberg
Associates) LA** in the old Cat and Fiddle building, where a young, freshly-graduated
Chris Do — just having started his own design studio — was brought in to work with
**Kyle Cooper** on projects including storyboards for *The Island of Dr. Moreau*; Chris
recalls this as his first exposure to title design ("I didn't even know that graphic
designers did these kind of things"). Karin narrates her own path: childhood super-8
cut-paper films, a Yale thesis "animated alphabet book" that professor **Michael Rock**
called "cinematic," a tip to see Kyle Cooper, a formative stint at **WGBH Boston**
(animating *Where in the World Is Carmen Sandiego?* under design VP Chris Pullman), then
years under Kyle Cooper at R/GA and Imaginary Forces. A recurring theme both speakers
return to: **constraints breed creativity** — old tools (Photoshop with one layer / one
undo, Illustrator outline-only mode, airbrushing, budget/deadline limits) forced
fundamentals and ideas rather than tool-chasing. Chris turns this into a direct PSA to
young viewers who want $5,000 computers and the newest software.

Karin walks through her **process** (immerse fully in the subject matter, then distill it
to one central metaphor and grow the sequence out from it) using Boardwalk Empire (Nucky
Thompson as the eye of the storm; liquor bottles washing ashore = Prohibition as his
destiny), God of War: Ascension (a live-action origin story; ashes of his wife and
daughter coat him to make him the god of war), and Counterpart (a split Go game for
parallel worlds). She gives her **test for a good main title**: does it truly describe
*this* show, and is it *unique* to it — "if this main title can run in front of a
different show, it's probably not specific enough" (contrast Boardwalk Empire vs.
Scarface). Her three traits: appropriateness to the material, a clever metaphor/aha
"surprise" moment, and craftsmanship down to the typography. She praises restraint and
"editing as the great unsung art" (Game of Thrones' board-game map, Mad Men's falling
man). A pun-riddle game segment and cheerleader banter fill the mid-show. She discusses
directing (learning on set by being on many sets first), her hero **Joan Ganz Cooney**
(Sesame Street — using the mechanics of advertising jingles to teach), and work-life
balance (partnership, full-time childcare, constraints of a rigorous schedule). She closes
with portfolio advice: **make things you like** (not what you think others want), show only
your ~5 best (editing shows you know what's good), and remember the work you do early
"sets the tone" for the work you get later. Chris recaps the title-design takeaways at the
end.

- (2018-05-19, Chris Do) Biographical: he and Karin met ~20 years ago at R/GA LA (the old

  $5,000 computer / the newest software version.
- (2018-05-19, Chris Do) Work on the fundamentals, don't chase tools — chasing the newest
  tools "is a never-ending chase"; if you work on fundamentals "you can do incredible
  legendary things." (He frames learning to light/paint a frame cinematically as
  fundamentals, recalling early flat work done in QuarkXPress / Illustrator.)
- (2018-05-19, Chris Do) Shared craft nostalgia establishing his era: he worked in
  Photoshop with one layer and one level of undo, and in Adobe Illustrator that could only
  work in outline mode (Command-Y to render/preview) — "that never stopped you from coming
  up with great ideas."
- (2018-05-19, Chris Do) On his own on-ramp to the industry: he "didn't have any real
  knowledge about cinematography, editing, what it means to be a director on set — so it
  was a pretty steep learning curve."
- (2018-05-19, Chris Do) End-of-show synthesis (his framing of the title-design lesson):
  a good main title (1) describes the show / is true to the central theme or emotion and is
  **unique** to the show — "this is the biggest takeaway for me" — looking at character,
  setting, storyline; (2) has a clever metaphor that leads to a surprise; and (3) is
  well-crafted.
- (2018-05-19, Chris Do) Interviewer's craft note: he plays back Boardwalk Empire's meaning
  himself — the title is unique because of Nucky Thompson and the seaside Prohibition
  setting, the bottles at his feet posing a lingering question the series eventually
  answers.
- (2018-05-19, Chris Do) He live-streams interviews specifically to answer audience
  questions in real time ("that's why I do live"), and closes by asking whether the format
  made the guest comfortable — his stated goal is to make more high-caliber people willing
  to come on the show.

### Karin Fong (CONTEXT — not persona data)

- (2018-05-19, Karin Fong) Ideas come from immersing yourself in the subject matter (script,
  story, period, characters, brand history/archives), then distilling it into one central
  metaphor and growing the sequence out from that single "nugget."
- (2018-05-19, Karin Fong) Test for a strong main title: does it truly describe *this* show
  and is it *unique* to it — "if this main title can run in front of a different show, it's
  probably not specific enough" (Boardwalk Empire couldn't front Scarface).
- (2018-05-19, Karin Fong) Three traits of a good title: appropriateness to the material,
  a clever metaphor / aha "surprise" moment, and craftsmanship down to the typography.
  Favorites cited: Dexter, Catch Me If You Can, Mad Men, Game of Thrones.
- (2018-05-19, Karin Fong) "Editing is the great unsung art" — whether cutting film or
  cutting an idea down; discipline and courage to keep to one central theme (Game of Thrones'
  board-game map; Mad Men's falling man) rather than including everything.
- (2018-05-19, Karin Fong) A TV title is designed for an arc across seasons (played in front
  of ever-changing scenes); a movie title sits at one very specific moment — a different
  storytelling challenge.
- (2018-05-19, Karin Fong) You learn directing by being on many sets before you're
  responsible for one; live shoots are "a living thing" full of variables and "happy
  accidents" — the opposite of a designer's total hand-control (kerning, keyframes).
- (2018-05-19, Karin Fong) Constraints help creativity (invent your own deadlines; learn the
  tool by doing under a deadline). Cites an urban-myth-caveated belief that athletes get
  better grades in-season because of the rigorous schedule.
- (2018-05-19, Karin Fong) Portfolio advice: make things *you* like, not what you think
  others want; show only your ~5 best (editing proves you know what's good); and the work
  you show early "sets the tone" for the work you'll be hired to do later — show the kind of
  work you actually want more of.
- (2018-05-19, Karin Fong) Hero Joan Ganz Cooney (Sesame Street) used the mechanics of
  advertising — repetition, jingles, short commercial-style segments ("brought to you by
  the letter A") — to teach children; a true pioneer who "didn't know what she was getting
  into," a career flexibility she urges on students.
