## Summary

Fifth episode of a running coaching series in which Chris Do mentors Rebecca Hyneman, a solo designer whose studio scaled fast in three months (now a designer, an assistant, and three clients, projecting to roughly double last year's revenue). The session diagnoses the classic freelancer-to-owner inflection: she is "working in the business" (heads-down on the craft) instead of "on the business" (biz dev, marketing), so the pipeline that got her here has gone quiet.

Chris's central prescription is a **mindset shift from maker to owner**: for every job that comes in, hire someone to do the hands-on work for less than you were paid, keep the margin, and spend the freed time on art direction and business development. He works through the economics of delegation (charge at least 2× a contractor's rate, plus ~30% padding, and mark up your own creative-director/art-director time), the ego block that keeps creatives clutching the mouse, and a concrete biz-dev playbook: reverse-engineer how every lead found you, fix LinkedIn (value-proposition headline, respond to profile-viewers and connections, post far more than once a month), execute SEO/website fundamentals before chasing PR, and reach authority blogs/influencers online rather than dying print magazines. He repeatedly pushes her off "magic bullet" tactics and toward "block-and-tackle" fundamentals, and frames marketing as a resellable skill she can later offer clients.

### Chris Do — pricing the delegation margin

- `[2018-05-22]` (paraphrase) Rule of thumb: charge at least 2× what you pay a contractor, because creatives underestimate their time (a "10-hour" job becomes 35) and clients change their minds; if your margin is too thin you end up effectively paying the client to do the work. [attribution: Chris]
- `[2018-05-22]` (paraphrase) Build the padding into the estimate rather than nickel-and-diming clients with overage charges — constant little charges make clients feel the bill jumps every time they "look at you wrong," and you lose them even if you get the money. [attribution: Chris]
- `[2018-05-22]` (paraphrase) Add roughly 30% time padding on top of the doubled rate, and mark up the whole estimate to include your own art-director/creative-director time (references a separate "run your company like a boss" video with Frankie). [attribution: Chris]
- `[2018-05-22]` (paraphrase) A wide margin is what makes delegation low-risk: on a $30k logo that costs ~$1,500 to produce, you only commit a percentage of the fee until the freelancer hits benchmarks, so you can "roll the dice" many times, fail, and still come out ahead.
