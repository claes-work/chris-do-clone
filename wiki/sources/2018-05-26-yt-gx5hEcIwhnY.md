## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona) — the four-criterion critique rubric

- `[2018-05-26]` Chris judges packaging redesigns on **four criteria, in no particular
  order**: (1) **brand integrity & product positioning** — being true to the brand and
  not positioning the product too far up- or down-market; (2) **shelf presence** — can it
  be recognized "among the sea of competitors from 6 feet away," will it "jump off the
  shelf"; (3) **improvement** — did you improve the original design in significant ways;
  (4) **clarity & communication** — did you communicate the unique selling properties
  clearly. (paraphrase; near-verbatim on the four items)

### Chris Do (persona) — packaging-design principles

- `[2018-05-26]` **Hierarchy first**: decide what the package must communicate and in what
  order. On the antifungal spray, shrinking the active ingredient (clotrimazole) created
  "hierarchy issues" because on the original it was as prominent as the "broad-spectrum
  antifungal" line. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Communicate the form/format**: the package must signal that the product
  is a spray (not a cream or pill) — e.g., silhouette of the bottle, or imply misting with
  a pattern of circles that fade downward. "Communicate that it's a spray and communicate
  the active ingredient... everything else is subservient to that." (near-verbatim)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Protect brand equity / legacy**: Connor moved the Minotaur mark from
  front to back — "you're losing a part of the brand legacy and the brand equity," and
  reducing the Minotaur to a plain bull made the lozenge read like a beef-stock/beef-jerky
  product (Jack Link's comparison). "The biggest crime here is the fact that you missed
  this part about the two parts of the lozenge which makes it unique." (near-verbatim)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Outline can beat silhouette**: an outline mark "implied... more
  dimension," whereas a silhouette "flattens everything out." (near-verbatim)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Don't omit essential regulatory/info elements**: on the Sensodyne
  pack, omitting/burying the net weight (3.4 oz) was "a big miss" — "that is essential
  piece of information... make made much more prominent." (near-verbatim)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Typographic craft**: tighten overly wide tracking on the wordmark
  ("Sensodyne") for a more compact, legible logo/wordmark; keep type "forced or justified
  left rag right." (paraphrase / near-verbatim)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Design the package as a 3-D six-sided sculpture**, not a flat layout —
  "think of it as a three-dimensional sculpture... consisting of six sides." Wrapping a
  graphic element from the back across to the front "is what makes a packaging designer a
  packaging designer, not an editorial designer." (near-verbatim)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Push the edges / create visual tension**: run type or info onto the
  edge of the box; reverse elements out; use grid lines to place elements consistently
  across faces. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Thicker borders read as shapes, not rules**: making borders much
  thicker turns a line ("a rule") into a grouping "shape" that draws the eye. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Rules (lines) group information**: lines "are really great to group
  different pieces of information together that don't look good together," giving the eye
  "the beginning [and] the end of the line" to lock onto; a dashed element outside a block
  also groups it. (near-verbatim)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Premium = less is more**: on the rosewater soap, covering up a
  beautiful three-ingredient product (rose water, glycerin, vegetable oils) hurt it —
  "usually when people cover things up they have something to hide." Cited Aesop / Clinique
  as references for minimal, restrained high-end beauty packaging; patterns read as
  "decorative... fancy and frou-frou," against the product's simplicity. (near-verbatim /
  paraphrase)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Design the product, not the parent company**: don't redesign the parent
  brand's logo (Khadi / like Johnson & Johnson) at the expense of the product — the house
  brand "should take second seat to the product itself." Celebrate that it's "rosewater
  soap or sandalwood soap." (near-verbatim / paraphrase)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Centered typography reads old-fashioned**: a very centered layout "makes
  it feel very old-fashioned"; add a little contemporary design, but "you don't have to
  overdo it." (near-verbatim)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Respect the design brief / don't change client tooling**: contestants
  were not supposed to change the original package shape. Changing the bottle shape
  (Spencer) "would cost them a lot of money to do retooling" — "this is where I think the
  young designer meets the old designer." You can repaint, add/subtract labels and
  elements, but not change the vessel. (near-verbatim / paraphrase)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Resolve one idea, don't hedge**: Spencer used two (really three) logos
  "because you couldn't decide" — "not a great rationale." "I would prefer in the future
  challenges if you were to spend more time resolving one of these ideas versus jumping
  from idea to idea." Get objective feedback: "which best communicates this brand." (near-
  verbatim)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Tie elements together into a cohesive unit**: extend a shape, ligature,
  or curve through the design so it "feels like it's one design and not three parts" —
  avoid isolated "islands" of design. Summary to Spencer: "simplify, refine and pick your
  best mark... so it feels like a cohesive unit and not three separate components." (near-
  verbatim)
- `[2018-05-26]` **Design solves a business problem, not itself**: closing lesson — "you
  as the designer are trying to solve a communication and a business and a positioning
  problem with the product itself. This is not design for design's sake." Understand the
  2–3 key things that make the product unique and celebrate them; if a decision doesn't
  help communicate those, "don't do it." (near-verbatim)

### Contestants (context)

- `[2018-05-26]` (Sharif, antifungal) — kept the original color palette so existing users
  would still recognize the product; redesigned the Arabic side for legibility, noting
  that in Egypt some customers ask the pharmacist for "the thing with a yellow top."
  (paraphrase; attribution: contestant)
- `[2018-05-26]` (Connor, Colonia lozenges) — wanted the design to "feel very
  pharmaceutical aesthetically" since the product is engineered for a specific dental
  health issue and "not purely a lifestyle brand"; used a grid, white space, and a
  consistent brand mark. (paraphrase; attribution: contestant)
- `[2018-05-26]` (soap contestant) — aimed to make the soap "look more premium /
  expensive" to match a price point ~30–40 rupees above an average soap in India, via a
  black band and subtle pattern. (paraphrase; attribution: contestant)
- `[2018-05-26]` (Spencer, GB body lotion) — used two logos because he "couldn't decide,"
  and changed the bottle to a non-rectangular shape, arguing he picked the worst original
  item and achieved the biggest before/after transformation. (paraphrase; attribution:
  contestant)
