## Summary

A Futur tutorial hosted by co-founder/host Ben Burns on how designers should choose a
domain name for their portfolio or brand, sponsored by domain registrar Porkbun. Burns
lays out naming "rules" (short, easy to spell/say/remember, make it yours), four idea
strategies (brand/business name, personal name, localized keywords, new domain
extensions), a fallback playbook for when the `.com` is taken, a list of things to
avoid (dashes, misspellings, acronyms, homonyms, accidental spellings), and two cheap
tests before registering. He motivates the video by noting that Futur portfolio
submissions increasingly use long, hard-to-say, hard-to-spell domains. Ends with a
sponsor walkthrough of registering `thefutur.design` on Porkbun and a viewer contest
to invent a personal-site domain for Chris Do (who "right now doesn't have one").

## Key claims

**All claims below are by Ben Burns (host / CONTEXT — NOT the subject).**

- [2018-06-26] Choosing a domain name is a relatively subjective decision, but following
  basic guidelines helps users find you; ease of use = good UX and more conversions.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] Naming rules: make it short, easy to spell, easy to say/pronounce, easy
  to remember, and "make it yours" — something you believe in and stand behind, whether
  a personal name or a brand name. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] Motivation for the video: across the many portfolio submissions the Futur
  receives, domain names are trending longer and harder to say, spell, and remember —
  making them hard to pull up during portfolio reviews. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] Four strategies to generate domain ideas: (1) use a brand/business name,
  (2) use your personal name, (3) use localized keywords, (4) try a new domain
  extension. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] Brand/business name — pros: more professional, projects a larger
  presence, ideal if you already have a brand; cons: doesn't help organic search (no
  keyword) and can feel odd to a hiring manager expecting a personal site. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] Personal name — you should own your own name's domain anyway; unique names
  are often available (good for job hunting/freelance); cons: common names are usually
  taken (esp. `.com`), some names are hard to spell, and a personal name may signal a
  smaller presence for an agency. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] Localized keywords (e.g. "Los Angeles logo design") help organic/local
  search, addressing the common complaint of not finding local clients; cons: can read
  as "car-salesman-ish" and Burns personally expects a lower-quality site from names
  like "best logo design Los Angeles." (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] New domain extensions (`.design`, `.agency`, `.art`) are his favorite:
  combine a brand/name with a keyword ("best of both worlds"), are widely available
  because new, and give users context without explaining ("a name that identifies, not
  explains"). Cons: people expect `.com`, may need explanation, can lengthen the name —
  but advantages outweigh disadvantages. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] His own approach: "use all of the above" — register a new extension like
  `.design` as primary, also buy `.com/.net/.nyc/.agency`, and redirect them all to the
  `.design` name for brand security and to catch mistyped extensions. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] If your `.com` is taken, step through: (1) is the brand name already used
  by another player in your industry? (if established, pick a new brand); (2) is the
  site actually in use, or just parked/for auction (you may get a deal); (3) is a new
  extension available; (4) add an actionable verb in front of the brand name (e.g.
  "try" as in "trythefutur") to pull users in faster. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] Burns calls `.biz` and `.net` "still lame" and questions using `.org`
  unless you truly are one. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] Things to avoid: dashes/hyphens/numbers/special characters; purposely
  misspelled words; acronyms (hard to remember); lengthy domains; homonyms (pear vs.
  pair); accidental spellings from joined words (e.g. "IT Scrap" → "itscrap").
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] Two tests before registering: (1) write the email address at your new
  domain four times to check if it's too long/hard to spell; (2) the "tell" test — say
  the domain to ten people and watch them type it to check if it's easy to type,
  remember, and spell. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-26] Sponsor note: Porkbun is a domain registrar out of Portland, Oregon;
  offered `$5` first-year `.design` domains; brands like Facebook, Adobe, Amazon, and
  Airbnb use `.design`. (paraphrase — sponsored content)
- [2018-06-26] At time of recording, Chris Do did not have a personal website; the
  episode ran a viewer contest to invent a personal-site domain name reflecting his
  personality. (paraphrase — biographical context, host-stated)
