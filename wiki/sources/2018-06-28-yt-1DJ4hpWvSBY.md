# How To Convince Your Clients— Don't!

> Captions present (manual .en transcript, 94 lines). NOT NO-CAPTIONS.

## Summary

Chris Do answers an audience question: how do you convince a client who knows nothing
about graphic design that your creative direction is the right one? His answer is a
reframe — you don't convince, and needing to convince is itself the symptom of a
failure upstream. If you have to convince a client, it means you never had a "meeting
of the minds" and are pushing an agenda different from theirs. Chris unpacks the word
"convince" (con → conned, tricked, deceived) and argues persuasion attempts fail
anyway — the bigger and more powerful the brand/client, the smaller your odds of
convincing them of anything. The alternative is discovery: spend time in research,
brainstorming, word lists and demographics; understand what the client wants AND what
their consumers want, and make sure the two align. The designer's role becomes
advocate for the consumer against the client's short-term self-interest, helping the
client "realize the truth" rather than being sold on it. He closes with vivid
analogies about the arrogance of designers who impose sweeping changes and then leave
the client to absorb the real-world costs (a $3M reprint from a name change; a
houseguest who rearranges your whole home; a menu redesign that turns three fewer
tables per restaurant service). Bottom line: empathize with the customer and do
what's right for the business, and you'll do well.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona)

- [2018-06-28] If you have to convince a client, it's because you didn't start with a
  "meeting of the minds" — you're carrying an agenda or objective wholly different from
  what the client wants. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-28] The word "convince" contains "con"; convincing is adjacent to being
  conned, tricked, duped, deceived — giving your confidence to someone who then screws
  you over. (paraphrase / wordplay)
- [2018-06-28] Instead of convincing, do the work upstream: research, brainstorming,
  word lists, demographics; spend time understanding what the client wants and, more
  importantly, what their consumers want — and make sure the two are the same.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-28] In this way the designer becomes an advocate for the consumer against
  the client's short-term self-interest. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-28] You can't convince clients of anything — and the more powerful they are
  (more money, bigger brands), the smaller the likelihood you convince them of
  anything. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-28] You help the client realize the truth themselves rather than persuading
  them; e.g., show them who their real customer is, how that customer behaves and
  talks, then ask whether the proposed solution actually fits. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-28] A core problem is designer arrogance — coming in to change everything,
  then leaving for the next project while the client lives with everything the one
  change impacts. (paraphrase)
- [2018-06-28] Changes have real downstream costs the designer doesn't bear: a company
  name change can invalidate $3M of printing and require buying a new URL; a restaurant
  menu redesign can turn three fewer tables in a single service. (paraphrase; figures
  as stated by Chris, illustrative)
- [2018-06-28] Do what's right for the business and right for the customer; put on the
  lens of empathizing with and understanding the customer and you'll do well for
  yourself. (paraphrase)
