## Summary

Chris Do runs a live, whiteboard-driven teaching session with design students about how to choose and evaluate an internship. He frames the internship as a **hybrid between school and work** on a sliding scale from "learning (while paying money)" on one end to "money" on the other, and argues the total value (money + knowledge) can exceed a plain job — but only if you pick the right place. He advises interning between junior and senior year (summer), because by then you know your tools and can be productive, and because graduating vaporizes your school-based resources, people, and connections.

The core method: **don't judge internships on gut feeling — write down what you want to gain, prioritize a top three, and use that list as a filter** to decide where to work. He crowd-sources a list from the students (experience, production, software/tools, efficiency, process, time management, leadership, communication, real-world experience, networking, portfolio), has each student pick their own top three, then walks two students (Giovanni, Brian) through scoring their most recent internships against their own criteria — most of which turned out to be unpaid AND low-learning, which he flags as the worst possible combination. He closes by noting you can obtain many of these same benefits (leadership, real-world experience, portfolio) yourself through books, seminars, workshops, and DIY projects if you have initiative — and that prioritizing a list is simply what organizations do, and "you're just an organization of one right now."

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2018-07-01] An internship should be treated just like work — a full 40 hours/week; doing it part-time alongside school is very difficult, and demanding is what you want. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-01] The internship is a hybrid of school and work: you give effort, they give you money AND knowledge, but they give you less money than a plain job — yet the sum (money + knowledge) can be greater, so choose carefully. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-01] The optimal time to start an internship is junior year / the summer between junior and senior year — as a sophomore you're too young and don't know your tools yet; senior year is too late because after graduation your school resources, people, and connections vaporize. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-01] After a summer internship you return to senior year with new perspective, tools, process, and mindset, becoming a "hyper learner" for that final year. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-01] Quantity of internships doesn't matter — quality does; there are high- and low-quality internships. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-01] Working unpaid AND not learning anything is a serious mistake — if the internship is just busy-work (dry cleaning, dropping things off) and pays nothing, you're better off working at Starbucks for $12–13/hour, which is a lot more than zero. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-01] Interning costs you money beyond time — gas, buying lunch — so an unpaid, low-learning internship is a net loss. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-01] The essentials of survival are to breathe, eat, and sleep — everything else you "need" from an internship is a want you should make explicit. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-01] Creative people go with gut/feeling/intuition; writing your criteria down in black and white makes the decision much clearer. (paraphrase)

- [2018-07-01] You can get the same benefits (leadership, real-world experience, portfolio, etc.) outside an internship — online, books, seminars, workshops, or DIY versions — the internship exists mainly because you want someone else to provide it for you; with initiative you can do it yourself.
