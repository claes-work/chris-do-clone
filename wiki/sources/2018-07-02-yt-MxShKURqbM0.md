## Summary

Short Q&A / talk clip on The Futur (@thefutur). **Chris Do** answers a student's question — *"When you started you didn't have any clientele; how did you build it?"* — by walking through his own origin story and then generalizing it into advice on networking and finding mentors.

He traces his early client base to relationships, not cold outreach: a short stint at Epitaph Records, then freelancing at hot Hollywood shops (he names **Novacom**), getting leads because his work was featured on an **Adobe After Effects CD**, and — most durably — the **network he built at Art Center**. Former classmates and peers who went into the industry hired him later because he had helped them when they were students (reciprocity). He notes his first car commercial (for **Buick**) came from helping out a friend from school.

He reframes the scary word "networking" as simply **being genuine, helping each other out, in the spirit of reciprocity**, and argues that a big reason people chase elite schools (Harvard) is the peers — pointing to Zuckerberg and Gates teaming up with classmates. He tells the (senior-year) student that the school is now their "family" — chosen or not — but they can still choose new friends by going out into the world.

The clip closes on **mentorship**: you can't just get a dream mentor (his example: designer **Aaron Draplin**) to take you under their wing. Citing **Brian Tracy**, Chris says you must *level up your game* — study, watch videos, read books, become interested in interesting things — so you close the gap and can hold a real conversation with the mentor, giving you common ground and making you "attractive" to them.

## Notable verbatim quotes

### Chris Do (persona voice — training data)

Reframing networking:
> "Networking is a scary complicated word. I mean being genuine, helping each other out, and in the spirit of reciprocity."

On reciprocity paying off:
> "I helped them do something, and if you do a good job, when they're successful or they have opportunity, they call you up."

On why the peers matter more than the program:
> "The reason why so many people are attracted to going to Harvard is because they have a great program, great instructors, but mostly it's cuz of the peers. Who are you going to meet?"

> "Look around in the room. That's who your classmates are. That's your network."

On choosing new friends:
> "You can choose your friends but you can't choose your family... But you can choose to have new friends. So go out into the world to meet new people and to be active."

On mentorship (the rhetorical challenge):
> "How are you going to meet Aaron Draplin? Why would he ever take you under his wing and teach you everything he knows? It's a rhetorical question."

The Brian Tracy "level up" framework:
> "In order for you to be attracted to those people, you must level up your game. So in the meantime what you do is you study as much as possible, you watch videos, you read books, and then you start to become interested in things that are interesting to other people."

> "What you want to do is close the gap between where you are and where your mentor is, and the way you do that is through books, videos and classes and anything else in between. So that you become attracted to them."
