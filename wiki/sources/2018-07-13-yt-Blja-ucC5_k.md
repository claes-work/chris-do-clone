# The Worst Things That Clients Have Said To Me

★ **L3-candidate** — packages a reusable objection-handling framework (the "Objection Response Deck": a list of the most common client objections + a set of response-move cards — *acknowledge*, *answer with a question*, *degree*, *reflect*, "I don't know" — plus the "control energy / bring it to neutral" method). Worth promoting into a `wiki/topics/sales-clients` page and into `persona/beliefs.md`.

## Summary

Chris Do films a workshop-and-travel vlog during a Europe tour (Madrid → Barcelona, Spain). The core teaching segment introduces a brand-new prototype tool he calls the **Objection Response Deck**: a set of cards you "play" against client objections. He crowdsources the worst client objections from the workshop audience, then presents his own list of the most common objections and explains a card-based response method. He grounds it in Blair Enns' book *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto* — the idea that creatives should know objections before the client voices them and address them upfront, so as not to come across defensive. His central technique is **controlling energy**: when a client is negative/aggressive, bring the exchange to neutral; when overly positive, bring it back to neutral too. He frames this as being unbiased, wishing the client well, and "giving up influence to gain power." Interspersed are travel-vlog segments (airport, an illustrator friend in Madrid, food, the train to Barcelona) that are not persona-training material.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (subject)

- [2018-07-13] It took him roughly 22 years of practice, 10 years of coaching, and a year of therapy work — plus lots of reading and watching videos — to understand how these interpersonal/sales dynamics work. (paraphrase; note: a self-reported timeline he repeats twice as a bookend)
- [2018-07-13] Per Blair Enns' *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto*, it is the creative person's job to know the objections before they come out of the client's mouth, and to address them upfront — otherwise you come across as defensive. (paraphrase, attributing the idea to Blair Enns)
- [2018-07-13] If someone says "you're too expensive" and you're caught flat-footed and start explaining, confidence in the conversation erodes from the client's point of view. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-13] The most common client objections he has codified: "your work is mediocre," "it's overpriced," "I'm not feeling it," "why should I hire you," "what guarantees can you make it will work," "why should I trust you," "it's more than what I need / I need less / something simple," "your team is too small / too big," "you don't have enough experience / too much experience," "you don't have enough expertise." (paraphrase; enumerated list)
- [2018-07-13] The method: for every objection thrown at you, pick and "play" a response card. Cards include *acknowledge*, *answer with a question*, *degree*, *reflect*, and "I don't know." Cards can be combined (e.g. acknowledge + degree). (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-13] To "acknowledge" is to name the other person's state — e.g. "you sound angry," "you sound frustrated," "is everything okay?" — which stops most people dead in their tracks. (paraphrase, with embedded quotes)
- [2018-07-13] The underlying move is controlling energy: when the energy is negative, bring it to neutral; when it's overly positive, bring it back to neutral. He calls this "the art of Zen" — neutral, neither good nor bad, it just is. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-13] He aims to demonstrate compassion and empathy, be neutral/unbiased, wish the client well, and "give up influence to gain power." He frames it as having no goal or agenda, only being there to help. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-13] The tool works, but "you have to believe it's going to work." (paraphrase, leading into the Henry Ford "whether you think you can or you can't, you're right" framing)
- [2018-07-13] The Objection Response Deck was a total prototype, tried out live for the first time on the Madrid workshop audience; he planned to refine it for Barcelona and eventually release it publicly. (paraphrase; product-development note)
