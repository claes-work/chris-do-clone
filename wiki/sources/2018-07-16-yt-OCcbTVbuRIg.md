# How To Scale Your Business, Multiply Your Time, and Delegate

## Summary

A coaching-style conversation in which Chris Do walks a designer/freelancer (the
coachee, treated here as CONTEXT) through how to break past a solo income ceiling by
formalizing their process, hiring, and delegating. Chris frames the core trap of skilled
entrepreneurs — "I can do it faster myself" — as a curse, then lays out a sequence:
(1) codify your intuitive process into no more than five major steps so others can
replicate your judgment; (2) hire even before you feel ready, because at the coachee's
target rates almost any competent hire is profitable; (3) accept that hires won't be as
fast or as good, and that clients cannot tell the difference between "high" and
"super-high" quality anyway; (4) let go of pixel-level perfectionism so you can focus on
higher-leverage work — onboarding clients, networking, speaking, and content. Chris
illustrates process-codification with a story from his teaching days at Art Center, where
a faculty advisor pushed him to name the five things his brain looks for when critiquing
storyboards, turning tacit expertise into a shared framework students could self-apply.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona)

- [2018-07-16] The curse of authors and entrepreneurs is that you can do the work faster
  than everyone else, and the rationale "in the time it takes to explain it and revise
  it, I'd have finished it myself" is a trap. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] Before you hire anyone, you must formalize your process — codify the
  decisions your "super-efficient brain" makes into no more than five major steps.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] The purpose of documenting your process is to get others to think the way
  you think and see the work the way you see it — a shared conceptual framework replaces
  mysterious, one-off critique. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] Once the framework is on the wall, the maker can critique their own work
  against it (e.g., "is this frame clear?") instead of depending on you — Chris used
  red/blue dot voting so students self-assessed clarity vs. boring-but-clear frames.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] You have to "download" your process into someone — on-site or off-site —
  so you stop doing all the heavy lifting yourself. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] At a high target rate (the coachee's goal of ~$500/hour so they can work
  half the time), almost anyone you hire makes you money, because most freelancers don't
  come anywhere near that rate. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] Even hiring at the top of the market (~$100/hour, ~$800/day), and even if
  the hire takes 1.5x to 2x as long as you, you're still far ahead — especially if you
  come in at the end with about an hour of high-level art direction. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] Delegation is an upfront investment of time that pays off on the back end —
  like tilling soil and planting a seed that becomes a fruit-bearing tree. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] You'll churn through several people before you find a "superstar" right-hand
  person; that person then trains the next hires and that is how you build the team.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] Hires don't need to work as fast or as efficiently as you. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] Clients cannot actually discern the difference between "high" and
  "super-high" quality; as long as work meets the minimum quality threshold, it's good —
  killing yourself or your team to reach super-high is wasted effort. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] You must learn to let go of pixel-level perfectionism (exact shade of red,
  exact pixel placement) and redirect focus to higher-leverage work: onboarding new
  clients, networking, speaking to gain influence, and writing content for YouTube.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-16] Chris says he worked through this same problem with the help of his own
  business coach, and has since helped others through it. [self-reported] (paraphrase)
