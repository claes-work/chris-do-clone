# Thoughts On Living A Creative Life Ep. 11 w/ Keir McLaren

## Summary

A short (~2 min) solo reflection by Kier McLaren on the psychology of fear, risk, and
self-worth for people in creative careers. His thesis: creatives tend to be poor risk
takers because artistic quality is subjective (no clear right/wrong like accounting),
which breeds insecurity. He argues that career growth requires the willingness to speak
your truth to superiors and to be fired — playing it safe keeps you "in a little safe
box" but frustrated. He reframes fear by naming the worst-case scenario (usually far
less catastrophic than imagined; the real fear is looking foolish or letting down
people whose support you depend on). He closes on identity: tying your self-worth to
what you do ("I'm an animator," "I'm a business consultant") is a "terrible mistake,"
because status, talent, and the world all eventually change — a stable sense of being
okay has to come from within, not from external prestige.

## Key claims

All claims are **Kier McLaren (CONTEXT)** — not Chris Do.

- [2018-07-17] Everyone has insecurities and feels not-good-enough in some arenas; this
  is unsurprising for creatives given ~80% of the world dismisses an art career. (Kier — CONTEXT)
- [2018-07-17] Artistic quality is subjective — unlike accounting, the standards for
  "good" vs "bad" graphic design are unclear across the gradients in between; this
  subjectivity is a reason creatives tend to be poor risk takers. (Kier — CONTEXT)
- [2018-07-17] The great artists are the big risk takers — they have the confidence to try. (Kier — CONTEXT)
- [2018-07-17] "If you're not willing to get fired, then you're probably not going to
  get what you want." Career advancement requires politely speaking your truth to people
  above you, accepting you may get fired. (Kier — CONTEXT)
- [2018-07-17] Reframe fear by writing down the actual worst-case scenario — fear thrives
  on the unknown, and the imagined failure is usually far overblown. (Kier — CONTEXT)
- [2018-07-17] The biggest fear is not material ruin but letting down / looking foolish
  or unsuccessful to the people whose support you count on. (Kier — CONTEXT)
- [2018-07-17] Identifying yourself with what you do is "a terrible mistake" — you are a
  person, not your job. When the work goes away (retired athletes, executives who lose a
  job at 55) people who identified with it fall apart. (Kier — CONTEXT)
- [2018-07-17] Prestige, status, and job titles come from the outside; talents lessen and
  the world changes, so a durable sense of being okay must come from within. (Kier — CONTEXT)
