## Summary

Chris Do argues that designers focus on the wrong thing: they compete on deliverables (logos, videos, rebrands — "shiny things") and on price, instead of on the business problems those deliverables are meant to solve. His central move is the **diagnostic phase**: before agreeing to make what a client asks for, ask questions and surface a problem the client didn't already know they had. If you only confirm what the client already believes ("your logo is ugly"), you have created no value and are forced to compete on price. If your process reveals a new, quantified business opportunity (e.g. lost revenue from low foot traffic between 2–5pm), you have added value and reframed the engagement around an outcome worth money.

The conversation is framed by a running example: a designer working with startups. Chris's point is that money follows big problems solved — a marketing contact says "we can't afford this," but the CEO/decision-maker "will find money" for something that solves a real problem (e.g. help raise a $20M round, extend runway). He reframes design skill as a "big creative brain" that can be pointed at higher-stakes problems than the deliverable in front of you. The designer's objection ("I can't sell it, my clients don't have the money") is challenged directly: "or so you say... you only reinforce what they believe."

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- `[2018-07-20]` (paraphrase) When you can help solve a big problem, clients will find the money — the person who says "we can't afford this" is usually not the decision-maker; the CEO/big decision-maker "will find money to afford this."
- `[2018-07-20]` (paraphrase) Clients come wanting deliverables ("make a video," "help us rebrand") — but that request is "just the beginning of the conversation, not the end."
- `[2018-07-20]` (paraphrase) The value is created in the **diagnostic phase**: asking questions before going down the deliverable path, so you can surface problems the client didn't know they had.
- `[2018-07-20]` (paraphrase) If a client already knows their logo is ugly and your audit just confirms it, you have created no value — so you end up competing on price because you added nothing to the conversation.
- `[2018-07-20]` (paraphrase) If instead your process reveals a new, quantified opportunity (e.g. low customer traffic 2–5pm; a business model to fill it could generate ~$4M/yr of new business), the client will happily refocus the engagement on that problem.
- `[2018-07-20]` (paraphrase) Telling the client "something they didn't know" is the critical part — that is what makes you a value-adder rather than an order-taker.
- `[2018-07-20]` (paraphrase) Designers should point their creative ability at bigger, different problems (e.g. helping a startup raise a Series round / extend runway) rather than only at the deliverable requested.

### Designer (CONTEXT — not persona)

- `[2018-07-20]` (paraphrase) Objects that he can't sell diagnostic/strategic value to his current client base because "they just don't have the money for it."
- `[2018-07-20]` (paraphrase) Concedes the reframe was "mind blowing" — he's being told things he didn't know, which is itself the value being demonstrated.
