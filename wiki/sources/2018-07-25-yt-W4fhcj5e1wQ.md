## Summary

Chris Do answers a set of student questions about school, dropping out, and finding one's
path. He reframes the stay-or-quit decision as an economic value-exchange: school is worth
it only if you actually receive the knowledge, experience, network, and contacts you pay
for. He then names the three most valuable things to extract from school: (1) public
speaking, (2) learning how to learn (and how to formulate questions), and (3) logic and
reasoning. On convincing parents to let you quit, he asks why their approval matters and
suggests treating parents as investors/stakeholders — bringing them a plan and a short
case study of successful people who left the conventional path (Zuckerberg, Jobs, Gates;
notes Elon Musk did not drop out). On hiring, Chris says he ignores résumé, school, and
degree entirely; he hires on quality of work, and will overlook shortcomings for
determination, drive, self-learning, communication, and playing well with others — betting
on high-ceiling potential. On finding your passion, he tells viewers to look at what they
do in free time when no one is watching, or to ask their parents what they loved as a
young child — the natural instinct.

Interviewer Elanor Jang (CONTEXT) shares her own experience of giving her parents a written
proposal/plan for how she would support herself, and frames parents as investors wanting a
return (a healthy, happy child).

## Key claims

### Chris Do (SUBJECT — persona)

- [2018-07-25] Whether you should quit school depends on whether you're actually getting
  what school is supposed to give you. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] You can't discuss going to or dropping out of school without discussing the
  finances/economics: what it costs vs. what you hope to get out of it. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] Every transaction happens because of a value exchange — you give money, the
  school should give knowledge, experience, network, and contacts; if those aren't upheld,
  reconsider being there. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] The three things to get out of school are: (1) public speaking, (2) learning
  how to learn, (3) logic and reasoning. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] Public speaking matters because most leaders, managers, and change-makers can
  speak to people directly; it's a key 21st-century skill. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] Everything you learn in school is already outdated by the time you learn it;
  once codified into a textbook it's "ancient history," so learning how to learn — and how
  to formulate questions — is the single most important survival skill. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] Logic and reasoning let you structure cohesive thoughts and have rational
  discussion/debate as a form of investigation and curiosity. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] On convincing parents: first ask why their approval matters to you — is it
  financial dependence, or that you don't act without their approval (which may itself be
  the problem). (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] View parents as investors/stakeholders in your future; make a case study /
  short biography of a few well-known successful people to show they didn't follow the
  conventional path (cites Zuckerberg, Steve Jobs, Bill Gates as dropouts; notes Elon Musk
  did not drop out). (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] In hiring, he does NOT look at résumé, which school you attended, your degree,
  or whether you went to school at all — none of that matters to him. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] He hires (freelance or permanent) on the quality of the work, and will
  overlook shortcomings if made up for by personality and attitude. (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] Traits that matter: determined, ambitious, driven, a self-learner, a good
  communicator, plays well with others; he wants to gauge maximum potential — if the ceiling
  is high he'll invest time, energy, and resources so both sides make money together.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-07-25] To find your passion, look at what you do in free time when nobody's watching
  and nobody cares (comics, video games, writing, drawing, shooting with a camera).
  (paraphrase)
