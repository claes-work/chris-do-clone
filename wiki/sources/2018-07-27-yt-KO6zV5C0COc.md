## Summary

A short, practical The Futur tutorial on how designers should build a stand-out
resume. The lead instructor walks through the anatomy of a good designer resume —
structure, contact info, education, awards/recognition, and skills — then covers a
do's-and-don'ts list. A co-host ("Emily") participates and delivers the closing recap.
The through-line: a designer's resume is itself a design artifact, so consistency,
legibility, and editorial curation matter as much as the content.

The video overlaps thematically with The Futur's "resume is dead / you don't need a
resume" messaging: it opens by conceding that a resume is not strictly required to get
a design job, but is still "helpful to have one in hand." Where the "resume-is-dead"
framing argues portfolio and reputation matter more, this piece is the pragmatic
companion — if you are going to make a resume, here is how to not make it bad.

## Key claims

### Lead instructor (non-Chris; identity uncertain)
- [2018-07-27] You don't really need a resume to get a job in the design industry, but
  it's helpful to have one in hand.
- [2018-07-27] List experience in reverse chronological order — though in some cases
  the most recent item is also the most relevant.
- [2018-07-27] Be consistent: a resume is a designer's first impression; sloppiness
  signals the person didn't think their system through.
- [2018-07-27] Four key pieces of contact info: name, website/portfolio link, email,
  phone number.
- [2018-07-27] Education can include more than college/university — e.g. attending a
  conference like Adobe MAX shows persistent, continuous learning.
- [2018-07-27] Include an awards & recognition section: gallery features, dean's list,
  scholarships, interviews, being featured on a website. Third-party (outside your
  school) recognition is especially valuable; enter competitions to earn it.
- [2018-07-27] Skills section can cover software skills (Adobe suite, Cinema, Rhino)
  and hardware/machinery skills (laser cutting, sewing, bookbinding).
- [2018-07-27] Personal interests are optional — don't list everything, just add a
  little personality.
- [2018-07-27] DON'T include an objective statement (especially the generic "I want to
  get a job" kind); a clear, specific goal statement can be acceptable.
- [2018-07-27] DON'T include proficiency-level bars (0–100 skill meters) — misleading,
  since there's never true 100% mastery of tools like Photoshop or After Effects.
- [2018-07-27] DON'T be "overly creative" — infographic-style resumes with too many
  tricks work against you.

### Emily (co-host)
- [2018-07-27] Recap of the design side: keep it simple, keep it consistent, keep it
  legible.
- [2018-07-27] Recap of the content side: curate and edit down to only what's relevant,
  and customize/personalize the resume for each employer rather than sending a generic
  one.
