# How To Talk To Clients About Money & Budget — Barcelona Workshop

★ **L3-candidate** — introduces a distinct, named communication framework ("the train of thought" — reverse the order: feeling → thought → reaction) for handling the low-budget conversation. This is a reusable persona/voice mechanic not captured by the existing pricing/money-conversation pages, which focus on *what* to say; this one is about the emotional *sequencing* of how to say it. ## Summary

Documentary-style B-roll of The Futur's 2018 Euro tour arriving in Barcelona (train from Madrid), interwoven with Chris Do teaching a live communication workshop hosted by local designer Albert de Ville. The teaching core is a single exercise: how a creative responds when a client says "the budget's too low" without either losing the job or blowing up the relationship.

Chris's premise: creatives are conflict-averse ("delicate little flowers"), so they either fight the client or "shut up and eat it." Both are the *reaction* — the wrong place to start. He argues that silence/obscurity is worse than directness in business, and that "the art of communication is really about saying what it is you think without offending people in the room."

The distinctive contribution is the **"train of thought"** metaphor, deliberately flipped from its idiomatic meaning. A response has parts arranged like a train: the **engine** at the front is the base *feeling* (from the limbic brain — pre-verbal gut sense), the middle cars are what you're *thinking*, and the **caboose** at the back is the *reaction* (e.g. "you get what you pay for"). Most people start at the caboose. Chris coaches starting at the engine: name the raw feeling first ("I feel uncomfortable / a little confused"), then articulate the thought, and only then deliver the reaction. Reordering this way "changes the entire complexion of the dialogue" and shifts a combative tone to a neutral one — the same content lands completely differently.

Supporting props/ideas: a printed **feeling chart** (he tells the participant to pick a constructive base feeling, not "loathing" or "ecstasy"); the limbic-vs-neocortex explanation for why raw emotion "comes out wrong" when it first hits words; and the mindfulness goal of "think about your reaction before you say things." Framed as full-contact, no-punches-pulled teaching where friction surfaces truth.
