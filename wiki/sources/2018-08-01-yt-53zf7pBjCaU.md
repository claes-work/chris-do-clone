# Simple Tips to IMPROVE your Design

## Summary

A design-critique tutorial on The Futur, **led entirely by Matthew Encina** (creative
director at Blind / The Futur). **Chris Do does not appear in this video.** Encina
reviews three viewer-submitted works across different mediums — a FIFA World Cup event
poster (by Liam), a zombie-apocalypse style frame (by Joel, a student in Encina's style
frames course), and a logo lockup for a design studio (by Herman) — to demonstrate that
core design principles (scale, contrast, legibility, hierarchy) apply the same way
across all design mediums. He performs live edits in Affinity Designer on iPad, which
sponsors the episode. Because Chris Do is absent, this page carries **no persona-voice
material** — Encina is a Futur instructor/context speaker, not the subject.

Attribution note: the tutorial is unambiguously Matthew Encina's (he introduces himself
by name at the start). No `attribution: uncertain` needed.

## Key claims

All claims below are **paraphrased** and attributed to **Matthew Encina** unless noted;
all dated **2018-08-01**.

- Encina (2018-08-01): Design principles — scale, contrast, legibility, hierarchy — are
  the same regardless of medium; that's why reviewing a poster, a style frame, and a
  logo together makes sense.
- Encina (2018-08-01): On the FIFA poster — the hierarchy could be pushed further so the
  reading order (what the viewer must read first, second, third) is clearer; turning the
  central info into a highlighted "band" and introducing a secondary color (bringing back
  an orange via color overlay) sharpens legibility.
- Encina (2018-08-01): Cramped/"uncomfortable" spacing between type should be relieved
  with more air in the margins; a slight scale tweak fixes it.
- Encina (2018-08-01): Minor tweaks to hierarchy and color make a large impact on
  legibility and reading order.
- Encina (2018-08-01): On Joel's style frame — one-point perspective and leading lines
  (even the invisible ones) that point toward the main character are a strength; the
  composition directs the eye to the key subject.
- Encina (2018-08-01): The frame was too dark; lift the blacks with a curves color
  correction, then use a painted mask to restrict the correction so bright areas don't
  blow out, and add visual contrast on the primary subject so the eye lands on him.
- Encina (2018-08-01): Craft work like this can become obsessive (endlessly turning
  values up and down over many hours); Joel already composed the frame well — the edits
  are minor finesse.
- Encina (2018-08-01): On Herman's logo — align the type with the illustration (the
  "baron"/mascot) to reduce awkward negative space, balance the spaces on left and right,
  and let the black typeface at the base give the lockup a sturdier feel; kerning and
  massaging would take a few more hours to perfect.
- Encina (2018-08-01): The three questions that judge any design across industries — Is
  it hitting your objectives? Does it communicate? Is it technically sound (following the
  general rules of design)?
- Encina (2018-08-01): His edits across all three pieces were fundamentally the same
  concern — where the eye goes, what you read first/second/third, and making sure what
  the viewer needs to see is crystal clear.
- Encina (2018-08-01, sponsor read): Affinity Designer is the first fully featured vector
  design app on iPad, a one-time purchase with no subscription, combining vector and
  raster editing in one app, optimized for the latest iPad and Apple Pencil.
