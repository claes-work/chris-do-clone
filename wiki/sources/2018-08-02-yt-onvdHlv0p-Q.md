# How to Submit Your Portfolio — Application Tips (Greg Gunn & Scott Rothstein)

> **CONTEXT PAGE** — This episode is led entirely by **Greg Gunn** (Futur instructor/collaborator, Blind Creative Director) and **Scott Rothstein**, who introduces himself as "the executive producer at Blind and The Futur." **Chris Do is NOT present and does not speak.** Nothing here is persona training data. The content is captured as CONTEXT: it documents how the Blind/Futur agency side wants portfolios and applications submitted, which is useful background on the subject's studio, but it is not Chris Do's voice or belief data.

## Summary

Greg Gunn and Scott Rothstein walk through the do's and don'ts of submitting a resume/portfolio email when reaching out to an agency (framed from Blind's receiving end, where they get a high volume of submissions). The advice is a practical checklist for creatives applying for agency/studio work: curate hard, clarify your role, show process, keep your reel current, tailor each application, and be responsive and clear on logistics (rates and contact info).

The two hosts alternate tips. Scott emphasizes the operational/staffing perspective (budgets, day rates, responsiveness). Greg emphasizes craft and positioning (best work first, case studies, customizing your portfolio per studio). No frameworks are named; it is a rapid-fire tips segment.

## Key claims (dated `[2018-08-02]`, grouped by speaker)

### Scott Rothstein (CONTEXT — agency/producer perspective)

- `[2018-08-02]` **Only show your best work.** Blind receives many submissions; they don't have time to sort through every frame or video going back to art school. Put your best work first and cut the rest — quality over quantity.
- `[2018-08-02]` **Clearly identify your role.** Multiple hands touch a project, so make it easy to understand exactly what you did — whether you're a specialist (one thing done really well) or a generalist. Tell them where your strengths lie, and don't lie about what you can do ("it hurts both of us").
- `[2018-08-02]` **Be responsive and follow up.** "If you see that email, respond to me right away, because the longer you take to get back to me the more likely it is I'm moving on and going to hire somebody else."
- `[2018-08-02]` **Be clear on your rates.** When a project comes in, they set budgets before staffing and juggle numbers to see who they can afford and how many people, for a certain date. State your regular day rate, weekend rate, and overtime clearly. They may negotiate, but don't spring a curveball later — it screws up production.
- `[2018-08-02]` **Make your contact info clear and complete.** Beyond the email itself, they want your website and phone number visible. "If I have to dig around and try to find how to contact you, I'm probably not going to do it."

### Greg Gunn (CONTEXT — craft/positioning perspective)

- `[2018-08-02]` **Show a bit of context/process, not just the final product.** Include early work-in-progress frames or different versions. "Case studies are great — tell us a story about how you got there... don't just tell us the answer, share with us how you think and how you approach work, so we know what your process will be like."
- `[2018-08-02]` **Keep your portfolio/reel updated (a "huge pet peeve").** If Greg (and the creative directors) see something outdated, he'll quickly move on to the next candidate. Know the role you want, specialize, and make it relevant.
- `[2018-08-02]` **Customize your submission per company.** Make a custom website or at least a custom page tailored to that company/ad so they feel "this person is perfect, they'd fit right in." You can maintain more than one portfolio and send different studios different curated arrangements of projects (reordered, recolored, rewritten) — you don't have to show everyone everything.
- `[2018-08-02]` **Let your personality show / fit culturally.** Beyond the work, they want to see personality and gauge whether it's a good cultural fit — comparing what the client asked for and what the role needs against whether the person fits both.
- `[2018-08-02]` **Never send generic, copy-paste cover letters.** "I hate generic cover letters... we know when it's a copy-and-paste job." Exercise: imagine you're the one hiring, you post an ad, and you get a flood of emails ("like a hundred a day") that all open with "To whom it may concern" — you start to see the pattern of people just going for mass exposure.
