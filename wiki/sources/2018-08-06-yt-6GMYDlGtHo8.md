# 5 YouTube Tips I've Learned From Casey Neistat, Peter McKinnon & Phil DeFranco

> Captions present (English). Source is a Chris Do talk/lesson delivered to a live design audience, teaching The Futur's own YouTube/content playbook.

**Speaker confirmation:** Chris Do leads and teaches throughout. Self-identifying markers: "our Channel," "we do that in our vlog now too," "we've been working 22 years," "we've won awards," "we've worked with big clients," and the reference to The Futur's viral "how to charge what you're worth" / overtime videos. Casey Neistat, Peter McKinnon, Phil DeFranco, and Aaron Draplin are named as **influences/examples being discussed (CONTEXT)**, not speakers.

## Summary

Chris Do gives a live design audience a condensed YouTube-growth playbook drawn from studying top creators, and argues that experienced professionals should be publishing because their voice is missing from a platform dominated by young creators with big personalities but little real-world accomplishment. He walks through five practical tactics — strong face-forward thumbnails with left-aligned big text, the "cold open," high-energy hosting with a signature prop/persona, a repeatable catchphrase (in/out), and consistency over shortcuts — then explains what makes content get shared: strong emotion, a clear point of view, and broad/universal topics. He frames The Futur's edge as a market gap: accomplished practitioners are too busy or too reluctant to speak transparently about money, clients, and process, and "their fear is our opportunity."

## Key claims (Chris Do)

- [2018-08-06] The thumbnail largely determines whether someone clicks; your face makes a good thumbnail because humans are drawn to look at other humans' faces (cites an advertising study about eyes looking at camera).
- [2018-08-06] Put big, descriptive text on the LEFT of the thumbnail, because on mobile the timestamp sits at the bottom right.
- [2018-08-06] Use a "cold open" (a TV term): skip the title sequence and jump straight into the most exciting, "craziest/coolest/weirdest" moment — content doesn't have to be linear. "Begin where it's exciting."
- [2018-08-06] After the cold open, tell viewers what they're going to see, or they'll feel their time is being wasted and leave.
- [2018-08-06] Host with high energy and develop a persona "that is you but not 100% you"; a recurring prop or signature (Peter McKinnon's coffee, Aaron Draplin's hat) becomes part of your character and audiences come to expect it.
- [2018-08-06] Develop a catchphrase / signature intro and outro (as Phil DeFranco does) — it becomes your signature and helps a lot.
- [2018-08-06] There is no secret formula or growth hack; it takes time — make high-quality content, great thumbnails, host well, and the reward comes.
- [2018-08-06] Experienced older professionals are needed on YouTube because the platform is filled with young people with big personalities who "haven't done jack squat," giving poor information yet scoring higher because they're prolific and know how to play the game.
- [2018-08-06] The Futur faces almost no competition because they're accomplished (22 years, awards, big clients) and speak passionately, honestly, and transparently about real work, process, clients, and money.
- [2018-08-06] More accomplished people exist but are busy running their agencies and don't value these platforms or want to talk transparently about money — "their fear is our opportunity, their reluctance is our game, their slowness to move is our window of opportunity."
- [2018-08-06] Shareable content stirs a strong emotion (positive or negative) and requires a point of view; "riding in the middle" doesn't engage people.
- [2018-08-06] Broader, more universal topics perform better; a narrow tutorial (e.g., fixing camera shake in Premiere) shrinks your audience, whereas a broad topic (e.g., not working overtime) reaches everyone.
- [2018-08-06] The Futur's "how to not work overtime" video went viral — ~4.5 million views and ~9 million impressions at last count.
- [2018-08-06] The "how to charge what you're worth" video was watched and shared even by people outside the normal audience (e.g., hairdressers); it stirred people up — many loved it, a small percentage of creatives called it dishonest/unethical — which generated conversation and engagement.
- [2018-08-06] People share for two reasons: it could help someone they know, or it strengthens their own argument — this leverages "confirmation bias" (illustrated with a cheesecake analogy).

## Notable verbatim quotes (Chris Do)

- "Don't begin at the beginning on this one. Begin where it's exciting. Begin as a car chase, the shootout, the bank robbery, the emotional breakup — that's where you want to begin. Something bananas."
- "You have to start to develop a personality or persona that is you but not 100% you."
- "It's called a catchphrase... it becomes your signature in and out and it helps out a lot."
- "Everybody wants a secret formula, a growth hack, some kind of shortcut. The real thing is it takes time."
- "YouTube is filled with young people who have big personalities but haven't done jack squat with their life, so they're giving really poor information into the world."
- "Their fear is our opportunity, their reluctance is our game, their slow to move is our window of opportunity."

- "The more it appeals to a broader audience and [the] more universal the content is, the better it is."
