## Summary

Episode two of a business-coaching series in which Chris Do pushes a designer to
raise her "business IQ" and formalize her operation. His core recommendation:
convert from a sole proprietorship to an LLC as soon as possible, primarily for
liability protection (a sole proprietor can be sued personally, exposing personal
and spousal assets) and for tax flexibility once the business earns "real money."
The bulk of the video is a scripted rundown of the three common small-business
entity types — sole proprietorship, LLC, and corporation — comparing them on
liability protection, ability to raise capital, filing costs, and taxation, using
California/Los Angeles County fee examples. Chris repeatedly defers to attorneys
and CPAs for actual setup.

### Scripted comparison segment (show narration)
- [2018-08-13] The three most common small-business entities are sole
  proprietorship, LLC, and corporation; they differ in tax status, ownership,
  ability to raise funds, and annual paperwork.
- [2018-08-13] Three questions before registering: (1) do you want asset/liability
  protection from lawsuits? (2) do you want to raise capital? (3) can you afford
  incorporation fees?
- [2018-08-13] Sole proprietorship yields the least liability protection;
  corporation the most.
- [2018-08-13] Sole proprietorship pros: simple filing, some tax benefits, low fee
  (LA County example: $26 for five years). Cons: personal liability for all
  business risk, higher audit rates, cited claim that you are "four times as likely
  to be sued as a sole proprietorship than a corporation" (attributed in-video to
  a LegalZoom article), cannot raise capital, no separation of personal/business
  credit.
- [2018-08-13] LLC pros: simple filing, reduced personal liability via separation
  of personal and business assets, tax benefits, ability to raise capital. Cons:
  higher filing/maintenance/tax costs (California example: $100 to register + $25
  maintenance + $800 annual corporate/franchise tax), must file articles of
  organization with the Secretary of State.
- [2018-08-13] Some single owners with adequate insurance and revenue "usually
  below eighty thousand dollars annually" may reasonably stay a sole proprietor.
- [2018-08-13] Corporation: exists separately from its owner; three player groups
  (shareholders, board of directors, officers); pros mirror the LLC plus unlimited
  shareholders; cons include higher filing costs, articles of incorporation,
  mandatory annual meetings/minutes, and double taxation (taxed at the corporate
  rate, then again at the shareholder rate on distribution).
