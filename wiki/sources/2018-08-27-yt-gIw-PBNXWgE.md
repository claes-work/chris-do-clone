# How Do I Raise Prices Without Losing Clients? (@thefutur, 2018-08-27)

## Summary

A live coaching / Q&A segment in which host **Chris Do** answers **Maria**, a
beginning graphic designer with a small illustration business who wants to raise her
prices but fears losing all her clients. Chris reframes the problem on three levels:
(1) mechanics — raising prices on *new* clients continuously, controlling supply to
push price up; (2) process — running a "value conversation" and qualifying clients on
"dollars, decision-maker, deadlines" before quoting; and (3) mindset — the emotional
block creatives have around charging, self-worth, and the inability to say "no."

The centerpiece is a live role-play: Chris casts Maria as *his* negotiator against an
imaginary client "Joel" (a Harley-Davidson dealership owner) and she instinctively
fights hard for a bigger fee, plays hard-to-get, and rejects a lowball. Chris uses this
to show that Maria will fight fiercely for a stranger's money but not her own, and
tells her to double her price tomorrow. The session leans deliberately toward mindset
and self-worth over pure tactics.

> Attribution note: Chris Do is the host/coach and source of all persona-relevant
> advice. **Maria** is the questioner (context only). At least one **audience member**
> supplies the "supply and demand" answer (context). "Joel" is a hypothetical client
> named by Chris in a role-play, not a real speaker.

## Key claims

All dated 2018-08-27. Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section.

**Chris Do (host/coach):**
- Raising prices on *existing / repeat* clients is a hard sell, because the client is
  anchored to what they've been getting for that price; they don't hear a rationale,
  they just hear "I want what I'm used to." (2018-08-27)
- The practical rule: raise your price on **every new client that comes in**. Do it
  each time and — because a new client arrives, then another, then another — within a
  year you're charging "double, triple, quadruple" what you charge today, without ever
  having to renegotiate an existing client. (2018-08-27)
- Price is fundamentally driven by **supply and demand**; lots of supply plus little
  demand pushes price down. Raising your price is therefore "not about magic or
  deception — it's really about controlling supply and demand." (2018-08-27)
- Analogy: when Banksy makes a painting there is only one; if you want it you pay a
  huge sum. The goal is to engineer that scarcity for yourself. (2018-08-27)
- **Restrict your supply to justify a higher price.** A concrete "trick" for raising
  the price on a repeat customer: use busyness as the reason — "I'm really busy, it's
  going to be a rush charge, I'll have to work the weekend / bring in other artists."
  Restricting supply causes the price to rise. (2018-08-27)
- **Position yourself as an expert, not a service provider** — a recurring refrain.
  Experts are busy; busyness is the excuse that lets you charge more. (2018-08-27)
- Run a **"value conversation"**: when a client commissions you, ask what it is about
  *this* project that solves *their* problem, so price is tied to value delivered, not
  to the fixed unit ("same painting") the client sees. (2018-08-27)
- **Qualify every client/project on the "three D's": dollars, decision-maker,
  deadlines** — establish these *before* talking price. Ask what the budget is, when
  the decision will be made, who the decision-maker is, and when the work is due.
  (2018-08-27)
- **Play hard-to-get.** If a prospect is vague ("maybe by Christmas"), tell them to
  call you back later (e.g. "call me in November"). Being maximally available,
  responsive, and willing to bend over backwards compromises the budget, the quality
  you produce, and your ability to solve a bigger, deeper problem. (2018-08-27)
- Caveat: if you literally need the check to eat, take the work — but if you're
  building a *career*, hold your ground; in the long run you build relationships with
  better clients who look to you to solve bigger problems. (2018-08-27)
- **Mindset is the real bottleneck.** Creatives feel bad raising prices because they
  seek approval, feel happy when clients like their work and give them more, and so
  repress their own desires and values. This has to be overcome. (2018-08-27)
- Theory: creatives love the work so much they'd almost do it for free (illustrating
  comics, making logos, hand-lettering), which undermines their willingness to charge.
  (2018-08-27)
- **Self-worth reframe:** people fight harder for others than for themselves. Chris
  cites a trick from his psychotherapist — imagine advising your own child / an
  imaginary person you'd take great care of, then extend that care to yourself. There
  is an "inner Maria / inner child" you should protect at least as well as you let
  clients treat you. (2018-08-27)
- **Learn to say "yes" and "no"** — "the two most unused letter combinations" for
  creatives. You can say no without explaining or apologizing, and do it with a smile
  so it isn't taken as offense. (2018-08-27)
- **Reframe payment as appreciation:** treat every dollar bill as a thank-you note —
  more dollars means the client appreciates the work more. Don't feel guilty charging.
  (2018-08-27)
- **Uncouple your art from ancillary services** (framing, etc.). An artist charges for
  the art; let the client handle framing and the rest — "that's not your problem."
  (2018-08-27)
- **Headline advice: double your price tomorrow.** Maria's price is "three hundred and
  some odd dollars"; Chris tells her to ask $600, no framing, and not to explain it. If
  a client doesn't like it, fine — move on. (2018-08-27)
- Maria's illustration work takes real talent, skill, energy and time (unlike, per
  Chris, work he can "command copy-paste and sell"); she is "selling herself too
  short." (2018-08-27)

**Maria (questioner — context, not persona data):**
- Self-describes as starting out as a graphic designer with a small illustration
  business; wants to raise prices but fears losing all her clients. (2018-08-27)
- In the role-play (negotiating *for* Chris), she instinctively demands the client
  prove himself, plays hard-to-get, and pushes the fee up — demonstrating Chris's point
  that she fights for others but not herself. (2018-08-27)

**Audience member (context):**
- Identifies **supply and demand** as the biggest driver of price when Chris opens the
  question to the room. (2018-08-27)
