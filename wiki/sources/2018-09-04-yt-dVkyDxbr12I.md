# How To Respond To Clients Who Have No Idea What They Want — Ep. 20 w/ Keir McLaren

Auto-captions present (English). Source is the YouTube auto-caption track; speaker
labels are inferred, not machine-provided.

## Summary

Kier McLaren answers how a creative should respond when a client won't (or claims they
can't) say what they want or what they'll spend. His core stance: the buyer almost
always has both a budget and a mental picture, and the creative's job is to draw those
out — anchor with real price ranges from past comparable work, refuse to work for free
or explore unpaid, and interview the client to surface the reference images already in
their head. He frames "just wow me" as an unfair negotiating tactic to be politely
declined, invoking the book *Getting to Yes* and its BATNA concept. All content is
Kier's; Chris Do is only mentioned in passing.

## Key claims

_All claims are **Kier McLaren (CONTEXT)** — not Chris Do._

- `[2018-09-04]` (Kier, CONTEXT) Clients who say "I don't know, you're the expert"
  almost always *do* know their budget; the buyer you talk to may be far down the agency
  chain but the organization knows what it must spend. If your contact doesn't know, tell
  them to go ask the person who does. *(sales-clients)*
- `[2018-09-04]` (Kier, CONTEXT) Never work for free and never let clients ask you to
  work for nothing — he gets aggravated when creatives are asked to. *(sales-clients, mindset)*
- `[2018-09-04]` (Kier, CONTEXT) The healthy way to handle a budget-dodging client is to
  anchor with real ranges from comparable past work (e.g. "we've done 30-second spots
  like yours for as much as $700k, and some good ones under $250k") and ask where they
  think they fall; offer to find a different solution if they need to spend less. *(sales-clients)*
- `[2018-09-04]` (Kier, CONTEXT) Clients have a picture in their mind of the finished
  work; the creative's job is to interview them to reconstruct it. Ask "don't tell me
  what you like — what have you *seen* that reflects this idea?" (a billboard, a kids'
  cartoon, an old ad). *(sales-clients)*
- `[2018-09-04]` (Kier, CONTEXT) Stay current with imaging in film, TV and everywhere
  else so you can offer references ("is it like the *Six Feet Under* open, or those goofy
  Geico ads?") to close in on what resonates. *(sales-clients)*
- `[2018-09-04]` (Kier, CONTEXT) Refuse open-ended "just wow me" briefs: you can't put
  paid staff to work exploring for free. Analogy — you wouldn't tell a lawyer to solve
  your problem first and then decide whether to pay. Cite limited bandwidth and other
  paying prospects. *(sales-clients)*
- `[2018-09-04]` (Kier, CONTEXT) "Wow me" is an unfair negotiating tactic. From *Getting
  to Yes* (Kennedy School) comes **BATNA** — best alternative to a negotiated agreement;
  his alternative to an unfair ask is to politely decline and not "bite." *(sales-clients, mindset)*
- `[2018-09-04]` (Kier, CONTEXT) Say your terms politely, with a smile; then the client
  either stays or goes — that's up to them. The biggest mistake is thinking you can
  control the other party; you can only control what you do, and as long as you're fair
  and honest you'll be fine. *(mindset)*
