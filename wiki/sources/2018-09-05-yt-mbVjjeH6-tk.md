# What Motivates You?

Auto-captions present.

## Summary

A short first-person monologue in which Chris Do answers what motivates him to
work at the intensity he does. He frames his drive not as work in the classical
sense but as sharing what he knows "in the most open and transparent way to lift
people up." His fuel is audience feedback — comments at the start and end of each
day, people crying on the phone about the impact he's had. He defines the way his
audience can "repay" him: not with money, but by being successful and living a
sustainable, fulfilled creative life — which he treats as proof of concept that
the model works. He then tells the origin of the "#10%" running joke (a coaching
moment where he told an entrepreneur to send "uncle Chris" a check for 10% once
successful) and the story of a viewer who mailed him a real check for $14.68 with
the memo line reading "a better life" — a moment that moved his wife to tears.

Speaker: Chris Do throughout (solo monologue). The young entrepreneur and the
check-sender are referenced third-hand within Chris's story (context only, not
persona-attributable). Chris's wife is mentioned but not named.

## Key claims

**Chris Do (speaker):**

- [2018-09-05] He works roughly 16–18 hours a day, seven days a week, but does not
  experience it as work in the classical sense. (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-05] His motivation is to share what he knows with the world as openly
  and transparently as possible in order to "lift people up." (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-05] Daily audience feedback — a comment at the beginning and end of each
  day — is what lifts him up and lets him keep going. (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-05] The way for people to repay the impact he's had is to be successful,
  live a creative and fulfilled life, and make it sustainable; that outcome is his
  "proof of concept" that the model/idea works. (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-05] The business is "built on a thank you economy model, pure [Cialdini]
  reciprocation." (near-verbatim paraphrase; the transcript renders it as "pure
  leon's reciprocation," likely a mis-transcription of a reference to reciprocity)
- [2018-09-05] Origin of the "#10%" running joke: while coaching a young
  entrepreneur on bidding and building his business, Chris told him to send "uncle
  Chris" a check for 10% once successful; commenters adopted #10% to signal the
  content wasn't free. (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-05] A viewer mailed him a real check for ~$14.68; his wife initially
  said he couldn't cash it. Chris decided returning it would insult the giver, who
  gave what they had as promised — declining it would be passing judgment. (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-05] The check's memo line read "a better life," which nearly brought his
  wife to tears. (paraphrase)
