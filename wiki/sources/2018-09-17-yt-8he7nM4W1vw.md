## Summary

A short coaching clip in which Chris Do pushes two people to stop building their "pet projects" the hard way and instead **prototype and validate** first. His argument: build a prototype that *looks* like the intended product but has no real functionality, then take it somewhere you can crowdfund it and see whether the market actually wants it. Testing early avoids spending years building something nobody wants or missing the window of opportunity.

He frames three benefits of putting a prototype in front of people: (1) you can raise money, (2) you learn whether people are genuinely interested, and (3) — the biggest — people contribute ideas you hadn't thought of, becoming "unpaid collaborators." This leads into his belief in **collective intelligence**: many minds beat any single person. He cites Pixar's story process and Google Labs as examples, then explains an "invert the pyramid" method: instead of building bottom-up from research/engineering, design what it looks like and how it performs first, get people excited, and only then bring engineers in to make it real. If people can't get excited about it, it's not a product.
