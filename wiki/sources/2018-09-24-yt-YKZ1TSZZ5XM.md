# How to build rapport — Lose the Small Talk, Ask Big Questions

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a compact, named rapport-building framework
(reveal-first reciprocity + "make big talk" / bigger questions + curiosity/listening
posture) that maps directly onto Chris Do's sales-discovery and relationship approach.
Worth promoting to `wiki/topics/` and `persona/` on a synthesis pass.

## Summary

Short clip in which a viewer/student tells Chris Do that they dislike small talk and
always want to get "to the root of something" in a conversation, and asks how to handle
it. Chris Do reframes: some small talk is a necessary warm-up to build rapport, but you
can quickly move past it by asking bigger questions. He references a TEDx talk, "Make
Big Talk," whose speaker reached the same conclusion — time is short, so ask bigger
questions (e.g. "what got you up this morning") — and mentions owning a physical
conversation-starter card deck. His core tactic: open by revealing something deeper
about yourself, and reciprocity leads the other person to return in kind (he applies
this to learning a prospect's business goals and finances by first sharing his own). He
illustrates with a personal story about a pastor whose openness about his past made
Chris feel compelled to confess in turn. He closes by advising practice: approach
people from curiosity, aim to be a great listener, and it will "build a new version of
you."

## Key claims

### Chris Do
- `[2018-09-24]` (paraphrase) To build rapport with people, you have to do a little bit
  of small talk first — it is a necessary warm-up, not something to skip entirely.
- `[2018-09-24]` (paraphrase) References a TEDx talk called "Make Big Talk"; the speaker
  concluded that because time is short, we should ask bigger questions in life — e.g.
  "what got you up this morning" — rather than defaulting to weather-level small talk.
- `[2018-09-24]` (paraphrase) He owns a physical deck of conversation-starter cards you
  can flip through to find a good prompt.
- `[2018-09-24]` (paraphrase) If you begin a conversation by revealing something much
  deeper about yourself, people will most of the time return in kind — though beware of
  oversharing; you don't want to drag out everything.
- `[2018-09-24]` (paraphrase) He applies this in business: to learn a prospect's goals
  and financials, he first tells them his own business goals and finances, which
  triggers reciprocity so they feel compelled to share back.
- `[2018-09-24]` (paraphrase) When people are vulnerable and willing to say anything,
  they become relatable and real, and hold nothing back — which builds connection.
- `[2018-09-24]` (paraphrase) Advises practicing the art of conversation with anyone
  (e.g. someone in line): approach from curiosity, aim to be a great listener, and it
  will build a new version of you.

### Viewer / interviewer (context, not persona)
- `[2018-09-24]` (paraphrase) Says they want every conversation to get deep and to know
  people as a person; they dislike talking for the sake of talking and their attention
