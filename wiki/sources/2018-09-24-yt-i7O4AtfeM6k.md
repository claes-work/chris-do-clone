# How to Pitch to Potential Clients

## Summary

A short debrief in which Chris Do reflects, immediately after a live client pitch,
on how he approached presenting creative work. The clip opens with a brief thank-you
from the client side, then Chris narrates his pitch process to what appears to be his
team/audience: he stresses front-loading a discovery/kickoff call to understand the
client's needs, framing every creative idea against the client's stated challenges and
goals, and making the client feel like a collaborator so they stay attached to the
work. He also describes his personal creative process (working until the last minute
under time pressure to surface unique ideas), his stance that pitching should be
objective rather than dependent on the client's emotional gushing, and his belief that
confidence — versus desperation — is audible to a client during a pitch.

This is a debrief/coaching-style monologue, not a role-play. Chris speaks in the first
person throughout ("my team", "these pitches"), consistent with his own pitching
doctrine. Only the opening thank-you (lines 1–2) reads as a different speaker (client
side) and is context, not persona material.

Relation to the "Pitch This!" presenting doctrine: overlapping theme (how to present
creative work), but this clip is a real post-pitch debrief. What is DISTINCT here is the
emphasis on (a) contextual framing of ideas against the client's own brief, (b)
engineering client ownership/collaboration, (c) the "work until the last minute" creative
process, and (d) not needing external validation because the case is made objectively.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (subject)

- [2018-09-24] A week before the pitch there was a creative kickoff call where the client
  gave a creative brief; Chris asked "a lot of deep questions" to clarify exactly what the
  client needs, the applications, who it will be shown to, and their current challenges.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-24] When pitching or showing creative ideas, you must give context; if ideas
  don't feel valuable or useful to the client they will reject them immediately.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-24] Framing an idea by how it addresses the client's challenges and goals makes
  it much better received and easier to walk through. (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-24] Presenting is also a way to check in, confirm you're still headed in the
  right direction, and make the client feel they are contributing and collaborating; if
  they feel they had no hand in generating the ideas, the work feels foreign to them and
  they won't feel attached. (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-24] He prefers to work up to the last minute — not from procrastination, but
  because the time crunch and pressure are where he generates his most interesting ideas;
  on this project he was still painting frames and writing five minutes before the
  presentation. (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-24] The pitch "only took an hour to put together but it took a whole week of
  thinking"; setup at the start (understanding the challenge) lets ideas incubate in the
  subconscious until the dots connect. (paraphrase / quote fragment)
- [2018-09-24] Some people who pitch need external validation — they need the client to
  gush; Chris says he personally does not need that. (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-24] For him pitching is objective: "here's what you said you needed... here's
  how we solved it," so he doesn't need an emotional reaction to know the team did a good
  job. (paraphrase)
- [2018-09-24] You have to be confident going into a pitch; if you're not confident in your
  own work, clients can hear the desperation in your voice and tone — but going in confident,
  "you're just already winning." (paraphrase / quote fragment)
