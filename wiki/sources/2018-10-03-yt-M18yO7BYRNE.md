# Pricing Strategies – Give Your Client Price Options

## Summary

Very short clip (a ~37-second fragment; the caption file begins mid-sentence at "number
one") in which **Chris Do** describes how he structures **three price options** for a
client. The transcript is a first-person monologue with a single speaker — no coaching,
no role-play, no second voice — so all of it is Chris-attributed.

The distinctive idea is *what differentiates the three tiers*. Chris says he first forms
a rough sense of what the project is worth to the client and the risks involved, then
**sets three prices before scoping** — a bottom price of **$10,000**, a middle price of
**$22,000**, and a top price of **$50,000** — and only *then* starts thinking about what
scope could justify each. The tiers are separated not by amount of labor but by how much
of Chris's **personal attention and ownership** each buys, and correspondingly by the
**likelihood of project success**:

- **Top ($50,000)** — Chris takes over as much ownership/control of the project as he can,
  requires the *least* from the client, and gives it the *most* of his attention;
  highest likelihood of success.
- **Middle ($22,000)** — more of a collaboration; less of Chris's attention.
- **Bottom ($10,000)** — the total DIY option: Chris comes in, diagnoses the situation,
  prescribes a course of action, and hands over a road map / report; the least of his
  attention.

Key twist: more attention does **not** mean more labor — "not necessarily more labor…
you probably could easily outsource the labor piece." What the client pays up for is
Chris's ownership and the higher success odds, which he can deliver while outsourcing the
production.

## Key claims

All dated `[2018-10-03]`. Paraphrased unless quoted. All attributed to **Chris Do**
(single-speaker monologue).

1. **Price the options before scoping them.** Chris starts from a vague sense of what the
   project is worth to the client and the risks involved, then creates three prices
   ($10,000 / $22,000 / $50,000) *first*, and only afterward thinks about what scope could
   fill each. (paraphrase — Chris Do)
2. **Three tiers, separated by attention/ownership.** The top option requires the most of
   Chris's attention, the middle less, and the bottom the least. (paraphrase — Chris Do)
3. **Attention ≠ labor.** The top option needs the most attention "but not necessarily the
   most labor"; the labor piece can be outsourced easily. (paraphrase — Chris Do)
4. **Higher tier = Chris takes more ownership, client gives less.** At the top option Chris
   requires *less* from the client and takes over as much control/ownership of the project
   as he can. (paraphrase — Chris Do)
5. **Middle tier is collaboration.** The middle option is more of a collaboration between
   Chris and the client. (paraphrase — Chris Do)
6. **Bottom tier is DIY diagnose-and-prescribe.** The lowest option is total DIY: Chris
   comes in, diagnoses the situation, prescribes a course of action, and gives the client a
   road map / report to execute themselves. (paraphrase — Chris Do)
7. **Price buys probability of success.** As you go up the tiers you increase the
   likelihood the project will succeed, because Chris is taking on more ownership. (paraphrase
   — Chris Do)
