# Starting Your Business: Budget Vs. Profit

## Summary

Solo monologue by Chris Do on foundational business-finance mistakes new
creative entrepreneurs make. Two themes: (1) collect payment (a 50% deposit) up
front — that is how you know a job is real and not just flattering talk; sales
and cash flow keep the company alive and independent. (2) Revenue is not profit:
there are two ways to make money — bid high to build a profit cushion, or spend
less to produce the project. He warns against chasing gross revenue ("a million
dollar job") while ignoring costs, since what matters is what you keep after all
expenses — including paying yourself and your partner.

Speaker confirmed as Chris Do (first-person advice to creatives, thefutur
channel). No coachee/guest present — all lines are Chris Do and eligible for
persona.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2018-10-04] (paraphrase) A common mistake new creative entrepreneurs make is
  doing work without collecting money up front; you need to get paid first.
- [2018-10-04] (paraphrase) Getting a 50% deposit up front is how you know the
  job is real — otherwise it is "just all talk."
- [2018-10-04] (paraphrase) Creatives get flattered when people show interest in
  their work; contain the excitement and convert it into something concrete via
  a proposal / budget and the upfront deposit.
- [2018-10-04] (paraphrase) Sales is everything; with robust sales and
  big-budget projects you can fund any kind of company and run it your way.
- [2018-10-04] (paraphrase) Without sales and incoming cash flow, a business
  will suffer severely.
- [2018-10-04] (paraphrase) There are two ways to make money: bid high and build
  yourself a cushion of profit, or spend less money to produce the project.
- [2018-10-04] (paraphrase) People fixate on higher gross revenue and forget to
  think about how much they spend to deliver it.
- [2018-10-04] (paraphrase) A $1M job on which you spend $999,000 is really a $1
  job; a $100K job on which you spend $50K yields $50K gross profit — most people
  would still choose the million-dollar job without thinking.
- [2018-10-04] (paraphrase) What matters at the end of the day is what you keep
  after all expenses are paid, including paying yourself and your partner, and
  what remains in the profit margin.
