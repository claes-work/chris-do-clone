# How to Work on Personal Projects After Working A Full Time Job

★ **L3-candidate** — reason: contains several of Chris Do's signature mindset/discipline
frameworks in one place (the "what are you willing to give up?" sacrifice/trade model,
the "convert knowledge/experience into equity" idea, "growth is the reward, not the
destination," "small commitments" / just-start, and "blend work and play"). Worth
promoting into `wiki/topics/mindset/` and `persona/beliefs.md` in a synthesis pass.

## Summary

A live Q&A / coaching segment on The Futur. A viewer named **Sharie** (coachee — context)
asks how to stay "self-motivated and self-disciplined" to work on his own portfolio /
spec projects after a draining full-time job. **Chris Do** (the SUBJECT) reframes the
question: you can't be *taught* self-motivation — either you want the thing more than you
want comfort, or you don't. He challenges Sharie's premise, separating "motivation" from
underlying "self-confidence / self-worth," and diagnoses that Sharie may be in an
unfulfilling job that requires "unwinding" in the first place.

Two Futur team members model the answer: **Aaron** ("Aaron Zak" / Zakielarz — context)
describes how Chris's mentorship rewired him to feel bad "wasting time" and good when
moving forward on personal projects; **Mark** (context) describes blending hobby and work
so the line blurs. A community member, **Spencer** (context), adds the "just read three
pages / just start" tactic. Chris closes with his core teaching points: find a **higher
purpose**, **blend** your activities so nothing feels like discrete "work," make **small
commitments** (Terry Crews gym anecdote), and above all **convert** what you consume into
output — otherwise consumption creates no wealth.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (SUBJECT — persona training material)

- [2018-10-07] You cannot be taught self-motivation; if you're asking how to *stay*
  self-motivated and disciplined, that means you currently aren't — which is a different
  problem than the one being asked. (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-07] "Self-motivation" questions often mask a deeper issue of self-confidence
  and self-worth ("I'm not sure what I'm doing is worth anything"). Name the real
  question first. (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-07] Everybody wants more (faster car, bigger house, more money, the
  promotion) — wanting is universal and separates you from no one. The real question is:
  **"what am I willing to give up?"** Everyone has the same 24 hours / 7 days. (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-07] Sharie is optimizing for **short-term reward** (movies, games, hanging out)
  over long-term goals; that's the distraction everyone falls for. (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-07] "Life is long and the amount of time in which you have to achieve
  something is short" — a lesson from his father: work hard now and reap rewards the rest
  of your life; spread the work out and you wake up at 53 not far from where you started,
  disappointed. (paraphrase, attributed origin: his dad)
- [2018-10-07] He personally trades things he wants (e.g. gym time / a six-pack) for a
  bigger goal — to "impact the lives of a billion people." Sacrifice is the trade.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-07] Three tools offered: (1) enjoy the **process** — growth is the reward, not
  the destination; (2) **blend** your interests so activities aren't discrete
  work-vs-not-work segments; (3) it's a wake-up call — at ~23/30 you should get serious
  about how far you are from your goals. (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-07] He made heavy sacrifices in college — gave up going out, video games, comic
  books — for library / computer-lab / project time, so he could launch his career
  immediately after graduating at 21–22 while peers were "still figuring out their life."
  (paraphrase, self-reported)
- [2018-10-07] Claims he established **Blind** as a company by age 22 and hit **$2 million
  in billings in the first two years.** (paraphrase, self-reported — flag: needs
  registry/independent verification)
- [2018-10-07] If your career is in alignment with what makes you happy, you don't need to
  "unwind" — he comes home and doesn't have to decompress because he loves his life.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-07] Terry Crews (Men's Fitness interview) hates working out but loves the
  results; discipline is "a series of small commitments" — get dressed, drive to the gym,
  walk in, commit to just arms — and three hours later you're done. Embrace the grind;
  become "the ultimate learning machine." (paraphrase, attributed to Terry Crews)
- [2018-10-07] His own routine: come home, eat dinner, watch CNN while multitasking/reading
  messages, build up a "reservoir of energy and angst," then head to a home office and
  "start the second shift" until tired. Every single day. (paraphrase, self-reported)
- [2018-10-07] He's motivated by a **higher purpose**; he doesn't watch TV to watch TV —
  he studies how Anderson Cooper delivers news, lower-third graphics, commercials, to
  solve problems in his subconscious. (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-07] The key word is **convert**: turn thoughts/experiences into output (a note,
  a sketch, an article, a screen-grab) — consuming without converting builds nothing.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-07] (Attributes to Jim Rohn) "Wealth is your ability to convert knowledge and
  experience into equity and capital" — "convert" is the operative word. (quote of
  Chris paraphrasing Jim Rohn)
- [2018-10-07] (Attributes to Jim Rohn) It's important to have big goals, but less
  important to *achieve* them than to become the person you become in the pursuit.
  (paraphrase, attributed to Jim Rohn)
- [2018-10-07] Closing prescription to Sharie: find a higher purpose, blend your
  activities, find the energy within yourself, and — active or passive learning — you
  must convert. (paraphrase)

### Aaron ("Aaron Zak" — Futur team member, CONTEXT, not persona)

- [2018-10-07] Chris's mentorship ("brainwashing") rewired him: sitting watching TV now
  feels like wasting time, and working on a project — even one that "sucks" — feels like
  moving forward. The goal is to feel productive. (paraphrase — context speaker)

### Mark (Futur team member, CONTEXT, not persona)

- [2018-10-07] What works for him is **blending free time with things he likes** — he
  loves shooting/editing video, so he films friends' events and travel, blurring the line
  between hobby and work. (paraphrase — context speaker)

### Spencer (community member, CONTEXT, not persona)

- [2018-10-07] From studying psychology: the tactic of **just starting** — commit to
  reading only three pages; getting started makes continuing far easier than the
  imagined "insurmountable" version. (paraphrase — context speaker)
