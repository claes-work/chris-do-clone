## Summary

An in-house Futur editor shares three video-editing lessons learned on the job that
raised his editing from film-school habits to studio standard: (1) keep strict file
organization / naming hygiene, (2) open with a hook rather than a slow montage, and
(3) edit toward one clear goal. He credits a colleague ("Matthew and the team") for the
studio's organization discipline and cites Jaws and The Dark Knight as examples of
strong openings.

## Key claims (attributed to the unnamed Futur editor — CONTEXT, not Chris Do)

- [2018-10-08] At a studio you are expected to have "good hygiene"; a key form of this
  is good file organization so another editor never wastes time locating a misplaced
  file. Film-school habit of dumping all files into one folder is "chaos."
- [2018-10-08] Use a specific, consistent naming system for everything — "creative
  directors hate" disorganized/unnamed files.
- [2018-10-08] Credits "Matthew and the team" at The Futur as very good at organization;
  everything has its own bin/folder.
- [2018-10-08] Start a video by grabbing the audience's attention; avoid opening on a
  long stretch of "pretty shots" (cited a 44-second example of what not to do).
- [2018-10-08] A strong open shows a "mini movie" of what the piece is about — Jaws
  opens with a shark eating a person (teasing the whole film); The Dark Knight throws
  the viewer into a heist that subverts expectations, engaging from the first scene.
- [2018-10-08] Edit toward a clear goal: decide what question the video answers, lay it
  out (he uses sticky notes) to test flow, and cut anything that doesn't serve the goal.
- [2018-10-08] Recap: (1) get organized before starting, (2) have a solid opening to
  attract the audience, (3) have a clear goal so you know the video's point.
