# How To Not Feel Like An Imposter When Comparing Yourself To Others

## Summary

A conversational Q&A in which Chris Do coaches a young creator (Connor, 23, from
Southampton, UK) who feels crushed by comparing himself to social-media accounts that
"blow up out of nowhere." Chris reframes comparison as a tool rather than a threat: the
harm comes from comparing-to-measure-worth, while comparing-to-learn is productive. He
introduces two distinct devices — a **"selective filter"** that only admits inputs that
help him grow (and discards anything that just makes him feel bad), and the
**"knowledge vampire"** metaphor for extracting what a successful person does well at no
cost to them. He grounds the "curated lives" point with the Anthony Bourdain example and
closes with a personal $30,000 sales-rep-contract story to extend the reframe from social
media to any bad event: take responsibility, extract the lesson, move on. A second
participant testifies that the core takeaway they learned from Chris is *mental
discipline over one's own thoughts*.

## Key claims

### Chris Do
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) Humans are contextual learners — we can't judge whether
  something is small/hot/cold until we touch something more extreme — so comparison is
  natural, but social media makes it dangerously easy.
- [2018-10-10] (quote) "Comparison is the thief of joy." (Chris cites it as a quote he is
  passing on, not original to him.)
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) People live "highly curated lives" that don't reflect their
  true life — they show the cool places, not the crappy work or the struggles — so
  social media should be viewed through that lens: "in a way, it's not real. Only some of
  it's real."
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) Anthony Bourdain looked like he was on top of the world
  (respected chef, hit CNN show, admired) yet took his own life — proof there is a
  "deeper, darker side to each person" we don't see behind the curated image.
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) Comparing to measure your worth is a "dangerous game";
  comparing in order to learn is healthy — the difference is whether the comparison
  makes you grow or just puts you down.
- [2018-10-10] (quote) "I have a very selective filter when I look at the world. The
  filter only allows things to penetrate my brain that allow me to grow. If it makes me
  feel bad, I just throw it away. I have no time for that."
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) When looking at a creator with huge subscriber counts, he
  doesn't think "they got there fast and I'm nowhere near" — he asks "what are they doing
  right?" (personality? subject matter? prompting comments? asking for subscribes?) to
  extract and learn.
- [2018-10-10] (quote) "I'm like a knowledge vampire. I see something that somebody's
  doing well, and I just want to take it. The cool part is it hurts them none. It costs
  them nothing, and it benefits me."
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) Someone else would be happy with the results you already have
  — a 40- or 50-year-old looks at a 23-year-old's following and feels miserable — so
  comparison cuts both ways and is a losing game when used to judge worth.
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) An exercise: look for the positive even in the worst
  situation. Extend the reframe from social media to genuine adversity — when something
  bad happens, resist the victim frame ("why did this happen to me?") and switch to "what
  did I learn from this?"
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) Personal story: due to his own oversight (not confirming a
  contract's end in writing), a sales rep held him to another year and he had to pay
  ~$30,000 to exit. His coach reframed it: obsessing over it is an opportunity cost /
  drain — better to pay, move on, onboard a new client, and earn the money back than to
  pay and stay bitter (because then "you're still suffering"). He recast it as "a $30,000
  lesson" to always follow up in writing — a bitter pill worth swallowing versus calling
  the person evil and calling himself stupid on repeat.

### Second participant (testimony about Chris; not persona material)
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) What they learned from Chris is mental discipline over your
  thoughts — when you see someone better or start thinking "I suck," refuse the thought
  ("No, not going to do that") and redirect to something else.

### Connor / interviewer (context; not persona material)
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) Social media makes him feel "really good" when he judges his
  own content as deeper/more valuable, or "like trash" when comparison feeds self-doubt
  and lack of confidence to the point that he stops making anything.
- [2018-10-10] (paraphrase) The biggest downfall of curated social media is that everyone
  keeps one-upping each other and nobody enjoys the process or shares it — only the
  "best life."
