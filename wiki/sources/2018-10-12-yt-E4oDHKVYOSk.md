# How to Position Yourself as an Expert If you have no Experience

> Captions present (uploaded English transcript). NO-CAPTIONS marker not applicable.

## Summary

Chris Do fields questions from a group of young/aspiring designers (chiefly one asking
how to seem credible and charge high fees despite being ~17–18, plus a 23-year-old
starting a YouTube channel). His core answer: on the internet nobody can tell your age
or pedigree, so positioning as an expert comes down to putting good work out there —
"you do good work... whether you're 17 or 77 it doesn't matter." He reframes self-worth
as internal conviction ("I say I'm worth it — too bad you can't see it, I'll find
somebody who sees the worth"), and argues you should keep charging toward a number you
believe you're worth while building the skills, learning, and experiences that make
others see it too.

He counsels patience against the impulse to "hyper shortcut" 20 years of learning, but
says mentorship/courses/books can compress 20 years to ~6. He pushes designers to broaden
beyond craft into psychology, philosophy, anthropology, humanities, literature, mythology,
rhetoric so they can solve bigger-than-graphic problems (his "recovering graphic designer"
line). On positioning: get known for one thing (like "Connor" is known for logo design),
then flip the switch and migrate toward what you *want* to be known for. On being a young
content creator: your youth and honesty ARE the asset — audiences grow up alongside young
creators (MKBHD, Logan Paul, Casey Neistat, LinusTechTips), and vulnerability/transparency
("come along on the ride, we'll grow together") beats posturing as a finished expert. He
closes by naming four valuable career paths for beginners and telling young people to be
fearless.

  until somebody actually sees the same value that I see in myself." (paraphrase gloss:
  this requires acquiring greater skills, more learning, more experiences).
- `[2018-10-12]` (quote) "hi my name is Chris doe I'm a recovering graphic designer... I
  don't want to solve graphic design problems anymore because I'm capable of solving much
  bigger problem now."
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) Cites Brian Collins framing his work as an "experience
  design company" — solving problems via copywriting, image making, UI, whatever is
  needed, using the full extent of creativity.
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) The answer can't always be "you need a new logo"; you must
  understand the actual problem (e.g. a conversion / bounce-rate issue) and find an
  appropriate solution, even doing things you're not trained to do.
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) Advice to young designers: learn foundational craft and put
  in the 10,000 hours, but broaden into psychology, philosophy, anthropology, and the
  humanities, literature, mythology, rhetoric — those help more than more craftsmanship.
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) When asked what expertise he claims: "I'm an expert at
  solving problems for a lot of money."
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) You can't expert-at-everything yet; everyone starts
  somewhere. Don't be impatient. Mentorship, books, seminars, videos, courses can shave
  ~14 years off the learning curve (20 years down to ~6).
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) Positioning is a transition: get known for one thing (his
  example: "Connor" is known for logo design, so people call him for logos), keep
  developing your whole creative self, then "flip the switch" and migrate from what you're
  known for to what you want to be known for.
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) Self-disclosure: he only truly understood what branding
  meant and how to do it in the last five years; before that he was designing marks and
  identity systems, not doing branding — because branding is far more complicated.
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) He wouldn't be giving this advice as a 23-year-old — he's
  "double your age" and has the life experiences to reflect on.
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) Four valuable beginner career paths to focus on instead of
  competing on Fiverr/Upwork/99designs: (1) UX design, (2) UI design, (3) social media /
  content marketing, (4) building impactful websites.
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) A young creator isn't telling people what to think — just
  telling their story from their perspective, and that's valuable.
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) On YouTube he's "the anomaly"; the platform is full of young
  people sharing their story, and the community grows up with them (cites MKBHD/Marques
  Brownlee, Logan Paul, Casey Neistat, LinusTechTips who started young).
- `[2018-10-12]` (quote) Recommended positioning for beginners: "just say like I'm still
  figuring it out I'm not sure what it is I'm supposed to be doing but here's what I
  figured out so far if this helps you keep watching stay along with me come along on the
  ride and we'll grow together."
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) Vulnerability makes you relatable; real-time honest drama
  (struggle then breakthrough) would out-perform a channel that just presents finished
  accomplishments. Don't be afraid to produce a video — be honest and transparent, that's
  what people want.
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) Self-aware weakness anecdote (Philippines talk): asked his
  wife his weakness; she said "he believes he doesn't have any" — maybe that (not
  recognizing your own weakness) is his weakness.
- `[2018-10-12]` (paraphrase) Young people should be fearless, throw caution to the wind,
  and try everything because they have little to lose and recover fast — "don't waste your
  youth." Turn "being young" from a negative into a positive.
- `[2018-10-12]` (quote) "I am your big brother you guys I'm here to help you I'm here to
  share... I'm actually here to help you."
