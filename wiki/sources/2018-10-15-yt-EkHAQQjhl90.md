# Mindset Shift: Long vs Short Term Goals

> Source: YouTube video "Mindset Shift: Long vs Short Term Goals" — The Futur (@thefutur), published 2018-10-15.
> Format: informal Q&A / office coaching session. Chris Do answers questions from team members and community members about how he developed and maintains a long-term mindset.

## Summary

An unscripted coaching conversation. A young community member (referred to as **Connor**) opens by asking Chris Do when he realized a long-term mindset mattered and whether it was hard to build. Chris answers with his origin story: an undisciplined, highly emotional teenager who hit "emotional rock bottom" at 17–18, and from that dark place decided who he wanted to become and "flipped a switch" toward long-term focus and short-term sacrifice.

A second participant (referred to as **Aaron**, a team member/coachee) shares his own rock-bottom experience (~age 20–21) and asks how Chris has *maintained* focus over decades. Chris answers with several recurring frameworks: the "slay the wolf" metaphor (from the movie *300*) for extracting the full lesson from a hard experience; being "addicted to success"; out-hustling more talented people; celebrating internal wins (audience-retention example); divorcing himself from his emotions (his admiration of Spock); and a closing "life hack" — a daily gratitude practice — to retrain the brain toward positive self-talk.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (SUBJECT — persona-eligible)

- [2018-10-15] In high school he was not disciplined or focused; he "rode the emotional roller coaster" of highs and lows and struggled through relationships. (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-15] School came easily to him, so he under-invested — drawing, reading comics, and playing video games instead of studying, to his parents' dismay. (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-15] His parents' blueprint for success was a 4.0 GPA and a good college; despite his dad's encouragement and lectures, he "couldn't get it together." (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-15] He hit an emotional rock bottom around age 17–18 and, in that dark place, decided what kind of person he wanted to become — a turning point he says teenagers are especially prone to making in emotional extremes. (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-15] The core realization: "I am wasting this opportunity… you're young once and your life is less complicated when you're young." A sense of urgency ("I'm in my Prime right now") made him flip a switch toward long-term goals and short-term sacrifice. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2018-10-15] He believes the result "was going to wait for me" if he worked toward it — the basis for making large short-term sacrifices. (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-15] "Slay the wolf" framework (from the movie *300* / Spartan coming-of-age): survive a hard situation AND extract the full lesson, or the problem recurs. He diagnoses Aaron as having "survived but maybe you didn't slay the wolf." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2018-10-15] He is "all about squeezing the maximum learning from a situation so I don't repeat it again" — cites the "study history so as not to repeat it" idea. (quote + paraphrase)
- [2018-10-15] He has an "addictive personality and nothing is as addictive to me as success"; small sacrifices that yield short-term gains reinforce the behavior. (quote)
- [2018-10-15] In college he stayed in the computer lab/library and did extra work for professors while peers went out; he could see he was "moving faster and further than most people," which confirmed he was on the right path. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2018-10-15] By his second year, classmates who took the identical classes were asking him for help; he attributes the gap to their not being "fully committed mind body and spirit and soul into learning." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2018-10-15] Self-positioning: "I might not be as talented… as good-look[ing]… as articulate as the next person but man I am going to out hustle you… I'm going to learn and I will not stop." (quote)
- [2018-10-15] On celebrating wins: there should be no cap on internally celebrating accomplishments; the line is between private self-recognition (fine) and outward boasting ("I'm the baddest mofo in the room" — "that's not confidence"). (paraphrase + quote)
- [2018-10-15] Self-description: "I love myself, I love everything I do, I love my life, I love my wife… and things that I don't love I just get rid of." (quote)
- [2018-10-15] "Those dark dark thoughts" ("you suck, you should quit") no longer enter his mind — the "shield / wall has been so fortified" that they can't get through; but this must be built "one step at a time." (quote + paraphrase)
- [2018-10-15] As a teenager he constantly compared himself as "less than" (parents not rich, no nice clothes, not popular) and "just wanted to disappear and be invisible." (paraphrase)
- [2018-10-15] He chose to "trade the high highs and the low lows to be somewhere in the middle" — trading emotional volatility for stability. (quote)
- [2018-10-15] His childhood hero was Spock; he admired running life through logic ("the needs of the many outweigh the needs of the few") and wanted to "strip away the emotional part and just do it." (His therapist called Spock "a tragic character.") (paraphrase + quote)
- [2018-10-15] Reframe negative self-talk ("you're not good enough, nobody likes you") into positive self-talk ("I am valuable, the things I create few can do, clients are lucky to work with me"). (quote)
- [2018-10-15] **Gratitude "life hack":** start and end each day being grateful for what you already have — nightly, write ~4–5 things you're grateful for; better still, tell the people you're grateful for. A prompt version: write 3 things you're grateful for, 1 person you're grateful for, and 1 positive lesson learned today. This trains the brain to seek positive things. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2018-10-15] Gratitude is "the one thing you give to somebody that you get more in the act of giving" — the giver often feels better than the receiver; people who thank him for his content, he suspects, feel even better than he does. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2018-10-15] Endorses Aaron's audience-retention reframe: judge a video by what you set out to accomplish and by retention, not raw view count — a "grateful mindset" of appreciation. (paraphrase)
