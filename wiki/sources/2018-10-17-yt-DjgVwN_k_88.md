# How to Negotiate a Lowball Offer

## Summary

Short clip in which **Chris Do** teaches how to handle a client's lowball offer, built entirely around the concept of **anchoring** — the idea that whoever states a number first sets the mental reference point the rest of the negotiation is judged against. Chris opens by challenging the common belief (the "car dealership" model) that you should let the other side name a price first so you can lowball them; he says a book he read convinced him the opposite is true — whoever says the number first wins.

He runs a live **role-play with an audience member** to demonstrate. Playing a "business coaching 101" seller, he announces he "charges $1,000 an hour" and then asks the participant how much they can afford; the participant, anchored by the $1,000 figure, feels their own intended number (e.g. $400) is now too small to say and answers "zero." Chris uses this to prove the anchor's psychological effect in real time.

He then tells a **first-person story of a client who anchored him**: the client claimed a small budget ("ten"), Chris's colleague **Ben Burns** urged him to ask for 15, Chris initially deferred to the low figure, but after the anchor's effect "wore off" he ignored it, dropped a new anchor of 30, and after some back-and-forth the client came back saying they'd "make the money work." The lesson: don't get stuck on the client's number — cut off their anchor, drop your own, and if needed make the client negotiate against themselves.

## Key claims

All dated 2018-10-17. Paraphrased unless quoted. Attributed to **Chris Do** unless noted; role-play lines noted as such.

1. **Anchoring runs a negotiation.** Whoever states a number first sets the reference point; the other party then mentally evaluates every subsequent number relative to that first number. (Chris Do)
2. **The "say it first" rule is counter-intuitive.** The common car-dealership teaching is to let the other side name a price first so you can lowball them; Chris says a book he read convinced him this is backwards — whoever says the number first wins. (Chris Do; the specific book is not named — attribution: uncertain on title)
3. **Start high.** Chris says an early project of his was underpriced; when he asked a mentor how much he should have charged, the mentor said "double that," and Chris agreed it was justified by the amount of work — so his rule going forward is to start high. (Chris Do)
4. **Anchoring demonstrated live (role-play).** By announcing a $1,000/hour rate before asking what the participant can afford, Chris makes the participant unwilling to name their own (lower) number; the $1,000 anchor makes any smaller figure feel embarrassing to say, so they answer "zero." (Chris Do, seller role / audience participant)
5. **A high rate also signals worth.** The participant reasons that if Chris charges $1,000 he "must be worth a thousand," which reframes the whole exchange. (audience participant, in role-play)
6. **Clients will anchor you low.** Chris recounts a client claiming a tiny budget ("ten") to pull Chris's price down; his colleague Ben Burns advised asking for 15. (Chris Do; Ben Burns attributed)
7. **Let the anchor wear off, then re-anchor.** After going home and realizing he'd "fallen for" the client's anchor, Chris waited, ignored the client's number, and sent a new offer: he could help them find someone for 10, but if they wanted Chris himself it would be 30. (Chris Do)
8. **Deprioritizing / walking away can flip the client.** Once Chris accepted the low-priority "I'll just refer you to someone" path, the client re-engaged on their own — "we totally want to work with you, we'll make the money work, let's get creative." (Chris Do, recounting client)
9. **Heuristics keep the first number sticky.** Chris frames anchoring as a heuristic — a mental shorthand — which is why whatever is said first is what people get stuck on. (Chris Do)
10. **Counter-strategy when a client lowballs you: ignore their number and make them negotiate against themselves.** Say "that's not going to work, can you come up with another number?" — because if you engage their low number you get stuck on it, so state your own number first. (Chris Do)
