## Summary

An episode of "The One-Sheet," a Futur mini-series that fuses movie fandom with
design analysis. Hosts Stuart Schuster (film) and Ben Burns (design) break down the
1993 *Jurassic Park* theatrical one-sheet poster. The discussion centers on why the
minimalist logo-on-black poster works: sheer simplicity that stands out on a crowded
theater wall, symmetry used to create tension/conflict, and clear visual hierarchy
that forces the eye to the logo. The hosts highlight the poster as the first notable
use of "meta branding" — the in-world park logo (stamped on lunchboxes, jeeps,
banners in the film) becoming the film's own advertising and merchandise engine.
They contrast it unfavorably with the busier 20th-anniversary re-release poster,
then close with movie trivia and a knowledge-check question to viewers.

## Key claims (grouped by speaker)

### Ben Burns (design host — NOT the subject)
- `[2018-10-22]` The poster's compositional simplicity is why it "popped off the wall"
  next to other early-90s films. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-10-22]` Designers use symmetry to communicate conflict — the eye can't settle
  when everything is perfectly centered (cites the symmetrical Darth Vader stairwell
  shot in Star Wars as an example). (paraphrase)
- `[2018-10-22]` Visual hierarchy is "super clear": the logo dominates, everything else
  is secondary. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-10-22]` Strong red-on-black sets the movie's tone — crimson reads as
  anger/blood, juxtaposed against a black "canvas of the unknown." (paraphrase)
- `[2018-10-22]` In the logo, the palm trees were drawn very small to emphasize the
  scale of the T-rex — a hierarchy/context device. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-10-22]` The film revolutionized CG in features, yet the poster deliberately
  shows none of it — restraint that "speaks volumes." (paraphrase)

### Stuart Schuster (film host — NOT the subject)
- `[2018-10-22]` Could not find a credited designer for the original poster in research;
  landing the logo as the primary ad was an executive decision by Steven Spielberg. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-10-22]` The way the park is advertised inside the movie is the way the movie
  itself was advertised — "a flash of genius." (paraphrase)
- `[2018-10-22]` This was the first use of "meta branding," where the film's logo also
  exists inside the story world as the park's logo — which paid off in merchandise. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-10-22]` The 20th-anniversary re-release poster went the opposite way (gates,
  jeep, jungle, backlight) and would have been a less effective launch poster than the
  bare logo. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All quotes below are from the Futur hosts, NOT Chris Do. Not persona/voice training data.

- Ben Burns: "compositionally it is so simple that when this was in theaters next to
  all the other early 90s flicks I think that this probably popped off of that wall
  just due to its sheer simplicity."
- Ben Burns: "a lot of designers and a lot of visual creators use symmetry to
  communicate conflict."
- Ben Burns: "the hierarchy is so super clear on this — you want to look at that logo
  all day long and everything else is secondary."
- Stuart Schuster: "the way that people advertise the park in the movie is the way that
  the movie is advertised, and I think that's sort of like a flash of genius."
- Stuart Schuster: "this was actually the first time that they've used this concept of
  meta branding where the park in the film — this was the park's logo."
- Ben Burns (on minimalist decision-making): "there's like that one dude in the
  conference room who's like 'put it on black, we're done, have a nice weekend
  everybody.'"
