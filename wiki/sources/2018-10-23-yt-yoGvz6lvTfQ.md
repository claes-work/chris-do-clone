# You're charging too little — Why You're Angry When Clients Call

## Summary

Short clip in which **Chris Do** delivers a single-speaker teaching segment on the emotional cost of underpricing. His central claim: the dread or irritation you feel when a client calls is a **pricing signal** — if you resent a client's demands (revisions, hand-holding, customer service), it is usually because **you charged too little**, not because the client is unreasonable.

Chris frames the client relationship as a power negotiation: the client has power, but not *all* the power — "we have as much power as you're willing to give to them." He argues that concerns about schedule, budget, or tricky scope must be **addressed upfront**, so both sides can "act like adults and professionals and run a real business," rather than griping about it after the fact.

He cites a line from **Blair Enns'** *Win Without Pitching Manifesto*: designers underbid projects and then resent the resulting client. The remedy is proper pricing — at a high enough fee, the burden becomes worth it ("if a client's gonna pay me a million dollars you can call me as much as you want"). He closes by extending the same "have the hard conversation upfront" logic to **partnerships and exit scenarios**: go in with optimism, but discuss how you'll separate before it happens, because "eventually partnerships do fall apart — nothing is forever."
