# Ask More Questions Until The Problem or Solution Becomes Clear

## Summary

A short teaching monologue by Chris Do (delivered to a live audience/workshop) on how
he demonstrates value to a client through questioning rather than answering. The core
move: when a client asks a question, resist the reflex to answer — instead "hold a
mirror up," invert the question, and reflect it back with another question until the
client reaches clarity on their own. Chris frames this as "I don't sell you my idea, I
sell you your idea": by asking progressively deeper questions, the client concludes you
understand their business deeply, when in fact you only asked questions until they
articulated it themselves.

He grounds the technique in a psychology thesis: ideas that are not our own "sound
foreign," and foreign ideas are resisted like an invader — the same way a new
song/book/movie needs repeat exposure before we accept it, and the same way people
reject being told what to do by a spouse or friend. Because we can solve others'
problems but not our own (we lack perspective, objectivity, and are driven by emotion),
the questioner's job is to ask until the problem becomes clear — and only then move to
whether it's a problem "worth solving," which opens the transition to talking about
what solving it is worth (i.e., price/engagement).

The video includes a brief improvised role-play (a "Mary" scenario) illustrating how an
invited dialogue of questions leads someone to their own conclusion ("maybe I need to
make it bigger") rather than resisting an instruction. "Mary" here is a hypothetical
example, not a real named person — context only.
