# How I Got Started as a Designer — Will Paterson

## Summary

An impromptu on-the-floor interview recorded at Adobe MAX 2018. Chris Do runs into
**Will Paterson** — a UK-based, self-taught graphic designer, lettering artist, and
YouTuber (logo design, lettering, design critiques) — and pulls him on camera as a
"special guest." The conversation is **Will's origin story**: how a self-taught
designer from a small town got started, built an audience on YouTube, and wrestles
with imposter syndrome.

**Attribution note:** This is Will Paterson's biography, elicited by Chris interviewing.
Will's life facts and statements are **context only** and must not enter Chris Do's
persona. The persona value here is in **Chris's interview framing, the principles he
teaches off the back of Will's answers, and the encouragement/advice he gives** — those
are Chris Do voice/beliefs data.

## Key claims

_All dated 2018-11-01. Paraphrased unless quoted. Speaker attributed._

**Will Paterson (CONTEXT — not Chris persona):**
- Will is completely self-taught with no formal design education; describes design as an
  ongoing learning process and says he is still learning (including from Chris's videos).
- ~6–7 years ago, when Will couldn't find a YouTube video/tutorial answering a technical
  question (e.g. scaling type without distorting it), he would work it out himself and
  then make a video teaching it — his content system started as helping other people like
  him, and gave him something to do when he had no clients.
- Will reasoned that his face in a small town would only attract small local clients (a
  few hundred pounds), whereas going online offered "endless opportunities" to be seen,
  teach, build an audience, and establish authority.
- Will still has imposter syndrome; it flares most when good things happen (e.g. landing a
  client he sees as big) — he feels he'll "be found out," then it works out and reinforces
  the fear. He copes by "riding the wave" and reminding himself it's about the work, not a
  piece of paper.
- Will frames design as a **journey with milestones, not a peak** — his first milestone was
  his first paycheck; he's still grateful to make money and doesn't see himself as
  exceptional, "just some guy on the internet that makes videos about design."
- Will attributes his path to his Christian faith: he failed high school, briefly studied
  what he thought was business at a technical college (turned out to be secretarial
  training), then at ~16 saw a documentary about design and felt called to it. He believes
  God told him to pursue it.
- Will did not go to university (says he couldn't get in), took out a loan, and spent ~a
  year studying design 8–10 hours a day; he is dyslexic and relied heavily on audiobooks.
- Will's parents didn't believe anything would come of it but let him try; things fell into
  place — client work, then YouTube growth (recalls hitting ~10 subscribers) — until he was
  being provided for monthly. His parents still don't understand how he makes money.
- Will notes designer friends he considered more skilled than him weren't getting work.

**Chris Do (PERSONA — framing, principles, advice):**
- Chris opens by praising Will for building "a career in making" AND "a career in community
  building," and frames Will as an example of what Chris "encourages our audience to do all
  the time." (persona: content/personal-brand advocacy)
- Chris distills Will's start into **two reasons to start now**: (1) not finding an answer
  yourself is a signal others want that answer too — a content prompt; (2) getting online
  overcomes the geographic limits of a small town and exposes you to more people.
- **Advice on imposter syndrome / pricing self-worth:** clients don't have much free time,
  so the fact that they reached out, emailed, and sought you out already qualifies you —
  and when you send a number and they agree, they're fine with it, so you should "believe
  what they believe" and see yourself through their eyes.
- Chris frames imposter syndrome as "one of the biggest killers" of furthering success
  because you put roadblocks in front of yourself; the fix is to ignore it or surround
  yourself with people who'll prove you're not an impostor.
- **The mountain metaphor (mindset):** there is no final peak; when you climb the mountain
  it's just the base of another mountain. Learn to enjoy the process and the journey;
  don't beat yourself up — any given point is "just a timestamp." Enjoy the growth instead.
- Chris frames the creative profession as inherently fraught: "we're always in doubt, we
  need that assurance," whether self-taught or formally educated — so the advice is to
  enjoy the process and be a happier, healthier person.
- **On being known (personal branding / getting work):** the work needs to be good — there's
  a base level, but beyond that most "normal people" can't tell the difference between good
  and amazing (only designers can). What set Will apart is that he **made himself known,
  told his story consistently, and ran the risk of exposure** — "people can only hire people
  that they know." You can be "the world's best kept secret" as a designer and get no work.
- Chris humorously guards against misreading the message — he explicitly does NOT want the
  takeaway to be "quit school, believe in God, and knock on Will's door"; the real point is
  Will found his own answer.
- Chris gives Will's parents credit for letting him try when other parents might have
  forbidden or disowned him.
