## Summary

A ~2.5-minute scripted role-play demonstrating how a designer should respond when a
client, mid-project, tries to swap in a brand-new logo direction (a napkin-sketch they
"doodled yesterday") during the final revision round. The designer stays non-defensive
and does not judge the sketch. Instead he: (1) redirects to the project's goals and
objectives ("does this address the goal of setting you apart?"), (2) makes the tradeoff
explicit — the last round was reserved to tighten and finish, so exploring a new
direction consumes that time and risks not landing by the deadline, (3) frames it as an
**overage / change order** with a ballpark cost (~$2,000–$2,500) rather than a free
extra, (4) refuses to commit a price on the spot ("I need time to process… I'll bring it
to my team"), and (5) keeps momentum with a next step ("let's think overnight, I'll send
something in the morning"). The closing reflection states the designer's stance: the job
is to solve the client's business problem, not to protect a portfolio piece — "they're
not paying me to be an artist, they're paying me to be a designer, a problem solver."

The framework (address goals → surface time/deadline tradeoff → convert scope change into
a priced change order → buy time to scope → set next step) is a clean, reusable
client-management pattern. It is captured here as **context** (Matthew Encina / thefutur
house method), not as SUBJECT persona material.

## Key claims

### Designer (role-play; likely Matthew Encina — attribution: uncertain, NOT subject)

- `[2018-11-12]` (paraphrase) Before reacting to a client's new direction, re-anchor on
  the project's goals and objectives, and ask whether the new idea actually serves them.
- `[2018-11-12]` (paraphrase) Don't judge or shoot down the client's sketch; if the person
  paying believes it solves their problem, don't fight that.
- `[2018-11-12]` (paraphrase) Make the tradeoff explicit: the final round was scoped to
  tighten and finish; spending it exploring a new direction risks not reaching the end
  goal by the deadline.
- `[2018-11-12]` (paraphrase) A new direction late in the engagement is an **overage /
  additional round** — a change order that costs more time and money (ballpark
  ~$2,000–$2,500 here), not a free extra.
- `[2018-11-12]` (paraphrase) Don't quote a firm price reflexively; say you need time to
  process and will bring it to the team, then return with a considered number.
- `[2018-11-12]` (paraphrase) Keep control of the clock and set a concrete next step
  ("think overnight, I'll send something in the morning") so the project doesn't drift.
- `[2018-11-12]` (paraphrase) The designer's job is to solve the client's business problem,
  not to make a cool portfolio piece — "they're paying me to be a designer, a problem
  solver."

### Client (role-play, context — not a real person)

- `[2018-11-12]` (paraphrase) Wants to pursue a self-made sketch because "it means
  something to me" and feels it fits the brand; the stated project goal is to "set us
  apart from the competition."
