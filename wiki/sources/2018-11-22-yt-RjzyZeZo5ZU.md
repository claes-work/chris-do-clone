# UX Design Process — First Questions to Ask (The Futur, 2018-11-22)

## Summary

A short instructional segment on the opening questions to ask at the start of a UX
project. The instructor frames two "cornerstone" questions — (1) *what makes / should
make our users feel good?* and (2) *what variables can we control?* — and works through
the sub-questions needed to answer them: who you are designing for (user archetypes /
profiles), the user's goal and motivation, the tools the user needs, and the stakes if
the user fails. The second cornerstone is explained via a "six senses" model (visuals,
sounds, smells, tastes, touch, plus a sixth channel the speaker calls "message" or
"vibe"), and the principle that a designer controls their *output* but not the user's
*input*, which is filtered through the user's bias, ability, and comprehension to
produce a received message and an emotional response.

## Key claims (non-Chris instructor)

- `[2018-11-22]` Strategy and discovery are a *must* because the opening question shifts
  from "what makes our users feel good" (a guess) to "what *should* make our users feel
  good." (paraphrase)
- `[2018-11-22]` Before building a user profile you must know who you are designing for;
  base all design decisions on a user archetype — the same artifact designed for a
  puzzle enthusiast vs. a four-year-old yields completely different decisions.
  (paraphrase)
- `[2018-11-22]` Four discovery questions about the user: what is the user's goal in this
  interaction; why do they want to accomplish it; what tools do they need (e.g. an audio
  UI for a visually challenged user); and what is at stake if they fail — the stakes
  indicate how deeply you need to test. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-11-22]` The second cornerstone question — "what variables can we control?" —
  reduces to "six senses": visuals, sounds, smells, tastes, touch, plus a sixth channel
  the speaker calls "message" or "vibe" (the overall feeling). (paraphrase)
- `[2018-11-22]` You control your output but not the user's input; output is filtered
  through the user's bias, ability, and comprehension, producing two results: the
  *message received* and the *emotional response*. (paraphrase)
