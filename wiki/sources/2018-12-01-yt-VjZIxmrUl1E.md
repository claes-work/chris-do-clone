# How I Started My Business — Joey Cofone of Baron Fig, Pt. 1/2

## Summary

Part 1 of a 2-part interview on The Futur, hosted by **Chris Do**, with guest
**Joey Cofone**, co-founder of stationery brand **Baron Fig** (New York City,
founded 2013). This is **Joey's origin story** — Chris is the interviewer, drawing
it out. Joey recounts noticing at art school (School of Visual Arts) that design
students all used the same laptops but wildly different notebooks, spending three
years mulling the problem, then quitting freelancing to design the "Confidant"
notebook, which raised ~$168K on Kickstarter against a $15K goal. The conversation
turns personal: Joey's early hardship (loss of his mother as a child, being adopted,
being put out of the house at 18, sleeping in his car and on couches) and how that
shaped his relationship to risk. Chris reflects with his own framing on risk,
resilience, and — briefly — his own background and parenting worries.

**Attribution note:** Nearly all biographical content here belongs to **Joey Cofone
(context, NOT Chris Do persona data)**. The persona value is in **Chris Do's
questions, framing, reactions, and reflections**. Part 2 is a separate (later)
ledger item.

## Key claims

All dated **2018-12-01** (date of this interview). Paraphrased unless quoted below.

**Joey Cofone (CONTEXT — do NOT route into Chris's biography/persona):**
- Baron Fig was founded in 2013 and (as of recording) is still operating; Joey
  co-founded it with Adam Cornfield.
- At SVA, Joey observed design students used two tools — a laptop and a notebook —
  and while laptops were identical, notebooks varied wildly by size, type, and brand;
  this became the product insight.
- Joey emailed 500+ "thinkers" worldwide (designers, writers, illustrators,
  architects, even accountants) asking one question: what do you like in a
  sketchbook/notebook? He had 400+ conversations and used that to design the notebook.
- The idea sat in his head ~3 years, followed by ~5 months of intense design.
- The Kickstarter sought $15,000 for a first production run and raised ~$168,000 in
  30 days (~10,000 notebooks), enough to start the company the next year.
- Joey first studied literature and philosophy, graduating into the 2008 financial
  crisis, then returned to art school (SVA) for four more years, graduating 2012;
  freelanced ~1 year before the 2013 launch. He was 25 at launch.
- Joey earlier turned down a full-time agency job offer because it felt "too easy,"
  against advice from family and SVA's design dean; freelancing then scaled from a
  $200/6-week first project to $4,000/week within a year.
- Joey's view on risk: fewer things in life are truly risky than we believe; worst
  case (can't pay rent/feed himself) is what friends and family are for, so that fear
  shouldn't stop action. He also cites having "seen what bad is" — hardship — as the
  source of his risk tolerance.
- Personal history (context only): mother had cancer and died when he was ~13;
  adopted (second time by his grandfather Lou/Luigi, a master plumber, and step-
  grandmother); grandfather died when Joey was 17; at 18 the step-grandmother told him
  it "wasn't working out" and he had to leave; he slept in his car and on couches
  before a cousin (a fireman) took him in. He now regards his wife's parents as his
  mom and dad; married in June 2018.
- Brand philosophy: Baron Fig treats the notebook not as a commodity but as a
  "ritual" / "sacred space," explicitly analogizing to how Starbucks reframed coffee
  (a way of life / ritual) versus Dunkin' Donuts (a commodity).
- First year of the business: goal was simply to survive (aware ~80% of startups
  fail early); he and Adam initially planned to ship notebooks themselves from a
  studio and signed a ~2-year lease, but a fellow Kickstarter veteran warned them off
  self-fulfillment, so they switched to a fulfillment center; the two of them did
  everything (social, marketing, writing, production design, customer service) from
  two tiny desks.

**Chris Do (PERSONA — his framing / reactions / advice):**
- Chris frames the central interview question: quitting client work with "really no
  validation" beyond informal conversations is a gutsy move, and warns that social-
  media enthusiasm ("love the idea") often evaporates when a product actually ships.
- Chris references a *Time* magazine article on people who grow up with very little:
  two paths — many break and need lots of help/therapy, while some "galvanize and
  become really strong" — placing Joey in the resilient latter group.
- Chris shares his own framing (context about Chris, persona-relevant): as a
  first-generation refugee to America he grew up with "not a lot," and once believed
  that hard environment made him who he is.
- Chris voices a genuine parenting tension (persona voice): his kids grow up with
  more than he ever had (upper-class neighborhood, private schools), and he wonders
  whether comfort robs them of the chance to become tough — while acknowledging he
  can't yet know the outcome.
- Chris observes a psychological pattern: some people fear success itself — "you want
  to succeed but you don't want to succeed too much" — and that big, realized wins
  can feel scary.
- Chris reframes Joey's brand story approvingly: the notebook becomes "part of an
  experience," and rituals matter greatly to brands with strong followings.
- Chris probes the core strategic question — do customers buy the product as a
  commodity, or are they buying the story/philosophy? — surfacing Joey's brand-vs-
  commodity distinction.
