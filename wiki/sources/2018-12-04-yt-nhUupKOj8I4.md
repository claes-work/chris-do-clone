# Come up with Better Ideas – Unlock The Power Of Your Subconscious

> ★ **L3-candidate** — This is a self-contained, step-by-step ideation framework
> ("subconscious incubation" method) that Chris presents as his personal 20+ year
> creative process. It is distinct enough to warrant a dedicated `wiki/topics/`
> ideation page and likely a `persona/beliefs.md` entry on his creative method.

## Summary

Chris Do walks through his personal creative-ideation process for arriving at strong
design ideas, framed as "activating the subconscious." Speaking (apparently to a client
in a coaching/dialogue setting), he explains a repeatable sequence: (1) force clarity
on what the client actually wants by making them articulate it multiple times and
building shared consensus; (2) reduce the problem down to two or three very specific
key words; (3) saturate the brain with tangentially related external stimulation — art,
music, literature, philosophy, images, videos — filtered through those key words;
(4) deliberately stop and take a break so the subconscious can process, because it is
"much smarter than my conscious brain"; (5) prime the mind right before sleep by
reflecting on the problem, then let answers surface overnight, in the shower, or on a
walk/run (autonomic, low-effort activities); and (6) always capture ideas immediately
with a notebook/pen (even waterproof paper in the shower), because ideas are fleeting
"like cotton candy." He cites the author of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde (Robert Louis
Stevenson), Einstein, and Thomas Jefferson as historical users of subconscious
incubation.

A distinctive sub-thread: the clarity-building phase is also **client onboarding** —
by asking questions and diagramming, Chris makes the client co-author the answer so
that when he reveals it, they have "already bought into it."

Single speaker throughout (Chris Do). A second person, "Donavan," is referenced as
present in the dialogue but does not speak in the transcript.

## Key claims

### Chris Do
- [2018-12-04] Every well-known great mind — Einstein, Thomas Jefferson, and others —
  has used some form of activating the subconscious to achieve things they never thought
  possible. (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] After two decades of work, he now knows what he needs to do to reliably
  "arrive at a magical place" and has a shareable process. (paraphrase, self-reported)
- [2018-12-04] Step 1 — Force clarity: make the client articulate what they want, asking
  them to repeat it; by the third time they state it most clearly. Then ask more
  questions and build consensus so everyone shares one understanding of the problem.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] Asking the client more questions gives clarity not just to Chris but to
  the client — they begin to understand what the answer will look like too. (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] The clarity phase is also onboarding: by bringing the client along and
  making the decision together (including diagramming/visualizing it), when Chris reveals
  the answer the client has "already bought into it." (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] Step 2 — Reduce the problem down to a few very specific key words (two or
  three); he does not accept just any words. (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] Step 3 — Saturate the brain with as much external stimulation as possible
  (art, music, literature, philosophy, static images, movies, photography), all filtered
  through the two or three key words. (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] He gives himself internal prompts to connect abstract ideas — e.g. is
  there a life anecdote, a literary reference, a childhood experience, a current problem
  someone else is dealing with — and lets his mind wander as far from the key words as
  possible. (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] Step 4 — Take a break: stop consciously thinking about the problem so the
  brain can process on a subconscious level, "because it's much smarter than my conscious
  brain." (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] Step 5 — Prime before sleep: right before bed, reflect on the day, the
  stimulation consumed, and the three words/problem, then go to sleep; answers often
  arrive overnight, upon waking, or in the shower. (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] Ideas surface during autonomic, low-effort activities (showering,
  breathing, a leisurely walk or run) because they don't require much conscious effort.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] Step 6 — Always capture: keep a notebook and pen (even waterproof paper in
  the shower) handy, because ideas are fleeting; writing them down makes them concrete.
  (paraphrase)
- [2018-12-04] "Almost all of my problems have been resolved this way," and many highly
  productive people use the exact same technique. (paraphrase, self-reported)
- [2018-12-04] The author of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde (Robert Louis Stevenson, unnamed in
  the video) would literally instruct his subconscious to write the next story overnight
  while he slept, then wake and capture the dreamed stories. (paraphrase; historical
  anecdote, attribution to the author is Chris's claim)
