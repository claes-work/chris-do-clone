## Summary

Short teaching clip on The Futur (@thefutur). **Chris Do** runs a live Q&A / role-play with a small group of students on the question *"How do we get a mentor to spend time with you?"*

He rejects the students' first instincts (flattery, praise) and steers them toward a **value exchange**: the mentor has time; you must offer value in return. From there he builds a tactical sequence. First, **make a small ask** — request 15 minutes rather than an hour, because the entire objective is to get them to say *yes* (a huge ask like "24 hours of your time" or "go on a road trip" gets an automatic no; "can I buy you a cup of coffee?" gets a yes). Second, he introduces **scaffolding**: you don't start at the top (a mentor giving you all their time); you start with something very small and **trade up each time**, like climbing a ladder — the same principle as why a client won't hand you $30,000 for a logo cold.

He then teaches **valuing the exchange**: price the mentor's hour (his example: **$1,000/hour**, "that's how much I charge"), break it into quarters, and recognize that asking for 15 minutes is like asking for ~$250 of value — so you must deliver **more** value than that to make it an easy yes. He generalizes into a mini-lesson on **commerce and value systems** (a watch, shoes, a Louis Vuitton bag — a deal happens only when each party values what they receive more than what they give), and stresses you must **get into the person's head** to learn what *they* find valuable, because everyone's value system differs.

The clip ends with a **role-play**: a student ("Anastasia") approaches Chris as if after a talk and delivers a near-perfect ask — she doesn't assume what he'd value, brackets her request ("10–15 minutes"), and offers to earn the time (coffee or work). Chris praises it as a model example and unpacks exactly why it worked.
