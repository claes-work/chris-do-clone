## Summary

A short roleplay clip in which Chris Do plays a designer negotiating with a client (played by a coaching participant addressed as "Jung" — context person, not the subject). The client wants to know the designer's hourly rate to understand where an $18,000 logo fee is going. Chris uses Socratic questioning to dismantle hourly billing: he asks why the client wants the rate, then walks the client through the internal contradiction of hourly pricing — that it punishes speed and skill, rewards slowness, and lets the client "have it both ways" (demand hours but cap them). He reframes the fee around outcome and value: a logo delivered in five minutes is worth *more*, not less, because it saves the client time and lets them go to market immediately. He closes by pricing the work on the value it delivers today ($18k) versus later ($26k in a year), asserting that the value of good work appreciates.

Speaker note: All substantive pricing arguments are Chris Do's, delivered in-character as the designer. The client's short replies ("Yea", "Sure", "It's worth more!") are the coaching participant playing the client — context, not persona material.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (subject)

- `[2018-12-26]` When a client asks for your hourly rate, ask them *why* they want to know it before answering. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-12-26]` Hourly billing is internally inconsistent: if working fewer hours means charging less, then working more hours must mean charging more — the client cannot demand an hour count and simultaneously cap it. "It has to have some symmetry to the logic." (paraphrase + quote)
- `[2018-12-26]` Charging by the hour punishes the designer for being efficient and good — the faster and more skilled you are, the less you earn. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-12-26]` Most business people value time above money; time is the most important thing they have. Therefore a solution delivered faster is worth *more*, not less, because it saves the client time and lets them go to market immediately. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-12-26]` The alternative to paying for skill is cheap junior labor: you can hire four interns for a couple hundred dollars, but you'll wait, keep revising, fail to articulate what's wrong — and while you do, you're not running your business. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-12-26]` The value proposition is an outcome: an amazing logo you won't have to change for a long time, that people look at and clearly understand what you do — that is worth $18,000 today. (paraphrase)
- `[2018-12-26]` The value of good work appreciates over time: "$18,000 today. Come back in a year. It'll be twenty-six." (paraphrase + quote)

### "Jung" (coaching participant, playing the client — CONTEXT, not persona)

- `[2018-12-26]` As a business person, would rather pay for effort/quality and, when pressed, concedes that a faster result is worth more. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (subject):
