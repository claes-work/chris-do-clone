## Summary

A 2018 year-in-review from The Futur counting down ten "best moments" from the year's
videos. Chris Do narrates the countdown and introduces each clip; the substantive tips
come from a mix of speakers. The reel spans design craft (visual hierarchy), personal
authenticity, social-media/personal branding, logo philosophy, value-based pricing,
sales role-plays with coached designers, and brand strategy. Two segments carry strong
Chris Do voice and signature frameworks: the "retreat and follow" negotiation move

## Key claims

### Chris Do — PERSONA

- `[2018-12-31]` **Retreat and follow.** When a client's budget is low, Chris coaches a
  "retreat and follow" move: signal reluctance ("I think I know what you want, I'm just
  not sure I'm the guy to do it for you") to see whether the client leans in and chases.
  When they do, they prove to themselves why they should work with you, and you move from
  order-taker to trusted consultant. (Edinburgh role-play with "Sarah", a coached
  designer — Chris is the teacher/demonstrator; Sarah is CONTEXT.) → **strong Chris voice**
- `[2018-12-31]` **Your value is set by the quality of the questions you ask.** Detail
  questions ("what shade of red?", "how big should the type be?" — likened to "you want
  fries with that") make you an order-taker. High-value questions get to purpose, make
  clients reflective, and surface deeper meaning — start with "why" and "how" to reach
  the root problem. That is how you raise your value. → **strong Chris voice; signature
  framework**
- `[2018-12-31]` Framing/narration of the year's clips (e.g. introducing Emily's first
  portfolio review, and describing each guest). Chris hosts and narrates throughout;
  narration is light persona material.

### Other speakers — CONTEXT (do NOT train persona)

- `[2018-12-31]` **Visual hierarchy** (instructor, name garbled in captions as "in a
  suit"): hierarchy rules apply to any artifact (logo, poster, style frame); use leading
  lines and contrast to control and direct the viewer's eye through a reading order.
  `attribution: uncertain` (a Futur instructor, not clearly Chris).
- `[2018-12-31]` **Be your authentic self** (male guest; self-deprecating about weight —
  "hey medium-sized guy" airport bit; declined to pretend enthusiasm for a Nike running
  job in Portland): pretending to be someone you're not is disingenuous; sharing your
  true self gives others courage and can make you a voice for people who identify with
  you. `attribution: uncertain` (guest speaker; NOT Chris).
- `[2018-12-31]` **Show your story, not just your work** (female guest — self-taught
  photographer/social influencer; shoots on a smartphone, post-processing turns snapshots
  into art; built audience by sharing personal life through a divorce with a one-year-old,
  including her dating life). Chris is the interviewer here; the tips are the guest's.
  CONTEXT.
- `[2018-12-31]` **A logo identifies, it doesn't communicate** (guest — captions "Segi
  have Eve", almost certainly **Sagi Haviv**): "a logo is not communication, the logo is
  identification… the period at the end of the sentence, not the sentence itself"; the
  less said, the better. CONTEXT.
- `[2018-12-31]` **Value-based pricing** (**Jonathan Stark**, named): once convinced a
  project is urgent, ask why they'd hire someone expensive, why not do it in-house, why
  cheaper options aren't real alternatives; then ask "what would a home run look like?" to
  define 100% customer satisfaction. CONTEXT.
- `[2018-12-31]` **Charging for "thinking"/strategy** (coached student — narration names
  "Melinda", likely **Melinda Livsey**; went from giving strategy away free to charging
  $5,000+, transitioning from design practitioner to brand strategist). Role-play asks the
  client about budget and the underlying growth goal, framing the logo/rebrand as a vehicle
  to the goal, not the end. CONTEXT. `attribution: uncertain` for which lines are Chris's
  coaching vs. Melinda's delivery.
- `[2018-12-31]` **Brand strategy** (**Brian Collins**, named; "one of five master
  designers from Fast Company"): brands live in two places — authenticity (internal: is
  this who we are?) and relevance (external: what is the world looking for and are you
  congruent with the culture?). A rebuttal follows ("I don't think that's true… it's about
  being remarkable, extraordinary; if you're not extraordinary no one pays attention") —
  speaker of the rebuttal is unclear from captions. CONTEXT. `attribution: uncertain` for
  the rebuttal.
