# Live YOUR Life – 2019 is Your Year

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a distinctive, reusable Chris Do metaphor ("legacy OS" — don't inherit your parents' operating system as your guiding principle) plus his recurring mortality/regret framing (Bronnie Ware's *The Top Five Regrets of the Dying*). Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/mindset/` and a dated `persona/beliefs.md` entry if not already captured.

## Summary

A New Year motivational message drawn from a live talk/workshop. Chris Do uses mortality as a motivator: he draws life as a line from birth to death, argues the midpoint is "fantastic news" because there is still time, and warns that momentum works against you the longer you wait. He introduces Bronnie Ware's *The Top Five Regrets of the Dying* (top regret: wishing you'd had the courage to live your own dream, not someone else's) as the emotional core. He addresses the fear of disappointing immigrant parents with his "legacy OS" metaphor: parents run an operating system that was right for their time, but the world has changed too fast to inherit it as your guide. He closes by framing a regret-free life as facing mortality and using the power of choice consciously, from a place of kindness and compassion toward yourself.

**Speaker:** Primarily a single-speaker monologue by **Chris Do**, delivered to a live audience ("you guys," "each and every one of you"). There is one brief exchange with an audience member — a young Asian creative he addresses as **Rebecca**, who says she feels passionate about being creative but it's "scary… cuz I'm Asian" with immigrant parents (lines 26–29). Only Chris-attributed material trains the persona; the audience member's lines are context, not persona training data.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-01-01] It is never too late to change your life: "if you're still above ground it's not too late." (quote)
- [2019-01-01] Life is a line from birth to death; if you're around the halfway point, that's good news because you still have time to figure it out — but don't wait, because the longer you go the more momentum works against you (you accumulate obligations). (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-01] Every time you change careers you "start over," and the depth of expertise (the vertical of a "T"-shaped skill set) resets to shallow — that starting-from-zero is the problem with waiting too long. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-01] Immigrant / first-generation parents want a stable life for you because they suffered and gave a lot up; they love you and want the best, but "they just don't have the information" — nobody can look into your heart. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-01] "Legacy OS" metaphor: your parents run an operating system that worked for their time (itself radically different from their own parents'), but the world now changes so fast that you should not inherit their legacy OS and use it as your guiding principle. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-01] The move is to lovingly tell your parents the world has changed — pointing at a phone as proof of how fast one device displaced whole industries — rather than obey an outdated model. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-01] Cites Bronnie Ware (caption garbles the name as "brawny"), an Australian nurse who cared for terminally ill patients, asked what they regretted, noticed a pattern, and wrote *The Top Five Regrets of the Dying*; the #1 regret was wishing for the courage to live the life you dreamt of instead of living somebody else's dream. (paraphrase, third-party source)
- [2019-01-01] The tragedy he wants to prevent is someone realizing in their final minutes "this was not my life, I had a bigger dream for myself" — and that is why he does what he does: to give people the courage, tools, and mindsets to be the creative human being they're meant to be. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-01] It's okay if this path isn't for you — you may be "in your explore phase," which is fine. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-01] To live a life free of regret you must face the fact that you will die, accept that you have the power of choice, and use that power as wisely and consciously as you can — but from a place of kindness, gentleness, and compassion toward yourself, which then flows out to everyone else. (paraphrase)
