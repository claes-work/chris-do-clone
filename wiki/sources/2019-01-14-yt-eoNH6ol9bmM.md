# 5 Things You Should NEVER do on a Sales Call - 3 min

★ **L3-candidate** — This is a named, self-contained framework (Chris Do's "5 things you should never do on a sales call"). It is a landmark sales-call-mistakes checklist worth promoting into `wiki/topics/sales-clients/` and into `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), not just held at L2.

## Summary

Chris Do lays out five mistakes to avoid on a sales call. The through-line is discipline and restraint: don't project desperation, don't dominate the conversation, don't assume the client's needs, and don't keep selling after you've won. He frames the ideal call as one where the salesperson speaks ~10% of the time and lets the client speak ~90%, driving the conversation with high-quality questions so the client reveals what they actually value. He closes with a plug for The Futur's Pro Group community.

Speaker: single-speaker monologue by **Chris Do** (first person — "me in the past," "I would assume," references "the Pro group" and the weekly coaching call). No co-host or guest speaks in this transcript. Jordan Belfort is mentioned as a third-party anecdote, not present.

## The 5 mistakes (framework)

1. **Don't go in desperate** — clients can smell it; you'll feel you must justify everything they ask.
2. **Don't cut off / interrupt the client** — interrupting signals you're not listening and makes them wary.
3. **Don't assume what they want, need, or find value in** — ask the right questions and have them say it; stay neutral and unbiased.
4. **Don't go past the sale** — once the client agrees to price and terms, get off the phone and out of the way before you screw it up.
5. **Don't fixate on your script / next question** — listen to the response instead of running through prepared questions; let the client drive.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-01-14] Going into a sales call desperate is detectable — "the clients can smell that" — and pushes you into justifying every point the client raises. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-14] Interrupting a client while they answer your question is a sign you're not listening and damages trust. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-14] Don't assume the client's needs or what they value; you must ask questions and let them state it. He admits he used to assume clients needed "a logo and a brand identity" and how to use it, when they'd said none of that — so it wasn't valuable to them, he was "just shoving it down their throat." (paraphrase, self-critical)
- [2019-01-14] Remaining neutral and unbiased helps you close clients, build rapport, and earn trust. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-14] Once a client agrees to the price and terms, stop selling and end the call — continuing only risks undoing the sale. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-14] Cites Jordan Belfort's car-buying story: Belfort was ready to buy and just wanted the paperwork, but the salespeople were "trained like dogs" and couldn't stop selling, so he left. (paraphrase, third-party anecdote)
- [2019-01-14] Don't go in fixated on your script or the next prepared question; he used to prep ~10 questions off the RFP/documents and ask them without listening to the responses. (paraphrase, self-critical)
- [2019-01-14] Let the client drive the conversation by asking high-quality questions. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-14] Done right, you speak ~10% of the time and the client speaks ~90% — "they are spilling their hearts out," telling you what's valuable and what they need. You have two ears and one mouth, so listen twice as much as you speak. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-14] Learning is "an open learning loop, not a closed learning loop." (quote fragment)
