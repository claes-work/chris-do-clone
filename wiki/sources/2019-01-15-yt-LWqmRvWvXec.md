# How to Transition From a Full-Time Job to Freelance?

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do's "build a bridge" method for leaving a job for freelance is a named, self-contained framework: keep the day job, use spare hours to build a positioned website + canned intake scripts, win side jobs, then *outsource the work to a cheaper freelancer and pocket the arbitrage* while employed, and only quit once side income exceeds the salary — so you never negotiate from desperation. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/business/` (and cross-referenced from pricing/sales-clients) plus a dated entry in `persona/beliefs.md`, not just held at L2.

## Summary

Chris Do coaches a live questioner on how to go from a full-time job to full-time freelance *without* taking a pay cut. His answer is a staged "build a bridge" plan: because a US full-time job only occupies ~8 of 24 hours, sacrifice some sleep/personal time to carve out extra hours, and spend them building a website that positions high-quality work, getting SEO right, and writing reusable canned responses that pre-qualify inquiries on price (e.g. "my work typically starts at $5,000 — is that something you can afford?"). Crucially, when a side job comes in, he advises *not* doing the work yourself but hiring a cheaper freelancer (his example: a friend in New Delhi at ~$400 on a $5,000 job) and keeping the difference — you are the bridge/broker between a client who can't source or art-direct talent and a talent who can't find clients. Do this repeatedly until side income beats the salary, then "burn that side of the bridge," quit, and cross over. He stresses the strategic value of keeping the job during the transition: the salary gives you leverage and lets you decline bad-fit or rush clients, so you never enter freelance desperate.

Speaker: primarily a single-speaker monologue by **Chris Do** answering an audience Q&A. A brief unattributed **questioner** opens the clip ("how do I transition from the part-time freelance to the full-time one with the same revenue stream…") and Chris also references a second audience question ("this also relates to Cherise's question… how do you stay motivated"). ## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-01-15] The transition strategy is to "build a bridge" — don't quit first; keep the salaried job and grow freelance income alongside it until you can cross over. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] A US full-time job is ~8 of 24 hours, so by sacrificing "a little bit of sleep" and "a little bit of personal me time" you can carve out ~2 extra hours a day (he frames it as up to six extra hours) to build the freelance business. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] Spend those hours building a website that positions high-quality work — "work that people want to hire other people for" — and getting your SEO and positioning right. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] Because you keep repeating intake, optimize it: keep canned responses in a Notion board, Trello board, or TextEdit saved to the cloud, written well one time. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] The canned response should pre-qualify on price before a call — state that your work "typically starts at this kind of price point, let's say $5,000… is this something that you can afford to spend?" A "no" ends the conversation; a "yes" tells you it's worth getting on the phone. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] Getting paid well is itself the motivation to keep doing after-hours work ("$5,000… I can buy two laptops, that's motivating, I'll get out of bed for that") — his answer to the second audience question about staying motivated. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] When you land the side job, do NOT do the work yourself — hire another freelancer (his example: a friend in New Delhi you admire) and ask what X, Y, Z will cost; the number "almost guaranteed" will be far less than you were paid. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] On a $5,000 job paid to a freelancer at ~$400, you keep ~$4,600 "for nurturing the lead," because you invested the one-time effort to build the site, SEO, and positioning and to put yourself out there — the freelancer is still relying on Fiverr and Upwork. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] This is arbitrage — "buy low, sell high." It's ethical because if the client knew who to hire and could art-direct and manage the project they'd cut you out, but they can't; and the freelancer doesn't know where the clients are or how to reach them. You are the bridge that brokers the deal, so you take the lion's share and pay the freelancer what *they* think is fair. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] "What they think is fair" is what matters when paying the freelancer — pay them their number, not yours. Your ability to negotiate up with the client and manage the project determines your profit. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] When side-job income exceeds your salary, "it's time for me to leave" — "burn that side of the bridge" and cross to full-time freelance. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] Keeping the job during the transition preserves leverage: with a salary covering "diapers and rent," you can price right and walk away from clients that feel wrong or are too much of a rush. "Do not give away that power." (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-15] The danger of quitting first: "when you quit your job all of a sudden you become desperate for the work and you start to take things that you don't need to." (paraphrase)
