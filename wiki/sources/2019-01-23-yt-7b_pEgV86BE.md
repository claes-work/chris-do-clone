# How to Build a Passive Income Business — w/ Melinda Livsey Ep. 14

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do's most complete single-video treatment of the *passive-income-business-for-designers* model: the "build once, sell many times" definition, the three product paths, the digital-vs-physical argument, the impact/effort facilitation graph, and the effective-hourly-rate close. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/business/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated). Note: this is a coaching dialogue, so only Chris-attributed material below trains the persona.

## Summary

A live coaching session (Ep. 14 of the "Melinda" series) in which Chris Do walks recurring Futur colleague Melinda Livsey through building a passive income business as she pivots away from client service work. Chris defines passive income as "build once, sell many times" (not "do nothing"), lays out three product paths for a designer (templates/tools, a course, or a derivative of an existing framework), argues strongly for digital over physical products, and then facilitates a decision by drawing an impact-vs-effort graph to rank Melinda's product ideas. He sets up an annual revenue target, the "divide by ten" monthly math, a non-linear sales curve (Black Friday spikes), the "nothing sells itself" promotion warning, and closes on the effective-hourly-rate argument for why this path beats client work.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-01-23] Passive income is misunderstood — it does NOT mean "do nothing." The model is "build once, sell many times": it takes a lot of time/effort/energy to build a product first, then you must market it (shows, blog posts, promotion) for it to earn over time. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] Even world-famous celebrities still have to promote — they hit press junkets and talk shows; only a few very powerful actors negotiate zero-promotion clauses. Most working people must be "our own PR agent, our own star, and our own show host." (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] Three ways a designer can build passive income: (1) create templates, brushes, and tools — cheap individually but they add up when bundled; (2) create a course; (3) build a *derivative product* of an existing framework you've already modified into your own flavor/voice. (paraphrase — framework)
- [2019-01-23] Discourages making physical products, especially when testing the waters: they require capital investment, reliance on manufacturers, operations/logistics of shipping worldwide, returns and breakage, and are hard to scale — sell-outs can take months to resupply, and inventory needs physical warehouse space. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] Digital products are the safer route: easier to develop, ship, fulfill, and handle returns. Downside — they can be pirated/torrented easily (Futur products have been). Physical products are harder to replicate, "until you get to that point... they're making knockoffs, and then I will consider you extremely successful." (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-01-23] People falsely equate value with things they can touch, but the trend — especially in younger generations — is away from physical copies (DVDs/Blu-ray/CDs → streaming; Kindle/ebooks carry a hundred books). (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] Facilitation is largely helping a person get clarity and make a decision. His method: draw a graph with impact (measurable in money, free time, OR joy/happiness) against easy-vs-hard, plot each idea, and — using "design sprint methodology" — prioritize the highest-impact/lowest-effort work first, then work backwards. (paraphrase — framework)
- [2019-01-23] Set a high financial goal deliberately to force different thinking: you don't reach a big number by incremental change, you have to "jump the curve" (crediting Guy Kawasaki). Endorses Melinda's ambitious $250k goal as a forcing function. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] To pace an annual goal, divide it by ten (crediting "one of my other talks") — $250k/year ≈ $25k/month average. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] Sales are not linear across the year: they spike around November/Black Friday ("when companies go into the black and get out of the red") and drop in December. Plan quarterly promotions/launches to create spikes so the big months carry the slow ones. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] "Nothing sells itself." The "build it and they will come" belief is a delusion that leaves people depressed when nobody comes. Promotion is as important as (or more important than) the product itself. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] Three things every product launch needs: (1) make the product as good as it can be; (2) give it clear, accurate descriptions of what it is; (3) let people know it exists. (paraphrase — framework)
- [2019-01-23] Before launching, you must first build a community/audience — most people skip this "groundwork/legwork," so even a great product can "hit with a thud." One fix: connect with someone who already has an audience and launch on their platform. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] Affiliate marketing is a powerful launch strategy — cites "Ron/Ran Segal" (Flux), who did six figures (~$135k) on his first course launch by partnering with influencers plus leaning on his established YouTube channel. (paraphrase — third-party example; see garble note)
- [2019-01-23] Consider launching on someone else's platform (Udemy, Skillshare, Lynda/LinkedIn Learning): a smaller revenue cut but much higher sales volume — analogous to a licensing model where you do almost none of the work. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] The effective-hourly-rate close: if the remaining work to get Melinda's video bundle "across the finish line" is ~10 hours and it stands to make ~$7,000, that effective hourly rate is unmatched anywhere else — "one more nail in the coffin of why you need to go down this path." (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] "GBO" = Global Business Objective — a term he credits to Jose Caballer. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] YouTube content is evergreen: it builds an archive that fans discover days, weeks, months, or years later — a reason to keep publishing. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-23] Idea-planting metaphor (Inception): it's hard to plant an idea but not as hard as the movie makes it seem — plants "seeds" with coachees that resurface later as their own ideas. (paraphrase)

### Melinda Livsey

- [2019-01-23] (Context) Her global business objective, in her own words, is "to be my own client" — pivoting after ~two decades of client-service work because projects have been stalling and higher fees mean longer sales cycles. (paraphrase — Melinda)
- [2019-01-23] (Context) Sets a 2019 goal of $250k in passive income specifically because it's high enough to force a completely different business. (paraphrase — Melinda)
- [2019-01-23] (Context) Her existing/planned products: a brand strategy kit (~$400/unit, video + instructional ebook), a $97 video bundle, a $50/month subscription Facebook group (~300 members ≈ $300/mo... corrected in-session), "Prelance" ($197), and "Preline" (~$250, a tool to communicate/sell strategy, built with a partner "Zack Ramez"). (paraphrase — Melinda; names uncertain, see garbles)
- [2019-01-23] (Context) She modified Futur's "Core" framework "so much it's almost unrecognizable" — the basis for her own brand strategy kit. (paraphrase — Melinda)
- [2019-01-23] (Context) Prefers licensing over manufacturing physical products (no capital investment, only time cost); has spoken with a licensing contact ("Saif") whose process is on his YouTube channel. (paraphrase — Melinda; name uncertain)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do:**

- "A passive income really is... it takes a lot of time, effort, and energy to build a product first, and then you market that product, and that product can earn money over time. So to build once, sell many times."
- "We need to be our own PR agent, our own star, and our own show host, and to make stuff so people become aware of it. So passive income, it's a little deceptive — it does require work."
- "Generally speaking, the best kind of product to make is the digital one."
- "None of this stuff sells itself, and that's a delusion many people tell themselves: 'I will build it and they will come,' and nobody comes and they get really depressed."
- "You have to jump the curve, and in order to do so you're going to be investing quite a bit of time into building a passive income business."
- "If you come up with a dynamite product but nobody hears about it, you're not gonna get a lot of sales."
- "You need to make sure the product is as good as it can be, you need to make sure that you have clear and accurate descriptions as to what the product is, then you have to let people know what the heck the product is — and that's it."
- "It's easy to extract information, it's very difficult to plant in an idea, according to the movie [Inception]. But it's not as difficult as they make it seem." (on planting seeds/ideas)

**Melinda Livsey (context, does not train persona):**

- "I am the queen of building something like a brand, registering the business name, and then not doing anything with it after that."
- "These all have been my side things that I didn't expect to be the main thing, and now I'm looking at this and it gets me so excited."
