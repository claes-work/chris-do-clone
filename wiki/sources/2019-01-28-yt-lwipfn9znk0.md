## Summary

Chris Do lays out five rules for running a successful sales call. Rule 1: talk about money early — it's the professional thing to do and answers the "are we a fit" question both sides are already asking, saving everyone from wasted hours. Rule 2: style is more important than substance when building rapport (credited to Tony Robbins) — match and mirror the client's pace and energy, because people remember how you made them feel. Rule 3: "show me, don't tell" — you don't educate clients by lecturing them about process; the root of "educate" is *educe*, "to draw out, not put in," so you educate by asking questions. Whoever asks more questions controls the conversation, and the quality of your questions determines your value (directed but open-ended; why > how > what). Rule 5: build trust by remaining neutral and unbiased — your only job on the call is to give the client information to make the best decision *for them*, even recommending a competitor. He closes with a live role-play with a coaching participant ("Aaron") to illustrate that fear of losing a client is fear of losing something you never had, and that clients often hire on know/like/trust over pure qualifications.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-01-28] **Rule 1 — Talk about money early.** Get over your fear of it; talking about money up front is the professional thing to do. Both the client and you are trying to determine if it's a good fit, and price answers that question — otherwise you spend "45 minutes, an hour, multiple calls" only to realize they can't afford you. Not talking about it fails to answer "the number one question on both of your minds." (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-28] **Rule 2 — Style is more important than substance (for building rapport).** Credited "straight up from Mr Tony Robbins." People remember how you made them feel long after they forget your words, so the tone, phrasing, and energy matter. Match and mirror: if someone talks fast, talk fast; if they talk slow, talk slow, and you sync up with them. Most people run at one monotone energy level; you need to learn to modulate your energy. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-28] **Rule 3 — Show me, don't tell.** Design schools say "educate the client," but people patronize clients by talking about their process, which clients don't care about. The root word of "educate" is *educe*, which means "to draw out, not put in" — so you educate by asking questions, not by lecturing. People don't want to be lectured or told; the more you tell them, the more it feels like selling/pitching/high-pressure, and they're most likely to say no. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-28] **Whoever asks more questions is in control of the conversation** — you can play it as a game and keep score: a point each time they ask, a point each time you ask. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-28] **The quality of the questions you ask determines your value.** Use directed but open-ended questions. Vague open questions like "how do you feel today" don't help; "how do you feel about your brand in terms of serving your overall global business objective" is far better. Stay away from yes/no questions. In order of importance: **why questions are highest value, then how questions, then what questions** — "start with why and end with what." (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-28] **Rule 5 — Build trust.** In sales the objective is to give the client information to make the best decision *for them, not for you*. The information they need: price, timeline, help figuring out scope, and whether you're a good fit — and if you're not, recommend somebody else. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-28] **Remain neutral and unbiased; take yourself out of the equation.** He aims to remove himself from the sales process three times within the first sales call — if he knows someone who can do a better job more cost-effectively, he recommends them right away. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-28] On the fear of losing business by recommending competitors: "How can you lose something you did not have?" Missing/losing a client you never had is fear-based — it creates a disempowering emotion. Analogy: it's like feeling you lost "that supermodel girlfriend" you were never with; your life was going along just fine. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-01-28] **Many clients value a trusted relationship more than price.** Example: hiring a cinematographer/editor — he'd hire the person he knows, trusts, and likes over someone more qualified (e.g., "the kid who studied finance in school"); more qualified people probably exist, but know/like/trust wins, and those things "are kind of not to be decided on a call like this." (paraphrase)
