# When A Client Wants More But Doesn't Want To Pay (Role-play)

## Summary

A short single-scene role-play demonstrating how a vendor should handle **scope creep** — a client (named "Nancy") who has already agreed to $800 for a video and then asks the vendor to "just throw in a couple of stills" as if it's a small, free favor. The vendor refuses to give the extra work away: he pauses to reset the frame, states that photography is normally a separate, separately-priced service, anchors the true package value high ($1,400), then offers a discounted bundle. When the client pushes back on price, he holds his ground, makes one modest concession, and — crucially — hands the client the freedom to walk (find someone else, or pay rush fees given the two-day deadline). The scene resolves amicably: rather than getting free stills, the client de-scopes back to the original $800 video-only deal, and both sides part on good terms with the relationship intact.

Speaker: **Role-play — Chris Do performs BOTH parts** (the vendor/photographer-videographer and the client "Nancy"); it is a single continuous acted scene with no separate spoken debrief in this clip. The client's lines (asking for free stills, calling the price "unreasonable / so expensive," probing "where are you coming up with that number") are **in-character acting**, not Chris's real belief — they illustrate the pressure a client applies. The **vendor's lines are the demonstrated coaching position**: how Chris teaches a designer to respond. Because there is no explicit debrief, the "lesson" is what the vendor character models, not a stated theorem — attributed below accordingly.

## Key claims

### Coaching position — demonstrated through the vendor character (Chris's real teaching)

- [2019-01-30] When a client asks you to "just throw in" extra deliverables for free, don't reflexively agree or argue — buy a beat to reset the frame ("I need a minute to process this... I came into this conversation with a totally different assumption"). (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-30] Additional deliverables outside the agreed scope (here, still photos) are a **separate service with a separate fee** — "normally when people hire me to do photographs I charge a totally separate fee." (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-30] **Anchor to the real value** of the full package before discounting: state that the video-plus-stills package is usually ~$1,400, then offer a relationship discount rather than free work. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-30] Justify the number by reference to standalone market rates — event/photo coverage alone would run ~$800 — so "a couple of stills" is not trivial. (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-30] Make **one modest, genuine concession** ("if we can do it for a thousand, let's do it") but don't collapse to the client's implied "free" or "split the difference." (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-30] **Give the client the freedom to walk** without pressure or resentment — acknowledge they have choices, respect that they may hire someone else, and calmly surface the real consequences (the event is in two days; going elsewhere risks rush fees). (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-30] Let the client **de-scope themselves** back to the original deal rather than the vendor giving away work: the acceptable outcome is the client saying "maybe I can find someone else to do the stills, let's just stick with [the original]." (paraphrase)
- [2019-01-30] Close the negotiation by **protecting the relationship and reaffirming delivery** — "I'm gonna make you the best video we agreed to and I'm gonna deliver," ending warm and mutual. (paraphrase)

### Role-play — client's pressure tactics (in-character, NOT Chris's belief)

- [2019-01-30] The client frames extra work as a tiny favor: "if you could just throw in a couple of stills... is that gonna be a problem?" (role-play, in-character)
- [2019-01-30] The client attacks the added price as unfair: "that seems a little unreasonable... where are you coming up with that number... I just need a couple of stills, why is it so expensive?" (role-play, in-character)
- [2019-01-30] The client tries to split the difference and guilt the vendor: "that doesn't sound quite like in the middle... I'm a little disappointed that you couldn't just snap these stills for me," dangling future work ("I'd love to give you more work in the future"). (role-play, in-character)
