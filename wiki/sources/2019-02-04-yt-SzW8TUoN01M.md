# 4 Different Buyer Types and How to Respond

★ **L3-candidate** — This is a named, self-contained taxonomy (Chris Do's "4 buyer types") with a specific response tactic for each type. It is a landmark qualifying/sales framework worth promoting into `wiki/topics/sales-clients/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), not just held at L2. Flag only — not inlined here.

## The 4 buyer types

1. **Price buyer** — wants the lowest possible price; the bargain shopper ("Is there a discount? Is that on sale? That's not your final price, right? I could go to Joe down the street and get it cheaper"). **Response:** identify them quickly and refer them out. Send them to Fiverr / 99designs, or refer to an up-and-coming designer/friend for a **10–30% referral fee** (Chris suggests 10–15% as typical). Don't try to win them.
2. **Indecisive buyer** — "I'm not sure, let me think about it, I have to make a few more calls." No urgency; "I'll know when I see it." They lack context — they don't know good from bad, low from high. **Response:** don't waste time selling. Tell them to comparison-shop and "call me last"; warn them up front that **you're going to be the highest bid**; give them enough information to make an informed decision.
3. **Know-it-all buyer** — plays art director; tells you which tools to use ("Aren't you using Illustrator? Put a lens flare in that sucker"). Often enthusiasts or frustrated/"pretend" designers. **Response:** embrace and **agree with everything they say** — including that the work is simple and they could get it done cheaper elsewhere — then hold your price ("to get me to do it, it's going to be $5,000") and see what they do.
4. **Value buyer** ("the Holy Grail") — doesn't care about price, only results. Buys on **emotion**, then uses logic to justify later. High urgency; knows they have a problem, knows the value of their time and the cost of getting it wrong; wants it done right the first time. **Response:** don't treat them like a price buyer, don't drown them in features/specs, and **do not go past the sale**.

## Summary

Chris Do walks through four buyer types a designer meets on a sales call and gives a distinct response tactic for each. The organizing idea is **qualify fast, then respond to the buyer's actual psychology** rather than defaulting to price. Price buyers get referred out (ideally for a referral fee); indecisive buyers get told to shop around and call last (pre-framed that you'll be the highest bid); know-it-all buyers get agreed with until they either back down or accept your price; and value buyers — who decide on emotion and want results — get served without feature-dumping or over-selling. He ties the value buyer to the want-vs-need distinction (wants are emotional, needs are features) and to the Porsche-buyer analogy: once the emotional decision is made, don't "go past the sale."

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-02-04] There are four buyer types — price, indecisive, know-it-all, and value — and you must speak to each differently. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-04] The price buyer decides only on lowest price; the fastest move is to identify them quickly and refer them to someone else or to Fiverr / 99designs. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-04] Referring a too-small job to a friend/up-and-comer for a **10–15% referral fee (up to 20–30%)** is legitimate, not "giving it away" — The Futur itself pays sales reps a monthly stipend plus a percentage for leads, and good leads are exactly what most designers struggle to find. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-04] If a job is below your **minimum level of engagement (MLE)** — e.g. they'll pay $1,500 but your minimum is $5,000 — hand it off and ask for a referral fee. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-04] The indecisive buyer has no urgency and lacks context (can't tell good from bad, high from low), which is human nature — like getting three bids for roof repair to find the high, medium, and low. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-04] Respond to the indecisive buyer by telling them to compare, "call me last," and warning them up front that you'll be the highest bid — then give them information to decide. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-04] The know-it-all buyer acts as art director and tells you how to do the work; the counter-intuitive tactic is to agree with everything they say — even that it's simple and cheaper elsewhere — while holding your price. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-04] The value buyer is the "Holy Grail": doesn't care about price, wants results, buys on emotion and justifies with logic afterward, and has high urgency (the opposite of the indecisive buyer). (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-04] Value buyers know they have a problem, know the value of their time and the cost of getting it wrong, and want the best, done right the first time. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-04] Distinguish want vs. need: a want is emotional ("I want to feel successful, I want the best"); a need is about features. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-04] Porsche analogy: someone walking into a Porsche dealership has already made the emotional decision — don't sell them horsepower, traction, or ABS; you must be careful **not to go past the sale**. (paraphrase)
