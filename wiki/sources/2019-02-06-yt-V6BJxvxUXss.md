# How to Promote Your Passive Income Business — w/ Melinda Livsey Ep. 15

★ **L3-candidate** — The sequel to Ep. 14 and Chris Do's most complete single-video treatment of *promoting/marketing* a passive-income product for creatives: the market-awareness matrix ("aware of the problem × aware of you"), the "separate feelings from facts/data" discipline, the freebie-must-align rule, expert-positioning as the only content that matters, the "whittle it down to its essence" distillation demo, the sounding-board method, and content-repurposing. ## Summary

Live coaching session (Ep. 15 of the "Melinda" series, following Ep. 14's build-the-product session). Now that Melinda Livsey has released her $97 video bundle ("Building the Designer's Business"), Chris Do reviews her live sales page and marketing plan and works through how to *promote* it. He audits the landing page (new products up top, "as seen on" logos must be labeled, add a short promo video, state the three concrete outcomes, switch copy from "our/we" to "you/your"), then pushes hard on market awareness — separating gut feeling from facts and data, mapping the "aware of the problem × aware of you" matrix, and pointing out her audience is finite. He reframes her 150-unit goal against her 1,200 newsletter subscribers, stresses that a freebie/email trip wire must align with the end product, argues all content should establish her as an expert at solving one specific problem, and demonstrates whittling the course down to its essence ("help you find your unique story") before scripting an onboarding video on the spot. He closes on the sounding-board idea (writing/talking things out with a neutral party), content-repurposing across platforms, and a humble hand-off of the "fancy digital marketing" to Ben Burns.

Speaker: **two-person dialogue** between **Chris Do** (the SUBJECT — coach/mentor) and **Melinda Livsey** (recurring Futur colleague, NOT the subject — the coaching client). Only Chris-attributed statements train the persona; Melinda's are context. Third parties named (not present): Russell Brunson (*DotCom Secrets*), Ben Burns (Futur co-founder, handles digital marketing), and "Matthew" (a Futur person taking a course on building online courses). Platforms referenced: SlideShare, Wistia, LinkedIn, Instagram, YouTube.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-02-06] Newly launched products should live at the *top* of a page, not the bottom — people will not scroll to the bottom to find them. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-06] Displaying other brands' logos ("Logo Geek," "The Futur," etc.) without a label reads as "these are our customers" and creates confusion. Label them "as seen on" / "featured on." You must never create confusion or mistrust, because "at any point in the flow from top to bottom you lose some of these trust... they're gone in less than 60 seconds." (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-02-06] The sales page needs a short *promo* video up top (not a sample lesson) — people are short on time and need to know right away what they're getting. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-06] State the concrete outcomes immediately after the headline promise — e.g. "the three things I'm going to walk away with after I spend $97." When you build your own product you become so familiar with it that you overlook the fundamentals (what the buyer actually gets). (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-06] Copywriting fix: use "you/your," not "our/we" — change "underpricing projects isn't always a result of not knowing *our* worth" to "*your* worth." It lets readers put themselves into the situation. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-06] If the offer isn't in the right spot, written with compelling copy, and surrounded by amazing visuals, "you're going to lose people." (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-02-06] **Market-awareness discipline** — before promoting, ask whether the audience is even aware they *have* the problem. "Separate feelings and emotions from facts and data." Validate gut feelings with polls; don't assume. (paraphrase — framework)
- [2019-02-06] Your following and newsletter list are *finite*. Once you blast the launch to your existing audience, "the real work is going to begin in converting other people who aren't aware of you at all." (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-02-06] **The awareness matrix** — buyers fall into combinations of (aware of the problem / not) × (aware of you / not): worst case is aware of neither. If you can assume they're aware of the problem and just need to become aware of you and your solution, selling gets "a lot easier." (paraphrase — framework)
- [2019-02-06] Sample warm-audience ad copy (off the cuff): "Hey y'all, you know that problem you have — I got the answer, and it's going to be a lot cheaper than you think." (quote — illustrative ad copy)
- [2019-02-06] Warning against the assumption "they know, so once I create this thing sales are going to be flying out the door... we're going to just blow up" — you may be disappointed when that's not the case. (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-02-06] Reframes the numbers: the goal is only 150 units, not 15,000. If Melinda already believes ~150 interested people exist on her list, she doesn't need ad campaigns — she just needs to have conversations with those people. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-06] A freebie / email trip wire (free downloadable content in exchange for a subscribe) must *line up with the ultimate product* — Melinda's earlier business-card-mockup freebie didn't align with where her business went, so a new one is needed. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-06] **Expert-positioning** — everything you post on social and write in a blog should be driven toward establishing you as an expert at solving one specific kind of problem; "everything else is garbage." When people know you as the expert with the solution, resistance to buying is minimized. (paraphrase + quote fragment; he attributes the underlying idea to a conversation with "Matthew" about a course on building online learning courses, and harks back to "episode one — the post about the dog doesn't help.")
- [2019-02-06] **Distill to the essence** — whittle the offer down: "Building the Designer's Business" → "branding yourself as a creative agency" → "I'm going to help you find your unique story." Strip away everything else to the core, lead the marketing with that hook, then "fill in all the gaps." (paraphrase — framework; demonstrated live)
- [2019-02-06] Demonstrates the principle by scripting Melinda's onboarding/promo video intro on the spot ("Hi, it's me Melinda... you go through the exercise to find your ideal client, but what you realize is there's something missing in all this — and it's you... I'm going to give you six prompts... download this free deliverable... consider taking the rest of the course"). (paraphrase of demo)
- [2019-02-06] "You have to just apply your own framework to your marketing" — the same methods you use on clients apply to your own promotion. (quote fragment)
- [2019-02-06] **We have blinders on our own stuff** — "this is why your website sucks and your logo sucks, but everything else that you do for your clients is fantastic": with our own work we're all over the place. It's not a sign someone can't teach a subject. (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-02-06] **The sounding board** — commit abstract thoughts, gut feelings and raw emotions into tangible things by writing them down; it helps to talk them through with a neutral, unbiased party who has your best interest at heart (a friend, cousin, sibling not deep in the design business). "It's not so much that you need them to give you amazing advice — you just need to be able to put your thoughts into words, cuz otherwise it's like this messy chemical soup in your head." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-02-06] **Content-repurposing** — once you produce a piece of content it should live in as many places as makes sense: YouTube channel, Instagram story, chopped-up clips; turn a downloadable/email-tripwire into a SlideShare, post it there, and link to it via LinkedIn. "Get this information out there." (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-02-06] Post videos on Wistia ("Wisa" in captions) to get data on who watched and how far, and to embed things and get more sophisticated with marketing. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-06] Self-aware humility: "Before I shoot myself in the foot I'm going to stop talking about all the fancy digital marketing things, because it's not really what I do. I'm mostly coming from it from a content side. Ben Burns is the one who handles all the digital marketing stuff. I know enough just to be dangerous." (quote — delegation / self-positioning)
