# How do you Estimate the Cost of a Project?

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a named, self-contained framework: Chris Do's line-by-line **project bid build-up** (cost stack: line-item labor → marked-up rates → management/direction roles → equipment/overhead → subtotal → profit markup). It is a concrete, reusable estimating method with worked numbers, worth promoting into `wiki/topics/pricing/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), not just held at L2.

## Summary

Chris Do answers an audience question — how to budget a project for unexpected delays and missteps — with a live whiteboard walkthrough of how to build up a bid. Starting from a naive single-line estimate (one artist at $500/day × 10 days = $5,000), he progressively adds the costs a beginner forgets: mark up the artist's rate because artists underestimate the work; add a producer/coordinator to manage the project, an art director to direct it, and equipment/workstation rental for overhead; then add a profit markup on the whole. The naive $5,000 bid grows to a correct $20,400. The core lesson: every role and cost must appear in the bid even if you perform them yourself — otherwise you're absorbing the risk of delays for free, and the "extra" money you make when you do those jobs yourself is your margin.

Speaker: primarily a **Chris Do** monologue delivered to a live audience/class at a whiteboard. An **unnamed audience member** asks the opening question ("how do you budget for unexpected things that goes wrong in the project"); all substantive content is Chris-attributed. Only Chris's material trains the persona.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-02-11] If you submit a bid at the raw labor cost ($5,000 for one artist at $500/day × 10 days), you've done yourself a disservice — you're exposed if the artist takes longer than estimated and you end up paying for the overrun. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-11] Mark up the artist's day rate — e.g. from $500 to $750/day — "because artists always underestimate what it's going to take to do the job." At $750 × 10 days the line becomes $7,500, an extra $2,500 of cushion/margin if all goes well. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-02-11] "If all goes well — which it never does in visual [effects]" — you've made the extra $2,500. (paraphrase, with wry aside that projects never go perfectly). (quote fragment)
- [2019-02-11] Add a producer/coordinator to manage the artist — e.g. $400/day × 10 days = $4,000. Whether or not you actually hire and pay a producer is irrelevant; it must be in the bid because someone has to manage the project. If you produce it yourself, that $4,000 is money you've made. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-11] Add an art director to direct the artist — e.g. $900/day. You don't art-direct every day, so prorate the time across the job (he uses 5 days), giving a $4,500 line. Prorating spreads your involvement across the length of the job rather than billing every single day. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-11] Running the labor lines together: $7,500 (artist) + $4,000 (producer) + $4,500 (art director) = $16,000 — versus the naive $5,000 you'd have submitted. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-11] Add equipment/overhead: artists work on a machine with attached software, so bill a workstation rental — e.g. a 3D/PC workstation at $500/week × 2 weeks = $1,000. (He notes you can't bill for the laptop used to write the bid, but you can bill the production workstation.) Subtotal now $17,000. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-11] Finally add profit as an explicit markup — this is where you cover overhead (utilities, rent) and make the business money. Profit runs "anywhere between 10 to 30 percent" depending on country and industry; he uses 20%. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-11] 20% of $17,000 = $3,400, giving a final bid of **$20,400**. "That's how you do a bid and that's how you stay in business." (paraphrase + quote) [Note: caption garbles the markup as "thirty three thousand four hundred"; the arithmetic and his stated result ($20,400) confirm the markup is $3,400.]
- [2019-02-11] Tell the client the markup is your profit outright — "everybody that runs a business understands that," because if you only paid out costs the business would make no money. (paraphrase + quote)
