# Project Management - How to Break Down Projects

★ **L3-candidate** — This is a named, self-contained framework (The Futur's project "chunking" system: big projects → phases → weekly sprints → tasks, with locked waterfall milestones and a "Tetris" task-packing heuristic). It scales from team delivery down to solo yearly/quarterly/weekly planning, and is worth promoting into `wiki/topics/business/` and (for the personal-planning application) `persona/beliefs.md`, not just held at L2.

## Summary

A screen-share walkthrough of how The Futur organizes client projects from start to finish using **chunking** — taking something big and breaking it into progressively smaller pieces: big projects → phases/increments → weekly sprints (starting Mondays) → individual tasks. Phases end in **locked milestones** (waterfall methodology: each phase depends on the prior, and once finished it's "done and locked" — you can't go back) until delivery. Weekly planning is likened to a game of **Tetris**: packing as many tasks as comfortably fit into each week, estimating from past history (solo) or team estimates plus padding, with client postings once a week driving the next week's tasks. The same method scales down to solo planning: year → four quarters (with revenue/product/content goals) → weekly self-assignments. Closes with a plug for The Futur's project-management course.

## Key claims

_All attribution uncertain (see above); dated to publication._

- [2019-02-13] The Futur's project approach is based on **chunking** — taking something really big and breaking it down into smaller and smaller pieces. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-13] Hierarchy: big projects break into **phases/increments** → phases break into **weekly sprints** (run Mondays, concluding the following week) → sprints break into **individual tasks**. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-13] A project can be broken into **three big phases**, each ending in a locked milestone, until delivery. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-13] At the start of each week they decide the tasks needed to hit the milestone, assign them to team members (confirming everyone's okay with it), and adjust along the way. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-13] Weekly planning is "a game of Tetris" — fit as many tasks into one week as comfortably possible, distinguishing short vs. long tasks so they pack efficiently week after week. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-13] Estimate task time from your own **past history** if solo; if working with a team, have them estimate and then **add padding**. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-13] Sprints align with client **postings once a week**; feedback comes early the next week and determines the next week's tasks. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-13] Phases follow **waterfall methodology** — phases depend on each other; once phase one is finished it's "done and locked" and you can't go back, then you move to phase two, and so on until delivery. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-13] Example three phases for a **video project**: (1) storyboarding + designing style frames, (2) animatic — timing, audio, and blocking, (3) animation — filling in and rendering to final delivery quality. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-13] The method works for web projects, logo projects, "pretty much anything" — it's deliverable-based Client Services where the end result is known. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-13] The same methodology scales to **solo/personal planning**: treat the year like a project → break into four quarters with per-quarter goals (revenue targets, products/projects to complete, content to make) → break each quarter into weekly sprints → give yourself weekly assignments. "No one else sees this," but it helps organize your own tasks, and it works identically for solo client projects. (paraphrase; first-person, likely Chris Do)
