## Summary

Matthew Encina teaches how to structure a video so ideas come across clearly, using the
outline The Futur uses for most of its own content. The core is a three-part skeleton —
an Overview (cold open + value promise + brief expert bio + why it matters), the "meat"
broken into 3–5 recipe-like steps, and an Outro (recap + something to act on + a call to
action). The clip is presented as a short excerpt from a 2-hour Pro-member conversation
on content creation that Encina led.

## The 3-part content framework (Matthew Encina)

1. **Overview (the open)** — (a) cold open: within 5–10 seconds tell the viewer what the
   video is about; (b) tell/show them what value they'll receive (show the end result for
   a tutorial, or make a promise "by the end you'll be able to…"); (c) a brief self-intro
   to frame you as the expert — a short bio, not your whole résumé; (d) why it matters —
   an anecdote or reason the information is impactful. Because YouTube is "just-in-time
   education" (the world's #2 search engine) and The Futur plays in the education/value
   space, the first 10–15 seconds must establish who you are, what the video is, and what
   the viewer might achieve.
2. **The meat — 3–5 steps** — break the concept into 3–5 steps like a recipe (a recipe
   shows the beautiful final result, then the few steps to replicate it). Don't exceed 5
   steps or it gets too complicated; give only the big steps needed to reach the promised
   result.
3. **Outro** — recap the ideas; give the viewer something concrete to act on (Encina
   cites Ben's series that ends with a challenge, e.g. "make 30 cold calls this week")
   because a small win gives them a reason to come back; and include an explicit call to
   action (like / comment / subscribe / share) — people won't do it unless you ask.

## Key claims

### Matthew Encina

- [2019-02-20] Most Futur content follows one outline: Overview → 3–5 steps → Outro.
  (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-20] A cold open must tell the viewer within 5–10 seconds what the video is
  about, because people use YouTube for "just-in-time education" — it's the #2 search
  engine in the world — so give the answer right away. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-20] After the cold open, state the value/result the viewer will get: show the
  end result for a tutorial, or make an explicit promise for a concept. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-20] Give a brief self-intro to frame yourself as the expert — a short bio, not
  your entire résumé of every project and award. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-20] Include why the topic matters, via an anecdote or a concrete reason it's
  impactful for the viewer. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-20] Break the teaching into 3–5 steps "like a recipe"; a recipe works because
  it shows the beautiful final result, then the few steps to replicate it. Don't go past
  5 steps or it gets too complicated. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-20] In the outro, recap and — better — give the viewer something concrete to
  act on; a small achievable win makes them likely to return and report results.
  (paraphrase; cites Ben's challenge-based series)
- [2019-02-20] Always include an explicit call to action; people won't like, share, or
  subscribe unless you ask them — Encina says he even does this in his blog articles.
  (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-20] Don't make content "completely passive" like just another YouTube video —
  engage and pull the viewer in so they continue the journey with you. (paraphrase)
