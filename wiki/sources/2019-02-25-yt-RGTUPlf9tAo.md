# How to Build a Passive Income Business For Creatives - Dustin Lee

> Note: This is a **guest interview**. The overwhelming majority of the value here is
> the guest's (Dustin Lee's) product/passive-income playbook — that is **context, not
> persona training material**. **Not an L3 persona candidate.** Only the sparse
> Chris-Do-attributed material below may train the persona, and it is minor.

## Summary

Dustin Lee — founder of **RetroSupply Co.** (a digital-goods shop selling brushes,
fonts, textures and other assets for creatives) — walks through his three-step model for
building a passive-income business on creative skills: (1) choose the right product,
(2) make products for a specific "your people" audience, and (3) find something you love
about marketing. He illustrates with RetroSupply's mid-century Procreate brush pack (15
brushes, $15), emphasizes customer research ("ask people what to make, then make that"),
niching down instead of chasing trends, and a "start now, be prepared to fail" mindset.
Chris Do hosts and speaks very little in this segment; his one substantive interjection
reframes Dustin's customer-research point as a rapport-building recipe.

Speaker: host **Chris Do** (interviewer, The Futur) with guest **Dustin Lee** (founder,
RetroSupply Co.). Dustin does ~95% of the talking; this segment is largely his monologue.

### Dustin Lee (guest — context)

_All of the following is Dustin Lee's playbook and story — CONTEXT ONLY, does not train
the Chris Do persona._

- [2019-02-25] Three fundamentals to build passive income from creative skills: choose
  the right product, make a product that stands out, and find something you love about
  marketing. You don't need to be a "design celebrity" or special credentials. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-25] Three questions to find a profitable product: what's popular (on Creative
  Market, Instagram, design blogs), what do you have the skills to build well, and where
  do you have leverage (traffic/audience access). (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-25] Product-idea origin: on Dribbble he kept seeing unanswered "how did you
  get that texture?" questions and decided to build products that answer those questions —
  "if people are asking this question then there's probably people willing to pay for it."
  (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-25] Be honest about your own strengths/weaknesses; a strength of his has been
  recognizing what he's not good at and partnering with others rather than spending two
  years studying before moving forward. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-25] Leverage comes down to traffic/audience — e.g. mutual connection Diane
  Gibbs and a relevant story got him onto the show, putting him in front of new people;
  use every asset (high-profile job, groups you know) to help people find you. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-25] Buying and enjoying products yourself makes it easier to understand the
  psychology of why people buy. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-25] Example product: the "mid-century Procreate brush pack" — 15 Procreate
  brushes. He co-built it with illustrator Brad Woodard over ~50–60 hours on Skype,
  making ~100 brushes then chiseling down to the 15 highest-leverage ones. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-25] Goal is to make people go "whoa, I can't believe they went to that extent";
  even at $15 the product must feel like a no-brainer, giving buyers "three to five times
  the value of what you're charging." (paraphrase + quote fragments)
- [2019-02-25] Make products for a specific person: identify who is NOT your people, who
  you follow/respect (he names Andy J Pizza as a persona he designs for even though Andy
  doesn't buy), and do the "dirty work" for heroes you can't out-draw. "If you make stuff
  for everyone you're kind of making stuff for no one." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-02-25] Cites a real customer/artist "Edville" in Mexico City who loves and uses
  RetroSupply products — "no surprise... because we quite literally built products
  thinking about people exactly like him." (paraphrase) _(caption-garbled name; likely a
  handle/username — verify spelling)_
- [2019-02-25] Artists must become marketers if they want to make money: "it's very rare
  that people will just randomly find things and start buying them." He loves copywriting
  ("the art of writing persuasive text that helps sell a product") and email — subscribes
  to many lists, reads/clicks, unsubscribes to study flows, and keeps "swipe files" of
  email copy he adapts. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-02-25] Niching down: when Creative Market (acquired by Autodesk) grew and brush-
  script fonts / watercolor illustrations trended, he chose to double down on retro rather
  than chase the "mass migration," accepting he'd either go bankrupt or win big by being
  the one who doubled down. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-25] Customer research is the engine: best-selling products came from emailing
  customers "if I could make anything for you what would it be?", then building what the
  most people ask for — "stop guessing and start listening." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-02-25] Cites marketer/author Jeff Walker's one-question survey ("if there was one
  thing that had to be inside a product for you to buy it, what would it be?"); you can
  paste customers' own words back into your sales copy — "how do you not buy that when it
  describes exactly... what they asked for?" (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-02-25] "Start now and be prepared to fail." Cites a songwriter's claim that it
  takes ~100 songs to write a good one; success comes from consistency, not talent —
  release predictably. When he started, his only goal was to release something every week,
  and people came to expect it "every Tuesday at 3:00." (paraphrase + quote)
