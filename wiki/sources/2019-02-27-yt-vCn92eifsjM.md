# Inbound Marketing – How to get Clients to Come to You

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains Chris Do's core, self-contained articulation of his inbound/differentiation playbook with named metaphors ("race to the top vs. race to zero," the campfire + magnifying glass, the "tip of the spear," "more money than time"). Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/content-strategy/` and `wiki/topics/sales-clients/`, plus dated entries in `persona/beliefs.md`.

## Summary

Answering a question about how to graduate from Fiverr to "real" clients, Chris Do argues the fix is a marketing problem: you must differentiate yourself in a way that is *meaningful to the specific clients you want* — not to you or your peers. If the untrained client eye can't tell you apart from competitors, they buy on price and you're "doomed to race to zero"; the way to "race to the top" is a sharp, specific niche. He uses a campfire/magnifying-glass metaphor: focus your limited energy on one narrow point to "start the fire," then broaden once the fire is going. He recommends producing evergreen content (video blogs, LinkedIn posts) around a narrow specialty so an ideal prospect lands and thinks "I can't believe this exists, this is exactly what I need." Finally: target clients with **more money than time** — high enough to write checks to solve problems, not so high they intimidate you — and be the person who says "tell me what the problems are and I will fix them." A co-host adds a tactical lead-gen tip: search for companies in your vertical that *just got funded* and make content aimed at them.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-02-27] Getting off Fiverr and winning real clients is fundamentally a **marketing** problem: "you have to do some marketing," and the marketing piece is differentiating yourself meaningfully. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-27] The differentiation must be meaningful **to the clients you want to work with** — "if it's meaningful to you and your peers it doesn't matter" if the target market doesn't see the difference. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-27] When clients can't tell competitors apart, the one thing they *do* understand is price; they'll pick someone near the middle almost purely on price. If you look the same as everyone else, "you're doomed to race to zero… you have to race to the top." (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-27] There is no silver bullet for the differentiator because it's different for everyone, but it must be "really specific." (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-27] Vagueness "screams desperation": he cites meeting someone who said "I'm a designer," then "web design… interior design… sometimes theater sets" — doing anything and everything. Your marketing's "tip of the spear needs to be pointy, pointy, pointy." (paraphrase; "screams desperation" and "pointy pointy pointy" are near-verbatim)
- [2019-02-27] You can broaden *after* winning a client — "even if once you take on a client you do other things too, that's fine" because you've built a one-on-one relationship and trust — but the *marketing* stays sharply focused. (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-27] **Campfire / magnifying-glass metaphor:** scattered kindling won't ignite on its own; focus a magnifying glass on one point and you start a fire. Focus is how beginners turn limited resources into a fledgling business; once the campfire is going you can "put the magnifying glass down." (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-27] Create **evergreen content** — pieces that "will continue to benefit you in a year, two years, three years" — but don't mass-produce content "until you know that you're on to something." (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-27] If you're going to pick a target market, "you might as well pick the one with the most money that you feel like you can confidently interact with." (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-27] Target a market where you can "operate as a peer and a partner" with people at the highest levels — not so high you feel intimidated, but high enough that they'd rather solve problems "by writing checks." (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-27] The more money someone has, the less they value a given amount ("$10 to me when I was 18 was worth a lot more… than it is now"); what wealthy clients value instead is their **time**. Find people with "more money than time." (paraphrase)
- [2019-02-27] Position yourself as the problem-solver: "not tell me what to do, but tell me what the problems are and I will fix them" — such clients "write you checks all day long" because you save them time. (paraphrase; near-verbatim)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do:**

- "You have to differentiate yourself from all the other people in a way that's meaningful to the people who you want to work with."
- "If you look the same to the clients that you want to work with, you're doomed to race to zero. There's no way around it — you have to race to the top."
- "In your marketing, the tip of the spear needs to be pointy, pointy, pointy — that is sharp, focused."
- "If you take a magnifying glass and focus the energy on a particular point, you can light a piece of wood on fire… that focus is what you need to do to start the fire."
- "You might as well pick the one [target market] with the most money that you feel like you can confidently interact with."
- "You want to find people who have more money than time — who are not so high up that you feel intimidated by them, but are high enough up that they would really like to solve problems by writing checks."
- "Not 'tell me what to do,' but 'tell me what the problems are and I will fix them'… and they'll write you checks all day long because you're solving problems for them that save them time."
