# Stop Losing Money – How to Find Your Break Even Point

★ **L3-candidate** (topic wiki, not persona) — Contains a named, self-contained financial framework: the **break-even calculation for a creative/design business**. It is a strong candidate for a `wiki/topics/business/` (and `pricing`) page. NOTE: the framework is delivered by the **guest, Errol Gerson**, not by Chris Do — so it does NOT train the persona. ## Summary

An interview-format episode in which Chris Do questions **Errol Gerson** — a business consultant/accountant who has advised creatives for 47+ years — about managing a creative business's money. Gerson's core message: don't try to *predict* income (impossible, "like predicting the stock market"); instead know your **break-even point**. He gives a concrete formula — sum your fixed expenses (all expenses + salary + income tax + retirement), divide by your 0.5 (50%) design-business profit margin to get required monthly revenue, then divide that by your blended hourly rate to get how many hours you must bill each month to break even. He also urges keeping **six months of fixed overhead** in the bank to survive recessions, and offers a pay-cut-over-layoffs tactic. Chris adds that as you charge more, sales cycles lengthen, which is *why* you need cash runway, and recounts hitting a "sense of peace" at three months' runway in his own business.

## Key claims

### Errol Gerson (guest — context only, NOT persona training)

- [2019-03-04] You cannot predict monthly income any more than you can predict the stock market — "you wet your finger and hold it up and see which way the wind's blowing." Don't predict income; know your break-even instead. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-04] **Profit** is "the residual of revenue minus expenses." (quote/definition)
- [2019-03-04] A design business's profit margin is usually **50%**. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-04] **Break-even formula:** take fixed overhead = all expenses + salary + income tax + retirement; divide that number by 0.5 (the 50% margin) to get required monthly revenue (his worked example: "$12,000"); then divide $12,000 by your blended average hourly rate to get the number of hours the company must bill each month to be profitable. Then ask: is that a realistic number? (paraphrase) — *Arithmetic check: dividing fixed costs by 0.5 doubles them, i.e. at a 50% margin you need 2× fixed costs in revenue to cover them; internally consistent.*
- [2019-03-04] Not knowing your break-even is "like flying an airplane" without instruments. Knowing it makes your sense of realism and control over the business "skyrocket." (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-04] Keep **six months of fixed overhead** in the bank; if you can save it and not touch it, you get peace and confidence and will "survive any recession, no question." (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-04] In a downturn, laying people off is not the only option: sit the whole staff down and propose an across-the-board **10–12% pay cut** so everyone can stay together. (paraphrase) — cites the **1976 recession**, when firms had to turn to savings to survive.
- [2019-03-04] Large design firms also use a bank **line of credit** (e.g. a $50–80k revolving line) once profitable, but it must be paid back and zeroed out by year-end. (paraphrase)

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- [2019-03-04] The Futur's standing advice is **"charge more"** (repeated for emphasis). (quote/refrain — consistent with established Chris position)
- [2019-03-04] As you charge more, the **time it takes to close a sale gets longer**: you can close a $500 sale in a day, but a $50,000 or $500,000 sale takes months. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-04] In his own business, sales cycles at the ~$10,000–$15,000 deal size ran one to two months to close, which is why he wanted more **runway in the bank**. (paraphrase, self-referential)
- [2019-03-04] He and his team had one deal worth a **quarter-million dollars that took over a year to close** — not enough urgency, but a good project, so they held on. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-04] Hitting **three months of runway** gave him a felt "sense of peace" and lifted a burden; six months would be even better. (paraphrase, joke: "I wouldn't even come to work — just kidding")
- [2019-03-04] Mission framing: money and public speaking are people's two biggest fears; he wants to put the audience "in the driver's seat" with the **business acumen and language** so they don't flinch when clients bring up money. (paraphrase — recurring Futur mission theme)
