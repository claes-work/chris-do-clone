## Summary

Two unnamed Futur instructors discuss how a creative studio should manage clients
during project execution. The core principle they repeat is "the process is the
product": be collaborative and radically transparent with clients (give them a project
overview, milestones, and a schedule; tell them after each post what happened, what's
next, and what feedback is needed) — but do not let clients dictate the engagement, set
milestones, or tell you how to do your job. They cover a "decision tree" logging habit
(subject-line versioning like "logo posting 1 of 3"), building client-feedback windows
into Gantt charts so slippage is visibly the client's fault, handling missed milestones
(client forfeits a round of revisions), "bending"/reallocating slack between
deliverables without sacrificing the team, and managing conflicting decision-makers by
making stakeholders "fight it out" and forcing one clear prioritized direction.

### Unlabeled Futur team members (dialogue)

- [2019-03-06] "The process is the product" — invoked as the reason to be collaborative
  and transparent with clients. (quote fragment)
- [2019-03-06] Be transparent (put clients in your project-management tool, send them a
  timeline) but do not let them dictate the engagement, set milestones, or tell you how
  to do your job; give them only as much visibility as they need to make their
  decisions. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] At project start, give the client an overview/breakdown of all major
  steps, milestones, and a schedule beginning-to-end; then after every post tell them
  what just happened, what's next, and what feedback you need for this round.
  (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] Use versioned email subject lines (e.g. "logo posting 1 of 3") so the
  process state is unambiguous — a trick attributed to **Ben**. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] Keep a "decision tree" — a log of every decision made with the client
  through the project — so that if a client later gives contradictory feedback (e.g.
  round three contradicts round one) you can point back to the earlier decision and ask
  how to proceed. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] Build client-feedback windows into Gantt charts/timelines (e.g. "3 days,
  5 days") and share them, so a late decision visibly delays the project — "it's kind of
  your fault" — which keeps the client in check. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] They typically give clients three rounds of revisions per deliverable; if
  a client misses giving feedback on a milestone, they lose a round of revision toward
  the end, keeping the deadline intact — and clients accept this because they needed the
  extra time. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] "Bending": if one deliverable goes smoothly (approved in a single post),
  reallocate its leftover time/money to a deliverable that needs more resources — but be
  transparent about what that costs (e.g. removing a round of revision because a delay
  cost production days). (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] Don't "bend over" for clients all the time — negotiate only where it makes
  sense and doesn't hit your bottom line; don't sacrifice your team or your time because
  the client didn't do their half. It's a half-and-half collaboration and you must be a
  "good shepherd" for both team and client. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] Managing conflicting decision-makers: when Mary, John, and Bobby give
  contradictory feedback, tell them transparently you heard three different things, need
  one clear decision to take back to your team, and ask which is priority — then let the
  stakeholders "fight it out." (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] If stakeholders are bickering, you can step in as a neutral third party
  with a recommendation ("I recommend Direction B over Direction A... of course you're
  the decision makers"). (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] Nine times out of ten the decision makers don't even realize they're
  disagreeing or that their requests conflict; surfacing the feedback and asking them to
  prioritize "works every time." (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] Ideally have one point of contact; if you get multiple feedback channels,
  consolidate ("Mary said this, John said this, Bobby said this") and force a clear
  direction to move forward. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-06] Closing plug for The Futur's *Practical Project Management* course.
  (paraphrase)
