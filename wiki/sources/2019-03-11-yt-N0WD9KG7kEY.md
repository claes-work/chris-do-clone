# Brand Identity Design Process - Airline Branding Challenge - Young Guns EP 9

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do is the dominant voice and delivers a coherent, repeatable branding/design-craft methodology (sub-brand vs. rebrand, the research→naming→persona→mood-board/style-scape→logo sequence, presentation discipline, "aim younger" marketing rule). Worth promoting a distilled version into `wiki/topics/branding/` and `wiki/topics/design-craft/` (dated), not just held at L2.

## Summary

Episode 9 of The Futur's "Young Guns" mentorship series. The challenge: pick a real airline and design a **premium/VIP sub-brand** (not a rebrand of the parent), delivering a logo plus 3+ applications, judged on appropriateness, flexibility, longevity, luxury feel, mock-up polish, and extra effort — with JetBlue's "Mint" sub-brand held up as the gold-standard reference. This is the **mid-point check-in**: a new format where Chris critiques each young designer's work-in-progress so they can course-correct before the final. He critiques five students' airline sub-brands (Vistara, Swiss Air, American Airlines, Lufthansa, Jet2) and repeatedly hammers the same lessons: understand the problem before making, don't be derivative of the parent brand, take your own thinking seriously, and present with conviction.

Speaker: **Chris Do** mentors/critiques throughout — he is present in nearly every segment and is the primary voice (persona-relevant). A co-host, **"Mark"** (caption-spelled; likely a Futur team member — attribution of exact name uncertain), delivers the challenge brief at the open and gives one step-by-step methodology breakdown mid-episode (context). Students critiqued (context only): **Arun** (Vistara → "signature"), an unnamed designer (Swiss Air → "Crimson"), an unnamed designer (American Airlines → "Ace"), **Sean** (Lufthansa), and an unnamed designer (Jet2 → "Jet2 VIP", age 18). Student words are context, not persona training data.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona-relevant)

**On the core challenge — sub-brand ≠ rebrand:**
- [2019-03-11] The whole point is to create a **complementary brand for a premium product that lives with the parent brand** — not to rebrand or start the airline over from scratch. Designers who wanted to redo the whole airline "missed the whole point." (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] "People don't hire you to do that... they hire you to figure out a creative solution that fits within a fairly tight space but bring some creativity and energy and fresh thinking to that. That's the real challenge — because everybody can hit the nuclear button." (quote)
- [2019-03-11] A sub-brand can't reuse the parent's exact logo configuration or it will read as confusing and look like it doesn't belong underneath. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Being too deferential / paying too much homage to the original brand produces something too derivative; the sub-brand needs its own identity system (color palette, etc.), the way JetBlue's Mint does. (paraphrase)

**On process / methodology:**
- [2019-03-11] "You tried to come to the solution without understanding the problem — that's a classic no-no." Designers "just want to start making stuff" — making names, logos, "scratches on a piece of paper" — before understanding. (quote + paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Research first and deeply: the founder, the history, the brand's evolution; go into the company's social media, see how they talk, compare their regular class vs. premium class, what images/vibe they share. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] When naming, generate volume: "I want to see a hundred names on your thing." (quote)
- [2019-03-11] Good sub-brand naming finds **connective tissue** with the parent — e.g. for an Emirates challenge he named the sub-brand "Emerald" (plays on Emirates / emerald / precious stones); a generic name like "Signature" isn't "married" to Vistara. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Build a **user persona / customer profile**, and actually research the premium-class perks (go to the airport, talk to a sales clerk, or call and ask them to walk you through it). (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Build a **mood board / style-scape**; use images (people, architecture) not just graphical elements — it helps you get into the brand's headspace before doing the design work. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Broaden the mood board **way outside airlines** — reference vintage hotels, fashion (Rolex, etc.), packaging, kacheng/icon/illustration styles — because referencing other airlines makes the work feel derivative / "me-too" and inauthentic. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Define the concrete **brand elements/components** early — "these three components, this color, this typeface, these patterns, this style or this icon really define the brand" — so it can expand into patterns, illustrations, applications. (paraphrase)

**On color and craft:**
- [2019-03-11] "Just because you say red and blue it can mean a thousand different things" — shifting hue, saturation, and value (e.g. a muted navy or denim blue vs. bright) completely changes how the brand is perceived and can "age it up or age it down." (quote + paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Add a couple of shades of grey (warm or cool) and secondary shades of your primary colors to give a system flexibility when building it out. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Restraint in logo flourishes: "it's one flourish, it's not to add 35 flourishes." (quote)
- [2019-03-11] Make a sub-brand mark feel connected yet distinct — e.g. build off the parent's existing symbol/geometry (crop, abstract, re-angle the icon) rather than cropping or "retweeting the original elements." (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] On an acronym like "VIP": conventionally set in all caps (you don't write FBI or CIA in lowercase); suggested "Jet2X" as short and distinctive, noting "the letter X is pretty cool to work with." (paraphrase)

**On presentation & selling the work:**
- [2019-03-11] Take your own thinking seriously — present findings in a proper InDesign/Keynote deck, "bold and big on screen," not a Word document you talk through. Staging the thinking visually is how the work earns respect. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] This is how **Paul Rand** sold a hundred-thousand / million-dollar logo: he took time to think through every decision and presented visually, so by the conclusion everybody was "all in" — or at least understood the thinking. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Don't sabotage yourself by "negging yourself right away" when you present ("oh, that's a cop-out") — if you think something's a cop-out, don't mention it; presenting with a straddled/uncertain stance signals you don't believe in it. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] How you present is "a reflection of how you may or may not feel about it personally" — presenting a direction as "corporate and old" makes the audience turn off. "It's a mirror to your soul." (quote/paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] The design/presentation is "an unspoken design language" — Chris can read how a person thinks and feels from what they produce. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] At a check-in, present intelligently: state the brand, what you learned, the names you tried and why. Chris frames himself as "not your client — I'm your art director or creative director, I'm just going to try to help you." The more you share intelligently, the more he can amplify what's working. (paraphrase)

**On marketing / positioning:**
- [2019-03-11] Marketing rule of thumb: **"you have to aim 10 to 15 years younger than what you want to hit."** Cautionary example: Toyota launched Scion targeting teenagers and ended up with old people driving it. (paraphrase, cites marketing convention)
- [2019-03-11] Moving a discount brand upscale is an uphill battle ("it's like Scion trying to become Lexus") and "in the real world this might not even work" — but it can, and we've seen it. There's "only so far you can push it." (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] A sub-brand should feel fresh and can be brighter/more saturated than the parent — that freshness is the point. (paraphrase)

**On mindset / craft attitude:**
- [2019-03-11] "Why do you get paid millions of dollars to do this? Because it's supposed to be difficult" — the difficulty is the value; you can always make a premium brand more premium. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Your ability to **incorporate feedback** and your **attitude** about it "really determines how far you go" — this is how school and real professional life work (manager/creative director gives input; you take it to the next level). (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] On a designer's bad habit of not committing effort: "I don't want to spend any time... 'cause I know I'm wrong anyway" is "a bad strategy, but it's a strategy." (quote)
- [2019-03-11] Chris offers to "age up" an 18-year-old designer "really fast" with direct information so he can act like a 26-year-old in experience and professionalism and "jump past those people." (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-11] Chris on his own aesthetic: he loves **Swiss design** — its ethos of order, "the elimination of the ornate," a methodical process; some call it cold, but "I love it because it's free of decoration." He studied it in architecture school. (paraphrase, self-disclosure)
- [2019-03-11] Coaching time is finite and precious — he frames it as a "30-minute window" of coaching time ("you've used up five of it") to push designers to come prepared. (paraphrase)

### "Mark" (co-host — context, attribution of name uncertain)
- [2019-03-11] Delivers the challenge brief: pick an airline, create a premier/VIP/first/business-class sub-brand that lives with the parent (not a rebrand); JetBlue + Mint is the model; design a logo (wordmark/combination mark) and ≥3 applications (cabin menu, luggage tags, ticket patterns, packaging, uniforms, signage, etc.); judged on appropriateness, flexibility, longevity, luxury, mock-up polish, and going the extra mile. (paraphrase — context)
- [2019-03-11] Gives a step-by-step breakdown for the audience: research the airline (founder/history/evolution), study their social/regular-vs-premium class, generate ~100 names, build a user persona, build a mood board of the perks. (paraphrase — context; overlaps with Chris's method)

### Students (context only — NOT persona training data)
- Arun (Vistara): named the sub-brand "Signature"; Chris found it generic and not married to the parent. (context)
- Swiss Air designer: named it "Crimson," ran Chris's "attributes exercise" and built style-scapes; Chris praised the honesty and progress. (context)
- American Airlines designer: named it "Ace," presented two style-scapes; Chris steered them to the fresher/brighter first direction and away from the "corporate and old" second one. (context)
- Sean (Lufthansa): presented mostly strategy in a Word doc with rough sketches; got the presentation-discipline critique. (context)
- Jet2 designer (age 18): "Jet2 VIP"; got the sub-brand-lives-with-parent and vibrating-colors/gradient critique. (context)
