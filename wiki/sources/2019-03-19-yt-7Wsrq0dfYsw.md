# Convert Cold Leads into Paying Clients

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris teaches a named, self-contained framework: the three **lead temperatures (hot / warm / cold)** and the matched communication style, question, and bridge for each. It is largely adapted from **Russell Brunson's** work (attributed by Chris in the video), so it is a borrowed framework, not original to Chris — but it is a coherent lead-gen model worth promoting into `wiki/topics/sales-clients/` or `marketing/` with clear third-party attribution.

## Summary

Chris Do explains how to classify leads by "temperature" — hot, warm, or cold — based on how much the prospect already knows and trusts you, and prescribes a different communication approach for each. Hot leads (already aware of and trust you, on your email list, established relationship) need only to be told *what the product is*, using a personality-driven, story-sharing style. Warm leads (a common reference point / mutual connection, or reached via social media) need the *solution*, delivered by building a bridge through a trusted endorsement or affiliate. Cold leads (the hardest and most common in marketing/lead-gen) don't know you, your offer, or whether to trust you, and must be given clarity — first defining *the problem* in words they already understand before moving them up the awareness ladder toward the product.

Speaker: single-speaker monologue by **Chris Do** (first person plural "we," references The Futur's 30,000+ email subscribers and 700+ videos, addresses the YouTube audience directly). No co-host or guest speaks. **Russell Brunson** is cited as the source of the framework/book, not present.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-03-19] Not all leads are equal; you must understand the "temperature" of a customer as they enter your funnel, which depends on what they saw or thought about *before* arriving. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-19] "Preframing" or "priming" — the mindset a person is in before becoming aware of what you do — shapes the lead. E.g. someone who followed a link from an award site praising your studio arrives ready to go: a hot lead. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-19] **Hot lead** = someone who already knows you, is aware of you, has seen your work, has read about you / seen you mentioned, is on your email list, and/or has an established relationship (e.g. has watched The Futur's 700+ videos and knows what you stand for). (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-19] For a hot lead, use a **personality-driven communication style** — be yourself, share stories, share opinions, even reveal a bit of your private life. The question you answer for them is: **what is the product?** (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-19] **Warm lead** = someone who doesn't know you but might know someone you know (a common reference point, "six degrees of separation"); often arriving via Facebook, Instagram, or Twitter. There's general awareness but not full knowledge of you. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-19] For a warm lead, **satisfy the desire** by building a bridge — get an endorsement from a trusted source you both know; affiliate programs are a great tool to capture warm leads. The question you answer is: **what is the solution?** (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-19] **Cold lead** = someone who has no idea who you are, doesn't know what you offer, and doesn't know if they can trust you. This is the hardest to convert and where *most* people fit when you talk about marketing and lead generation. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-19] For a cold lead, **provide clarity**: first understand and name the problem, using language broad enough that they recognize it — you may need to bridge from the words they understand into the industry terms you'd normally use (e.g. their "pain point" vs. your "strategy" as the solution). The question you answer is: **what is the problem?** (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-19] Cites Russell Brunson's book: to move someone from cold to hot when they're unaware of you or even the problem, first tell them the problem in terms they understand, build a bridge from general questions to something more specific, move them from problem-aware to solution-aware, then tell them the product and a personal story. (paraphrase, third-party framework attributed to Russell Brunson)
- [2019-03-19] The Futur has over 30,000 email subscribers and over 700 videos on the channel — subscribers and long-time viewers are examples of hot leads. (paraphrase, self-reported metric as of 2019-03-19)
