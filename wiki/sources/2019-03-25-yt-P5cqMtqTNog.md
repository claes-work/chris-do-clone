## Key claims

### Chris Do (mentor / critic) — all [2019-03-25]

**On process discipline & showing your work**
- Getting "lost in the woods" mid-project happens to everyone; he wishes designers had shown him more at the midpoint so he could give feedback earlier. Committing to design work early and "putting yourself out there" is how you earn the feedback that improves it. (paraphrase)
- Show your design **in context / in use** (in a mock-up, in the real world) rather than only flat on screen — it lets you and the client properly evaluate it and make considerations. Called this "a good tip for all you guys." (paraphrase)

**On logos & brand extension**
- A strong brand-extension strategy is to take the parent brand's mark, "pull some of that DNA out," and reconstitute it into something new — related, sharing DNA, but not derivative/a copy of the original. Praised Arun's deconstruction of the Vistara star into a lotus mark, and Sean's abstracted "A" for American, as examples. (paraphrase)
- Marks he looks for are "solid, simple, recognizable" — "all those things that we look for in a mark." (quote fragment)
- A "**slap-on logo**" is when "you take a logo, you slap it on something" with nothing else done to it — a weakness. Fix by integrating pattern, edge treatments, or design elements so it feels considered. (paraphrase + quote)

**On kerning / typography (letter-spacing method)**
- Method for kerning a wordmark: find the **most difficult kerning pairs first** and design/space everything else around them. Think of it as **volume, not linear space** — "pouring sand into" the gaps and asking how much sand each gap holds; balance the volumes. (paraphrase)
- Diagonal letters next to open letters (e.g. next to a "T," or the leg of an "R" beside an "e") create hard kerning problems. (paraphrase)
- Letter-spacing is "one of the most foundational skill sets you're gonna have." (quote fragment)
- Customize a stock typeface with a small tweak (e.g. drop the leg of the "R," or set an oversized initial cap) so "it feels like they can own it" — otherwise it just looks like you typed something in. (paraphrase)
- "**Letting**" (leading — spacing between lines) that's too open makes you "read the negative space between the lines"; tighten body copy so the air doesn't break up the read. (paraphrase; caption spells "letting")
- Type rules he gave Sean: don't use too many different point sizes or weights; align and match sizes for a cleaner result. (paraphrase)
- He is "never a fan of centered type" for premium collateral — it "feels a little lazy... too easy." Break away from default centered layouts. (paraphrase + quote)

**On negative space / "white space"**
- Reframes "white space" as "**negative space**": it does NOT have to be white. Black can be negative space, gold can be negative space — negative space just means "there's not a lot of activity going on." Many people wrongly equate premium white space with literal white. (paraphrase + quote)
- Too much copy all at similar size kills negative space; break the hierarchy up or make elements smaller so the layout can "breathe." (paraphrase)
- Three colors in three chunks of information is "too much." (paraphrase)

**On appropriateness (matching the brand to its audience)**
- Critiqued Arun's pastel "Serenity" style-scape: a mature, well-traveled businessman in the imagery clashes with a pastel palette that "feels like it could be a wellness spa... a makeup brand" — a "feminine cosmetic beauty salon vibe" that's **not appropriate** to the premium-airline brief. Even if executed perfectly, wrong tone = "missed the mark." (paraphrase)
- A premium travel ad that literally shows "somebody sleeping in the clouds" is the typical, cliché airline image to steer away from; prefer something "more surreal or abstract or representative of the feeling of premium class." (paraphrase)

**On premium / print & finishing techniques**
- To signal premium in print, use finishing techniques: **metallic hot-foil stamping** (should "sparkle," catch the eye, have "a brilliance"), colored/gold or silver card edges, vellum overlays, hot-wax seals, handwritten notes, embossing. On his "emerald" project with Emily they used every print trick "in an imaginary world [with] an infinite budget." A color copy printed on card is what premium is NOT. (paraphrase)
- "All these little touches, these little printing techniques... can add a lot of that premium feel." (quote)
- Subtlety reads as premium: a barely-visible embroidered monogram on a uniform cuff is "classy," whereas overt monogramming "can get a little cheesy." (paraphrase)

**On mock-ups & art direction**
- Choosing the right photograph "is everything" — praised Sharif color-correcting a passport to read as Swiss, which "completes the image." (paraphrase)
- Bad art direction / stock-looking photography, and clouds "comped in... like you slapped a layer on top, hit screen in Photoshop and called it a day," undercut the work. (paraphrase + quote)
- Praised Spencer's 3D-extruded metal letterform mock-up as "the best-looking mock-up we've seen thus far" — convincing metallic finish, reflections, beveled edges. (paraphrase)
- On a billboard/large format: a pure-white overlaid mark looks slapped on; making it a translucent spot-varnish / etched-glass effect (seeing the image through it, slight diffraction) reads as designed, not applied. (paraphrase)
- Billboards: cut the fine-print "legal mumbo-jumbo" — you have only "a few seconds" to read a billboard; make the headline 1.5–2× bigger; use selective focus/blur + darkening to aid legibility. (paraphrase)

**On strategy & clarity (the Connor / Jet2 critique)**
- Choosing a super-budget airline for a premium brief is self-limiting — "you have to find your own brief in a way that's very limiting." Whatever company you pick, if you got stuck, "who do you have to blame? ...that person is looking at you right back in the mirror." (paraphrase + quote)
- Hates "**premium economy**" — the two words "don't even belong together, it's like jumbo shrimp"; it's "marketing speak to sell you a slightly more expensive seat." Put me in economy or business, not the in-between. (paraphrase + quote)
- If you commit to a deliberately low-rent / "undesigned design" look, you must **follow it through consistently** — mixing utilitarian with decorative retro (twine, paper tags) lands you "stuck in no-man's land." (paraphrase)
- "Undesigned design" (making it look like nobody designed it) is very hard to pull off and still look like you know what you're doing. "It's hard to make things very simple look good, and that's why not very many people are successful." (paraphrase + quote)
- Massimo Vignelli (caption: "Massimo big Nellie / McNally") achieved high-end feel using just Helvetica, one or two colors, kept minimal and disciplined — a model for restraint. (paraphrase; name garbled in captions → `attribution: uncertain` on exact reference)
- Cited **Damien Hirst** (caption: "Damien hearse") using pharmaceutical-packaging inspiration — changing scale, cropping, limited palette — as a route to beautiful utilitarian design. (paraphrase)
- Sub-brand consistency: don't remove the parent name ("jet") from the logo while keeping it everywhere else; the child brand must be legibly tied to the parent, not "the same but different just to be different." (paraphrase)

**On coaching young designers / talent**
- Recalled three types of students from 15 years teaching at Art Center: the open "sponge" who tries everything and follows the teacher; the one who says "I know what's good for me / my career / my portfolio" and does their own thing; and those in between. Chose not to "tame that wild stallion" (Connor) — "let it run" and see the results. (paraphrase)
- Encouraged the youngest/rawest talent (Sean) to ignore age, "cool," and whether they went to school — "how good you are... there's some really raw deep talented person inside of you that's waiting to scream and break free." (paraphrase + quote)
- Doing work you have fun with shows in the output and "is really important." (paraphrase)
- Aspirational business note: airline identity systems are very hard, "that's why very few companies do it and they get big bucks — so if you want to make the big bucks, aim high." (paraphrase + quote)
