## Summary

Livestream interview in which Chris Do hosts an Adobe Stock visual-trends forecaster who
presents Adobe's "four major digital trends for 2019": **Natural Instincts**, **Creative
Democracy**, **Disruptive Expression**, and **Brand Stand**. The guest illustrates each
with campaign examples (Google home tech, Tommy Hilfiger's Drive collection, Nike's
"Dream Crazier" and Colin Kaepernick campaigns, Pampers "Love the Change") and Adobe
Stock search-term growth data. Chris's contribution is host-side: sharp framing questions
(is a "brand stand" genuine or opportunistic?) and a closing reframe that "trend" is the
wrong word — the real point is being **visually fluent** and **culturally relevant**.

**Speaker:** Two speakers. **Guest (Adobe Stock forecaster, name uncertain — likely Brenda
Milis)** delivers ~90% of the substance (the trends framework, all data, all examples).
**Chris Do** is the host: introduction, questions, and end-of-show recap. Nearly all
teaching content is guest-attributed and does not feed the persona.

## Key claims

### Guest (Adobe Stock forecaster — name uncertain; does NOT train persona)

- [2019-03-28] Adobe releases a "visual trends forecast" each year before the year begins;
  it combines qualitative and quantitative research to predict where viewer/audience
  interest is scaling. Culture is changing faster than ever ("content velocity"), so
  creatives must be "visually fluent." (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-28] **Trend 1 — Natural Instincts:** a reaction to the growing role of
  technology; people (consciously or not) seek balance via imagery of nature, natural
  makers/ingredients (crystals, organic food), and inner/spiritual connection ("the most
  evolved moment of wellness"). Even home-tech brands (e.g., Google) adopt earthy tones and
  rounded, tactile forms to participate. Adobe search growth cited: veganism +93%. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-28] **Trend 2 — Creative Democracy:** born from smartphones and user-friendly
  creative apps; a democratized creativity started by Gen Z / millennials sharing authentic
  snapshots. Elements: authenticity, diversity, vivid color, color blocking. As of 2018,
  **social — not fashion — now dictates color trends**, a major shift. Search growth cited:
  Gen Z +207%. Video and YouTube are the dominant, most-retentive asset type. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-28] **Trend 3 — Disruptive Expression:** a more intense branch of Creative
  Democracy — confident, passionate, sometimes aggressive personal/artistic expression
  (graffiti-like) that cuts through the media barrage and holds viewer attention. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-28] **Trend 4 — Brand Stand:** imagery conveying a brand's role/purpose on
  social and environmental issues; brand loyalty is increasingly determined by business
  practices, not just products. Must be **authentic and on-brand** or audiences (especially
  Gen Z/millennials) will call it out. Examples: Nike/Kaepernick, Nike "Dream Crazier,"
  Pampers "Love the Change." "Good for people, planet, and profits." (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-28] A single image can belong to more than one trend (e.g., "Dream Crazier" is
  both Disruptive Expression and Brand Stand). (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-28] "Trend" is a troublesome word: a visual trend is not fashion (here today,
  gone tomorrow) — it is an image type that has *risen* in appeal to a tipping point of
  mass/mainstream interest, and usually stays around a long time. (paraphrase)
- [2019-03-28] Diversity and inclusion are no longer a "trend" but a baseline social
  standard running through all four trends. (paraphrase)

### Chris Do (host — the few genuinely Chris-attributed points)

- [2019-03-28] Presses the guest on cynicism about Brand Stand: it's easy to ask whether a
  stand is genuine or just the marketing department chasing what's trending; brands risk
  being seen as jumping on a trend. (paraphrase, framing question)
- [2019-03-28] Reframes "trend": if you call a design "trendy" that's a *negative* — it means
  you jumped on something already over, or something dated. There's a separate, valid sense:
  a shift in what counts as contemporary, where being off it means you're "unaware" and "not
  fluent." He proposes dropping the word "trend" for **"visually fluent"** and **"culturally
  relevant."** (paraphrase — his own reframe of the guest's material)
- [2019-03-28] Closing takeaways he emphasizes: **over-produced feels inauthentic**; give
  people a behind-the-scenes peek; you can have a great idea and use *stock* to tell the
  story rather than producing everything yourself; a brand stand must be "consistent with
  the culture" of the company, not "controversy for controversy's sake." (paraphrase, recap)
