# How do I Market my Product? — w/ Melinda Livsey Ep. 16

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains two named, reusable Chris Do ideas worth promoting: (1) the "sell the problem, not the solution" / agitate-the-problem marketing thesis (people are motivated by loss/FOMO, not gain), and (2) the "write your thesis paper" content-differentiation method (deep research + novel packaging → one product/career, illustrated with Seth Godin's Icarus and Simon Sinek's Wright-brothers stories). Both belong in `wiki/topics/marketing/` and `wiki/topics/content-strategy/` and in `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), not just L2.

## Summary

Episode 16 of Chris Do's ongoing coaching series with Futur colleague Melinda Livsey. Melinda wants to launch a brand-strategy kit / e-course and asks how to market and sell it. Chris reframes the whole problem: before selling the solution (strategy), she must deeply understand and speak to the *problem* her audience feels — the "design slave" / order-taker who was sold a creative dream that didn't materialize. He demonstrates by improvising the exact empathetic marketing monologue he'd say to a young, frustrated designer, then unpacks the principles behind it: get clarity on one archetype (composite user profile), agitate the problem à la Steve Jobs / Tony Robbins until the audience is compelled to act, and remember people move on fear of loss (FOMO) more than gain. On differentiation, he pushes Melinda to pick a lane against The Futur's own established offering ("different but better"), and pitches the "write a thesis paper" method: pick one profound idea, do deep original research, and package old material in a novel way (Seth Godin's Icarus, Simon Sinek's Wright brothers). He closes on his own current project — researching the history of commerce/currency to build an airtight case that selling time is irrational — as a live example of winning people "with the heart and with the logic."

Speaker: two-person dialogue between **Chris Do** (subject) and **Melinda Livsey** (recurring Futur colleague — context only, NOT the subject). Only Chris-attributed material trains the persona.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-04-01] Marketing a product starts before the product: first identify *who has a problem worth solving* and understand that person deeply. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] You must educate the audience on the *problem* even more than on the solution — most people "get caught up in the solution." (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] "Sell the problem before the solution": paint a rich, vivid picture of the pain (the frustrated order-taker / "design slave" who was sold a creative dream and lost all agency) so the prospect recognizes themselves and only then reveal the solution (strategy = "a plan of action to get from point A to point B," deciding *with* the client, not *for* them). (paraphrase; demonstrated live as an improvised marketing monologue)
- [2019-04-01] Clarity of audience defeats "speaker's block" — cites Seth Godin: when you know exactly who you're talking to, you just speak to somebody. (paraphrase, cites Seth Godin)
- [2019-04-01] When he wrote the monologue he wasn't talking to Melinda but to a "composite version" built from hundreds of correspondences across YouTube, Twitter, and Instagram — build an archetype / user profile from real audience comments. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] People are motivated more by what they have to lose than what they have to gain — FOMO / fear of missing out drives action; people "won't take action because they have something to gain, but they'll take action because they're missing out on something." (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] Aim marketing at people *earlier* in their journey (still in school, just starting to sense something is missing), not only at those already convinced and ready to buy — "there are fewer of those people out there," and the ready-to-go ones will comparison-shop you against bigger competitors. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] Technique: describe vividly what school/the status quo is genuinely good for; by implication the audience infers what it's *not* good for, without you dissing it. Cites Steve Jobs' first iPhone reveal (via Nancy Duarte's TED talk) — Jobs described "the way things are" for a long time to build tension/frustration before revealing the product. (paraphrase, cites Steve Jobs / Nancy Duarte)
- [2019-04-01] On positioning vs. an established competitor: "I'm forcing you to pick a lane." The Futur has 12 employees and a 4-year head start selling this; if Melinda sets up "two ice cream shops" (same offer) she's in trouble, but if she sells something genuinely different she wins. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] "You have to be different but better — because different for different is okay [not enough]." (near-quote / paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] Introduce your differentiation/positioning early in the sales cycle, not at the end — wait until the end and the prospect just says "fantastic, let's go to Chris [the bigger player]." (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] As a strategist he'd buy and study *everyone's* material he respected, then synthesize his own version — differentiation comes from "the way in which it's processed," each person's distinct thinking; why we read five books on the same topic. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] Critiques the "teach people how to do what you're doing" model (e.g. Lewis Howes selling a course on webinars *via* a webinar — "inception"): it teaches the mechanics but not the hard part — what if you have nothing worthwhile to say/teach? That's "where the real conflict comes in." (paraphrase, names Lewis Howes)
- [2019-04-01] The "thesis paper" method for building authority/differentiation: pick one core concept, go into deep research mode (a stack of ~10 books, tear pages out, highlight), uncover novel stories, and package it — "none of it's new, but the way you package it and the stories you dig up are novel." From that one paper you can build a kit, lectures, and micro-content — "your own entire life and career around one product." (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] Great thought leaders each own one core concept: Simon Sinek → "Start With Why" (researched the Wright brothers and their money-motivated competitors); Seth Godin → the Icarus story (went back to the original and revealed it's also a caution against flying too *low*, not just too high — millions of views). "Seth didn't invent a story. He just found out something about a story we all think we knew and brought new light to it." (paraphrase, cites Simon Sinek + Seth Godin)
- [2019-04-01] His job as a teacher isn't to tell people what to do — "if I told people what to do and they did it they'd be super successful," but telling someone 35 things means they do none. The real work is priming/helping them *want* to make the one change. (paraphrase, self-critical)
- [2019-04-01] Cites Tony Robbins' method: reiterate the problem "over and over and over again" until the audience is so ready they're compelled to take action — then deliver the answer. (paraphrase, cites Tony Robbins)
- [2019-04-01] Win people "with the heart and with the logic" — emotional agitation plus a rational/historical case, so they can rationalize and then move forward. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] Live example of his own current project: he's researching the history of commerce and currency (barter — fish for a basket as a value exchange, invention of currency as a substitute, the Industrial Revolution training culture that "your worth is your time") to build a story proving that selling/pricing by time is irrational — "it's always been about exchanging value, never about time." He's outsourcing the research to a hired researcher while he supplies the general ideas. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] On his own motivation: "I'm this weird business person, I teach people how to make money, but I'm not really motivated by money" — he wants to help people, trusting the law of reciprocity that help comes back around. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-01] Reframes "sales funnel," a term he's reluctant to use: really you're just educating people and offering a solution to those who want it. (paraphrase)

### Melinda Livsey (context only — does NOT train persona)

- [2019-04-01] Wants to launch a brand-strategy kit / e-course, has started an email list, and has committed to phasing out client work as she can afford to. (context)
- [2019-04-01] Her target audience: designers "sick of being order takers" — pushed around by clients making subjective decisions ("I like green better than purple"), acting as hands-only executors with no objective goal, usually underpaid. (context)
- [2019-04-01] Her own origin story: felt like a "design slave," frustrated to the point of wanting to quit and looking at other careers (naturopathy/holistic living); discovered Pat Flynn's Smart Passive Income podcast and saw that its guests were all *problem solvers*, which is what she craved. (context)
- [2019-04-01] Her struggle: knowing exactly *who* she's talking to (designers who already know strategy vs. those who don't yet) — "how far back do I go?" (context)
- [2019-04-01] Realizes she'd been aiming at people already on the cusp of wanting to learn, and needs to "rewind the tape" to reach them earlier. (context)
- [2019-04-01] Her blog is called "strategy stories," built around telling one-thing stories (e.g. finding the one word for a brand). (context)
