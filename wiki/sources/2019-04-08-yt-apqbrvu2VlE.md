# How do you find Customers for Your Product? — w/ Melinda Livsey Ep. 17

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do restates two recurring core frameworks here worth promoting to `wiki/topics/` and `persona/beliefs.md`: (a) the **problem → solution → product** cold-lead sequence (attributed to Russell Brunson's *DotCom Secrets*), and (b) the **"give give give" / law-of-reciprocity long-term play** as the engine behind The Futur's course sales. Flag only — verify against existing topic coverage before promoting.

## Summary

Episode 17 of the ongoing Melinda Livsey coaching series. Melinda reports back on prior advice: she stopped aiming her brand-strategy eCourse content at strategists who already know their problem, and instead backed up years in the customer journey to speak to the "rock bottom" version of herself — an insecure, overwhelmed designer who couldn't yet articulate the problem. Chris affirms this and teaches how to sequence a cold lead to a customer using **problem → solution → product**, where the "solution" is delivered through personal stories mapped along a timeline of life lessons. He covers audience-building platforms (email vs. Medium vs. editorial calendars), the funnel/attrition math of moving people from awareness to purchase, positioning ("streamlined clarity" as her differentiator, and finding the right noun for her target avatar), and closes on his philosophy of giving abundantly so people reciprocate. He uses The Futur's ~400-seat / ~$60K Style Scape course launch as a live example of reciprocity driving sales.

Speaker: two-person dialogue between **Chris Do** (subject, coach) and **Melinda Livsey** (recurring Futur colleague being coached; context-only, NOT the subject). Only Chris-attributed material trains the persona.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-04-08] To convert a cold lead you must sequence **problem → solution → product**: a cold lead doesn't even know they have a problem (they can't search for you), so you first define the problem, then present the solution, then the product. He maps this onto Melinda's business: product = the e-learning course, solution = a strategic framework, problem = what wasn't taught in school. (paraphrase; attributes the framework to Russell Brunson's *DotCom Secrets*)
- [2019-04-08] The "solution" is best delivered through personal **stories and lived experience**, not just how-to instruction. "Stories are what draw people in." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-04-08] Method for surfacing forgotten stories: anchor memory around **"spaces, places, and things"** — old objects unlock memories. He demonstrates with an old Texas Instruments TI-79 (caption garble; likely "TI-83/TI-89") calculator kept since community college. (paraphrase, demonstrated with prop)
- [2019-04-08] Build a story timeline: pick **five to seven points**, then realize there are five more points between each — before long you have a compelling story people follow. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-08] A funnel is an upside-down pyramid: you lose a large percentage at every step (link click → site → credit card), so a narrow top makes conversion slim by attrition. Cites Casey Neistat going ~1,000,000 views → ~8,000 → ~500 → ~5. (paraphrase; example likely drawn from Seth Godin's *This Is Marketing*)
- [2019-04-08] **People already on your email list are "hot leads"** — you can just tell them about the product and skip the funnel work; the funnel work is for cold leads. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-08] On audience platforms: email doesn't *grow* your audience (Thomas van Schneider is an exception because he already had a following and promised a personal, non-spammy story). A **broader platform like Medium** plus pitching stories to magazines'/news outlets' **editorial calendars** (e.g. timed to Teacher Appreciation Day) can win far more exposure. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-08] Warns Melinda she'll be **competing for attention against The Futur's own platform/products** if she just siphons the shared audience; better to *expand the pool* and bring in a brand-new audience that knows nothing about The Futur, so the existing lift and the new audience feed each other. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-08] On positioning: likens Melinda's streamlined process to **"four-minute abs / six-minute abs"** — same result, faster. Pushes her to name what problem the streamlined process (one persona, one creative direction) actually solves, and to find the right **noun for the avatar**: a "strategist" loves complexity and isn't overwhelmed, so the target is really the **"aspiring designer/strategist overwhelmed by the complication of the process."** (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-08] Admits The Futur's own students, even those who follow the CORE framework as directed, are "way better than they used to be but not that good" against the internal bar he sets by benchmarking against Deloitte / Touche / PwC. This creates a sweet-spot gap for a coach like Melinda to make the material friendlier and demystified for people who feel intimidated. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-08] On planning: **doesn't plan a year ahead** — "throw out your business plan" because market conditions, people, trends, and competitors all change (cites *Rework* by Jason Fried & David Heinemeier Hansson). As a self-funded startup, The Futur is in "survival mode," always focused on the next runway milestone; invokes the famous photo of Jeff Bezos in a cluttered dingy early-Amazon office. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-08] Reflects that experts **take for granted** that their skill is easy — live Style Scape attendees asked questions (e.g. "how did you decide which image goes where?") he never imagined, revealing a bigger-than-expected gap between the tool and the student. (paraphrase; credits a conversation with "Ben")
- [2019-04-08] The core growth philosophy: **"give give give"** in orders of magnitude — and giving isn't only how-to content; it can be a poignant story, reassurance, or "an expression of love and support and friendship." If you make people feel that, they find a way to give back. It's a **long-term play**. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-04-08] Frames this via the **law of reciprocity** (attributes it to "Robert Kelly, *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*" — caption garble; the author is Robert **Cialdini**). Uses the Style Scape launch as proof: sold ~**400 seats / ~$60,000**, which surprised him ("I didn't think anybody was gonna buy this"), and concluded people bought partly to **reciprocate** the value the channel had given them. Notes Scotty Valentine messaged that it's funny people pay to be taught things already taught for free. (paraphrase + quote)

### Melinda Livsey (context — does NOT train the persona)

- [2019-04-08] Reports that Chris's repeated question — "do your customers even know their problem?" — finally clicked; she had been targeting brand strategists close to purchase, but backed up years to the "rock bottom," pre-articulation version of herself and built a user persona of that person. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-08] After shifting content to speak to *the problem* (designer insecurity/imposter syndrome, not "brand strategist" pain), just two new Instagram posts produced multiple DMs from brand-new people connecting with the story. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-08] Identifies **"clarity"** as her differentiator (from asking past strategy-session clients); she streamlined her framework to **one user persona** (which she calls the **"brand's best friend"**) and **one creative direction**, which reduced revisions and misalignment. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-08] Suggests The Futur release very clear, sequenced step-by-step fundamental content rather than scattered videos. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do:**
- "In a cold lead you have to tell them what the problem is because they don't even know the problem... so we have to define the problem, then we have to tell them the solution, and then the last part is we tell them what the product is."
- "Stories are what draw people in and that's what you need to do right now, because you've got to get on the radar."
- "Look into your past, look at objects, places and spaces, and try to draw out that memory... scrub through that mental timeline of yours and find there's a moment right there — either it was a high or a low, and each has a learning opportunity baked into it."
- "We're all fishing the same pool, we're all competing for the same fish... so we need you to expand the pool."
- "A strategist is not overwhelmed because they love this kind of stuff... I think you're talking to the overwhelmed aspiring designer who wants to be more strategic."
- "Everything changes — market conditions change, people change, trends change, competitors change — so we don't have the luxury to sit down and say okay, here's a year's worth of planning."
- "You just give give give, given orders of magnitude that people don't even know what to do with... and if you can make people feel that, they'll find a way to give back to you. This is a long-term play."

**Melinda Livsey (context, not persona training):**
- "For some reason Melinda always knows what questions to ask." (Melinda quoting anticipated audience reaction)
- "The word that kept coming up over and over... the word is clarity."
