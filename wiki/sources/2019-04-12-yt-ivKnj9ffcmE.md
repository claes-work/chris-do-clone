# How To Charge For Design — Value Based Pricing

★ L3-candidate: canonical value-based-pricing statement for `wiki/topics/pricing` — this is a near-complete, self-contained articulation of Chris Do's value-based pricing method (sequence, diagnosis, question technique, anchoring, discount-for-uncertainty, single-page bid). Recommend promotion to a topic hub.

## Summary

Live conference session (AIGA 2019 Design Conference, live-streamed on The Futur's channel) in which host **Chris Do** teaches value-based pricing through a series of live role-plays with audience members. He runs a running scenario: a designer known for "webcams" (webcomps/website comps — the transcript garbles this term throughout) wants to also sell "brand strategy." Chris first lets participants attempt the pitch, deliberately demonstrating the WRONG way (selling/pitching, assuming, self-diagnosing the client), then re-runs the same scenario himself modeling the RIGHT way: stop selling and start serving, ask questions to diagnose, uncover the client's real problem, quantify the desired future state, and let the client name the value. The climactic role-play arrives at a client who could gain ~$480,000/year, and Chris closes a $40,000 engagement. He closes with a "lightning recap" of the full method and its underlying sequence: desired future state → success metrics → value → price → scope. Books cited: *Pricing Creativity* by Blair Enns; a Jim Rohn effort/result principle; and a Gary Vaynerchuk "value of a basketball" analogy.

Speakers: **Chris Do** (host/teacher — the persona subject). Audience participants named in the transcript — **Jody/Jodie** (the role-play designer character; also the name of a participant), **Jocelyn** (gave client-side feedback), **Wendy/Winny**, **Paul**, **Seth/Ted**, and others — are CONTEXT only; their lines are not persona data.

## Key claims

All dated 2019-04-12, all paraphrased unless quoted in the next section. Attributed to **Chris Do** unless noted.

- **Stop selling, start serving.** The way to close more jobs is to shift from selling to serving — "How can I be of service to you?" (Chris Do)
- **Serve, don't upsell.** Jody's instinct to "upsell" is self-focused ("the word sell is in there... it's all about me, not about them"). The move is to think about how to help the client achieve their goal. (Chris Do)
- **Diagnose before prescribing.** Amateurs give advice; experts (professionals) diagnose. Making recommendations with zero diagnosis time insults the client and mirrors a doctor diagnosing cancer on first meeting or a mechanic declaring you need a transmission. (Chris Do)
- **Turn your thinking into questions.** Instead of pitching, form all of your thinking in the form of questions to diagnose the problem. (Chris Do)
- **Ask questions to eliminate possibilities**, the way doctors rule out conditions, then lean in on the real problem once found. (Chris Do)
- **Whoever asks more questions controls the conversation.** Speak less, ask more questions. (Chris Do — stated as "the rule")
- **"Why" is the best question.** Questions start with who/what/where/when, but the best one is "why"; it surfaces the real problem. (Chris Do)
- **The goal of questioning is to find whether there is a real problem to solve** — and often the client, given space, admits they don't know what they're doing (i.e., they self-diagnosed). You shouldn't take advice from someone who doesn't know what they're doing — and the client says it themselves. (Chris Do)
- **Only quantifiable success metrics count.** Push past "fluffy" answers (awareness, brand integrity) to measurable numbers: "If I can't measure it, how do I know it's doing better?" (Chris Do)
- **The pricing sequence most designers use is backwards.** Wrong order: scope → time × rate → price (→ maybe value → maybe metrics), which makes it "all about you." Correct order: **desired future state → success metrics → value → price → scope/solution.** This elevates the conversation from tactical to strategic. (Chris Do)
- **Have the money conversation via value, not scope.** Starting with scope invites the client to attack the estimate line-by-line ("unbundle" it) and negotiate you down; leading with value avoids this. (Chris Do)
- **Let the client name the value/price.** When you name a price you risk underselling; when the client names value/price "in all instances, it's at the top end." "When you say it, you're selling. When they say it, you're closing." (Chris Do)
- **A number is neither high nor low — context decides.** (Attributed by Chris to his business coach.) Illustrated with the marker-size / photo-next-to-a-building analogy. (Chris Do, crediting his business coach)
- **Ask for a percentage of value created, discounted for uncertainty.** In the role-play: client gains ~$480,000/year → a 10% fee would be $48,000 → Chris charges $40,000, framing it as a ~90% discount because he can't guarantee outcomes he doesn't control (the client's operations). (Chris Do) [NUMBER NOTE: the "10% = $48K", "$40K", and "$480K/year" figures are internally consistent in the recap.]
- **Price anchoring.** Naming the big number ($480,000) first makes a client's $10,000 feel "anemic," whereas anchoring on $2,000 would make $10,000 feel huge. Anchoring bias is unavoidable, so anchor deliberately. (Chris Do)
- **Get verbal agreement on the price before building the bid.** "Say a price before you show a price" (cited as a rule from Blair Enns' book). Don't build a bid until the number is agreed — "I'm not in the bid making business, I'm in the getting money from the client to pay me to value me business." (Chris Do)
- **Use round numbers, one price, single-page bid.** Precise numbers (e.g., 40,272) imply a formula that the client can argue with and unbundle; round numbers ($40,000) resist this. Go from a multi-page itemized bid to a single-page one-price bid. (Chris Do)
- **When a client hesitates on the number, never assume it's too much.** Ask "too much or too little?" — some clients get nervous about spending too LITTLE (LASIK-surgery analogy: don't buy the cheapest). (Chris Do)
- **Qualify the buyer: you need a stakeholder AND an entrepreneur.** The method only works with a decision-maker who has skin in the game and is not risk-averse ("a person who doesn't value their time doesn't value your time"). If they're neither, refer them out and leave. (Chris Do)
- **"Just because it didn't work for you doesn't mean it doesn't work"** — the basketball is worthless to Chris (torn ACL) but worth a billion to LeBron; the tool works, the user must follow the program. (Chris Do, crediting the analogy to Gary Vaynerchuk)
- **Effort/result principle:** don't spend a small effort for a big result, or a big effort for a small result. (Chris Do, crediting Jim Rohn)
- **Learn to take a compliment and pivot.** Designers deflect compliments; instead embrace it and pivot immediately into the conversation. (Chris Do)
- **The client sells themselves to you.** By asking about their previous vendor experience, you let them articulate the value of responsiveness, communication, and respect — "You're the fish that jumped in the boat." (Chris Do)
- **Client-side reaction (CONTEXT, attributed to participant Jocelyn):** the pitchy approach makes the client feel "a tad bit insulted," as if their existing brand "basically isn't good enough"; clients are attached to their brand and it feels like "stepping on their toes." (Jocelyn — audience, not persona data; Chris endorses the point.)
