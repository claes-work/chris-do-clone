# Relevance in the Age of Social Media – Art Center BOLD 2019

## Summary

An edited highlight/recap of BOLD 2019, a single-day full-length workshop co-produced by
The Futur and ArtCenter College of Design, themed around staying **relevant in the age of
social media**. It interleaves Chris Do teaching (customers vs. audience, 1,000 true fans,
the "attractive character") with segments from the day's featured artist — a self-taught
photographer with a large Pinterest/Instagram following — who walks through ideal-client
definition and hands-on Instagram feed-curation technique. The through-line: creatives can
no longer outsource getting known to agents or salespeople; you must do your own marketing,
take a stance, and build an audience of true fans.

Speaker: **multi-speaker event recap**, not a solo talk.
- **Chris Do** — host/curator; delivers the framing and the two teaching frameworks
  (customers vs. audience; the attractive character). Also gives the biographical
  homecoming reflection (see biography note below).
- **Featured artist (a photographer; name garbled in captions — heard as
  "Bonnie" / "bunny", surname unclear; `attribution: uncertain`)** — runs the
  ideal-client and Instagram-feed-curation segments; her personal story (single mom,
  female empowerment) is used as the case study.
- A **co-founder of the BOLD symposium** (ArtCenter photography alum; name garbled, heard
  as "…Roman"; `attribution: uncertain`) is described in third person as having lost a big
  job when the client learned her social following was too small.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-04-16] Creatives "can no longer rely on other entities — agents, salespeople — to
  get us work"; marketing (the art of getting known) is now the artist's own
  responsibility, and you must be true to yourself even if you only have 400 followers.
  (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-16] **Customers vs. audience**: every brand has customers — a transaction, they
  pay for services rendered, and once the exchange is complete the transaction ends. An
  **audience is far superior** because they "show up happily to consume everything you make
  and do," support and share without being asked. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-16] For a sustainable six-figure ($100,000) lifestyle business, "all you need is
  1,000 true fans" (Kevin Kelly's idea, uncredited here). A **true fan** will "drive or fly
  200 miles just to see you speak." (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-04-16] A true fan can also be a **client** who, despite your inexperience, hires you
  sight unseen, treats you with respect, and "never haggles you over price." (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-16] "You have to play the social game if you want to be relevant. Trying to fight
  the internet is a losing game." Cites Gary Vaynerchuk: "the internet won, get over it and
  get yours." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-04-16] The quest for relevance is **not new** — only the platform is. Big brands
  with budgets have always sought icons to create content with: Irving Penn shot for
  Clinique; Annie Leibovitz and Mark Seliger shot icons for Vanity Fair. Chris says he (or
  the audience) could technically shoot that content, "but I'm not an icon so I wouldn't
  even be considered for that job." (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-16] The old idea is that "you create work to market and sell to other people
  online" — borrowed from advertising. What matters more is to **build an audience, send
  them a message, and put yourself into your story** — because "nobody can copy you."
  (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-16] The **"attractive character"** (Russell Brunson's framework, uncredited) has
  four components: (1) an interesting **backstory**, (2) **character flaws** — the
  vulnerability/authenticity, (3) **parable stories** you tell to help people understand the
  world, and (4) willingness to be **polarizing**. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-16] Being "polarizing" is often misinterpreted — the true intent is that "you
  have to take a position on an issue or a stance. You can't be for everybody because then
  you're for nobody." (paraphrase + quote)

### Featured artist (photographer — name uncertain)

- [2019-04-16] You can't please everyone — "like what Chris said" — so identify **who you
  want to work with**: pick an **age group** (for her, "moms… thirty to sixty-five,"
  already have a family), the **kind of job** they hold (e.g. magazine editors, creative
  directors in architecture/fashion), and their **lifestyle**. The ideal client "might not
  exist, which is fine — the exercise is for you to know what kind of content to create and
  who you're speaking to." (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-16] A smartphone plus a couple of apps is "a desktop workstation in the palm of
  your hands" — so "you don't have any more excuses not to create and share work."
  (paraphrase, per Chris summarizing her takeaways)
- [2019-04-16] Your Instagram **feed is your first impression** — "almost like a resume…
  your website, your portfolio." New followers judge the whole grid, not just single
  pictures, so curate for visual cohesion. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-16] Practical technique: she previews the grid before posting (the zigzag/preview
  tap), posts one image at a time, and avoids two humans stacked next to each other —
  e.g. she'd swap in a clean, minimal, human-free sky shot to keep the grid balanced.
  (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-16] Her audience connects to her **story** — a working creative mom raising two
  kids, living a stylish life — and she stands for **female empowerment**; being a single
  mom whose husband had left gave her a clear "somebody" to be for. (paraphrase; her
  personal biographical details, `attribution: uncertain` on specifics)
