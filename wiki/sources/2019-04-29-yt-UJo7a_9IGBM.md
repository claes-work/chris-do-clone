# Design Portfolio Review – Young Guns 2 Episode 2

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do delivers a rich, largely self-contained set of portfolio and design-craft principles here (curate ruthlessly, tell the story inside the visuals, it takes three projects to establish a pattern, a logo is "just punctuation," go-with-type vs against-type for premium brands, neutral Swiss aesthetic pays more, personal-vs-professional feed split). ## Summary

Episode 2 of The Futur's "Young Guns" Season 2 — an ongoing mentorship series coaching designers under 26 from around the world. Chris Do and Melinda Livsey review seven young designers' portfolios (all women this season: Annie Yang, Monika, Anastasia, Maitri, Ria, Megan Krieger, and Tolu/"Tolson"), grading each on four criteria they defined together — **personality, focus, clarity of what-you-do, and quality** — then secretly score 1–5 and reveal a winner (Annie Yang) and runner-up (Monika). Much of the value is Chris's running design-craft critique: how to present work, curate it, tell a story with images, and position yourself.

Speaker: **dual-host review** — **Chris Do** and **Melinda Livsey** co-lead, trading off; the seven **students** speak only in short reaction interludes (context). Captions are unlabeled and often garbled, so speaker attribution below is inferred from content and voice; the most detailed design-craft/positioning critiques are Chris, and Melinda frequently frames her comments explicitly "as an art director looking for a designer." ## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-04-29] The four grading criteria for a portfolio: (1) **personality / personal branding** — does it say something about you; (2) **focus** — are you going deep or wide ("at the end of the day we only remember the people who have depth"); (3) **clarity** — is it clear what you do, and does the body of work support that; (4) **quality** — fundamentals/principles of design (spacing, color), beautiful mock-ups, good presentation, and depth of exploration. (paraphrase; framework co-owned with Melinda, but Chris narrates it)
- [2019-04-29] The upper-left corner of a portfolio is the most important slot, not the latest thing — put your most important/representative work there. (paraphrase; Melinda attributes this rule back to Chris — "you were saying to us")
- [2019-04-29] Do as much of the storytelling as possible **inside the visuals themselves**, not in the written copy. Many viewers won't read; a thumbnail/image should communicate the type of brand and story on its own (e.g., photograph a product in its setting, with its ingredients/props around it). (paraphrase — recurring theme, one of the strongest points)
- [2019-04-29] "When we show less we become more curious; when we turn on all the lights there's no mystery." Restraint and darkness/ambiguity (his 50-Shades-of-Grey, ribbon-being-unwrapped thumbnail suggestion) can make an image more compelling. (paraphrase + near-quote)
- [2019-04-29] **It takes three projects to identify a pattern — no more than that.** If a designer shows three consistent projects, an art director can confidently predict what they'll deliver, which makes the hire an easy decision. (paraphrase — stated as possibly his own bias)
- [2019-04-29] **Curate ruthlessly.** Ask yourself: "How many pieces do you need to show somebody to make them feel you're good enough at doing that thing?" Take out any work that doesn't speak to the quality you put out consistently — if you littered your site with sub-top work you send the wrong signal. He recalls telling one designer on an earlier call to remove weaker pieces and is surprised they're still there. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-29] "A logo is just an identifier — it's not the communication, it's just the punctuation." Marks that "yell 'look at me, I'm so important'" fight the advertising/photography they sit in; overly expressive marks don't age well, especially for fashion/beauty brands. (quote + paraphrase)
- [2019-04-29] For broader employability, pursue a "more neutral **Swiss modern aesthetic**" — simple shapes, classic typefaces, not overly expressive — because it applies to many more brands "and you'll get paid a lot more money to make" them. (paraphrase + partial quote)
- [2019-04-29] For a "premium Belgian chocolate" brief you can go **with type** (pick a typeface that already feels premium — a serif or formal script) or **against type** (a masculine 3D typeface); in that specific case going with type would serve it better. An outline typeface plus a drop shadow made the shadow more prominent than the letterforms and hard to read at 4–5 feet. (paraphrase — formal typographic critique)
- [2019-04-29] On social media there's a time to be self-expressive and a time to build an audience around what you want to be known for. If you can't dedicate equal time to a personal and a professional feed, focus on the business account — "the thing that's gonna put bread on the table." Don't become "known for hanging out in costumes or throwing peace signs." (paraphrase; Melinda gives the "90% personal / 10% work — flip it" version, see below)
- [2019-04-29] Show work **in context** (in a mock-up, on a shelf, in the store, the full product line) rather than a flat image floating on a page — context gives the viewer a lens to evaluate it. (paraphrase — recurring across nearly every review)
- [2019-04-29] **Depth of exploration matters**: take an idea, "hit it out of the park," and do everything you can think of and more — thin/shallow portfolios (few pieces, no angle variations, no pattern extensions) cost you. (paraphrase)
- [2019-04-29] When two reviewers independently define objective criteria up front, they tend to arrive at the same ranking even working separately — evidence that shared, explicit criteria produce consistent evaluation. (paraphrase — his takeaway after he and Melinda matched scores)
- [2019-04-29] Beautiful mock-ups matter because the client/art director wants their own project to look that good — "whether they're borrowed, stolen, original, I don't care, they just look good" and signal the TLC you'll give their work. (paraphrase + partial quote)
- [2019-04-29] (Annie Yang, winner) Doing your own identity system is "probably the hardest thing to do"; hers was "dripping with who you are." He'd push her to bring her witty/playful sensibility into more corporate brands to find "what the edges are." When on-camera personality mirrors the work, "she knows who she is and she's not going to run away from it." (paraphrase + partial quote)

### Melinda Livsey (context)

- [2019-04-29] Framing herself "as an art director looking for a designer," she wants a portfolio ratio flipped from "90% personal, 10% work" to "90% work, 10% person" if the goal is to get hired. (context — not persona training)
- [2019-04-29] Give credit / state clearly what you personally did on team projects, and provide context for each project so the reviewer knows how to look at it. (context)
- [2019-04-29] Consistency across brand-identity projects can read as either good or bad — good if an art director wants a predictable, specific outcome. (context)

### Students (context)

- Reaction interludes only (Annie, Monika, Anastasia, Maitri, Ria, Megan, Tolu). Common thread they take away: tell the story through images/context rather than relying on text, add project context, and curate. Not persona training data. `attribution: uncertain` on several unlabeled lines.
