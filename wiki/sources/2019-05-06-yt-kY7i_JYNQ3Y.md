★ **L3-candidate** — Rich, multi-topic live coaching with several self-contained Chris Do principles (the "double your price, don't inch up" rule, the master-copy exercise, the "close the gap / make three projects sing" mock-up method, the "listen to understand, not to respond" + verbatim-repeat exercise, and a full sales role-play walking through a logo objection call). ## Summary

Live episode of The Futur's "Young Guns" Season 2: Chris Do coaches seven young women designers one at a time, answering their questions on pricing, confidence, presence, hard times, niching, and self-promotion. Recurring Chris themes: price is an exchange of value the *buyer* decides (not the designer's worldview), so jump prices by doubling rather than inching up; most people already know their answer and just need someone to shine a light; do less but better; and close the gap between your work and the work you admire. It closes with an unscripted sales role-play where Chris plays a designer defending a $3,000 logo fee against a client's objections.

Speaker: **Chris Do coaches throughout** (sole coach). Co-host **Ricky Lin** (context) interjects — runs the shot-clock, tells the Adobe Max "turns it on/off" anecdote, and adds the Seth Godin "professional shows up" note. The seven students (Rhea/Ria, an unnamed presence-questioner, Megan, Maitrayee, Annie, Monika, Tosin) are **context** — only Chris's coaching trains the persona. ## Key claims

### Chris Do

**Pricing & value**
- [2019-05-06] Any transaction between designer and client is an exchange of value; each side values it differently, and the goal is to find a price good for both — it will differ for every client. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] The client — not the designer — determines what's fair. "The buyer gets to determine what's fair. I get to ask and then they get to determine what's fair." A logo can be worth a $140k S-Class Mercedes to one buyer if it solves a $100k problem; you ask, they decide. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Don't impose your own sense of fairness, beauty, or value on others — you don't hold "the universal key" to value; like an auction, the same item is valued differently by each person. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] When raising prices, don't make incremental jumps — double. Going from $600–800 to $1,000 is "only $200 more"; jump to 2K or 3K instead. "Three's a nice number. It's a magic number." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Charging more is a virtuous loop: you can spend more time making it great, and hire experts (writer, strategist, even Chris himself) to collaborate — so higher fees let you buy world-class talent. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] You often don't need value-based pricing theory (which is "complicated and hard to implement") — first master the progression from hourly → deliverable/output-based → project fees, then simply change your prices and hold the line. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Luxury goods (Gucci, Prada, Louis Vuitton) don't explain or justify their price — the clerk sets the price, you decide if you want it badly enough. You can just say: "In order to do something, it's going to cost you to start 3,000 US. Is that something you're comfortable paying?" (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] The "European clients won't pay that / it's an insane amount of money" story is a self-imposed limiting belief — there are rich clients everywhere (Europe, India, China, Taiwan); someone is doing that work and making the money, so why not you. "In order for you to achieve something, you must first believe it to be true." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Uses Jonathan Stark's framing for justifying a premium: "why this, why now, and why me" — because you'll be more expensive than everyone else, and the client decides if it's a fit. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Referral leads cluster by budget ("birds of a feather flock together"), so cheap referrals beget cheap referrals; pre-qualify on money early — "Can you afford $1,000 to do a logo? … I don't want to waste your time." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] When a referral expects the old low price ("Bob told me you did it for $200"), explain it was a one-time favor at a point in time and that you can't run an equitable, surviving business at that rate. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Good work attracts unqualified leads who all think they can afford you; over time you must position yourself so people are "scared to call you because they know it's going to cost a lot of money." (paraphrase + quote)

**Mindset & self-worth**
- [2019-05-06] Believing there's a hard limit to what you can charge or to your worth is a *limiting belief* — challenge where it comes from before accepting it. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Formal design education correlates with skill but is only one metric, not the only path — plenty of art-school grads are terrible; celebrated designers (cites Danny Yount, Doyle Young) had only high-school education. The Futur exists precisely for people without access to those institutions. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Inexperience that makes you self-critical ("I should have asked that question") is an *asset* — it keeps you aware you have room to grow, whereas over-credentialed people take things for granted and improve less. It's a marathon, not a sprint (tortoise vs. hare); consistency and reflection win. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Chris still asks "what worked / what could I do better?" after 23 years — e.g., debriefing each Europe workshop with Mark and applying the feedback to the very next one — rather than beating himself up. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Most people arrive already knowing their answer; the coach's job is to shine a light on the path they can take. If two options are equal on money and engagement, pick the one that makes you happier — "then you have your answer." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Don't self-sabotage a premium fee by questioning your worth in front of the client; just say "10,000 bucks, that seems fair, I'll submit a proposal … once you pay me half up front," and do the best work you can. (paraphrase + quote)

**Listening & presence**
- [2019-05-06] Genuine presence isn't "fake it till you make it" — it's being genuinely interested in people. Cites John Maxwell: "to be an interesting person, you have to be interested in people." Chris closes his eyes when listening to shut off a sense he doesn't need. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Active listening and bringing high energy are both mentally draining ("burning brain calories"); over time it becomes less draining via muscle memory, and reframing from "energy it takes from me" to "opportunity to serve" becomes the rocket fuel. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Core coaching instruction: "listen to understand, not listen to respond." The stress of conversation comes from feeling you must reply cleverly; instead be okay with silence, don't rush to relate it to your own story. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Early-career anecdote: terrified and near-silent in a room of 20 senior people on a car-commercial shoot, he asked one careful question about type placement and a producer told him "I can tell you're a deep thinker" — he wasn't, he was just scared; meanwhile over-talkers read as blowhards. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Practice exercise (from his therapist, done with his wife): one person speaks, the other repeats back *verbatim* in the same order — being a "human tape recorder" forces total focus and builds the listening/presence skill. (paraphrase)

**Hard times & leadership (biography)**
- [2019-05-06] During the early-2000s dot-com bust, all his clients went out of business ("they're no longer in business" down the portfolio); his wife's question — "Do you have the team that you need to take yourself into the future?" — rocked him to his core. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] He had to lay off three or four people (like a submarine captain sealing a compartment to save the crew); it took a week and a half to phrase it, he lost sleep, some took it very badly, and he skipped work afterward. But the team rebuilt stronger — and this was neither the first nor last time. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Recovery lesson: it's "self-centered and egotistical to think that a person's life is in your control"; every laid-off person went on to more money and greater acclaim. Employment is professional, "not a marriage," not forever. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Cautionary tales: (employees) never assume lifetime job security — the moment you do, you're gone; keep evolving and leading, not just showing up. (employer) he hired "warm body, good personality" for immediate need instead of what the future required. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Invest in help (coach, CPA, financial advisor) when you're *successful*, not desperate — "desperate decisions" come from desperation; spending while you have it is "insurance against going obsolete." He got far more back than he paid out. (paraphrase + quote)

**Niching, content & design craft**
- [2019-05-06] "All experts specialize." Choose between growing a following that hires you vs. expressing everything you can do; the data (leads, revenue, engagement) tells you what to double down on — trust data over gut/intuition. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Cites the Pareto/80-20 principle: 80% of revenue comes from 20% of clients, yet people spend 80% of energy on the clients that don't matter. Score your categories (clients, revenue, engagement) and act on the top 20%. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Marketing (via Seth Godin, *This Is Marketing*): "marketing is the generous act of helping others solve their problems" — social media should serve the audience, not shove ads that say "I don't care about you, I'm going to interrupt your life." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Your brand is what other people think of you; if a "cute" reputation feels confining you can spread your wings, but owning a distinct style (cites Paul Rand, Saul Bass, Seymour Chwast, Pushpin, Herb Lubalin) beats chasing trends — "average is what disappears in the world." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] The professor's "make something ugly" note is really about *control* — Chris's own Swiss-modern work was too rigid; the fix was to spend less time, work faster/more lyrically (cites David Carson's deadline-driven experimentation). Reframe "ugly" as "let go of control." (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Tactical creativity exercise: list five variables you're uncomfortable with (time, tools, colors, subject, typefaces) and introduce just one as a constraint on your next project — you don't have to reinvent yourself, just swap one variable. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] The "master copy" exercise: rebuild a design you love as closely as possible — students come back with "mind-blowing results," learning new tools, techniques, and a new visual vocabulary; sometimes he can't tell master from copy. (Later reinforced with a motion-design firm that grew and sold after recreating someone else's demo reel shot-for-shot.) (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] Anti-perfectionism (via *Rework* / Jason Fried): do 10 one-week projects instead of one 10-week project; The Futur values velocity and experimentation over one "killer video a month." "Delete" exists — try, fail, it's not heart surgery. Prefer 10 experiments (9 fail, 1 new direction) over one perfect thing. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Self-promotion method: build fewer but better case studies and mock-ups — "instead of 35 projects on Behance, make three, but make those three sing." Look objectively at work you admire (cites Pentagram) and *close the gap*; don't wait for someone else's opinion — learn to self-critique. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Perceived value comes from presentation: a better photographer makes an object look 10× the value (cites Toby Peterson lighting a hardware-store cinder block to look like $200); use tools like Adobe Dimension and mock-up resources (mentions "yellow images") to make work "look super expensive." (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-06] On YouTube/personal brand: you don't need to be "professional" or "high-end" *on camera* — be true to yourself and engaging ("let the beast out"), built on a foundation of actually knowing your stuff. Invest the high-end in audio, lighting, and production value, not in acting stiff. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-05-06] Focus metaphor: you're a nearly-empty jar of peanut butter — spread over four sandwiches it's all bland; make one good (or even half a) sandwich. Find your lane and go deep; strategy sits "at the top of the pyramid," a good place to arrive but not to start. (cites James Lewis painting logos all day.) (paraphrase + quote)

### Ricky Lin (context — co-host)
- [2019-05-06] Adobe Max anecdote: after back-to-back interviews Chris "almost collapses," dead tired, then instantly turns big energy back on for the next person — "he turns it up and turns it down just like most people … just to a greater degree." Ties it to Seth Godin's idea of the professional who shows up and does the work even when they don't feel like it. (paraphrase — Ricky-attributed, about Chris)

### Students (context — not persona training)
- [2019-05-06] The students voiced the limiting beliefs Chris coached against: "I don't know if I'd be worthy of" a $10k project; "in Europe this is an insane amount of money"; fear of posting more than one thing until it's perfect; torn between UX/UI and branding. Included only as the prompts Chris responded to. (paraphrase, context)
