# Why I Don't Have A Business Partner Anymore

★ **L3-candidate** — HIGH BIOGRAPHY VALUE. Chris Do speaks first-person about his own
partnership history: he has tried business partnerships **three times** and all three
failed, "mostly because of my own personality." This is durable biography material about
Chris's self-assessed reasons for not having a partner, and it should be reconciled with
the existing Blind-founding record (see contradiction note below) and promoted into
`persona/biography.md` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated). No former partner is named in
the transcript.

## Summary

Responding to an audience question about whether to formalize a long-running working
relationship into a partnership, Chris Do gives a heavily caveated ("take this with a
giant bulldozer scoop of salt") answer rooted in his own record: he has attempted three
partnerships and none worked, which he attributes to his own personality — extreme work
ethic and efficiency (roughly 4x another person's output, breeding resentment), big/fast
vision with loose tactics that give partners "whiplash," and impatience with being
slowed, questioned, or asked to write four-year plans. His core reframe: the urge to find
a business partner usually signals a "hole" — a skill you haven't learned yet — and the
better move is often to go fill that hole yourself rather than give up equity and make
constant compromises. Practical cautions: be sure both partners are equally committed and
resourced financially (spouses become a de facto "third partner" when money gets tight),
and set up a "prenup" (who gets what) at the start because partnerships tend to go south
and former partners tend to stop talking to each other. He closes on his personal rule:
he separates personal from professional relationships because he can walk away from
professional ones.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-05-07] He has tried business partnerships **three times** and none worked,
  "mostly because of my own personality." (quote/paraphrase — biography)
- [2019-05-07] He works "like an animal," all the time, and also works smart/efficiently,
  so his output is usually about **four times** someone else's; the inevitable comparison
  breeds resentment because it's unrealistic to expect a partner to be that focused and
  productive. (paraphrase — self-assessment)
- [2019-05-07] He has clear vision but is "loose on the tactics" and moves very fast, at
  "breakneck speed," which gives partners "whiplash"; he isn't committed to a fixed path
  and lets feedback/metrics decide, which "creates problems for everybody." (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-07] He thinks "really really big" — so big that if a partner heard everything
  in his head they'd think he was arrogant/crazy — and he doesn't have time to convince a
  scared partner, doesn't want his enthusiasm dampened by their "dogma," and resents
  having to stop and sell them on each move. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-07] Asking him to "write the four-year business plan" or "get a Board of
  Advisors" before changing direction is the kind of slowdown he rejects — "I move, and
  it's scary for a lot of people." (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-07] All three of his partnerships felt doomed "from the minute we said let's
  do it." (paraphrase — biography)
- [2019-05-07] **The one pro of partnership:** a partner does something you don't (e.g., a
  creative teams with a businessperson, or with sales/production/animation/design), and it
  feels complementary. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-07] **His reframe on that pro:** the desire for a partner reveals "a hole
  inside of you" — like the missing piece in the Shel Silverstein book. His interpretation:
  when you feel you need a businessperson, the hole is telling you to go *learn* your
  business; when you're not ready to do animation, fill the hole by feeding/teaching
  yourself rather than finding someone. Soon you realize "that wasn't that hard — why did
  I give all this up, why am I making compromises all the time when I should have just
  learned how to fill my own hole." (paraphrase — a core Chris Do belief)
- [2019-05-07] **Financial caution:** partners must be equally committed to the financial
  burden and both have the resources. If one has more money, when things go south (and
  "it will go south at some point") that person ends up contributing more because the
  other can't. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-07] A married partner's spouse effectively becomes "a third partner" in the
  relationship — when money gets tight the spouse asks "what about us?" — making things
  much tougher. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-07] **Set up a "prenup":** agree up front on "what do you get, what do I get,"
  because when it goes south he wants both parties to come out whole and to still be able
  to remain friends. (paraphrase — actionable)
- [2019-05-07] Once a working relationship goes "from platonic to intimate" (i.e., into a
  formal partnership) you tend not to be able to go back; people tend not to talk to their
  former partners. He cites Ben & Jerry and Simon & Garfunkel as pairs that worked "for a
  while." (paraphrase — third-party examples)
- [2019-05-07] Partnership is "just a timeline — how much time do you want to buy." (quote
  fragment — paraphrase)
- [2019-05-07] He separates personal from professional relationships because he can walk
  away from professional ones; "the personal ones you can never escape." (paraphrase —
  personal rule; note this is also how the video opens and closes)
