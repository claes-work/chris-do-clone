# Packaging Design Challenge – Young Guns Season 2 Episode 4

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do's opening brief is a self-contained mini-framework for
packaging/product design: a set of judging criteria plus a "perception of value" and
"buyer × brand-attribute overlap = sweet spot" model. Worth promoting into
`wiki/topics/design-craft/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated) if a design-craft hub is
built out.

## Summary

Episode 4 of The Futur's "Young Guns" Season 2 mentorship series. Chris Do sets a
packaging redesign challenge: pick one product from the "ethnic foods" aisle of a
mainstream supermarket and redesign its entire package — with two hard constraints (you
may NOT change the logo or the brand colors; typefaces, materials, and finishes are fair
game). He lays out his judging criteria and a short design-thinking framework about
perception of value and designing for the intended buyer, then the students each
introduce the product they picked. Chris also announces a twist: three alumni from
Season 1 will re-compete to test whether the coaching they received makes a measurable
difference.

Speaker: Chris Do narrates the framing segments (challenge brief, judging criteria,
design philosophy, the Season-1-alumni twist, a Sony camera sponsor shout-out). The bulk
of the runtime is **individual students** (context) introducing their product picks —
white basmati rice, Bona Italian sauce, Japanese green tea, White Rabbit creamy candy,
Bragg's apple cider vinegar, mango nectar, bibimbap, feta cheese. No mentor other than
Chris is clearly identifiable in the captions; Melinda Livsey is not distinctly heard.
Student segments are unlabeled in the captions (`attribution: uncertain` per individual),
but they are clearly NOT Chris and do not train the persona.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-05-13] The challenge: go to the "ethnic foods" aisle of a mainstream supermarket, pick one product, and redesign the entire package. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-13] Two non-negotiable constraints: you cannot change the logo ("resist the temptation, that will disqualify you") and you cannot change the brand colors ("you have to be true to the brand colors"). Typefaces, materials, and finishes CAN be changed. (paraphrase; note: at line 27 the caption says "you can change typefaces colors materials and finishes" — likely a garble/misstatement since he just forbade changing colors)
- [2019-05-13] Judging criteria: (1) will it appeal to a higher-end AND mainstream audience; (2) does it have "appetite appeal"; (3) will it stand out on the shelf among many options; (4) does it show off the product's features and benefits; (5) is it appropriate. He encourages original illustration and photography. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-13] Purchasing decisions are made on **perception of value**: walk a store, associate colors/materials/design with a price point, guess which product is more expensive, then check the price — "chances are they usually are in alignment." (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-13] Like all design challenges, look at who the intended user/buyer is AND the product's brand attributes and positioning; "where those two overlap is the sweet spot." (paraphrase — reusable design heuristic)
- [2019-05-13] To attract a specific buyer, see through their eyes / walk in their shoes: what's their point of view, what do they surround themselves with — then design the product, the packaging, and the positioning so the buyer sees "this reflects my values." (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-13] Cites the Simon Sinek line "people don't buy what you do, they buy why you do it" as the rationale for values-aligned packaging. (quote of a third party, applied by Chris)
- [2019-05-13] For this first challenge he deliberately gives NO coaching and has "no expectations" — he wants to see how each person designs and works unaided, "throw them off the deep end of the pool and see how they react," and use that to gauge where the rest of the season should go. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-13] Framing the challenge as a life lesson: adding an assignment on top of school/work teaches how hard balancing life and work is. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-13] Twist: brings back three Young Guns from Season 1 to compete against Season 2, "wondering if through the coaching that they've received" they'll fare better than they would have without it — an implicit test of the mentorship's value. (paraphrase)

### Students (context — attribution: uncertain, does NOT train persona)

- Product picks and their stated rationales, e.g.: white basmati rice (easy carton to work with; make it "exotic" yet fit mainstream supermarkets); Bona Italian sauce (minimalist, strong shelf presence, wants to fix redundant dual labels); Japanese green tea (too crowded, simplify); White Rabbit creamy candy (personal connection, wary of it becoming a crutch); Bragg's apple cider vinegar (chosen on a sustainability criterion — low carbon footprint, minimal single-use plastic — plus yellow/red "appetite" color theory referencing McDonald's, Burger King, Heinz); mango nectar (stood out by being simplistic amid heavily-branded competitors); bibimbap (strong logo/colors, multilingual Korean typesetting challenge); feta/"cheetah" cheese (simple one-label Greek product to reposition as a fancy imported item for an Indian audience). (paraphrase — recorded as context only)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All quotes below are **Chris Do**:

- "You cannot change the logo — resist the temptation, that will disqualify you. And two, you can't change the colors: you have to be true to the brand colors."
- "When you walk around... look at what you associate with certain colors, materials and the way that it's designed, and then look at the price. First ask yourself which one is more expensive, and then check the price to see if those two are in alignment. Chances are, they usually are."
- "Who [is] the intended user, the buyer of this product, and where the product itself — its brand attributes and its positioning — where those two overlap is the sweet spot."
- "Walk in their shoes. What's your POV look like? What do they surround themselves with? And then design something... so that they see 'this reflects my values.'"
- "People don't buy what you do, they buy why you do it." (Chris quoting Simon Sinek)
- "I wanted to test them, throw them off the deep end of the pool and see how they react."
- "I'm going to bring back three people from Young Gun season one to compete with [those] from season two — I'm wondering if through the coaching that they've received, if they're going to fare any better than they would have if they hadn't had any coaching."
