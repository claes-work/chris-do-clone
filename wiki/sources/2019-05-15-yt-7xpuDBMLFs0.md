# How To Charge More?

## Summary

A cut-down from The Futur's "Young Guns" coaching session. A young designer asks Chris
Do how to know "the limit" of how much he can charge, admitting he feels his
non-institutional education (self-taught via The Futur and Lynda.com, no art-school
degree) makes him worth less. Rather than answer the pricing mechanics, Chris attacks
the underlying **limiting belief about self-worth**, then reframes pricing as a
contextual, per-client exchange of value — including how to handle a client who claims
what you do "isn't valuable" and how to invent systems that resolve client objections.

Speaker: two speakers — **Chris Do** (the coach/mentor) and an **unnamed young designer**
(the coachee asking the questions). Only Chris-attributed material trains the persona;
the coachee's lines are context. Danny Yount and Doyald Young are named as third-party
examples, not present.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-05-15] The barrier to charging more is often a **limiting belief**, not a real
  market ceiling — the coachee "just totally revealed" his own limiting belief without
  realizing it. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] Believing you're worth less because you didn't go to art school is a
  fallacy: it would imply people who went to a private art school are "therefore
  superior," which isn't true. (paraphrase, Socratic reframe)
- [2019-05-15] There is a correlation between education and what you know how to do, but
  it does **not** have to come from a four-year private art school; plenty of art-school
  graduates are "terrible." Skill has more to do with the individual's mindset, work
  ethic, and the instructors they were exposed to. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] A fancy art-school degree signals your ability to pay, ability to get in,
  and commitment to finish — but that's "only one dimension, one metric," not proof
  you're any good. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] Cites masters who lacked formal art-school training: **Danny Yount** (one
  of the most celebrated motion designers) went only through high school; **Doyald Young**
  (who *taught* at Art Center) had a high-school education yet was "a master letter-form
  designer." (paraphrase, third-party examples — names likely spelled "Danny out"/"doled
  young"/"dould" in captions)
- [2019-05-15] "We put too much emphasis on institutional knowledge"; there are many ways
  to acquire skill, and The Futur produces content specifically for people without access
  to formal schooling so they can work confidently in the creative field. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] Any transaction between you and a client is an **exchange of value**; each
  party looks at it differently, so the goal is to find something good for both — and it
  will be a different number for each client. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] Value is contextual to the client's stakes: a client with an emergency and
  a lot riding on the outcome puts far more value on the task; a small business that
  doesn't think about design/branding puts a much lower value on it. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] It is **impossible to convince** one client of the other's position — you
  can't talk a low-value client into thinking it's high-value, or vice versa. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] Objection-handling by inversion: if a client says the work "isn't valuable"
  to them, don't argue — ask "well, what *is* [valuable]? maybe I can help you with that."
  If they say nothing matters, tell them they should spend nothing on it and probably do
  nothing — "don't do it just because everybody else is doing it." (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] With a client who *does* value the project, have the value conversation by
  asking the questions motivating what they're trying to do, then listen "with a level of
  intensity" and take notes. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] **You can invent systems to resolve any client objection.** Example: a
  client burned by a previous vendor's poor communication → reassure them with a system,
  e.g. a guaranteed 24-hour response and your cell number "day or night," so they feel
  they won't hit the same problem. (paraphrase)

### Unnamed young designer (coachee — context, not persona training)

- [2019-05-15] Feels there is a limit to the value he can bring and doesn't know what it
  looks like in pricing terms. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] Found The Futur around his high-school graduation; The Futur plus Lynda.com
  (and client work) is "essentially my design education." (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-15] Worries that because he didn't go to school and learned from The Futur and
  Lynda.com, maybe he isn't "worth as much" as people who went to a regular school; also
  feels he has "limiting experience" — e.g. no million-dollar brand-identity project under
  his belt to point a skeptical client to. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do:

- "Just because you graduated from a fancy art school it doesn't mean that you're any
  good. It does say something about your ability to pay, your ability to get into the
  school, and your commitment to get through it — but that's just only one dimension, one
  metric."
- "I think we put too much emphasis on institutional knowledge, and there's many ways to
  acquire that."
- "Any transaction between you and a client is an exchange of value... what we're trying
  to do is find something that's good for both the client and for yourself, and that's
  gonna be a different thing for each client."
- "The person's coming to you saying this is not valuable to me — you're like, okay, it's
  not valuable to you, well what is? Maybe I can help you with that."
- "If they say nothing... then you should spend nothing on this and you probably do
  nothing. Don't do it just because everybody else is doing it."
- "You can invent systems to potentially resolve any client objection."

Unnamed young designer (context):

- "I know that there's a limit to the value I can bring to my clients and I don't really
  know what that looks like in terms of pricing."
- "The Futur... in addition to Lynda.com is essentially my design education, in addition
  to like client work."
