# Starting The Branding Process — Building A Brand, Episode 1

## Summary

Episode 1 of The Futur's **"Building A Brand"** documentary series, which follows a
real, paying-client brand engagement from start to finish. The client is **Hamilton
Family Brewery** (Rancho Cucamonga, California), owned by **Josh Hamilton** and his
wife **Kristin/Christine Hamilton** (name garbled between the two spellings in the
captions). The work is run through **Blind**, the Santa Monica brand strategy & design
consultancy (founded 1995) that is the parent studio behind The Futur.

Notably, **Chris Do himself does not appear as a named/attributed speaker in this
transcript.** The engagement is led by two Blind creative directors — **Matthew Ensign**
(creative director) and a second CD whose name is garbled in the captions as "Cena"
(likely a mis-transcription; possibly Greg Gunn or another team member — attribution
uncertain) — together with **Ben Burns** (digital director / brand strategist at Blind).
The narration/voiceover (which delivers the framework definitions of "brand," "rebrand,"
and "what makes a good logo") is unattributed in the captions.

Because Chris Do is not clearly attributed to any statement here, **this source
contributes essentially no direct persona (voice/beliefs) data.** Its value is context:
it establishes the Building A Brand series, the Blind/Futur real-client methodology, and
the framework vocabulary The Futur teaches (Marty Neumeier's brand definition; the
three-part good-logo test attributed on-screen to a designer garbled as "Ziggy Javie").

The episode sets up the engagement: Hamilton Family Brewery has run successfully for ~5
years on DIY branding, is now moving to retail/cans (competing on the shelf), and needs a
rebrand. Deliverables named: new logo, two packaging templates, a website, and a style
guide. Next episode previews the "discovery" process.

## Key claims

All dated 2019-05-22. Paraphrased unless quoted below. **No claim here is attributable to
Chris Do**, so none is persona data — all are context (team, client, or unattributed
narration).

- (Narration, unattributed) A brand, per Marty Neumeier, is a person's gut feeling about
  an organization, person, or company — influenced by visual aesthetic, the sound of its
  messaging, and overall market positioning.
- (Narration, unattributed) A rebrand is simply an adjustment of the customer touchpoints
  that affect the overall experience — sometimes an incremental update, sometimes a
  complete makeover.
- (Narration, unattributed) A good logo (attributed on-screen to a designer, name garbled
  "Ziggy Javie") must do three things: be appropriate for its context, be memorable, and
  be simple so it works at any scale (tiny on a phone or on the side of a building).
- (Ben Burns, context) The core problem: once a brand hits retail shelves it competes
  with everyone, including "the big boys," so Hamilton must be repositioned and its
  identity redesigned to stand out. Hamilton is "bursting at the seams" and ready to
  scale from DIY to retail.
- (Ben Burns, context) On first contact, budget-fit was uncertain and Blind might not have
  been able to help — but Josh came across as genuine and truly wanting guidance because
  his business was outgrowing his brand.
- (Matthew Ensign, context) Matthew and Ben are leading the Blind team on the Hamilton
  rebrand. Scoped deliverables: redo the logo, two packaging templates, a website, and a
  unifying style guide to influence all other touchpoints.
- (Josh Hamilton, client context) Origin story: fell in love with home brewing, sold his
  house in ~3 days, moved in with in-laws, and opened the brewery in ~7 months (vs. the
  ~1.5 years typical) with a near-zero bank balance on opening day; a line was waiting 15
  minutes before doors opened.
- (Josh Hamilton, client context) Mission/tagline: **"love people, love beer"** — ordered
  intentionally, people before beer, because the greatest beer means nothing with no one
  to share it.
- (Josh Hamilton, client context) His self-made logo packed in hidden meaning (Cucamonga
  Falls, city heritage, a Hamilton family tree that was actually accidental) that never
  communicated to customers; it's cluttered, dense, illegible small, and impractical on
  bottle caps/koozies.
- (Josh Hamilton, client context) New-flavor ideas come from everyday life (e.g., the
  Mango IPA started from $1 mangoes on sale at Stater Bros); his rule: brew it again only
  if it's a beer he'd want another pint of.
