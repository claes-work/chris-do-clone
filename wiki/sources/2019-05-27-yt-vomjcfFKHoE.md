# Package Design Critique Part 1 - Young Guns Episode 5

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do lays out an explicit, reusable **5-criteria packaging-judging rubric** plus a compact set of design-craft heuristics (observe-without-judgment, hierarchy/contrast, don't-hide-the-brand, tell-the-product-story, elevate-through-materials). This is dense design-craft persona training data worth promoting into `wiki/topics/design-craft/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), not just held at L2.

## Summary

Season-2 "Young Guns" packaging-critique episode: four student designers (Anastasia, Annie, Maithree, Megan) present redesigns of ethnic-food packaging (basmati rice, White Rabbit creamy candy, Japanese green tea) and Chris Do critiques each one live. The through-line is a repeatable design-craft method — first observe the original design "without judgment," identify the primary read via contrast, protect brand hierarchy, position for premium, and tell the product's story through materials, print finishes, and cultural authenticity. Chris also states the five judging criteria he uses (high-end feel, appetizing, shelf presence, product features, appropriateness) and repeatedly warns against clinical/sterile solutions for food products.

Speaker: The critique voice is **Chris Do** throughout (single mentor; first person — "it's time for me to score your design," "the five judging criteria," live marking-up of the designs). Students speak only in their own presentation and post-critique reflection segments (context). **Melinda Livsey is NOT detected** speaking in this transcript. Student-presentation and reflection passages carry `attribution: uncertain` because captions are unlabeled; only Chris-attributed critique/principles train the persona.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-05-27] Before redesigning, **observe the original design without judgment or attachment** — "sometimes in our zeal to redesign and redo everything we throw away a lot of very useful information." (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-05-27] Identify the **primary read**: the element the eye lands on first. Contrast is what creates it, and contrast is achieved through (a) value (black/white is highest contrast), (b) size and complexity (big next to small), and (c) texture / tonal qualities. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-27] **Five judging criteria for the packaging** (his stated rubric): (1) Does it feel high-end? (2) Does it look appetizing? — "you're gonna get knocked down many points if it doesn't look appetizing"; (3) Does it have shelf presence? — can only be judged from a photo of the design on the actual store shelf next to competitors, not an abstract mock-up; (4) Does it convey the product features? (e.g. long-grain, non-sticky, aromatic, origin); (5) Is it appropriate? — co-opting a foreign design language and pasting it on top is problematic. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-27] **Repositioning is nearly free leverage**: better packaging "doesn't cost the manufacturer any more money to make but it could have a dramatic impact on their bottom line if they're able to sell it for even 25 cents more." Many suppliers sell the same white-label product to multiple buyers — the packaging and branding are the only differentiator. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-27] **Don't hide the brand.** Designers habitually take a logo they dislike and "shove it in the corner." On the rice pack the brand is the maker (Ben Lian Foo), not "basmati" (the type of rice) — "it's like saying car and then we hide Ford somewhere and we can't tell what it is anymore." (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-05-27] **Food products must not feel clinical/sterile.** Cold blues, dot grids, and strict Swiss grids read like "cosmetic or medicine packaging." Inject warmth via hand-done qualities, texture (textured/French speckle paper), warmer colors, and photography. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-27] **Elevate through materials and print finishes** — foil / hot silver foil stamp, varnish and other coatings, embossing, wood or balsa packaging, seals, sleeves, mixed materials (e.g. corrugated cardboard wrap over the inner pack). "A lot of high-end packaging does this where they mix materials." (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-05-27] **Packaging tells a story and transports the buyer.** Analogy: like a coffee shop tells coffee's story (burlap bags, roasting, harvesting), packaging should carry the product/company history. Citing a separate episode with Brian Collins — "books are like rocket ships" — Chris says packaging is a rocket ship too: "a 30-cent cup of tea might temporarily take your mind on a little vacation to Japan." (paraphrase + quote fragments; the rocket-ship line is attributed to Brian Collins by Chris)
- [2019-05-27] **Cultural authenticity beats Western pastiche.** He dislikes "Asian typefaces designed by Westerners" / the "rickshaw typeface" — having been to Japan, nothing there looks like it; better to commission real Japanese calligraphy ("ask somebody on the internet who's Japanese who was really good at calligraphy") or use authentic ink-brush / watercolor art forms. (paraphrase + quote fragments)
- [2019-05-27] **Hierarchy is decision-making**: "where do you want my eye to go... I think we need to make a decision as to what we're gonna focus on." Too many similarly-sized elements make a vertical package hard to navigate; scale things up and down to fix it. (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-05-27] **Make the brand asset big and crop into it** — "that's what Andy Warhol did, he just took an average soap box and made it 200 times the size." When stuck, try cropping into elements and blowing them up; reference Charles S. Anderson for retro/nostalgic vibes and The Designers Republic for Asian-typeface influence. (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-05-27] **Think about the "billboard effect" of the shelf** — design so front/back panels create a repeating pattern when a retailer lines units up, instead of designing a single isolated unit. (paraphrase)
- [2019-05-27] Trying to "show everything, put everything in the box" makes a product "look really cheap" and leaves the eye nowhere to go — reducing/simplifying is usually the right instinct. (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2019-05-27] **Teaching-format caveat (transparency):** a real classroom critique would be ~20-30 minutes per person over many weeks of work; compressing it "for the sake of TV" and long-distance coaching means "there's a good chance I'm missing and skipping critical steps in the design-thinking process." (paraphrase)

### Student presenters (context — `attribution: uncertain`, unlabeled captions)

- [2019-05-27] Anastasia (basmati rice): removed the dual-language text and Taj Mahal, replaced with an Indian mandala pattern echoing the circular bowl of rice; reflected that her biggest insight was to **make decisions faster** and to **insert emotion/humanity** into ethnic food products (warmth, home, cultural experience). (paraphrase, not persona training)
- [2019-05-27] Annie (White Rabbit creamy candy): kept the red/white/blue palette and enlarged the bold Chinese wordmark; acknowledged the **hierarchy issue** stemmed from a messy process and time constraints, and explained she chose the product-forward layout to serve non-literate/older consumers who recognize the product visually. (paraphrase, not persona training)
- [2019-05-27] Maithree (Japanese green tea, brand Melasma from Sri Lanka): aimed for minimalism; reflected that adding **calligraphy brush strokes** and spending more time **understanding the product itself** would have made it read as a luxury product. (paraphrase, not persona training)
- [2019-05-27] Megan (J-Basket green tea): used a wabi-sabi minimalist aesthetic, reduced to the logo colors, illustrated the tea leaf and added a story panel; reflected she wished she'd stayed wabi-sabi and let someone else do the illustrations. (paraphrase, not persona training)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All quotes below are **Chris Do**:

- "Sometimes in our zeal to redesign and redo everything we throw away a lot of very useful information. So the first thing I'm gonna do... I just want to observe the design without any judgment or attachment."
- "Contrast is achieved through values, meaning white and black has the highest contrast; contrast in size and complexity, something that's really big next to something that's really small; and contrast in texture and tonal qualities."
- "It doesn't cost the manufacturer any more money to make, but it could have a dramatic impact on their bottom line if they're able to sell it for even 25 cents more... many manufacturers and suppliers sell to many other people as a white-label production, and all they do is the packaging and the branding."
- "The brand is not basmati, it's the type of rice. It's like saying 'car' and then we hide 'Ford' somewhere and we can't tell what it is anymore."
- "You'll notice a lot of cosmetic or medicine packaging is done with this very clinical look."
- "That's what Andy Warhol did — he just took an average soap box and made it 200 times the size, and then you get some really interesting design components."
- "On a different episode Brian Collins talks about how books are like rocket ships. I think in a way packaging is like a rocket ship — it transports you to some other place... a 30-cent cup of tea might temporarily take your mind on a little vacation to Japan."
- "Whenever I see that on the shelf I'm always thinking, wow, some designer's really thinking about the billboard effect of the shelf itself instead of thinking of a single unit."
- "The tendency is to try to show everything, put everything in the box, and what you wind up doing is you make things look really cheap — my eye does not know where to go."
- "This would normally be about twenty or thirty minutes per person in a critique in a classroom... for the sake of TV I have to condense a lot of information down, so there's a good chance I'm missing and skipping critical steps in the design-thinking process."
