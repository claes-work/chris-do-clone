# Building A Brand — Defining The Customer, Episode 2

## Summary

Episode 2 of The Futur's documentary-style "Building A Brand" series, following the
brand strategy consultancy **Blind** (Santa Monica, est. 1995) as it rebrands a real
client, **Hamilton Family Brewery** (owners Josh and Kristen Hamilton, of Rancho
Cucamonga, CA). The client wants to expand beyond its tasting room into distribution
(cans on retail shelves) and needs its packaging/brand to communicate what the
in-person hometown crowd already feels.

The episode documents a **discovery session** facilitated by two Blind strategists,
**Ben** and **Matthew**. The core topic is **defining the customer**: the team walks
the clients through identifying their tasting-room customer types (beer fanatic, beer
snob, brewery enthusiast) and then co-constructs two named **user personas** —
"Jessica" (a ~30-year-old married stay-at-home mom / side-hustler, ~$75k household,
shops Sprouts/Trader Joe's/Target) and "Curt" (a ~35-year-old single tech engineer in
LA, ~$120k, a beer snob chasing rare/edge beers). The session also does a first pass on
brand look-and-feel (weathered, heritage, modern-but-not-trendy, welcoming), using
Blind's pre-made "style-scape" wall to give the clients something concrete to react to.

## Key claims

All dated 2019-05-29. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Attribution per speaker.

**Blind team — Ben and Matthew (CONTEXT, not persona data):**

- A discovery session is a facilitated meeting with clients/stakeholders (hours to
  days depending on scope) where the team defines goals, identifies challenges,
  studies the customers and their pain points, and discusses brand attributes —
  by asking questions, challenging assumptions, and surfacing meaningful insights
  to package into a forward strategy. (Ben/Matthew, context)
- A facilitator can leverage coming in with "fresh eyes" to stay genuinely curious and
  extract more from the client; even experienced facilitators still get jitters before
  an all-day session. (Matthew, context)
- "There's no roadmap for entrepreneurship"; the consultancy's job is to "lift the fog"
  for the client — first in discovery (business goals, customers, brand), then in design
  (making things that help the client on that journey). (Ben/Matthew, context)
- Identifying the customer base is one of the most important discovery exercises: if you
  don't know who your customer is, you won't know where to find them or how to talk to
  them. (Ben/Matthew, context)
- Named user personas serve as a **filter and reference throughout the entire process** —
  the team repeatedly checks new design work against the persona ("would Jessica like
  this?"). (Ben/Matthew, context)
- Personas are also a **feedback-management tool**: when a stakeholder reacts to work out
  of personal preference, you can redirect — "we're not designing this for you, we're
  designing for Jessica." (Ben/Matthew, context)
- When the clients drew blanks on a welcoming-brand example, the facilitator improvised —
  had everyone stand up and react to Blind's existing wall of brand style-scapes —
  because "sometimes you just have to have something to react to visually instead of
  trying to develop the words out of thin air." (Ben/Matthew, context)
- The discovery output ("look and feel," then voice/tone, then brand behavior/personality)
  will be bundled and sent to the client to confirm, becoming the "Bible" for everything
  subsequently created; it then feeds the creative brief and the designers' style-scapes
  (previewed for episode 3). (Ben/Matthew, context)

**Client — Josh and Kristen Hamilton (CONTEXT):**

- Their current challenge is communicating on retail packaging (a can on a shelf away from
  their hometown) what the tasting-room experience conveys in person. (Josh, context)
- The brand was "born on a passion"; they want to separate from the "trendy snobbery" of
  craft beer and be the people-centric, hospitable, family-friendly brewery — a reaction
  to an early bad customer-service experience at another brewery. (Josh/Kristen, context)
- They want the brand to feel weathered/heritage (seem older than it is, "in the blood")
  and communicate quality/reliability, while staying modern and not throwing away their
  identity to chase every trend. (Josh, context)
