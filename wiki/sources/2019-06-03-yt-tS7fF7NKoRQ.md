# Package Design Critique Part 2 - Young Guns Episode 6

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do's live packaging critiques here contain a dense, reusable set of design-craft principles (typography optics, mockup discipline, appetite appeal, brand-heritage leverage, materials/finishes storytelling, contrast-of-volumes, anti-cliché). Worth promoting the NEW craft rules into `wiki/topics/design-craft/` and into `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), beyond L2. ## Summary

Part 2 (companion to Episode 5, 2019-05-27) of the Young Guns Season 2 packaging-rebrand challenge. Students present their redesigns of ethnic/grocery food products and Chris Do critiques each live, then scores them against the "five criteria" rubric established in Ep5 and announces the winner. He also brings back three Season 1 alumni (Spencer, Connor, Arune) to compete. The critiques are craft-heavy: typography optics and lettering, mockup quality, food "appetite appeal," leveraging a brand's real heritage instead of clichés, and using materials/finishes to tell a story and raise perceived value. He closes by restating that changing a design changes the perceived value of a product — a point "for clients to hear."

Speaker: primarily **Chris Do** (the critiquing voice throughout). Students present and reflect as context: **Monica**, **Ria Hardcastle**, **Tosun** (Season 2), and **Spencer**, **Connor**, **Arune** (Season 1 returnees); winner **Anastasia**. **Matthew Encina** appears briefly as context (consulted on mangoes). **Melinda does NOT appear** in this episode. Only Chris-attributed material trains the persona; student/guest statements are context. Speaker labels come from on-screen narration and Chris naming people; some caption spellings are uncertain (names, product names).

## Key claims

### Chris Do

Typography & lettering craft (mostly NEW to the corpus beyond Ep5):

- [2019-06-03] Horizontal strokes should be optically thinner than vertical strokes; a stretched typeface makes horizontals thicker than verticals, which reads as amateur — "some designer who doesn't know what they're doing has stretched a mango nectar typeface... that's a typography violation." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-03] In custom lettering, eliminate dead space; decorative flourishes on letters (e.g. the V and R) trap negative space, which is generally not desirable — bring baselines closer and use letterforms to fill the space instead. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-03] A letter that has been truncated to close up letter-spacing (a "C" with a cut-off bowl) reads awkwardly; prefer a more circular, complete form even if it leaves an open counter. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-03] Avoid force/flush-justified type on informational body copy — the forced spacing creates "rivers" of white; set it ragged instead. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-03] Old-timey/Victorian decorative boxes can anchor a lockup — but nesting too many boxes (package box + internal label boxes + nutrition box) gets "really boxy"; open it up with floating, un-boxed elements. (paraphrase)

Mockups & presentation:

- [2019-06-03] "Mock-ups can make or break your presentation" — a great mockup (right shape, lighting, then design applied) sells the work even if the render isn't a real photograph. (quote + paraphrase)
- [2019-06-03] For physical mockups, use a sharp X-Acto knife — sloppy cutting (the "boot of Italy" edging) undermines otherwise good work. (paraphrase; Arts Center craft standard)

Food packaging — appetite appeal & communication (partly NEW):

- [2019-06-03] "Appetite appeal" is a critical component of food packaging — if it doesn't look appetizing, the shopper moves on in about one second; show the actual food (open the jar, hero the product shot, beautiful photography of the fruit). (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-03] Photography/illustration must communicate what the product IS — you recognize an apple by its divot and stem; if the viewer can't tell it's an apple, the element isn't doing its job. (paraphrase)

Brand heritage & story (extends Ep5's don't-hide-the-brand):

- [2019-06-03] Leverage a brand's genuine heritage as an ownable strength — a brand "established in 1912" with real founders (health crusaders) has a story only they can claim; redesigning it away means telling the client "you just threw out a hundred years of history." Update the throwback, don't erase it. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-03] Fix hierarchy so the heritage supports rather than overwhelms — "est. 1912" matters but shouldn't supersede everything; be more economical with the real estate you have. (paraphrase)

Anti-cliché & cultural authenticity (echoes Ep5, with new examples):

- [2019-06-03] Avoid national clichés — the boot of Italy is "a little cliché"; authentic imported Italian products "don't scream at you... it's not like Mario from Mario Kart standing there." Communicate the country through material and typography, not the obvious icon. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-03] Cliché can still be acceptable IF it communicates — on the Greek feta the Greek-key motif is cliché "but if it communicates, that's what I'm interested in." (paraphrase; nuance/contrast with the anti-cliché point)
- [2019-06-03] Flagged an authenticity issue: a "traditional Greek cheese" actually "freshly crafted in India" and branded Greek raises authenticity questions the design has to reckon with. (paraphrase)

Materials, finishes & storytelling (extends Ep5's materials/finishes):

- [2019-06-03] Use materials and finishes to "transport" the buyer and tell a story — clean serif type, a stamp that says "imported from Italy," a seal you have to break, fibrous/rustic paper, hand-torn rough edges "as if a human being made it," two labels of two different papers/materials attached together, engravings/crosshatch to depict ingredients. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-03] A matte label with a glossy accent element reads as more premium and appeals to higher clientele. (paraphrase; stated by student Monica and endorsed in critique)

Layout & composition tricks (NEW):

- [2019-06-03] Increase contrast between volumes when dividing space — push the dividing line to a 4/5 vs 1/5 proportion so the two color fields contrast strongly. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-03] Play with cap lines and baselines as a visual trick — letting a bar sit on top of the baseline can make a color field read as "on top," e.g. a word stenciled over it. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-03] Make labels wrap around to another side / overlap to create dimension. (paraphrase)

Perceived value (ties design-craft to business/pricing — echoes packaging-as-rocket-ship):

- [2019-06-03] "By changing the design we can change the perceived value of something. This is for clients to hear and for you to communicate to your clients." (quote)

Meta / teaching:

- [2019-06-03] Named the scoring against "the five criteria we've defined" (the Ep5 rubric) and picked the winner primarily on photography, illustration, integrated pattern, and popping color. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-03] "Less can be more" — the cleanest S1 redesigns won by decluttering, simpler typefaces, tighter grids, and not trying to "yell." (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All **Chris Do** unless labeled:

- "Mock-ups can make or break your presentation."
- "Some designer who doesn't know what they're doing has stretched a mango nectar typeface... that's a typography violation, that's a penalty of ten yards right there."

- "This does not look appetizing to me. So I'm cruising down the aisle... I'm like, what is that? — I'm moving on pretty quickly. Appetite appeal is a critical component of your design."
- "They would have a hard time as a client saying you just threw out a hundred years of history with our company."
- "The Greeks watching this will say it's so cliché to use that, but if it communicates, that's what I'm interested in."
- "By changing the design we can change the perceived value of something. This is for clients to hear and for you to communicate to your clients."
- (Context — **Connor**) "Creative block is a myth. It doesn't exist. It's just a lack of perspective."
