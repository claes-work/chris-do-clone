# Build Your Audience and Connect with Them – w/ Melinda Livsey

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a named, reusable Chris Do teaching framework ("three learning styles: high-level / story / kinesthetic," and "teach while you learn, not what you know") plus a compact statement of his belief-system → massive-action model. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/mindset/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated) rather than sitting at L2.

## Summary

A Pro-Group-style coaching conversation between Chris Do and Futur colleague Melinda Livsey. Melinda reports progress on her audience-building system (Instagram as top-of-funnel "megaphone," newsletter + blog lead magnets / content upgrades, meeting her audience at their "rock bottom"), and Chris uses her updates to teach a set of ideas: the three learning styles and how to design lessons that hit all three; "teach while you learn, not what you know"; why people resist new ideas (belief system as identity, denial → anger); the belief → massive-action loop; compelling events as the trigger for change; and innovation vs. iteration inside his own company. He closes with mentorship advice (do everything your mentor tells you the first time; the Karate Kid analogy).

Speaker: **two-person dialogue** — **Chris Do** (subject) and **Melinda Livsey** (recurring Futur colleague / brand strategist; context-only, NOT the subject). Only Chris-attributed material trains the persona. Third parties cited: Jordan Peterson, Bob Ross, Van Gogh, Jeff Bezos, Jim Rohn, Debbie Millman, Ben Burns, "Carrie/Kerry," Rahul, and unnamed Futur staff (Matthew, Greg Gunn, Mark Contreras).

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-06-04] There are (at least) three learning styles, and lessons should be designed to hit all three: (1) **high-level** learners who can grasp a book from its title / table of contents and just want the top-line concept; (2) **story** learners — "pretty much everybody" — who derive meaning from narrative (why parables and movies work: "humans seek pleasure while trying to minimize pain," so we can "experience the lesson without the pain"); (3) **kinesthetic** learners who must do it themselves before they'll accept it. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-04] To teach the maximum number of people, structure every lesson as: state the high-level concept → tell a personal story / example → give tactical, do-it-yourself steps (a framework or process chart) so they experience it firsthand. He demonstrates with the "lace your fingers, then re-lace them the opposite way" discomfort exercise. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-04] "Experts don't make great teachers; people who are learning in the journey themselves make the best kinds of teachers" — hence **"teach while you learn, not what you know."** He'd rather be Bob Ross ("happy trees," bite-size, relatable) than Van Gogh (a master whose method — "cut off the ear, drink a lot" — doesn't transfer). (paraphrase; "teach while you learn, not what you know" is close to verbatim)
- [2019-06-04] He self-identifies as a **type-1 (high-level) learner** who values "efficiency, processes, and just optimized results" — "the shortest path between two distances [is] a straight line" — which is why coaching kinesthetic (type-3) learners frustrates him: "I often say that I'm the worst kind of teacher" because he wants to give a condensed piece of information and have someone run with it. His own growth arc is learning empathy — to slow down and teach people in ways that are good for them. (paraphrase, self-critical)
- [2019-06-04] In his own Art Center class (small sample, ~10–12 students at a time), a test he devised showed the dominant result was **type-3 / kinesthetic** learners. He flags this as a small, caveated sample, not a universal claim. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-04] When people fight a new idea (e.g., value-based pricing in his comment sections), they're fighting **change** and **their belief system**, which "becomes part of your own identity." The reaction runs denial → anger ("did I waste 30 or 40 years charging hourly?"). We rely on belief systems because "our understanding of the world is very small." (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-04] A **critical thinker** "can entertain an idea that's foreign to them in their own mind as if it were their own," systematically try to disprove it, and — if they can't — adopt it and throw out the old idea. Goal: "hold strong beliefs loosely" / "hold strong opinions loosely" — strong opinions, but drop them when a better idea arrives ("mental elasticity"). (paraphrase; "hold strong beliefs loosely" near-verbatim)
- [2019-06-04] He models this himself: ~4.5 years earlier he told Jose "I don't do sales, my sales reps do it"; later he learned to do sales calls and "got really good at it," coming "full circle" — investigate, throw out the old idea, adopt the new. (paraphrase, self-referential)
- [2019-06-04] **Belief precedes results.** "We rarely ever do what our mind and body do not commit to doing." If you believe something won't work, you'll ask half-heartedly / apologetically, sabotage it, try once, and quit — "how many times did you try before you believe something to be true?" "It starts with the mind. You must believe it to be true; the body starts to listen to the mind, and then you will it into becoming true." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-04] The people who get the best results are the small percentage who **take massive action** — do exactly what he prescribes without demanding all the evidence first. Example: he told Ben Burns to "fire all your clients"; Ben asked only *how*, made 53 phone calls the next day, three clients stayed, "his life is transformed." Example: "Carrie/Kerry" was stuck on pricing, finally accepted the belief challenge, asked for double, and doubled her income that year. (paraphrase, third-party examples)
- [2019-06-04] "The truth doesn't need you to know that it's true for it to be true" (if Mars is red, it's red even if you're colorblind). Provocatively: the thing he told "Carrie" that triggered her change "could have been completely 100% fabricated" — what mattered was that she believed it and took action. So "your truth" — what you believe — is what works. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-04] We mostly change for one of two reasons: **"our heart's been broken or our mind has been opened."** People who take massive action have often had their hearts broken repeatedly, or painted themselves into a corner with no option but out. The more enlightened path is to choose to keep an open mind because science keeps overturning long-held beliefs. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-04] **Compelling event**: "people don't change unless there's a compelling event" — a catalyst that "splits the road into two paths" and propels change because you have no choice. In business, a compelling event = launching a new product, downsizing/hard times, or explosive growth. Most people (esp. in the US) are "just okay — not miserable, not killing it," complacent, and won't change without one. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-04] **Innovation vs. iteration.** He tells his own staff (Ben Burns, Matthew, Greg Gunn, Mark Contreras) that "what you guys are calling innovation, I'm calling iteration" — doing more of the same. A 300%/year revenue growth goal is in "direct conflict" with iterating: "we can't be the same company, same people, same products, same marketing, same content if we expect to grow." He asks them to entertain only "super scary and crazy" ideas and "throw out every playbook that's ever worked." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-04] "Failure and success, failure and invention — they're inseparable twins" (attributes the sentiment to a Jeff Bezos quote). You must embrace failure as much as innovation, or "you're truly not innovating." The school system "squeezed the creativity out of us" by rewarding obedience — "the wrong operating system." He'd flip grading to reward daring failure over safe conformity. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-04] Debbie Millman called him an optimist, which caught him off guard; Matthew agreed ("you're the optimist; Ben and I are the pessimists because we keep fighting you"). Chris reframes: challenging everything is his "status quo," so he wouldn't even label it optimism — "I always believe everything will work out." (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-04] His deepest fear: "the scariest thought in my mind is that I haven't changed at all." He puts "very little value / stock in what was done before" because he can't credibly tell audiences to be innovative and throw out the old while his own company repeats itself. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-04] Life is a "very precious gift," finite and short — make the most of it: climb your mountain, help others massively, or "leave the planet a little bit better than you found it." He suspects most people lack that ambition because they were told "this is good enough," plus fear of failure. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-04] On a coaching call that morning, someone said they wanted to grow 10%; Chris: "Wow, you set the bar very very low" — he sets his at 300%. "You live up to the goals you set for yourself." But he's at peace not hitting 300% — "I don't punish myself, I don't feel depressed when we don't hit it, I just keep going forward." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-04] Cites the **Jim Rohn** idea he says he's now internalized: "learn to be happy with what we have while we pursue all that we want" — the two must be connected (chase-only = negative outlook; complacency = no pursuit). (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-04] **Mentorship advice**: seek a mentor / trusted source; there is no perfectly-aligned ideal mentor — pick whoever most closely aligns with what you want to achieve, "throw yourself at their feet," try their ideas earnestly and repeatedly before deciding they don't work, then find a different mentor. Analogy: **Karate Kid** — Miyagi's one condition is "do everything I tell you without questions"; "wax on, wax off" was karate the whole time. "Submit to your master / mentor / Shifu, learn as much as you can, then move on." His tip to Melinda: "just do everything I tell you the first time." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-06-04] The value of a mentor is compressing others' trial-and-error: "take the trial and error I've experienced in my life and help you get there without having to fail as often as we have." (paraphrase)

### Melinda Livsey — CONTEXT ONLY (does NOT train the persona)

- [2019-06-04] Her breakthrough was understanding the **funnel**: Instagram is not the relationship-builder but the "megaphone" / top-of-funnel awareness engine to get new eyeballs; you then funnel followers to a call-to-action (newsletter signup or a specific blog post). (paraphrase — Melinda)
- [2019-06-04] She nearly doubled her email list by adding **content upgrades** / lead magnets to her best (high-ranking) blog posts, mixing rock-bottom stories with practical teaching in her newsletter, and listening to her audience (a group call on lead generation spawned a waitlist for a group-coaching eCourse). Her paid Facebook group (enrollment opened every ~3 months) had one of its highest months in April. (paraphrase — Melinda)
- [2019-06-04] Big shift: meeting her audience (fellow designers/creatives) "where they're at," by revisiting her own career "rock bottom" ~10 years earlier when she wanted to quit design — rather than sharing what she's learning right now. (paraphrase — Melinda)
- [2019-06-04] She cites **Jordan Peterson**: you have to "own an idea" — until you apply it and understand it experientially, you don't own it. (paraphrase — Melinda, third-party citation)
