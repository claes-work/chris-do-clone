## Summary

Episode 3 of The Futur's **"Building A Brand"** documentary series, following Chris Do's
consultancy **Blind** (Santa Monica, est. 1995) as it rebrands **Hamilton Family Brewery**
(owners Josh and Kristen; brewery founded 2014, based near Rancho Cucamonga). This episode's
topic is **working on a design team**: how the strategy insights get translated into a
**creative brief**, and how three designers then research, present references, and receive
creative direction before building style-scapes in a later episode.

**Chris Do's presence: LIGHT / effectively absent.** Chris is not named or clearly voiced
anywhere in this episode. The creative direction and brief are led by two Blind
creative directors, **Matthew** and **Ben** (introduced by the narrator as the people the
designers reconvene with). The three designers — **Emily Che**, **Sam** (addressed as
"Saying"; the narrator's roster also lists a "Chun Min Chao"), and **Minh** ("Men"/"Minh") —
present research and receive feedback. Because no statement can be confidently attributed to

sometimes hard to separate on audio; several are marked uncertain.

## Key claims

All dated 2019-06-05. Paraphrased unless quoted. **None attributed to Chris Do.**

- **(Narrator, context)** Blind is a brand strategy & design consultancy in Santa Monica,
  California, founded 1995; it uses design to help clients stand out. The primary design team
  on this project — Sam, Chun Min Chao, and Emily Che — are Art Center College of Design
  graduates who have worked at Blind for several years.
- **(Matthew/Ben, attribution: uncertain — a Blind director)** After the strategy session, the
  next phase is the **discovery-to-brief translation**. The hard part is using words to describe
  images; the brief must take the client's mental idea and communicate it to designers in terms
  they understand. With a design team from many backgrounds, communication is "a massive
  challenge."
- **(Director, context)** A **creative brief** is an internal-team meeting giving a high-level
  overview: it surfaces discovery insights (goals, challenges, user needs), states brand
  attributes, and defines a clear set of **creative parameters** so designers can work. It ends
  with **clear assignments** so each designer goes off to produce deliverables.
- **(Ben, context)** Background on the brewery's **tasting room**: it feels like a "family
  garage"; the brand ethos is "it's your garage, we just brew beer in it." It appeals to people
  who want a high-quality beer in a non-bar environment — family-friendly (games, free juice
  boxes for kids), dog-friendly patio.
- **(Director, context)** The five **look-and-feel attributes** the client highlighted:
  **reliable, welcoming, maker culture, charming, adventurous.** Reliable = consistency/quality
  (possibly expressed via a design system). Welcoming = warm, and specifically **should not feel
  expensive/premium** ("that's the wrong direction"). Maker culture = a handcrafted, DIY feel —
  but keep it **gender-neutral** ("ride that lane right down the middle"), avoiding the masculine
  maker style-scapes.
- **(Director, context)** **Charming** captures owner Josh's personality — a big, bombastic,
  self-deprecating, funny guy; "the guy you want to go have a beer with." The client rejected
  "funny/witty" as wrong wording; "charming" fit who he is.
- **(Director, context)** **Adventurous** = experimental flavors; the brewery does things "the
  big breweries are too chicken to do" because its smaller scale lets it experiment, test, and
  scale a beer up or down based on customer response.
- **(Director, context)** Proposes **adding "heritage"** as a sixth attribute — history matters
  to the owner (family business, his legacy); he wants it to feel older than it is to tap into
  reliability and authenticity. Product-over-marketing point: Hamilton uses **cans not bottles**
  because bottling would make the beer taste worse, showing he values product integrity.
- **(Minh, context)** Counterpoint / tension flagged: the company started in **2014** and is not
  even five years old, so building a **heritage** feel (usually implying 50–100 years) will be a
  challenge.
- **(Sam, context — designer)** First research step is **competitor research** and gathering
  references, plus reading the user profiles and look-and-feel doc; notes the client likes **1970s
  beer labels**, which requires research ("I wasn't born in the 70s").
- **(Director, context)** Two **user profiles** from discovery: **Jessica**, a "soccer mom"
  holding onto her social life (primary/current user), and **Kurt**, a **beer snob** (aspirational
  user — "the person they want more of"). The design challenge is to hit the bullseye between the
  two.
- **(Director feedback to Emily, context)** Her label references (stamps, flourishes, type-heavy,
  overlays) can make the brand feel older than it is — good — but gold-foil / black premium looks
  risk feeling too **premium/exclusive** vs. **welcoming**. Direction: tighten into a **style-scape**,
  bridge ornate heritage with **pops of color to modernize**, add some utilitarian aspects; and
  since **phase one is the logo**, test whether logos fit the packaging.
- **(Director feedback to Sam, context)** Sam shares "DNA" with Emily but leans toward **premium,
  collectible, physical** goods (Star Wars-collector analogy: enthusiasts love physical/limited
  editions, e.g. numbered "bottle 87 of 100"). Notes matte/cold-press/**tactile** paper stock and
  raises a **production question** — will condensation on cold cans affect matte printing? (follow
  up with Josh). Praises the hand-carved plywood **beer carrier** and a "big belly" yellow bag
  packaging that "straddles the line" between utilitarian/heritage/DIY without being overly DIY.
  Direction: Emily owns the **label** direction, Sam owns **containers/wraps/packaging** — keep
  them separate for two distinct directions.
- **(Director feedback to Minh, context)** Minh has the **primary/broad** user (beer enthusiasts
  who prefer breweries to bars). His 50s/60s references (overprint, layered color passes, movie
  posters, luggage tags) read as a **snapshot of the era** rather than **heritage** — director is
  "not latching on." Surfaces the **retro vs. heritage** language conflict (below). Still finds
  usable elements: crests, ornate + negative-space cuts (echoing the 450 Alaskan / Pacific
  Northwest reference), and **stamp details** that could convey beer info (color/taste notes)
  rather than serve as the logo.
- **(Director, context)** Wrap-up: each designer has a strong direction; because no single
  reference matched the target, the next phase (**style-scapes**) requires combining multiple
  references into one cohesive panorama. A style-scape is "similar to a mood board but more
  refined." Next episode: Sam, Minh, and Emily present style-scapes; then Ben and Matthew travel
  to Rancho Cucamonga to present finals to Josh and Kristen.
