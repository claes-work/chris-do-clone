## Summary

A Young Guns Season 2 coaching call in which Chris Do takes questions one at a time from six young designers (all women) with co-host Ricky facilitating. The through-line is **improvement, accountability, and focus**: get clear on your goals, stop doing low-reward work that only regurgitates what you already know, and organize your life around growth and learning. ## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-06-10] Organize your life around growth and learning; everything else is a distraction. He'd rather a young designer watch a video, take a course, attend a seminar, network, or do a workshop than do more low-reward client work, because client work is "a regurgitation of what you know or don't know — it's not contributing to your own growth." (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Decision heuristic: in everything, **minimize risk while maximizing return** — "low-risk, high-reward." People confuse this and take high-risk, low-reward actions (e.g. unsatisfying client work that underpays); cut those options. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] If your current low-paying client work can't be sped up "by running the same mile faster," stop and get coached / level up the portfolio, so future work earns more money in less time. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Accountability coaching: he wants students to "be clear about your goals and hold yourself accountable," rewatch the coaching to "develop that inner voice" that judges whether an action is good for them. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] "Saying lots of things isn't actually getting clarity at all. Committing to one thing and choosing your words very carefully will help you get clarity" — and then decisions become easier. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] For self-initiated portfolio projects, the freedom is the trap (you keep changing the brief). Fix it with the **SMART goal technique** — specific, measurable, time-bound. Write down five *specific* project briefs (e.g. "design a menu for a fast-casual Mexican restaurant," "packaging for cycling helmets"), not a vague "cloud" like "branding" or "museums." If you can't choose, roll a five-sided die and commit to whatever lands, within a finite deadline. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Be strategic about niche: do all five projects in one industry (e.g. food & beverage) rather than spreading across game/tech/food/clothing — "it's hard to get clients when you're that spread thin." (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] The deeper skill is becoming **self-determined vs. other-determined, self-validated vs. other-validated** — generating your own motivation and direction from within. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] "The number one thing wrong with young people is you think you have a lot of time." Your body has been decaying since birth; remembering you're going to die is the antidote to overthinking — "make the most before you do." By the time you deliberate whether to do something, the person who just started has already done it. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Individual vs. studio branding doesn't matter — clients call because they want to work with *you*, the star. Anyone who gets between the client and the star just slows the process down. Don't invent a fake multi-person studio; if you can write a compelling profile for a fake company, write it for yourself. The name (`redheaddesigns.com` or whatever) is irrelevant; you can expand the name later if it starts holding you back. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] The bond with a client is determined by **the depth and richness of the conversation, not physical proximity**. He knows more people online than in person and rarely meets clients face-to-face; conference organizers have booked him via email alone without ever speaking. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] You demonstrate care and attention to a remote client through **listening and acting** — e.g. sending an audio recording, transcribed notes, a summarized email, and a clear plan of action (A/B/C/D + the links they asked for) after a call. A remote stranger delivering on a promise can impress *more* than a neighbor. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] For time-zone friction with overseas clients, present three options: (1) frame the offset as a benefit ("your end-of-day is my start-of-day, so I work while you sleep"); (2) "the time difference will be my challenge to solve, not your problem" — adjust your own schedule for the few critical calls (kickoff, midway, final review); (3) hire a virtual assistant on their time zone and charge more for it. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Bad habits designers have: they **overthink and don't take enough action**; undervalue themselves and underprice; jump to conclusions without a real thought process; confuse being a *designer* with being an *artist*; and don't learn about the world outside their trade (business, marketing, philosophy, psychology, sociology, history, English) so they "don't have a lot of dots that connect." (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] "You are more than the things that you make — the whole is greater than the sum of the parts." Don't define your whole self-worth by the thing you make; that's a narrow view of your contribution. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Good habits to build: gym once or twice a week, stop eating sugar, don't drink alcohol more than once a week, be intentional with your language and expand your vocabulary, surround yourself with people you admire and consume what they recommend, learn with the intention of teaching others, and be brave enough to put out imperfect work. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] The **"just to the ___" technique**: giant goals feel so overwhelming you never start, so instead of "finish the marathon," aim "just to the lamppost, just to the next block, just to the next mile" — "all goals are accomplished by taking small steps." (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Self-care is "a requirement, not a luxury" for the long haul. Citing Jim Rohn: "when you're at work do work, when you're at home do home" — the person who's mentally elsewhere is present in neither place, inefficient and distracted at both. When meditating or doing yoga, be fully there; don't feel guilty. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] "Tell me what you're doing in your spare time, I'll tell you what you become." Where your energy naturally goes reveals what your heart wants. Be smart about which recharge tasks you pick — choose ones that help you grow, and try to blur the line between work and play (e.g. do design work for wellness/yoga/meditation brands so your hobbies feed your craft). (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] "When I work I work really hard; when I play I play even harder." Hobbies may not pay off immediately but can be worked back in over time — his loves of fishing, MMA, and skateboarding became a fishing-lodge client, four seasons of UFC *Ultimate Fighter* show opens, three films with Stacy Peralta, and a first-ever Quiksilver commercial. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] On "commission" pricing, clarify the client means a **royalty**. You only have royalty leverage when "there's no replacement for you" (Justin Bieber can dictate terms because there's only one). First establish the proper project value, *then* negotiate a share of risk. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Packaging fees can't be dwarfed by the client's own printing costs — there must be scale between the two. A "small mom-and-pop launching a cereal nationwide" for $500 is incongruent; a real packaging job is roughly $5,000–$20,000. If they can't pay, get creative: (a) 100% upfront for a ~5% discount; (b) installments (50% up front / 25% at milestones / 25% on delivery); (c) a licensing deal, e.g. $5k plus 2% "of every package you print — not of what you sell, but what you make," sharing risk equitably. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] "Designers assume; professionals ask." Before pricing, ask: how big is the first print run, what does each package cost to print, what happens if it has to be reprinted — does the investment make sense for the gain. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] You don't need to know more than you know today to charge more — many designers are simply severely underpriced. But move in small steps (500 → 2,500, not 500 → 25,000). (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] "How we feel about a piece of information impacts how we say that piece of information." State your price (and your age) "like a fact, without any judgment added to it" — your face and voice betray your own doubt, and business people (professional negotiators) can see right through it. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] "What you do in the practice becomes what you do in competition" — bad habits rehearsed become bad habits under pressure, and nothing is more at stake than talking money with a client. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Lightning round: he'd choose **contract work over a full-time job** — "I don't need full-time, I don't like stability; run towards the thing that scares you the most." (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] A client needs to know three things about a designer: whether they can afford you, whether you can do the work, and whether they can trust you. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] "How to be more productive/efficient is the wrong question — the question is how to have clear goals. When you have clear goals you naturally get more done." (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Some things can't be learned online — how to work with people, and the subtle nuances of communication and bidding. The best way to learn them is to go work for someone you look up to and admire. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] On grief (student who lost her father): he offers a personal, non-clinical perspective — he tries to detach, sees himself as "from the earth" returning to earth and renewing the cycle, and doesn't assign extra meaning to a passing. Celebrate and honor the person's life by becoming who you were meant to be; dwelling and losing confidence "would probably hurt him more than anything." He explicitly says he's "not qualified to answer this" and urges her to get help (family, therapist) if she stays depressed. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-10] Closing note: "No matter where you are in your stage in life, when a client calls you, you've arrived fully formed — you need to be no more and no less than to be yourself." Take care of and learn to love yourself. (paraphrase)
