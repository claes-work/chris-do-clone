## Summary

Matthew Encina reports back from Adobe's 99U conference (theme: "create a future"),
first framing three challenges facing creatives — AI/automation is coming for
repetitive junior-designer work, organizations iterate instead of innovate, and we
are the most distracted generation ever — then offering three practical tools to
reclaim creativity and productivity. The three tools: (1) **Focus Sprints** (90-minute
distraction-free work blocks + 20-minute rest, from neuroscientist Dr. Sahar Yousef),
(2) **Better Brainstorms** (small groups, a defined challenge, a "naive expert," and a
"hot potato / yes-if" no-shoot-down round, from ex-Disney innovation head Duncan
Wardle), and (3) **Beautiful Boredom** (deliberately removing stimulation to let the
subconscious connect ideas). The video is sponsored by Adobe.

Speaker: single-presenter monologue by **Matthew Encina** (The Futur), NOT Chris Do.
Third parties cited (not present as speakers): Brian Collins, Dr. Sahar Yousef, Duncan
Wardle (former head of innovation & creativity at Disney), and artist Kyle T. Webster.

## Key claims

_All statements below are **Matthew Encina** (or third parties he cites) — none are
Chris Do, and none feed the persona. Recorded for context only._

### Matthew Encina

- [2019-06-14] "The gap is closing between what you can dream and what you can make" — self-publishing and better tools have removed old gatekeepers and barriers to entry. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-14] He has been a creative professional (designer, animator, content creator) for over 15 years and has seen the industry change rapidly; tools are better, education is online, idea-to-solution is faster than ever. (paraphrase, self-reported)
- [2019-06-14] **Challenge 1 — "the robots are coming":** citing Brian Collins, AI can already do most junior-designer work (mass iteration), so designers' required skills will change; the human edge is connecting disparate dots, expressing emotion, and dreaming up what doesn't yet exist. (paraphrase, attributed to Brian Collins)
- [2019-06-14] **Challenge 2 — innovate, don't iterate:** most organizations pursue incremental improvement; as we age we become reductive, rigid, and risk-averse, eliminating possibilities before exploring them. We must be "comfortable being uncomfortable" and grow our imagination (credited to Duncan Wardle). (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-14] **Challenge 3 — we're distracted:** cites Dr. Sahar Yousef — the average office worker is distracted every 90 seconds, gets only ~3.5 productive hours of an 8-hour day, and multitasking is a myth that roughly doubles task time versus single-tasking. (paraphrase, attributed to Dr. Sahar Yousef)
- [2019-06-14] **Tool 1 — Focus Sprint** (from Dr. Yousef): a 90-minute chunk of uninterrupted, distraction-free work aimed at sustainable peak performance. Steps: turn off all distractions/notifications and hide the phone; assign one realistic goal broken into subtasks; set a 90-minute timer and go; rest 20 minutes (walk, sunlight/vitamin D, close eyes, or stare at something green to lower stress); repeat through the day. He tried it and ran three sprints in one day with strong results. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-14] **Tool 2 — Better Brainstorms** (from Duncan Wardle): set a well-defined challenge; break into four-person groups with one facilitator/recorder; invite a "naive expert" from outside the department; play "hot potato" where each person adds to the prior idea and nothing is shot down (adopt "yes, if" instead of shutting ideas down); document each idea on a sheet with a title, a concise summary, and a quick sketch to make it "a complete idea." (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-14] **Tool 3 — Beautiful Boredom:** best ideas arrive while brushing teeth, showering, or driving because we're bored and undistracted, letting the subconscious thread unrelated ideas together. Steps: turn off all stimuli, be bored, let the mind wander, and document what surfaces. Unlike the focus sprint there is no objective other than being bored. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-14] Cites artist Kyle T. Webster, who — bored after lunch — noticed the texture left by a beet salad, photographed it, turned it into a Photoshop brush, and sold ~$100,000 of that digital brush; the point being that filling idle time with a phone would have cost him the idea. (paraphrase, third-party anecdote)
- [2019-06-14] Closing thought: "creativity is at a high point"; creators have everything at their disposal to build any future they dream up, and it is their obligation to make it. (paraphrase)
