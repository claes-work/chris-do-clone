## Summary

A Futur podcast-style episode explaining the product-manager (PM) role and how to break into it. Hosts
Matthew and Sina interview Justin Zaccardi (PM at Tonal), with occasional input from ex-Google producer
Mark Contreras. Justin defines product management as continuously balancing customer, business, and
technology to deliver value (the "usable / valuable / feasible" triad), uses a symphony-conductor metaphor
for the role, and covers MVP thinking, the "jobs-to-be-done" framework (Clayton Christensen / the milkshake
story), PM "superpowers," day-to-day tooling, and his advice that the best way to become a PM is to just
ship a small product. The episode is sponsored by Airtable.

Speaker: **Multi-speaker interview. Chris Do is absent.** Voices are the Futur hosts **Matthew** and
**Sina**, guest **Justin Zaccardi** (subject expert), and **Mark Contreras** (occasional). All substantive
product-management claims are Justin's. Caption has no speaker labels, so host lines are only broadly
separable.

## Key claims

_None of the following is Chris Do. Recorded for Futur/context completeness only; not persona training data._

### Justin Zaccardi (guest PM, Tonal)

- [2019-06-18] Product management is a role that "attempts to balance at all times" the customer, the
  business, and some technology, to ensure the company delivers value; the PM is essentially responsible
  for delivering value. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] The three fundamentals of product management: products must be **usable** (customer-focused),
  **valuable** (business-focused), and **feasible** (technically possible / profitable to build). (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] Metaphor: a PM is "like a conductor of a symphony" — not composing the music, but ensuring
  it is played at the right balance and mix. (quote/paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] A PM must own and articulate the **why**, then translate that why into the **what** to
  prioritize and order the work. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] Software vs. hardware: software has short, cheap cycle times and easy re-publishing; hardware
  is expensive and slow ("thousands of dollars on a template... a guy in China... build a tool"), so you
  "really need to be right" before committing. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] The modern PM's job is to form a hypothesis and design a test/experiment that builds a team's
  confidence that what they're building is valuable to the customer. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] A good PM distills many features/ideas down to the one big benefit — you don't need all the
  bells and whistles; that's the idea of the **MVP (minimum viable product)**, which exists in both hardware
  and software, at startups and big companies. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] Principles of PM are the same regardless of scale, but tactics differ between a big company
  vs. a small one, or millions of users vs. zero users. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] The PM is a "conduit for information to and from the execution team and various stakeholders
  and customers" — a running joke is that "the product managers' job is to go to meetings so that designers
  and engineers don't have to." (paraphrase/quote)
- [2019-06-18] Two layers of people a PM works with: (1) the core execution team (designers, engineers)
  who build solutions, and (2) stakeholders who want to be informed/have input but stay high-level — you
  give stakeholders bigger update chunks, not the nitty-gritty. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] Diplomacy, communication, and translation are essential PM skills throughout a PM career;
  only the people you translate to/from change. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] **Jobs-to-be-done**: people "hire" products to do a job in their life — you're not buying a
  drill, you're buying a hole. Coined by Harvard Business professor **Clayton Christensen**; illustrated by
  the milkshake study (a fast-food chain hired an innovation group; research found people bought milkshakes
  to keep themselves entertained on the morning commute, leading to the breakfast-smoothie innovation).
  (paraphrase, third-party framework)
- [2019-06-18] The "adjacent possible" — innovative product ideas often come from adjacent/related
  industries (the "Uber for X" pattern). (paraphrase, attributed to an old boss)
- [2019-06-18] PM "superpowers" spread across a good team: curiosity / understanding why; being a
  multi-disciplinarian who knows how things are made (to weigh benefit vs. cost); vision & intuition (seeing
  around corners, ~5 years ahead); and "getting stuff done" — PMs should be measured on their ability to
  ship things and deliver value. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] Day-to-day PM work lives in spreadsheets, docs, JIRA, Airtable, Google Docs, note cards —
  collecting, sorting, and prioritizing so no good idea is lost but only the most important things get
  worked on; most modern teams run agile with a roadmap and a backlog. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] There is no degree in product management; his three pieces of advice: (1) understand the
  role/responsibilities (podcasts, articles, Hacker Noon on Medium), (2) and above all **ship something** —
  it doesn't have to be an iPhone; a book, PDF, or Airtable spreadsheet counts. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] He prefers client-side/in-house over agency work because agency work is project-to-project,
  so you never get a deep sense of the customers, stakeholders, and true business needs; PM value takes time
  to show and rarely fits an agency's short timeframe. Analogy: in-house gives you more "at-bats" to learn
  what creates value. (paraphrase, personal bias/opinion)

### Futur hosts (Matthew / Sina) — framing only

- [2019-06-18] Framed for the Futur's design/brand-strategist audience: many PM traits (identifying strategy
  and moving it through to a final product) relate closely to what strategists and designers do. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-18] Cited an on-screen salary chart (tech market only): data analytics ~$137K, product management

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Justin Zaccardi (guest, not Chris Do):**

> "The definition of product management in my mind is it's a role that is attempting to balance at all
> times... the customer, the business, and some sort of technology, and to ensure that the company is
> delivering some sort of value."

> "The metaphor I like to use is like a conductor of a symphony... the product manager isn't developing the
> music, but they're ensuring that the music is played at the symphony at the right balance and in the right
> mix."

> "You don't need all the bells and whistles in order to deliver that one little thing — that's the idea of
> the MVP."

> "The product managers' job is to go to meetings so that designers and engineers don't have to."

> "When you buy a drill, what are you really buying? You're buying a hole."

> "There's nothing stopping you — go out and try and find something to make, and build it, and ship it."

**Futur host (Matthew/Sina — framing):**

> "A lot of the people that are on our channel are either brand strategists or... strict designers, but a
> lot of the traits where you're trying to identify strategy and move that through into a final product...
> really relates."
