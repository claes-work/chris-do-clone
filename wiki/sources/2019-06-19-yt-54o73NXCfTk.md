## Summary

Blind's Ben and Matthew present four "style scapes" (mood/direction boards, not
finished designs) to clients Josh and Kristen for the Hamilton Family Brewery rebrand.
The episode is a working demonstration of a client-presentation philosophy: prime the
client with the right *descriptor names* before showing work, explicitly tell them the
boards are "not designed for you" (conversation-starters, not deliverables), and
involve the client in every step so they co-own the direction rather than being sold a
finished answer. The clients converge on the "merit badge" direction with elements
pulled from others; Ben and Matthew debrief that the only feedback they fear is "you
missed the mark completely — start again," which they did not get.

Speaker: multi-speaker documentary. **Narrator** (voiceover), **Ben** (Blind, lead
presenter this episode), **Matthew** (Blind, co-presenter), **Josh** (client),
**Kristen** (client). Chris Do is absent. All quotes below are labeled by best-guess
context (`attribution: uncertain`).

## Key claims

### Blind presenters — Ben / Matthew (context, attribution: uncertain)

- [2019-06-19] Style scapes are "our interpretation of those words" from the discovery
  session — the bridge from words to images — and are explicitly **not** finished
  design: "none of this stuff is designed for you… nothing is designed yet." They set
  "the compass direction." (paraphrase, Ben)
- [2019-06-19] Using the right *descriptors/titles* "can open up the minds of a client
  to the work you're showing them"; pitchers invest a lot of energy finding the right
  words to do that job. (paraphrase — matches narrator framing)
- [2019-06-19] Naming a direction summarizes the whole look and feel into one thing;
  the exact words matter because "the words you use are kind of like the entryway into
  the idea" — the right title "frames the perception" of how the work is read and
  starts the narrative. (paraphrase, Ben)
- [2019-06-19] Part of a consultant's job is "not only to listen to what the client
  says but to watch what they do and listen to how they say things" — they observed
  Josh is a natural teacher on the brewery tour and built the "merit badge" education
  motif to reflect that. (paraphrase, Matthew)
- [2019-06-19] Deliberately limit choices — they cut Emily's near-duplicate style scape
  so as "not to give them too many choices" and to keep good contrast between the
  options presented. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-19] A young admission of a past mistake: "I thought I had to have all the
  answers… I would show the work and think I have to sell them really hard to make them
  love my idea. That was the wrong way." Success now comes from involving the client in
  every single step. (paraphrase, Ben — note: Ben's self-critique, NOT Chris's, though
  it echoes Chris's "the work does not speak for itself" theme.)
- [2019-06-19] If Blind disappeared and came back with "all the right answers, even if
  they were perfect," the client (Josh, used to making everything himself) "wouldn't go
  for that" — clients want to be part of the process and feel they are building the
  brand *with* the agency. (paraphrase, Ben)
- [2019-06-19] Style scapes exist because "sometimes clients need to see something to
  react to it… they're all just conversation starters" — give the client something to
  respond to. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-19] The only feedback they hate to get is "you guys missed the mark
  completely, we don't see anything applicable, start again"; any other feedback in
  this phase "is awesome." A client latching onto a third option with mixed elements is
  "pretty typical." (paraphrase, Ben/Matthew debrief)
- [2019-06-19] Ben pushed the clients with "difficult questions" to force a decision —
  e.g. asking which single board "feels like Hamilton Family Brewery" and which they'd
  "crumple up and throw away" — to harden a preference. (paraphrase)

### Clients — Josh / Kristen (context)

- [2019-06-19] Josh values seeing "a pure version of what I've been going after" so he
  can judge whether his personal preference actually resonates with his customers, vs.
  it being "just a me thing." (paraphrase, Josh)
- [2019-06-19] Comparing distinct named boards side by side with direct questions
  helped the couple find consensus faster than their usual back-and-forth: "this was
  extremely helpful for us to find what it is that I like… we've never done it that
  way." (paraphrase, Josh/Kristen)
