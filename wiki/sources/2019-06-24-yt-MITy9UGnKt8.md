# Book Cover Design Challenge – Young Guns Season 2 Episode 8

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do's book-cover judging rubric here (concept / "aha moment" / hidden image → conveying the main theme → shelf presence → cohesion) is a compact, reusable design-craft framework worth promoting into `wiki/topics/design-craft/` and possibly `persona/beliefs.md` (dated). ## Summary

Chris Do sets the brief for the second Young Guns S2 challenge: redesign covers for three classic works of fiction as a cohesive set. He specifies hard constraints (a supplied template, max four colors, no core photographic imagery, mandatory publisher logo + UPC) and lays out how entries will be scored — concept first, then theme communication, shelf presence, and cohesion. He walks through curated example covers (Macbeth skull/crown, multiple *1984* interpretations, Pelican/Penguin covers, *Misery*) to model conceptual thinking, and coaches designers to research prior work, read the synopsis, and reduce each book to one of a few core conflicts. The participants then react to the assignment on camera, and Chris closes with a design principle about being the communicator of a story's truth.

Speaker: **Chris Do** frames the brief, judging criteria, inspiration walkthrough, and the closing principle (first-person: "I want you to…", "here's how you'll be scored"). ## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-06-24] The brief: take three classic works of literary fiction, redesign the covers as a set of three, linked by theme, color, or style so "they feel like they came from one creator" — cohesion is key. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-24] Constraints: use the supplied template; a maximum of four colors; imagery may be illustration, graphics, vector, or stock (you don't have to make it yourself); resist using photography as the core of the cover (photographic *elements* are allowed, but the cover cannot be photographic at its core); must include the publisher's logo and the UPC code to look legitimate; consider finishes, inks, and materials to make it memorable. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-24] Scoring criterion #1 and most important is **concept** — achieving "the aha moment," an element of surprise where closer inspection reveals something different; he frames this as the easter egg or "the image within the image." (paraphrase; "aha moment," "image within the image" are quote fragments)
- [2019-06-24] Second criterion: **convey the main theme** of the story — "you need to understand the story in order to be able to communicate it." (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-24] Third criterion: **shelf presence** — "the job of design is to draw somebody and to get them curious enough to at least want to investigate and spend some of their money," i.e. bring them in. (paraphrase, with quote)
- [2019-06-24] Fourth criterion: **cohesion** — do the three covers feel like part of a set. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-24] When starting a new project it helps to look at what's already been done to inform your direction; when studying inspiration, try to understand the *conceptual framework* behind it (e.g. a double exposure, or an image hidden within another as a silhouette/outline), which then suggests ways to interpret your own book. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-24] You must read the synopsis and identify the main themes; as it turns out there are only a few core conflicts in life — "man versus nature, man versus man, man versus time, man versus himself" — then find a nuanced way that conflict relates to the specific book; you have to figure out what is being said "in between the lines." (paraphrase, with quoted list)
- [2019-06-24] On the curated examples: praises a Macbeth cover that "reads both as a skull and the crown"; shows many different *1984* covers to demonstrate that one problem (being watched by Big Brother, the eyes motif) has many valid solutions across different artists; admires retro Pelican covers as "simple, very graphic, very powerful" with a light conceptual component, and Penguin covers for "graphic simplicity" that tell an abstract narrative. (paraphrase)
- [2019-06-24] Closing design principle: "you have to know the truth… because you're gonna be the communicator [of] that truth, so if you don't understand it you're just making stuff up, and that's where most people spend their time — just making stuff up." (paraphrase, with quote)

### Participants / students (context — attribution: uncertain)

- [2019-06-24] One participant frames the assignment as very hard, "almost impossible," because classics are "packed with huge ideas," and worries about doing justice to the writers; says after the first assignment they learned to "stress a little less over the result and focus a little more in the process." (paraphrase, context)
- [2019-06-24] Another notes book covers are far more open-ended than the prior airline/identity brief — an art, illustration, or painting — offering "creative freedom" even under the color restriction. (paraphrase, context)
- [2019-06-24] Another had the assignment before "in third year of university" and plans to apply what they learned, working across three directions (minimalistic, geometric, traditional illustrated). (paraphrase, context)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All quotes below are **Chris Do** unless marked:

- "I want you to resist the urge to use photography on the cover itself; you can use elements of photography but it cannot be photographic as its core."
- "The most important thing is concept… achieving the aha moment — is there an element of surprise where upon closer inspection [you discover] something different? You can also think of this as the easter egg, or the image within the image."
- "The job of design is to draw somebody and to get them curious enough to at least want to investigate and spend some of their money."
- "There are only a few main themes in life: man versus nature, man versus man, man versus time, man versus himself… then within that, try to find a nuanced way of how it relates to the book itself. You need to figure out what is being said in between the lines."
- "When you're looking at inspiration, try to understand the conceptual framework of what they're doing — whether it's a double exposure, or an image hidden within the other image."
- "You have to know the truth… because you're gonna be the communicator [of] that truth. So if you don't understand it, you're just making stuff up — and that's where most people spend their time, just making stuff up."
