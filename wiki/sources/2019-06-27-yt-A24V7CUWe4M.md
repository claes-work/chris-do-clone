# Logo Design Process with a Client — Building A Brand, Ep 6

## Summary

Episode 6 of The Futur's documentary series **Building A Brand**, which follows the
design consultancy **blind** (Chris Do's Santa Monica agency, founded 1995) rebranding
**Hamilton Family Brewery** for clients **Josh and Kristen Hamilton**. This episode
covers the **logo design process**: moving from an approved style scape into logo
exploration, an internal design review, and two rounds of presenting logo directions to
the client.

The narrative arc: (1) the style scape is finalized and approved, putting agency and
client "on the same page"; (2) three designers present first drafts internally — **Emily**
(refined typography), **Min** (the monogram / letter H mark), and **Emmanuelle Ricci**
(hand-lettered, hand-drawn logotypes from his sketchbook); (3) the creative directors
(**Ben** and **Matthew/Matthieu**) refine roughly 10–12 concepts down to four and build
real-world **mock-ups** (beer can, building wall, bottle cap, black/white); (4) four
named directions are presented to Josh and Kristen — *Stamp of Approval*, *Good Luck
Horseshoe* (hand-lettered, Emmanuelle), *Bridging the Gap* (circular, Emily), and
*Cornerstone* (hand-lettered bowtie, Emmanuelle); (5) the clients narrow to two
hand-drawn options and choose Emmanuelle's rounded hand-drawn logo, then move to color;
(6) after a week's silence the client email is NOT the expected approval — Josh asks to
bring back his old "tree" symbol and wants **meaning** behind the H monogram, triggering
a step-back into the next episode.

Core teaching thread throughout: **a logo's job is to identify, not explain.** The team
also self-corrects on terminology (a "monogram" is technically two or more combined
letters with a decorative flourish, not just a decorative single letter).

Note on attribution: the auto-generated captions carry **no speaker labels**, and the
on-the-ground creative leads presenting to the client in this episode are **Ben and
Matthew**, with **Chris Do largely in an off-camera / voiceover-philosophy role**. Many
first-person lines below cannot be cleanly separated between Chris Do and his team, so
they are flagged `attribution: uncertain`. Only clearly Chris-Do-attributed material is

## Key claims

All dated 2019-06-27 (publication date). Paraphrased unless in the quotes section.

**Logo/identity philosophy (aligns with Chris Do / The Futur doctrine; on-camera speaker uncertain):**
- A logo's job is to **identify, not explain** — the mark should make the brand
  identifiable, not depict what the business does. (attribution: uncertain — Futur team narration)
- With identity design you **never want to explain, you always want to identify**;
  a beer logo does not need a literal hop, because "there's only so many ways you can
  draw a hop." (attribution: uncertain — Futur team; the "only so many ways to draw a hop"
  aside is spoken jointly with the clients)
- Up to the logo stage the agency's role was to **help the client refine their vision**
  and **uncover insights** to feed the creative process, not yet to make things.
  (attribution: uncertain — Futur team)

**Design process / creative direction (Ben & Matthew, context):**
- The creative brief sends designers to generate **as many logo options as possible** in
  the time allotted; the creative directors' job is to **refine those concepts** down to
  the three or four presented to the client. (attribution: Ben/Matthew, uncertain)
- Designers routinely produce **hundreds of options**; seeing that breadth and depth is
  valued by the creative director. (attribution: Ben/Matthew, uncertain)
- Workflow funnel: ~10–12 strong concepts → cut to ~4 → build **mock-ups** placing the
  logo in real-life contexts (beer bottle/can, building side at huge scale, bottle cap at
  tiny scale, black-on-white and white-on-black) so the client can see how each concept
  "will live and breathe." (attribution: Ben/Matthew)
- A "monogram" technically means **two or more letters combined with a decorative
  flourish** — the team admits they had been using the word wrong and looked it up.
  (attribution: Min / team, context)
- Clients with **long names** (Hamilton Family Brewery) are hard to work with; the goal
  is to abbreviate the full lockup down to a single identifying mark (the letter H).
  (attribution: team, context)
- Design-craft critiques given in review: hierarchy/versatility of a typographic lockup;
  an H monogram reading as a "P" and needing to disconnect; rounded letterforms not
  carrying "heritage"; reducing busy "accident lines"; keeping serif thickness consistent;
  balancing counter space between H, A, M letters. (attribution: Ben/Matthew, context)

**Client-presentation practice (Ben & Matthew presenting; prime "how to defend creative work" material, but attribution to Chris is uncertain):**
- When presenting, explicitly frame **mock-ups as mock-ups** — "the mock-ups are purely
  mock-ups... there's nothing designed for your brand" — so the client understands these
  are demonstrations of how the logo performs, **not a final implementation**, which
  "comes later." (attribution: Ben/Matthew)
- Guide client choice with **context questions** rather than abstract preference: e.g.
  "if you could picture this painted on the wall in the brewery, behind the bar, which one
  would look better there, which one will feel more at home?" (attribution: Ben/Matthew)

**Client-management / dealing with cold feet (Chris-Do-attributed doctrine, spoken by Ben/Matthew here):**
- It is **common** for a client to get "cold feet" during a rebrand and become attached
  to their old identity — the team notes they've had clients spend a quarter million
  dollars and then decide to retain their original logo. (attribution: Ben/Matthew; reflects
  Chris Do / blind institutional experience)
- The right response to unexpected/contradictory client feedback is to **hop on a call and
  unpack the motivation** ("figure out where this is coming from," "unpack his motivation
  before we proceed") rather than immediately executing the requested change.
  (attribution: Ben/Matthew)
- A late icon change is high-stakes because the logo has already been **applied across the
  whole brand system** — changing the icon "changes a lot of our approach." (attribution:
  Ben/Matthew)
- Long silence with no communication after a presentation is one of the hardest things a
  project lead endures — the longer the wait, the higher the odds of more work or starting
  over. (attribution: Ben, context)

**Client voice (Josh & Kristen Hamilton, context — not persona data):**
- Josh likes that the proposed logos don't look overtly like a beer logo (no literal hop),
  matching how their current logo works. (attribution: Josh, context)
- Kristen is torn between the calm "horseshoe/ranch" hand-drawn option and the "fun and
  exciting" one; discusses which represents who the company wants to become.
  (attribution: Kristen, context)
- Business-owner practicality (wall colors, buying new chairs, ordering merchandise) can
  "stifle the creative side," so Josh values a designer who can design "with none of the
  other strings attached." (attribution: Josh, context)
- Post-approval reversal: Josh says the team "nailed it" except the monogram looks like a
  zero; he then asks to bring back a simplified version of his old **tree** symbol, wants
  **meaning** behind the H (proposing the arch reads as a lowercase h / a door, and the
  angle as a roof), and doesn't want to leave his former identity behind. (attribution:
  Josh, context)
