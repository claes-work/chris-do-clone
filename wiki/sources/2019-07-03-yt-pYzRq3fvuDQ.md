# When Clients Change Their Mind — Building A Brand Ep. 7

## Summary

Episode 7 of The Futur's documentary-style "Building A Brand" series, following the
design consultancy **Blind** (Chris Do's Santa Monica studio, founded 1995) as it
rebrands **Hamilton Family Brewery** for real client **Josh Hamilton** (and his wife
**Kristen**). This episode's theme is **what to do when a client changes their mind
late in the process**. After the Hamiltons had chosen a circular hand-drawn logo, Josh
sent back a marked-up sketch requesting a new direction — infusing meaning (a hop, a
tree, a house) into the monogram — during the final revision round.

The bulk of the episode is handled by Blind creative directors **Matthew Encina** and
**Ben** (with designer **Men/Min** doing the exploration work). **Chris Do himself
appears only briefly**, in the cold-open framing lines about withholding judgment
until you hear the client's reasoning. Most of the client-management substance in this
episode is voiced by the team (Matthew/Ben), not by Chris, so this is primarily
**context** material with a small amount of Chris-attributed persona voice at the top.

The team works through the frustration of a "moving target," decides to honor the
client's request rather than resist it, explores dozens of marks, and lands on a single
icon combining a hop, a tree, and a house — successfully presented to Josh at the end.

## Key claims

All dated 2019-07-03 (source publication date). Paraphrased unless in the quotes
section. Attributed per speaker.

**Chris Do (framing, cold open):**
- When a client rejects your work, you feel uneasy and take it personally ("why don't
  you like me?") — so the right move is to **push pause and withhold judgment until you
  hear the client's actual reason** for the objection. (attribution: Chris Do — voiced
  in cold open; see quotes)

**Matthew Encina (creative director, Blind) — context:**
- A client drawing over your work and sending it back is the most frustrating feedback
  a designer can get because it feels like disrespect for your process — but the
  charitable read is that the client wants a voice and to be heard, and there can be a
  genuine idea buried in it worth carving out.
- The purpose of a logo is **to identify, not to explain** — echoing Ben's position.
- Hidden meaning in a mark (the FedEx arrow, the Amazon A-to-Z smile) is a nice bonus
  that tells a bit of the brand story, but it must not compromise a strong, iconic mark.
- To win a collaborative client's final approval you have to **prove you explored
  everything else** and that this is the one answer — showing sketches and walking the
  client through the process makes them feel part of it.
- Documenting where the work came from (sketchbook photos, exploration grids) is a
  little extra effort that pays off; having "a defense / a justification for everything"
  is where you want to be in a final presentation.

**Ben (creative director, Blind) — context:**
- Bullish that a logo should identify, not explain.
- Frustration at hitting a "moving target": the whole process had agreed to avoid an
  icon/illustration, and now backtracking to square one this close to the finish is
  demoralizing — "what is enough enough?"
- Reframes the late change positively: "this is how diamonds are made" — pressure in the
  11th hour forces the best work.
- Became a "champion" for keeping the legacy tree because it's a nice transition from
  the old brand to the new.

**Business/scope reality (attribution: uncertain — Matthew or Ben in voiceover):**
- Blind took this job **at a reduced rate to be able to produce the show**, so the usual
  lever — telling the client a late change is an **overage** — is off the table, which
  is why the last-minute direction change threatens both the project and the company.

**Josh Hamilton (client) — context:**
- Wants the mark to carry meaning: the story of selling his house, hops (the beer), and
  his original family tree (heritage from the current logo).
- Could be happy with either the tree or the monogram H, but wants a real reason behind
  a monogram if they go that route.

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do (persona voice — client-management / withholding judgment):**

> "Until you hear the reason you kind of feel uneasy — it's like, why don't you like
> this thing, why don't you like me? … What's the reason, did I do something wrong?
> You're hyper critical about all this judgment, which is usually why I push pause. It's
> like, I'm gonna withhold judgment on anything that we've done — cause I think it's
> great — let me wait till I hear his reason." — Chris Do (cold open)

**Matthew Encina (team — context, NOT persona voice):**

> "I know that's the most frustrating thing in the world that a designer can get,
> because it feels like they're disrespecting your process. I get that. But what I take
> from this is that I have a voice in this — I want to be heard — and I think there's an
> idea here." — Matthew Encina

> "The purpose of logos is to identify, not explain." — Matthew Encina

> "If you think of the most popular logos in the world, do they even mean anything?
> Apple — what does that have to do with computers? … It's nice if there is some hidden
> meaning in there — the FedEx logo, seeing that little arrow; Amazon, love it or hate
> it, there's that smile connecting A to Z because they sell everything." — Matthew Encina

> "You have to prove that you explored everything else and that this is the one answer …
> that's why I was on the hunt for sketches yesterday, because I wanted to … literally
> walk him through the process, right, that way he feels like he's been a part of it."
> — Matthew Encina

**Ben (team — context, NOT persona voice):**

> "This is how diamonds are made. This is when the pressure is all over you and you're
> forced to produce something good … I feel like we always produce our best work."
> — Ben

> "We've built this thing together throughout this entire process, and backtracking to
> square one … that's what's bringing me to this kind of point of frustration. So what
> is enough enough?" — Ben

**Scope/reduced-rate note (attribution: uncertain — voiceover, Matthew or Ben):**

> "One thing that we haven't told you guys before is that we took on this job at a
> reduced rate to be able to produce this show … Normally with a normal client it would
> just be, hey okay if we go over, that's gonna be an overage — but I know that that's
> not on the table."
