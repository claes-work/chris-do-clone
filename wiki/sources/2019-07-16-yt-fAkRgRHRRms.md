# How To Get A Promotion

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do lays out a reusable set of traits that make an employee valuable/promotable (do your homework → self-direction → alignment/agreement → anticipate steps → generate ideas), and he ties "becoming more valuable" directly to his results-over-process client philosophy. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/business/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated). It is a coaching session rather than a crisply-named single framework, so promote as a synthesized "traits of valuable/promotable people" page.

## Summary

A recorded internal coaching session at The Futur. Chris Do coaches his own team members — who have said they want to "move up in the company and become more valuable to the whole operation" — by extracting the common traits of the people on the team who are visibly succeeding (Matthew, who made a 2-million-view video; Greg). The traits he draws out: do your homework so your guesses are educated (research/data over feelings/anecdotes); be self-directed and take a directive across the finish line without hand-holding; but keep self-direction in alignment/agreement with company goals; anticipate the next steps (the more steps you can anticipate, the more valuable you are); and generate the ideas that become big directives (bottom-up, not top-down management). He synthesizes all of it as one trait — self-direction = taking initiative, diagnosing the problem, reverse-engineering the solution, and proposing it to get alignment — which he calls "a trait of leadership." Midway he draws an explicit parallel to client work: understand the client's goals, set clear benchmarks, then leave them alone, because the only thing anyone cares about is results, not how you got them.

Speaker: multi-speaker coaching conversation. **Chris Do** is the coach/boss and gives all the teaching and framework content (first person as the company leader — "the way that we want to manage this company," "if I say I want you to increase the traffic"). His **team members** (Ricky is named as the one who raised the alignment point; others recount an argument with Matthew and describe their own work) supply the questions and examples. Third parties **Matthew** and **Greg** are discussed but not confirmed as speaking. `attribution: uncertain` on the reflective "basically you're saying…" active-listening lines — consistent with Chris Do's coaching style but a co-host/facilitator cannot be ruled out from the caption text alone. Only Chris-attributed teaching trains the persona.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (coach)

- [2019-07-16] The people who do well "do their homework — they study before they form their opinion, from research and data versus feelings and anecdotes." Matthew's viral video wasn't luck; it was a series of educated guesses built on knowledge from producing many videos and talking to their YouTube advisor. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-16] Too many people make assumptions about how things work and just go "stabbing things in the dark" — sometimes you hit, sometimes you get nothing. The more information you have, the more educated (less random) your guess becomes. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-16] Self-direction is a common thread among the people who do well: they don't need much conversation about what their next move should be — they just go at a steady pace, and course-correct if told otherwise. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-16] Self-direction alone is not enough — it must be **in alignment with the company's goals**. Example: bringing in planters and growing "a whole tropical forest in the back room" is self-directed but not aligned. Once a goal is clear, the valuable person needs no hand-holding to take it across the finish line, but still has a follow-up conversation to confirm alignment before running. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-16] **Anticipating steps is what makes you valuable**: "the more steps that you can anticipate, the greater your value becomes to whoever you're working with or for." If you have to be told every micro-step (open the garbage can, release your hand, drop it in), you've figured out zero steps; if you walk in, see the place needs cleaning, and handle it — then ask *why* it keeps getting messy and fix the root cause — you've demonstrated initiative. (paraphrase; garbage-can/root-cause analogy)
- [2019-07-16] **Client-work parallel:** to minimize client revisions you don't just run off with the assignment — you understand their goals, how they measure success, set clear benchmarks, then leave them alone. "At the end of the day the only thing anybody ever cares about are the results. People are less concerned about how you did it; they're more concerned that you got the result they wanted." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-07-16] Generating ideas is itself a required trait: The Futur is run **bottom-up, not top-down** — "the best ideas actually come from you guys." You need to come up with the ideas that become the big directives; if you can't, you're stuck waiting for someone to hand you something to jump on. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-16] Traits should be tested against real people: pick one person in a leadership/high-responsibility position, isolate a single trait, and test it — extract what the people who've done well actually did. (paraphrase, method)
- [2019-07-16] Synthesis — the traits are "really not three things, they might just be one." Self-directed means: taking initiative, thinking about what the problem is, reverse-engineering the solution, and then proposing those ideas to get alignment and clarity. "It's really a trait of leadership." (paraphrase + quote)

### Team members

- [2019-07-16] Stated motivation for the session: "we want to move up in the company and just get better and become more valuable to the whole operation." (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-16] A team member recounts an earlier argument with Matthew, questioning whether he knew what it takes to make a good video — i.e., Matthew was speaking from authority without yet having evidence of it; now that he has a 2-million-view video, they'd listen more carefully. (paraphrase; illustrates "remain skeptical until I see evidence")
- [2019-07-16] Ricky's point (surfaced by Chris): once a goal is clear, valuable people don't need a lot of instruction — they can take it across the finish line. (paraphrase)
