## Summary

Episode 13 of Young Guns Season 2 — a Futur coaching call in which the (non-Chris)
host works through young designers' challenges around **communication, presenting, and
critique**. Students bring questions on public speaking, asking better questions,
handling feedback, imposter feelings before an interview, and generating concepts
faster. The host's core advice: **slow down and get comfortable with silence**, drop
filler words, give yourself permission to mess up, illuminate your thought process
instead of forcing a "smart" answer, ask questions with clear intent (the "ask why
three times" drill), reframe inadequacies as things-to-learn, and remove the critical/
cynical voice by treating others as you'd want to be treated. Two ideas are explicitly
credited to Chris secondhand — the **"creativity is a rubber band"** metaphor and Chris
being the model of calm, silent, objective delivery.

**Speaker:** single coach (context; **not Chris Do** — see attribution warning) plus
students **Annie, Maitrayee ("Me Trey"), Amy, Tosun, Megan** (all context). Chris Do,
Ben Burns (named as ex-cop, self-taught COO), Ricky, Jonah, Aaron, and Tony Robbins are
referenced in the third person; none speak.

### Host (Futur team member — CONTEXT, not Chris)

- [2019-07-30] Communication is the single most important skill for creative
  professionals; it lets you present ideas, articulate them, and ask good questions.
  (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-30] The biggest fix for rambling/mumbling: **slow down and be okay with the
  silence** — taking your time "tends to sound more confident than anything." (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-30] Cut **filler words** ("cool," "awesome," "you know"). His team used a
  physical "filler jar" in the conference room — a dollar per filler word — as an
  awareness device. (paraphrase; personal anecdote)
- [2019-07-30] Give yourself **permission to mess up** — being allowed to say "I used
  the wrong word, what I meant was..." removes the pressure to be the smartest person in
  the room. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-30] When you don't know what to contribute, **illuminate your thought
  process** and ask questions rather than force a conclusion: state your gut feeling,
  why you feel it, what specifically triggers it, offer several options, then get
  agreement. He plays "the curious dumb guy" and **crowdsources** the answer from the
  smart people (including the client) by asking questions. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-30] When receiving vague feedback ("I don't like this design"), respond with
  "what is it you don't like about it?" — never act on unclear input. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-30] When giving feedback, first ask **"what is your goal / intent, and what
  kind of feedback do you want?"** to make sure your input is actually useful — "box it
  in." (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-30] To ask a better question, **ask yourself "why" three times** (why am I
  asking, what do I need to know, what happens if I don't) — a question needs a clear
  intent, not just words. (paraphrase; drill he ran live with a student)
- [2019-07-30] Frame client questions with **"I"** and then **"we"** rather than "you"
  so they feel collaborative and non-accusatory ("putting your arm around" the client).
  (paraphrase; presenting technique)
- [2019-07-30] It's fine to **punt** a response — "I need a moment to process my
  thoughts," or defer to a follow-up email hours/days later. "The person that feels
  awkward with silence is the awkward" one. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-30] Reframe **inadequacy as opportunity**: "look at all these things I can
  learn." Uses the mountain/summit metaphor — reach one summit, the fog clears, another
  mountain appears — to describe continuous learning across crafts. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-30] The most senior meeting (with the CEO) is "the most expensive meeting" —
  being invited to it proves you are enough. (paraphrase; encouraging a student before
  an interview)
- [2019-07-30] Remove the **critical/cynical voice** by applying the golden rule — don't
  judge others by "the cover of the book"; awareness lets you catch and correct the
  judgment. (paraphrase; describes himself as formerly cynical)
- [2019-07-30] Look at every conversation through a **lens**: "this person is
  interesting, I probably have something to learn, and I want to be helpful" — this
  removes the pressure of being the smartest/judging person. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-30] For generating concepts: creativity is **connecting two disparate dots**;
  a good concept is "like telling a joke" where you set up and the **audience delivers
  the punchline** — if you have to deliver it yourself, it's a weak concept. (paraphrase)
- [2019-07-30] **Reverse-engineer** work you admire ("that's amazing → how did they do
  that → research the techniques") and break it down like a recipe to close the skill
  gap. (paraphrase; his personal method)
