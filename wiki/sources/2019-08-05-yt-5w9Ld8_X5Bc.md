# Is Instagram Necessary For Work? – Young Guns 2, Ep 12

## Summary

A "Young Guns" S2 coaching session: Chris Do coaches two young designers on social
media and outreach. **Megan** (Instagram handle "mindful pursuits") no longer enjoys
Instagram and asks whether a design business can survive without it. Chris says yes —
it's optional, it's a long game, and she should treat her feed as "public journaling"
rather than performance, engage genuinely without an ulterior motive, and
reverse-engineer successful accounts into abstract frameworks. **Anastasia** asks how
to move from "I like your work" to "I want to work for you" when DMing a studio. Chris
walks her through building the relationship slowly (Socratic questioning, being
specific, researching the hiring decision-maker on LinkedIn) and introduces "lumpy
mail" / thoughtful physical gifts as a way to cut through the digital clutter.

Speaker: multi-speaker coaching episode. **Chris Do** (coach — the persona subject).
**Matthew Encina** (co-host, intro + outro narration — context, not persona). **Megan**
and **Anastasia** (students being coached — context, not persona). Only Chris-attributed
material trains the persona. Jim Rohn, Allan Dib, and "Gemma O'Brien" (caption-garbled
as "Jim O'Brien") are cited third parties, not present.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-08-05] It is 100% okay for a creative entrepreneur to NOT have Instagram — you
  can leave it off a business card. It's not critical. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] Social media is a long game; it's a fantasy to think a couple of
  beautiful posts will get you hired immediately — that's the exception, not the rule.
  (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] Advises against forcing any activity that creates stress and stops you
  being a happy, productive person; much of the anxiety is "the lens in which you look
  at this," not what's really there. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] Social-media envy is distorted: people are "looking at people living
  these wonderful lives," but a big portion is highly curated and not a reflection of
  reality. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] The benefit of posting: you gain people who like what you do (often
  other designers, not clients initially — that's okay); they buy into you and your
  story, invite you onto podcasts/panels, and want to collaborate. "People can't hire
  you if they [don't] know who you are — you don't want to be the world's best kept
  secret." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-08-05] Engage with others on Instagram in a real, genuine way, without an
  ulterior motive. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] Treat your feed as "a form of public journaling" — like a private diary
  you don't stress about, posting how you feel, a win, a lesson, something that sparks
  joy — and don't stress if people don't respond yet. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-08-05] Early in a career it's too soon to declare "this is what I'm all about";
  it's fine to delete posts you don't like and to take breaks (even a long one) —
  there's no pressure. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] The ideal is to merge your two worlds (personal life + work) into one
  organic feed; cites "Gemma O'Brien" [caption: "Jim O'Brien"], a lettering artist with
  a large following (~217k), who integrates herself into her posts. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] A personal habit: he studies very successful people — even ones whose
  work or personality he doesn't like — and "reverse engineers" an abstract framework
  from what's working (e.g. product on white background → try it yourself). (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] Cites Jim Rohn: "success leaves clues" — instead of feeling far behind
  someone, just pick up the clues. (paraphrase, quotes Rohn)
- [2019-08-05] Everybody starts somewhere; we only notice people once they're already
  popular, because no one follows them when they're not yet good — expertise is built
  over time. (paraphrase)

**On DM outreach / getting hired (with Anastasia):**

- [2019-08-05] Don't rush the relationship — like being rushed to order food or make a
  decision, pressure repels; first learn to build genuine relationships. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] When DMing, be specific: the recipient may get 20–100 near-identical
  messages a day, so a generic "hi, I like your work" makes you sound like everyone
  else. Pinpoint WHY, show you paid attention. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] The messages Chris himself dislikes are bare "how are you?" openers (dead
  end); what earns a reply is something personal or a specific reason the sender
  connects to his work — that gives him an opening to talk. (paraphrase, self-report)
- [2019-08-05] Your worry that an expert will vet whether you're "an expert" is
  unfounded — the probability a busy famous person looks you up is low; Chris often
  just replies and moves on unless something's interesting. (paraphrase, self-report)
- [2019-08-05] Use the Socratic process: ask easy-to-answer questions that follow a
  logical path toward a conclusion you want (e.g. seven or eight conversations later,
  toward work). (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] Research the hiring decision-maker: look up the company on LinkedIn to
  see roles/titles (HR, creative director, art director, recruiter); or DM whoever runs
  the social account asking "how do you find new designers to work with." (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] Reframe: think about the world from the other person's point of view —
  use imagination and empathy about what they're thinking. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] "Lumpy mail" (idea credited to Allan Dib, *The 1-Page Marketing Plan*):
  old-fashioned, non-flat physical mail now cuts through the clutter because people
  abandoned mail for cheaper social/DM channels. (paraphrase, cites Dib)
- [2019-08-05] Don't give a gift YOU like (that's what a self-promotional piece is);
  give something thoughtful and personal to THEM — the more you consider the other
  person over yourself, the more they feel you took the time to know them. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] Example: send small-batch vacuum-packed coffee beans you love from your
  country, or print a design/stamp on a dollar bill ("the next cup of coffee is on
  me"), include your card and a handwritten note — "this is not a bribe, I'm looking
  for work, your studio inspires me." (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] It doesn't matter if the idea is yours or not — "what matters is you made
  effort… it's effort that's what people care about." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2019-08-05] Example of effort winning: a current intern was hired because his
  application video was funny and showed he knew the brand — "as soon as I saw it, I
  told Matthew, hire him." (paraphrase, self-report)

### Matthew Encina (co-host — context, not persona)

- [2019-08-05] Intro framing: creative professionals miss opportunities by not DMing /
  reaching out and building real relationships with peers and potential hirers.
  (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-05] Outro takeaways: (1) if something doesn't feel right, don't post it, but
  don't underestimate being a content creator — content around what you do/learn
  "enrolls people into your journey"; (2) "DM is the new email" — approach it as a good,
  genuine, curious human, ask clear direct questions, and have empathy for what the
  other person will value. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do:**

- "It's a fantasy to think that you make a couple of beautiful posts and somebody will
  hire you right off the bat."
- "People can't hire you if they [don't] know who you are, so you don't want to be the
  world's best kept secret in terms of a design resource."
- "I want you to look at your Instagram feed and a lot of your social media stuff as a
  form of public journaling. You don't care what you write in your book, right? It's for
  you."
- "I like to see people who are very very successful — it doesn't matter if I like their
  work or if I even like their personality — I'll reverse engineer what I think is
  working for them and pull out an abstract framework."
- "Success leaves clues — just pick up the clues." *(attributing Jim Rohn)*
- "There's a good chance that they're getting 20, 30, maybe a hundred messages a day
  that sound just like yours. So if you come in with something super generic — 'hi, how
  are you, I like your work' — you're gonna sound just like everybody else."
- "What you don't want to do is give them a gift that you like… and that's what you
  would be essentially doing by sending a self-promotional piece."
- "It doesn't matter if it's your idea or not. What matters is you made effort to take
  the steps or say the words. That's what matters — it's effort, that's what people care
  about."

**Matthew Encina (co-host, context):**

- "DM is the new email — like it or not, this is how a lot of conversations and business
  development is actually going on online these days."
