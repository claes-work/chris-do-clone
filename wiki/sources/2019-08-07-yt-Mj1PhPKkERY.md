# How To Build Expertise While Learning

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a named, self-contained expertise formula ("life experience + knowledge + skills = expertise") plus Chris Do's "teach/share while you're still learning" thesis and his reframe of the 10,000-hours rule. This is a core Chris theme (learn-by-modeling, share-before-you're-ready) and worth promoting into `wiki/topics/mindset/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), not just held at L2.

## Summary

Chris Do argues you should start sharing what you know **before** you feel like an
expert. He offers a working definition — expertise = life experience + knowledge +
skills — notes that a strict reading (plus the 10,000-hours rule) would disqualify
almost everyone, and reframes the popular meaning of "expert" as simply "someone who
knows a little more than you" or "someone who can help another person reach their
best." When you're not the domain expert, the move is to talk to two or three domain
experts, learn the questions they would ask a client, and become good at running
top-level conversations rather than drilling into every detail. He pushes hard on a
learning discipline — read a book or two, watch a video, attend a workshop before
spouting unvalidated ideas — and names imposter syndrome as the thing robbing people
of the ability to help others. His closing charge: don't edit down what you share;
be honest and transparent ("I'm still figuring it out, but here's what I've got so
far"), and the audience will grow alongside you.

Speaker: single-speaker monologue by **Chris Do** (first person — "I'm double your
age," references "we have this discussion... at the office," "you will pay me and I
will read it for you"). No co-host or guest speaks. Marques Brownlee ("Marcus
Brownlee" — caption garble), Logan Paul, and Casey Neistat ("Casey nice that" —
caption garble) are named as third-party examples, not present.

## Key claims

All claims are **Chris Do** (single speaker).

- [2019-08-07] Working definition: **expertise = life experience + knowledge + skills**. He notes he's always quoting "the same exact books" — "no more than five books" — that redefined this for him. (paraphrase; formula stated near-verbatim)
- [2019-08-07] Taken strictly, that definition (plus the "10,000 hours" rule) would make very few people experts and discourage most from ever sharing what they've learned — so we should question whether that bar is really true. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-07] When you are **not** the domain expert (which he assumes is most of the time), the move is: call the domain experts, find out what questions they would ask this client, and by the time you've talked to two or three of them you become an expert "really quick." What you need to become expert at is running **top-level conversations**; you don't need to drill into the bits. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-07] The popular meaning of "expert" is just "somebody who knows a little bit more than you" — and how long they've known it isn't even important. Think of coaches: an expert is "somebody who is able to help somebody else achieve their best, their optimum." (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-07] You still have to start somewhere: read books, go to seminars, watch videos, take courses — doing so can "shave off 14 years of learning down to six." (paraphrase; near-verbatim)
- [2019-08-07] Complaint from the office: people "walk around with these dangerous ideas that are never validated," not backed by evidence or even their own critical thinking, and "spout off things out of their face" on subjects they know nothing about. The fix: read a book or two on the subject, watch a video, go to a workshop — "does it even matter if they're good," just make sure what you're saying "lines up with someone who spent some time thinking about it." (paraphrase; fragments verbatim)
- [2019-08-07] Reading five books on one subject is a very attainable minimum, and from there "you start to develop your own expertise." (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-07] You can be famous and know nothing — but that's not the goal; you need to actually learn, get the information out there, and be able to help people, which requires greater skills, more knowledge, and more experiences. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-07] **Imposter syndrome / not wanting to be a "fraud" or "charlatan" is the thing robbing you** of the ability to help others and do better for yourself. (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-07] Advice to young(-ish) or young-in-mindset creators: don't be afraid to produce a video; just be honest and transparent, "because that's what people want from you." (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-07] Marques Brownlee, Logan Paul, and Casey Neistat all started young; their audience/community "grows up with them." On YouTube, Chris says he is "the anomaly" — the standard is young people sharing their story and life experience. There is value in the audience "learning along with you and traveling along your side." (paraphrase; names garbled in captions)
- [2019-08-07] Don't posture as the finished expert ("I figured this stuff out, this is what you need to do"); instead say "I'm still figuring it out... but here's what I figured out so far — if this helps you, keep watching, come along on the ride and we'll grow together." (paraphrase; near-verbatim)
- [2019-08-07] Why he's fired up: many people "aren't willing to do the work" or read the book, so they wait for someone else (like him) to read it and pay for the summary. If you do read the books in the topics you want to be known for, **don't edit down** what you give to the community/world/clients/staff — "they will get more than what you think you're giving." (paraphrase)
- [2019-08-07] Closing line: not editing what you share is "how you'll become an expert." (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All quotes are **Chris Do**:

- "Life experience plus knowledge plus skills equal expertise — so that would make very few of us experts."
- "By the time you talk to two or three domain experts, you become an expert really quick... what we need to become is an expert of doing top-level conversations."
- "An expert is just somebody who is able to help somebody else achieve their best, their optimum."
- "You shave off 14 years of learning down to six, and that's pretty awesome."
- "Read some books — does it even matter if they're good — just so that you make sure what you're saying lines up with someone who spent some time thinking about it."
- "People don't want to be a fraud... they have a heavy dose of imposter syndrome and they don't want to be some charlatan — but what you're doing is you're robbing yourself the ability to help other people."
- "Don't try to stand there and say 'I'm the expert, I figured this stuff out.' Just say 'I'm still figuring it out... but here's what I figured out so far. If this helps you, keep watching, come along on the ride and we'll grow together.'"
- "Do not edit what you give to the community, to the world, to your clients, to your staff — they will get more than what you think you're giving."
- "That's how you'll become an expert."
