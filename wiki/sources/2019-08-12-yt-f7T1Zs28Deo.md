# What You Believe Becomes Reality — AIGA 2019 Full Talk

A ~30-minute solo keynote by Chris Do at an AIGA conference (audience of ~1,400
designers), presented as a compressed version of his 2-hour mindset mini-workshop. The
talk is almost entirely first-person and is prime persona/beliefs/voice material: it
covers belief systems, interpretation, reframing language, resilience, pricing
psychology, and the inner critic. A brief MC introduction opens the video (context, not
Chris); a short closing reflection (lines 825-838) appears to be Chris speaking about
the talk afterward.

## Summary

Chris Do opens by asking why some people succeed while others fail, rejecting the "born
with it" explanation because, as a teacher, he must believe success can be learned. He
structures the talk around six points about mindset and belief systems, explicitly
declining to show his portfolio or design process.

He tells the story of "Carrie," a coaching client stuck at an $8,000 ceiling for
websites, who broke through simply by changing what she believed was possible — billing
more in six months than in her entire previous year, with nothing changed in her
offering except her belief. From there he builds his core model: a five-spoke belief
cycle (thoughts → words → actions → behavior → identity) that grooves ever deeper like a
record needle, and the monkey-ladder experiment as a parable for inherited, unexamined
beliefs ("that's just your BS" — belief system).

He then introduces interpretation as the critical hidden step between input and output:
the same input yields infinite outputs depending on how you look at it (the glass of
water; the comma-shifted sentence about a woman and her man). He covers becoming a
better storyteller about yourself (seeing yourself as those who love you see you, or as
a loyal dog sees you), "War Jitsu" — reframing limiting words to strip them of power
(impossible → I'm possible, fail → first attempt in learning, fear → fantasized
experience appearing real, safe → stay average forever), and reacting to bad events by
saying "congratulations" and hunting for the learning. He grounds each with personal
stories: one son's low self-confidence, the Latin-test story with the confident
son, and the phishing-hat ("fish you") story where he "designs his life" so he wins
either way. The talk's emotional climax is the inner-critic exercise, drawn from his own
therapy and the book *Conquer Your Critical Inner Voice*: a three-column journaling
method to separate the internalized critical voice (often a parent's) from your true,
compassionate voice. He closes by telling employees to cross out their business-card
title and write "partner" — acting "as if."

## Key claims

All dated 2019-08-12; paraphrased unless quoted.

- Success is learnable, not innate — Chris says that as a teacher he *has* to believe
  people can learn to succeed rather than being "born with it." [self-reported worldview]
- Belief drives results more than tactics: a coaching client ("Carrie") broke through an
  $8,000 pricing ceiling to bill more in six months than her prior full year, with
  nothing changed but her belief in what was possible. [self-reported, coaching anecdote]
- A comparable practitioner ("Benny") charged $20,000–$50,000 for the same work in the
  same market, proving the ceiling was a belief, not a market limit. [self-reported]
- The belief cycle has five spokes: what you think → what you say → what you do → your
  behavior → your identity, repeating and deepening like a record groove; the longer you
  play a track, the harder it is to move the needle.
- Beliefs are often inherited and unexamined (monkey-ladder experiment): people enforce
  rules whose origin no one remembers. If you dislike your results, re-examine your
  belief system — "that's just your BS."
- Between input and output sits interpretation; the same input has an infinite number of
  possible outputs depending on how you look at it. "If you want to be angry you will
  find a way to be angry... I just happen to choose to be happy."
- There is subjective/individual reality versus objective/physical reality, and they
  sometimes rub against each other; your life depends on how you look at the world.
- Humans are uncomfortable with not knowing, so we assign meaning and make up stories
  (cave-dweller myth-making); we can use this storytelling instinct to our advantage.
- Ask whether the story you tell about yourself matches what someone who loves you
  (best friend, sibling, parent, mentor) would say; learn to see yourself as they
  already see you (smart, generous, talented, inspirational).
- "War Jitsu" — reframe words that hold power over you: impossible → I'm possible;
  fail → first attempt in learning; no → next opportunity; fear → fantasized experience
  appearing real; pain → precursor to notable things; and invert positives, e.g.
  safe → "stay average forever" / "always follow everyone."
- Think more binary about inputs (does this empower or disempower me?) to protect mental
  health, rather than living in the "murky middle."
- When bad things happen, say "congratulations" and find one reason it could be exciting
  or a learning opportunity.
- "Failure is the tuition I pay for future success." Chris says a $30,000 loss was "the
  beginning to a graduate degree." [self-reported, biography-relevant]
- Retroactive storytelling (from his storyboarding teaching): a story's true meaning
  isn't revealed until the end (*The Sixth Sense* example), so you can reinterpret your
  own past. Nudge negative self-talk toward neutral or positive (add "yet"; reframe
  pricing objections as advantages).
- The ultimate enemy is within — the critical inner voice. Most people develop
  self-defense "armor" in childhood (from rejection, neglect, or hostility) that helps
  them survive but becomes a heavy burden in adulthood; discovering death young
  (age ~3–7) teaches us not to fully invest in life. Source attributed to psychiatrist
  Dr. Firestone and the book *Conquer Your Critical Inner Voice*.
- Inner-critic exercise: three columns — (1) write the critical voice as "I" statements
  for ~15 minutes without judgment; (2) rewrite as "you" statements and read aloud to
  feel it; (3) rewrite in your true, compassionate voice. This creates separation and
  reveals the voice is often a parent's, not your own.
- Closing career advice: dress/act for the job you want — employees should cross out
  their business-card title, write "partner," and "act as if" until leadership sees them
  that way; partners are accountable, seek challenges, and never wait.

### Biography-relevant / autobiographical [self-reported]

- Chris has (at least) two sons; one is described as very confident, the other
  struggled with self-confidence — Chris says he sees himself in that son and
  invented reframing games ("War Jitsu") to build the boy's resilience.
  _(Chris names this son in the talk; name withheld here per SUBJECT.md family-privacy
  policy — treat family members' names as not-public.)_
- The confident son scored 15/115 on his first Latin test; Chris reframed it positively
  ("you got 15 right — how many more Latin words does he know than you?") rather than
  punishing, and the son went on to ace subsequent tests (now in his 2nd/3rd year of
  Latin). Chris is married (references his wife; no name stated).
- Chris grew up under a strict "tough Asian dad" upbringing with a harsh grading scale;
  he says he forged report cards after getting a C. He references hearing his father's
  voice in his own head as the disciplinarian impulse.
- Chris was in therapy; the inner-critic material comes partly from his own therapeutic
  work ("who's that stranger in my house?").
- His stated big-hairy-audacious goal: to teach and impact 1 billion lives.
- He ran the Santa Monica motion-design/branding agency Blind for ~two decades (per MC
  intro) and founded The Futur, an online content and education platform. (MC intro —
  context/attribution: MC, not Chris.)
- Self-described as Asian and punctual ("I'm Asian, we do things on time"); not
  religious ("I'm not even religious, I'm wearing a hat"); a self-described slow reader
  who reframes it as absorbing more, which makes him a good teacher.
- The "fish you" phishing story: Chris nearly sent (or sent) $100 for custom snapback
  hats to a scammer whose email handle spelled "fish you," reframing the loss as
  worthwhile social-media content — "legitimate I win, illegitimate I still win... I like
  to design my life."
