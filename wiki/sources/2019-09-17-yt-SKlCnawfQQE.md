# Are you Becoming Obsolete? Innovate Don't Iterate – Chris Do

★ **L3-candidate** — This is a self-contained keynote framework: how to stay relevant by innovating (creating new value) rather than iterating (repeating past success), plus a concrete "empathy exercise" (task / gap / opportunity) for finding market white space. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/mindset/` and `wiki/topics/business/` and into `persona/beliefs.md` (dated). Expands the "innovation vs iteration" idea first seen in the 2018 Melinda talk (`2ju9b4SgvoQ`) with named case studies, the empathy-exercise mechanics, and the "12 one-week projects" tactic.

## Summary

A short edited keynote in which Chris Do argues that looking to past success to guide future decisions is a reliable path to obsolescence — "innovate, don't iterate." He grounds it in his own story of overcoming his fear of appearing on YouTube (pushed by former classmate José, and his coach's dictum "run towards your fear"), then his warning to fellow educators at Art Center that digital technologies would disrupt them. He names four fallen leaders (Western Union, Blockbuster, Tower Records, Kodak) who each let past success define them by *what they do* rather than *who they are / their purpose*, and offers a practical method for finding "white space": an empathy exercise (task → gap → opportunity from the audience's point of view) plus mental-bandwidth practices (stop firefighting, unplug, get bored) and a bias-to-action tactic — swap one 12-week project for twelve one-week projects. Closes on his signature image: the windshield is bigger than the rear-view mirror.

Speaker: single-speaker keynote monologue by **Chris Do** (first person — "I used to teach at Art Center," "my business coach"), with a brief closing voiceover reflection (also Chris). No guest or co-host speaks. José (former classmate who invited him onto YouTube) and an unnamed business coach are referenced as third-party anecdotes, not present.

## Key claims

All claims are **Chris Do** (single speaker).

- [2019-09-17] "Failure and invention are inseparable twins" — to make something new you must be prepared to fail. (paraphrase / near-quote)
- [2019-09-17] Looking to the past to inform future decisions is "a pretty sure-fire way of failure"; what got you here will not get you there. (paraphrase)
- [2019-09-17] We have a general fear of the unknown because it carries a real chance of failure; when José invited him onto YouTube, all he could think of were the ways it would fail — those were excuses to avoid doing something he knew nothing about. (paraphrase, self-critical)
- [2019-09-17] His business coach's rule: there are only two kinds of people — those who run away from fear and those who run towards it — "run towards your fear." He chose not to be the one who runs away. (paraphrase, attributes rule to coach)
- [2019-09-17] The Futur / his YouTube presence began "right around 2014"; over time he got comfortable on camera and saw the impact on viewers. (paraphrase, biographical)
- [2019-09-17] He warned fellow career educators in an Art Center boardroom that digital technologies could deliver the institution's knowledge more efficiently and cheaply, and asked whether they wanted to preserve that knowledge or let it die; colleagues dismissed him as an alarmist — "we've been around forever, we will be around forever, this is how people want to learn." (paraphrase, biographical)
- [2019-09-17] Four one-time category leaders let past success determine their direction and missed the transformation that was theirs to make: **Western Union** should have invented the internet; **Blockbuster** should have become Netflix (a movie/TV studio, not just a rental place); **Tower Records** should have become Spotify (digital music streaming); **Kodak** should have been an image-capturing company, not a filmmaking company. (paraphrase, case studies)
- [2019-09-17] The first step toward innovation is to study the failures of companies that didn't recognize massive change — they got locked into repeating past success and failed to see what they truly did for their audience. (paraphrase)
- [2019-09-17] Redefine yourself by your mission/purpose (who you are), not by what you do. (paraphrase — the load-bearing distinction of the talk)
- [2019-09-17] "Find the white space and fill those gaps." A gap is "a problem that everybody has ignored." (paraphrase / quote fragment)
- [2019-09-17] The Futur's original white space on YouTube: the platform was full of charismatic amateurs ("youtubers") who taught without real-world experience, while celebrated, award-winning designers had no interest in teaching — the gap was experienced professionals who would teach. (paraphrase, biographical)
- [2019-09-17] The **empathy exercise** (taught to him by someone unnamed) uses three prompts — **task, gap, opportunity** — done from the audience's point of view: build a three-column grid, write out a day in that person's life (what they think/feel, say/do, see/hear, hope/fear), find the gap that eases their pain, then write the solution as an *opportunity* (not as "work"). (paraphrase, named framework)
- [2019-09-17] To have the mindset for innovation you must step back from running the business. Red flag: if most of your working days are spent putting out fires, you'll never have the mental bandwidth to recognize these patterns — fix those problems first. (paraphrase)
- [2019-09-17] Dedicate certain days when you're totally unreachable and unplugged; "you need to have a space to be bored" so your archiving brain can connect what your problem-solving brain has figured out, and great ideas can be born (e.g., a 3–4 day road trip; you return refreshed with clearer eyes). (paraphrase — caption garbles "archiving brain" / "doctor brain," meaning inferred: default-mode vs. active problem-solving)
- [2019-09-17] "Long-term business plans are a fantasy — pick a direction, adjust as you go," because you can't predict market conditions, consumer behavior, the economy, technological disruption, or the competition. (quote / near-quote)
- [2019-09-17] Bias to action: instead of one 12-week project, run twelve one-week projects and keep adjusting; "a person who makes a thousand wrong decisions is better off than a person who makes no decision at all." (paraphrase / quote)
- [2019-09-17] Closing image: your windshield is much bigger than your rear-view mirror because "where you are going is much [more important] than where you've been." (quote — caption drops a word) 
- [2019-09-17] (Closing voiceover) The biggest takeaway is to recognize risk as an opportunity; many people keep the door closed when opportunity knocks because it feels like danger — "there's opportunity everywhere." (paraphrase)
