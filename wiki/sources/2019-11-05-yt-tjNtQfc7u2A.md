# Book As Passive Income Business

## Summary

A Futur livestream in which **Chris Do interviews the Hoodzpah sisters — Amy Hood and Jen Hood** — about how their self-published freelance book grew into a passive-income business. The bulk of the substance is the **guests describing their own business model**: a self-published book (physical + e-book) that became 25% of their income (~75% profit), plus workshops, fonts, and other digital products. Chris drives the conversation with questions and contributes his own principles on self-publishing to a built-in audience, the traditional "book → talks → workshops" author model, writing by talking out loud, and negotiation/sales reading.

> ⚠️ Despite the ingest brief's hypothesis, this video is **not** about Chris Do's own book *Pocket Full of Do*. That book is never mentioned. No biographical detail about Chris's own publishing appears here.

Speaker: **multi-speaker interview.** Host = **Chris Do** (first person, asks the questions, adds commentary). Guests = **Amy Hood and Jen Hood** ("the Hoodzpah sisters," twin designers running the studio Hoodzpah). The two sisters are frequently indistinguishable in the auto-caption, so guest claims are attributed collectively as **Hoodzpah (Amy/Jen Hood)** with `attribution: uncertain` between the two. ## Key claims

### Chris Do (host — persona-eligible)

- [2019-11-05] If you already have a **built-in audience** ready to buy, self-publishing is the obvious choice — "you don't need anyone to help you sell it, people want it... it's easy, you can figure it out." (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] Writing a book that answers the questions your audience repeatedly asks positions you as the person who can talk about that subject — the book becomes both product and platform. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] The workshop path is "a more traditional author model — you create a book so that you can do the talks and the workshops, and that's how you sustain yourself." (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] Fonts don't get impulse-bought; a typeface needs ongoing marketing so people remember it when the right project triggers the need — "at some point you're gonna hit a trigger and they're gonna say 'I need that.'" (paraphrase; developed jointly with the guest)
- [2019-11-05] A good way to write if you're not a comfortable writer: talk out loud, record yourself, transcribe, then clean it up with an editor — "you write to discover what you know," "you gain clarity through articulation." (paraphrase; Chris and guests build this together)
- [2019-11-05] Recommends the negotiation/sales reading *Never Split the Difference* (Chris Voss) and *Socratic Selling*, noting Voss's technique of **labeling emotion** — you reduce the intensity of an emotion by naming it out loud. (paraphrase, book recommendation)
- [2019-11-05] Friction and negotiation are unavoidable in client work; many designers dislike it because it feels like a personal reflection on their work — "get used to the friction, that's life, that's how it works." (paraphrase)

### Hoodzpah — Amy / Jen Hood (guests — CONTEXT, not persona training data; `attribution: uncertain` between the twins)

- [2019-11-05] Their self-published freelance book unexpectedly became **~25% of their income, of which ~75% is profit**; had they listened earlier to the constant emails asking how they run their business, they'd have discovered the product sooner. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] Physical book retails at **$24.95**, the e-book at **$10** (digital is nearly all profit — no overhead but sweat equity, delivered via "Easy Digital Downloads"). (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] They **refuse Amazon** ("they take such a big chunk") and sell direct; for overseas they build **wholesale/distribution "line sheet" decks** (treated like a proposal deck, packed with stats and five-star reviews) to get the book into bookstores with 10+ locations so buyers avoid ~$25 international shipping. This channel had **not worked yet** at time of recording ("full transparency, it's happening"). (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] Surprisingly, **physical outsells digital** — even overseas buyers want the tangible copy ("nostalgia... your hands went through it"). (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] Workshops model: partner with orgs like **AIGA or AAF (Ad Federation)**, who front travel, lodging, and venue costs and want content for their dues-paying members; you become presenting sponsor, offer members a discount (e.g. ~$165 vs ~$180), and it's near-pure profit with no liability. They ran 4-hour workshops; most people can do 1.5–2 hours. Stop capping seats. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] **Font licensing / passive income anecdote:** Amy designed a font ("Avenue"-adjacent) for **Dustin Lee / RetroSupply Co**, who *owns* it and has made **over $100,000** on it while she only gets a referral fee — so she now makes fonts she owns herself (new font "Beverly Drive," $25). She's working out tiered licensing (extra for logos, more for bigger companies; web use and app licensing are where the real money is). (paraphrase, third-party biographical anecdote about Dustin Lee)
- [2019-11-05] **Pricing-tier jump:** to move up you must take fewer, bigger jobs and accept **more "no"s**; test one **calculated risk per month** by doubling/adding ~30% to a number — the safest time to test a higher price is **when you're too busy to take the work anyway**, and being too busy is itself a signal your price is too low. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] **Client management:** sometimes you have to "be the bad guy" — put your foot down, remind the client they hired you for a reason ("are you gonna trust me or not"); framed as a parent serving broccoli, not cake. But use it sparingly — ~99% good cop, pick your battles. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] A personality/style mismatch with a client is a normal part of business, not a failure; they once did three free extra revision rounds, proved every option matched the agreed discovery and mood board, then agreed to part ways. Toughening skin shortens the emotional-recovery time (days → hours). (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-05] Present **black-and-white comps first** so color doesn't distract the client from whether the mark works and communicates. (paraphrase)
