# Your Work [ALONE] Won't Get You More Work – Chris Do & Priscilla

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris articulates a clean statement of a core belief here ("the mistake creatives make is thinking better work gets more work; you also have to market, tell your story, and build a personal brand") anchored to two memorable illustrations (the concert-violinist-in-the-subway story from *The 1-Page Marketing Plan*, and the Aaron Draplin / Tibor Kalman "story and work are inseparable" examples). Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/branding/` (personal brand / positioning) and `persona/beliefs.md` when synthesized.

## Summary

A one-on-one coaching episode: Chris Do coaches **Priscilla**, a French-born, New-York-trained, Utah-based in-house/agency designer and Futur Pro Group member who wants to go out on her own. Her presenting problem is that she has no real website (a 17-year-old portfolio), works "behind the scenes" as a subcontractor for a PR/marketing woman who owns the client relationships, and doubts that her personality and story matter — she believes the work should speak for itself. Chris's counter-argument is the video's thesis: for the ~95% of creatives who aren't in the sought-after top 5%, better work alone will not generate work; you have to move upstream toward strategic thinking, differentiate through positioning and a personal brand, control the framing of who you are, and put your own face and story in front of clients rather than relying on an employer or an intermediary. He gives her a concrete starter plan (three strong case studies → relaunch a bare-bones site with an about page → upload and correctly tag/title work on Behance).

Speaker: two-person coaching dialogue — **Chris Do** (coach; persona subject) and **Priscilla** (coachee/Pro Group member; context only). A narrator/voiceover also opens, timestamps, and recaps the episode (Futur team, not Chris). Only Chris-attributed material trains the persona.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-11-22] Design, branding, and marketing are separate disciplines you could each study for 8–10 years; a designer running a business doesn't need to master all of them, but does need enough marketing (probably content marketing) to showcase work attractively to prospects. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-22] Only a "very rare top 5%" of creatives are sought after purely for creativity and craft and "don't care about anything else"; everyone else is in a sea of undifferentiated designers and must compete on more than the work. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-22] Positioning logic: too many competitors means you're undifferentiated and will compete on price; too few means you've niched down so narrowly there's not enough opportunity to sustain a practice. Offering "logo or design services" puts you against tens of thousands worldwide, so you must move upstream toward the strategic thinker where there's less competition. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-22] "Whoever commands the relationship with the client makes the most amount of money." The PR intermediary can't afford Priscilla's rate because she must mark up on top, and she carries the risk (she pays Priscilla even if the client doesn't pay, or if work goes wrong) — so Priscilla should be on the front line doing the hard client conversations herself. (paraphrase) — this is the "own the client relationship" principle applied.
- [2019-11-22] A website is usually not the first point of contact (that's social media or referral) but is a "very good second and third point of contact" — so a bad/outdated site is dangerous. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-22] Lean into who you are — your identity is a facet of your personal brand. "I'm a bald asian guy" doesn't describe his whole universe but it describes something; running away from yourself feels disingenuous and inauthentic. Priscilla's "sophisticated European + New York street-smarts + proper Salt Lake City" mix is a brand asset she should embrace and communicate. (paraphrase / self-referential)
- [2019-11-22] "French" and "New York" function as code words — people fill in assumptions (European sophistication, art/culture history, Paris; NYC competitiveness and survival instinct) — so choose the words in your simple story carefully. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-22] Deeper, more personal customer relationships come from enrolling clients in your backstory, beliefs, values, mission, and vision. Structure: beliefs and values sit at the bottom of the pyramid (they never change over time), the mission sits on top and is informed by them. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-22] "It takes work to get work" — and we *want* it to be difficult, because a threshold of pain and professionalism keeps the riffraff out; if it were easy everyone would have work. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-22] "The thinking that created the problem you have right now is not going to be the same thinking that's going to solve it" — the person who designed your current situation isn't the best person to solve it, which is why we work with coaches and consultants. (paraphrase; echoes an Einstein-style framing in Chris's own words)
- [2019-11-22] Core thesis, citing Allan Dib's *The 1-Page Marketing Plan*: the mistake creatives make is thinking that doing better work will get them more work. Good work is worth it and necessary — but believing that work alone (while ignoring marketing, storytelling, narrative, and communicating beliefs/values) will win is the mistake. (paraphrase — Chris explicitly clarifies he is NOT saying good work isn't worth it)
- [2019-11-22] The concert-violinist illustration (from Dib's book / the Joshua Bell "social experiment"): a world-class violinist who earns ~$1,000/minute (~$28,000 for the set) in an opera house made about $18.75 in tips playing the same ~$3M violin for the same time in a subway. Same musician, same instrument, different framing/venue → totally different results. The expectation (suit, expensive ticket, plush seats, peers, status) tells the audience what it's worth; subway commuters are in a "get from A to B" mindset. Lesson: "you're allowing other people to control the framing of who Priscilla is, and you have to do it on your own." (paraphrase, third-party anecdote) — caption garble: transcript says "$18 and 75 cents"; publicly this figure is usually cited as ~$32.
- [2019-11-22] Even the beloved top-5% creatives have very strong personal branding. Tibor Kalman became famous for his thinking AND his ability to give newspapers great sound bites and being opinionated/absolutist about design. Aaron Draplin: "his story and his work are inseparable" — the outsider Midwesterner, moved to Portland, underdog, big bearded man who doesn't wear pants, kisses his dog; his marks are "good... okay" in the pantheon of great logos, but the story carries them. (paraphrase; note "Draplin" appears garbled as "droplet/Aaron droplet" in captions) — caption garble: "Tibor Coleman"/"my signally" = Tibor Kalman.
- [2019-11-22] Minimum viable action beats waiting for perfection: at minimum just show your work — even one fabulous case study can generate work; three is better. A great case study covers the problem, challenges, customer needs, insights gained, and the process to the end product, and doubles as content for Behance, Dribbble, LinkedIn, Instagram, and Facebook "in that order." (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-22] On Behance you must title case studies correctly (what are people actually searching for — e.g. "identity system, healthcare, brand" — not "Priscilla graphic design") and tag them with as much relevant data as possible: "if you don't know how to play the search game you're first-place loser." (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-22] Concrete plan given to Priscilla: (1) pick your three best case studies; (2) revisit sketches/mockups and make them beautiful and powerful (cites "farm design" for beautiful presentation photography); (3) relaunch a bare-bones site with the three case studies + an about page that surfaces what makes you unique; (4) upload and properly title/tag them on Behance. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-22] Encouragement/observation: he pushes back on her self-dismissal ("I don't have the personality of a go-getter"), noting he'd watched her actively visiting Pro members on social media — something he says he wouldn't/doesn't do himself — and tells her she's "selling herself short." (paraphrase)

### Priscilla (context — does NOT train persona)

- [2019-11-22] Bio: originally from France, in the US 30+ years; finished high school in New York, studied design in France for 4 years, then the Portfolio Center in Atlanta (which taught her the client/real-project skills her French training lacked); worked at a big NY firm, moved to Utah for her husband's job, went in-house, then to a small family-owned agency serving the financial industry that was hit by 2008 and failed to adapt. Now works part-time (cut from 4 to 3 days/week after having her son) and subcontracts for a PR/marketing woman. (context)
- [2019-11-22] Presenting challenge: she has the design skill but feels she needs marketing and sales; she's "the best kept secret" with no website (a 17-year-old portfolio); she doubts her face/story matter and believes "it's going to be my skills and my offerings" that get work, not her personality. (context — this is the belief Chris argues against)
