# Find Work-Life Balance – The Struggle

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a self-contained reframe worth promoting: **"a lifestyle business is a freedom business"** plus Chris Do's compartmentalization principle ("walled-off sections" of work-time vs. family-time). Also carries fresh biographical detail about Chris's own family/work trade-offs. Worth lifting into `persona/beliefs.md` and `persona/biography.md` (dated) rather than leaving only at L2.

## Summary

A coaching-session episode from The Futur's Priscilla series. Chris Do coaches Priscilla — a seasoned in-house designer going out on her own, a working mom, and a Futur Pro Group member — on work-life balance and on her resistance to change. The core teaching: you get "messed up" when you think about work during family time and family during work time, so wall off dedicated blocks (family days vs. work days). He reframes the "lifestyle business" not as a euphemism for working less but as a **freedom business**, argues that money buys freedom (using friends who mismanaged their careers as a cautionary tale), and shares his own trade-off — front-loaded intensity and sacrifice (systems, protocols, giving things up) so he can now coast, travel, and choose his time. He presses Priscilla to let go of the past through a string of metaphors (train station, Karate Kid submission, swimming across a pool toward a towel/meal/$20,000), because "what got you here won't get you there." A Futur team narrator opens and closes with timestamps and a recap.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-11-25] We get "messed up" when we think about family during work and worry about unfinished work during family time — then we're good to neither. The fix is **walled-off sections**: designated "mommy working time" vs. family/"off the grid" time. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-25] Chris dedicates **full days as family days** and separate days as work days; when he works he needs to actually work, because otherwise he can't do his reading/writing time. (paraphrase, personal practice)
- [2019-11-25] When it's family time, clients can't reach him — "you cannot pay me enough money to interrupt this." (paraphrase → see quote)
- [2019-11-25] A **"lifestyle business" is code for "I don't want to work that much, I don't need to make that much"** — but properly understood it is a **freedom business**: doing what you want when you want without worrying about money. He challenges Priscilla's self-imposed parameters as the difference between a validating lifestyle business and being "a glorified freelancer." (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-25] **Money buys freedom.** He cites friends who mismanaged their careers, opportunities, and money (assuming income would always come), and are now mid-life with little money — a source of personal and professional conflict, and no ability to help family in a tragedy. (paraphrase, third-party anecdote)
- [2019-11-25] On himself: he appears to "work all the time" / be "on vacation all the time" because he has **aligned his life to do what he wants** — works from home, works whenever, makes good money doing what he loves, with great flexibility and freedom. (paraphrase, self-report)
- [2019-11-25] The mechanism was **front-loaded sacrifice**: intensity, focus, setting up systems and protocols, and giving things up "when I could," paid upfront, so he can now coast into his "golden years" and travel/camp/fish with his boys whenever. (paraphrase, self-report)
- [2019-11-25] The way to buy that freedom is to **increase demand and decrease the supply of you** — elevate your profile, make it seem special and uniquely you, so you get maximum return on your time. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-25] Beyond a certain level **the work is good enough**; it's the law of diminishing returns — greater effort does not equal greater result (often the opposite). Demand is created by getting the word out about who you are, not by improving the work further. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-25] If a nanny/au pair costs more than you'd earn in that time, you're better off being the nanny to your own kids. He shares that he had **au pairs from France for ~4 years** while working "like crazy" and missing family time — kids being "educated by a third party." The worst outcome is being absent *and* not making it financially worth it. (paraphrase, personal/biographical)
- [2019-11-25] **"What got you here won't get you there."** Priscilla has waited (framed as ~17 years) at "the train station" for a spark of inspiration; she must let old ideas die, submit, and accept that past decisions won't carry her forward. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-25] People fall in love with their history and past decisions because they feel safe ("the known knowns"); letting go and jumping into the unknown triggers fear (drowning, running out of energy). But the reward is reachable if you let go — otherwise "the food will go cold, the towel will get wet, and the money will be gone." (paraphrase, via pool metaphor)
- [2019-11-25] **Change can happen in the blink of an eye — within 24 hours — but the speed at which you change is entirely up to you.** (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-25] Success/validation (opportunities, working with more clients) builds confidence; a lack of opportunities can erode self-confidence and self-worth. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-25] Building a social following (as he has done "transparently") is something Priscilla should try in a version that fits her lifestyle; going all-in follows logically from the fact that Futur courses already improved her business. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-25] Family/work balance is a **team effort** — Priscilla may need a meeting with her husband about what matters, what to compromise on, and how to do it together. (paraphrase)

### Futur team narrator (NOT Chris — non-training)

- [2019-11-25] Recap: time is your most valuable, non-renewable asset; overwhelm from too many choices can prevent action. Prioritize the one thing with the most impact, go deep on it, then keep it or iterate/move on. (paraphrase; editorial, `attribution: uncertain` re: Chris)

### Priscilla (context person — NOT training data)

- [2019-11-25] She wants a lifestyle business for the next 5–10 years while her kids are in school; a minimum of ~$80k/year; no agency, no employees; and built-in flexibility (she tested taking a month off and working remotely in another time zone, which worked). Framed as context on the coachee, not the subject. (paraphrase)
