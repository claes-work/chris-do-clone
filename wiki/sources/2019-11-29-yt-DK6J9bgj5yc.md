# How To Get More From School

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a self-contained, named framework (Chris Do's "80/20 of school" / "Column A vs. Column B" instructor-prioritization method) that extends his existing design-education-is-broken and value-over-credentials material. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/mindset/` (or a content-strategy/education page) and into `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), not just held at L2.

## Summary

In a live student Q&A/coaching session, Chris Do argues that a rigorous design program demands more than any student can humanly deliver to every class equally, so the winning move is to deliberately concentrate effort. He applies the Pareto (80/20) principle to school: roughly 20% of your instructors will teach you almost everything of value, and it is your job to identify that 20% and give the other 80% little-to-no effort. He walks a student through a "Column A / Column B" binary sorting exercise for classes, using two filters — (1) is it aligned/practical to the career you want, and (2) is it portfolio-worthy AND an example of the work you actually want to do — and reframes chasing an A in every class as a path to being "average." Matthew delivers the outro recap and a viewer challenge.

Speaker: primarily **Chris Do** teaching/coaching a room of design students (first person — "if I were a senior I would tell incoming students"). At least one **student ("George")** and other unnamed students respond. **Matthew** (Futur team) delivers the closing recap and call-to-action. Attribution below is per speaker; only Chris-attributed material trains the persona.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2019-11-29] The Pareto / 80/20 principle applies to school: you can learn "everything from 20% of your instructors," and it's up to you to decide which 20% — "don't even bother with the other 80, they're gonna waste your time." (quote/paraphrase)
- [2019-11-29] In a rigorous design program each instructor demands so much that "you cannot humanly do all the work to an equal level," so concentrating effort is a necessity, not laziness. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-29] The mistake is to put effort into a class you won't get value from and give less effort to the one really teaching you — you must decide which instructor is giving you what you need. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-29] Practical method: think in a binary way, Column A (classes to pay attention to) vs. Column B (classes to coast on); list your teachers and sort them. If the split looks even, shift effort into ~2 classes — you can do really well in maybe 2 out of 6 and just "do enough to get by" in the rest. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-29] Trying to get an A in every class is the common mistake: chasing the Dean's List across the board leaves you "average" — "that's what that A stands for, average." You want to excel in two and possibly get a C, or even flunk, the rest — "it doesn't matter." (quote/paraphrase)
- [2019-11-29] Two filters decide Column A: (1) how closely aligned/practical the class is to what you actually want to do, and (2) whether it's portfolio-worthy. But portfolio-worthy alone isn't enough — it must also be "an example of the work I want" to do; a class can be portfolio-worthy in something you hate (e.g. packaging) and still not qualify. It has to meet all the criteria. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-29] There's "no one-size-fits-all" for what's portfolio-worthy — "you all get to decide," which he frames as "the beautiful thing about life." (quote)
- [2019-11-29] Contrasts portfolio/career value against "intellectual exercise" classes ("let's explore shape — well, shape's not gonna give me a job"). (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-29] On learning advice generally: "listen to what you're told, but make your own judgement" — take what you need and disregard the rest; even choosing to ignore all of a given source is a valid exercise of that judgment. (paraphrase)
- [2019-11-29] As a senior he'd say this to incoming students "that clearly" — i.e. he endorses telling students bluntly to ration their effort. (paraphrase)

### Student ("George") / unnamed students

- [2019-11-29] Students prioritize classes based on a specific field/area of design they're more interested in, and on "what we think we can get from those projects" — the experience that helps once they're in industry. (paraphrase — student view, not persona training)
- [2019-11-29] Portfolio needs vary: sometimes illustration-focused, sometimes case-study/process-focused ("you're either going to visual or you're getting really shown the thought process"). (paraphrase — student view)

### Matthew (recap)

- [2019-11-29] Recap: focus on the classes developing your portfolio and your voice; put little or zero effort into classes with no impact after graduation, because "the moment that you leave the classroom that class doesn't care about you anymore." Shape and bend your education/curriculum toward what you want to do after you graduate. (paraphrase — Futur team, not Chris)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do:**

- "You can learn everything from 20% of your instructors. It's up to you to decide which 20. Don't even bother with the other 80 — they're gonna waste your time, and I'll say it that clearly."
- "The mistake that we all make is we try to get an A in every single class... and then you wind up just being average. That's what that A stands for — average."
- "You just want to excel in two and possibly get a C in every other one — possibly even flunk. It doesn't matter."
- "You can do really well in two classes probably out of six, and the other ones just do enough to get by."
- "There's no one-size-fits-all, which is the beautiful thing about life."
- "You can work on an editorial class or a packaging class, but you hate packaging. It is portfolio-worthy, but it's not what I want to do... it has to meet all the criteria, otherwise you don't put in category A."
- "Listen to what you're told, and then use your judgment to keep what you want."

**Matthew (Futur team):**

- "The moment that you leave the classroom, that class doesn't care about you anymore, and now you're out there in the real world fending for yourself."
