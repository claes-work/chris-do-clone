# When Client Says "Your Price Is Too High" — How To Respond Role Play

## Summary

A live sales role-play from The Futur (2019). **Chris Do** plays the video-production
service provider; his co-host **Mo** plays a small-business client ("Mel"/"Mo") who
wants a "banger" promo video but only has a $1,000 budget. Chris demonstrates how to
handle a price objection without lowering price or getting defensive.

The episode runs in three movements:

1. **Round 1 — value/ROI + walk-away.** Chris refuses to argue price. Instead he uses
   discovery questions to turn the vague ask ("build awareness") into a concrete
   revenue number, then reframes: if the video is only worth $1,000 to the client, it
   probably isn't important enough to do at all. He is willing to walk away — even
   recommending the client spend the money elsewhere (or on Fiverr for "five bucks").
2. **Meta-commentary.** Chris and Mo step out of character to explain, for beginners,
   why hunger for a small job leads to underpricing, endless revisions, and a weak
   negotiating position — and why you need "runway" to be able to say no.
3. **Round 2 — risk reversal + anchor.** Given the constraint "you can't say no / can't
   refer to Fiverr / must book the job," Chris shows an alternative: offer to do the
   video **for free in exchange for a percentage of the results** (e.g., $50 per new
   customer / 50% of new net revenue, no cap). The offer is deliberately structured so
   the client, doing the math, prefers to just **pay Chris his minimum ($4,000)** and
   keep all upside. Client agrees; Chris closes with a proposal and $2,000 upfront.

The episode closes with Mo's coaching note: **everything is a negotiation** — you don't
have to accept the client's price *or* their deliverable; change the scope to match a
fair price.

> Role-play note: throughout, Chris voices the **service provider** and Mo voices the
> **client**. Only Chris-as-service-provider and Chris-as-teacher reflect Chris's own
> selling philosophy. Client lines (whether spoken by Mo or, in commentary, by Chris)
> are demo objections, not anyone's literal belief.

## Key claims

All dated 2019-12-26. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Attributed by speaker/role.

- **Don't solve for the budget; qualify the problem first.** (Chris, service-provider
  role) When a client leads with a low budget, Chris's opening move is to ask whether the
  video is important and *what problem it solves*, not to quote a price.
- **Convert a vague goal into a hard revenue number via discovery.** (Chris) Chris walks
  the client from "build awareness" → "2–3 more people/day walk in" → "~$100 avg sale" →
  "~$250/day extra" → "×30 days = ~$7,500/month of new revenue." The number is
  co-created with the client so the client owns it.
- **Reframe price against the value it produces.** (Chris) Spending $1,000 to generate
  ~$7,500/month is proportionate; Chris uses the ROI framing to show the price is *low*
  relative to the outcome — the opposite of the client's "too high" premise.
- **If it's only a "$1,000 problem," it may not be a real problem.** (Chris) People spend
  money on what they value (BMW M5 outside, refrigeration, AC, polished floors); a truly
  important business problem warrants real investment. A small budget signals the client
  doesn't actually value the outcome.
- **Be willing to walk away — that's the source of your power.** (Chris) "When somebody
  comes to me and they say I don't have a lot of money to do something, my first instinct
  is: let's not do it, it's not important." Chris actively discourages the client from
  spending the $1,000 with *anyone*, and jokingly refers them to Fiverr.
- **Beware "video envy" / keeping up with the Joneses.** (Chris) Clients often want a
  video only because competitors have one; that's a poor use of money and worth naming.
- **Doing it right requires a long discovery conversation — which is disproportionate to
  a $1,000 problem.** (Chris) The effort of properly scoping goals/targeting doesn't fit
  a $1,000 engagement, another reason to decline.
- **Runway lets you say no.** (Mo, teaching) You didn't have that $1,000 before the call;
  you were going to survive regardless, so don't let a single call pressure you into a bad
  deal. Chris adds: don't adopt the mindset "because they called, I have to close."
- **Risk reversal: work for free for a share of the results.** (Chris, service-provider
  role, Round 2) "I'll do the video for you for free if we get the results you want — I'd
  like a percentage of the results." Proposed at ~$50 per new customer, or 50% of all new
  net revenue, *no cap*. Rationale cited: "All profit comes from risk" (attributed to
  Peter Drucker) — Chris takes the risk, so Chris earns the upside.
- **Use the uncapped share to anchor toward the flat fee.** (Chris) When the client tries
  to add a non-exceed / cap clause, Chris resists and presents two clean options: (a) pay
  $4,000 flat, done, keep all future revenue; or (b) free build + 50% of new net revenue,
  uncapped. Doing the math, the client chooses the $4,000 flat fee — which was Chris's
  goal. ("$4,000, that's the minimum.")
- **$4,000 is the stated minimum engagement; his stated per-video floor elsewhere is
  "minimum 1,000 miles for video" (garbled — likely "$1,000/video minimum").** (Chris)
  Example given: a client wanting six videos at that floor = "six grand."
- **Close and structure payment.** (Chris) On agreement he submits a proposal and takes
  **$2,000 upfront** to begin.
- **Everything is a negotiation — negotiate scope, not just price.** (Mo, teaching) If the
  client won't meet your price, you don't have to meet their deliverable: e.g., offer a
  30-second video for $1,000 instead of the one-minute video they wanted. Don't be afraid
  to change the scope of work to reach something you consider fair.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do unless labeled. Client lines are role-play demo data.

**Chris — as service provider (Round 1):**

> "I'd love to do that for you. What's your budget?"

> "Is this video important to you?"

> "Okay, so how much awareness do you want it to build?"

> "So you're looking at about 200 to 300 dollars worth of increased revenue. ... So let's
> say 250, split the difference."

> "Does spending a thousand dollars seem appropriate for seventy-five hundred dollars of
> revenue per month?"

> "I'm not saying that at all. I'm just trying to measure effort versus result."

> "When somebody comes to me and they say I don't have a lot of money to do something, my
> first instinct is: hey, let's not do it. It's not important. Would that be your instinct
> too?"

> "Where else can you spend your thousand dollars to make a bigger impact on your
> business? Because I don't wanna take your money to do something that's not important."

> "So I find that in my life and my experience that people tend to spend money on what
> they value."

> "I know what happens a lot of times — businesses like yours, the other businesses
> produce video and then we have video envy, and then we [make] a great ad to say, yeah,
> we're keeping up with the Joneses. I don't know that's a good use of your money."

> "It would seem disproportionate — the length of that conversation for your time and my
> time — to talk about a thousand-dollar problem. What would you like to do?"

> "Then you shouldn't, but I'm not the person to do that for you. And I would encourage
> you not to spend that money — with anybody, not just me."

> "Yeah, I hear [there's] a site called Fiverr, they could do it for five bucks."
> *(said tongue-in-cheek)*

**Chris — teaching / meta-commentary:**

> "You didn't have that thousand dollars before they called you. You were gonna make life
> work regardless. Don't fall into that mindset like, now just because [of] the call I
> have to close the job — that puts you in a horrible, disadvantageous position."

> "If you had set the parameters as 'Chris, you can't say no to the client, you can't
> refer them to Fiverr, or you must book the job' — I've got another way to do it."

**Chris — as service provider (Round 2, risk reversal / anchor):**

> "I understand. A thousand dollars is a lot for you. I hear your concern. I'd like to
> propose something else."

> "I'll do the video for you for free — if we get the results that you want. I'd like a
> percentage of the results. Can we do that? No risk to you at all."

> "How about, since you say each customer is going to spend a hundred dollars with you —
> let's go half, 50 bucks. No risk to you. I take all the risk. And I rely on your
> honesty."

> "You know Peter Drucker? ... All profit comes from risk. ... I'm taking on the risk. Or
> you just pay me what I'm worth, and you keep all the profit."

> "What are you worth? — Four thousand. That's the minimum."

> "The two options that we have on the table right now [are]: you pay me four thousand
> dollars, we're done, you keep all the money from that point forward. ... The other one
> is I do it for free, but I take 50 percent of all new net revenue, because I'm taking on
> no limits."

> "I can see that you're doing the calculations in your eyes right now ... you probably
> think [it] is a better and more prudent decision just to pay me the 4000. It's your
> choice."

> "So I'm going to submit a proposal to you. You [send] me two thousand dollars upfront
> and we'll get goin'."

**Mo — as client (role-play demo lines):**

> "I was hoping that you could cut up one of those banger videos that you make that I see
> on your website. ... I have a thousand dollars." *(Mo, client role)*

> "We're trying to build awareness. No one's walking into the store." *(Mo, client role)*

> "Are you going to guarantee me seventy-five hundred dollars a month?" *(Mo, client role)*

> "I'm concerned that you're going to make a banger video ... and then we're gonna get a
> lot of clients in the door, and then I'm gonna be out 50 percent of that revenue."
> *(Mo, client role)*

**Mo — teaching:**

> "Everything is a negotiation. Just because he proposed to you those 1000 dollars ... it
> doesn't mean you have to do everything. ... They're not meeting you at your price — you
> don't have to meet them at their deliverable."

> "You got to have a runway to be able to tell people no. But also being able to ask these
> educated questions." *(Mo, teaching)*
