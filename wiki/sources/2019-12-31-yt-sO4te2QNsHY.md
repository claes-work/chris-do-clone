## Summary

A short crash-course clip from The Futur in which host **Chris Do** asks brand strategist **Marty Neumeier** to "set the record straight" on what branding actually is. Neumeier answers by first clearing away common misconceptions (a brand is not a logo, not a product, not merely a promise, not the sum of advertising impressions) and then giving his own definition: a brand is a *result* — a customer's gut feeling about a product, service, or company. He stresses that because each customer forms their own impression, a company is really creating millions of brands (one per person), and that nearly everyone in a business affects the brand. Chris Do contributes the framing question and a "brand is like a reputation" analogy, and closes by noting the answer was unscripted and drawn from Neumeier's decades of experience.

## Key claims

All claims dated 2019-12-31. Paraphrased unless quoted below. Speaker attributed per claim.

- **(Marty Neumeier)** People wrongly use "branding" interchangeably with logo design, identity design, or typography; the record needs setting straight. *(setup echoed by Chris Do in the framing question)*
- **(Marty Neumeier)** A brand is **not a logo** — a logo is a useful tool for a business and a *symbol* for the brand, but it is not the brand itself.
- **(Marty Neumeier)** A brand is **not a product** — when people say they're "buying a brand," they're really buying one product or another; the brand is not that.
- **(Marty Neumeier)** A brand is **not (merely) a promise** — the popular "a promise the company makes to customers" line has some truth (a brand does end up *acting* as a promise) but that is not what it *is*.
- **(Marty Neumeier)** A brand is **not the sum of advertising impressions** — that framing is useful mainly to someone trying to sell a lot of impressions, not to understanding the business.
- **(Marty Neumeier)** A brand is a **result**: a customer's gut feeling about a product, service, or company — it lives in their heads and hearts, built from whatever raw materials the company gives them.
- **(Marty Neumeier)** Because each customer makes their own version, creating a brand means creating **millions of brands** — one per person in the audience.
- **(Chris Do)** Reframes the definition as: a brand is like a **reputation** — your business reputation, which will differ slightly for everyone, and that's fine as long as it's "corralled" mostly where you want it and beneficial to the company. *(Neumeier assents.)*
- **(Marty Neumeier)** Designers tend to view branding from the producer's side (telling a story, making a claim/pitch), but that is the *input*, not the brand; if you don't start from the brand-as-result, you don't actually know what you're doing.
- **(Marty Neumeier)** From the client's side, branding is often treated as a checklist (logo ✓, tagline ✓, ad campaign ✓ = done), which misses what actually matters: the reputation created in people's heads.
- **(Marty Neumeier)** Branding encompasses almost all of business — products, messaging, look and feel, culture, and employee behavior all affect the brand; even finance is involved because it green-lights these things. Nearly everyone in a company is doing something *with*, *for*, or *against* the brand.
- **(Chris Do)** Endorses Neumeier's articulation as unscripted, decades-deep, and very clear.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "A logo is a very useful tool for a business but it's not the brand. It's a symbol for the brand." — Marty Neumeier

> "A brand is a result. It's a customer's gut feeling about a product, a service, or a company. It ends up in their heads, in their hearts." — Marty Neumeier

> "When you create a brand you're not creating one brand, you're creating millions of brands — however many customers or people in your audience, each one has a different brand of you." — Marty Neumeier

> "Almost everybody in a company is affecting the brand — doing something with the brand, doing it for the brand, or hurting the brand. You've got to think of it that way." — Marty Neumeier

> "A brand is like a reputation, right? So it's your business reputation, and everyone's gonna be a little bit different about what that reputation is — and that's okay, as long as you've got it corralled mostly where you want it and it's beneficial to the company." — Chris Do (persona voice)

> "This is unscripted — Marty's just talking from decades of experience, and writing and articulating this. It's very clear to me." — Chris Do (persona voice)
