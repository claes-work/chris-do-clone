# The PERFECT Sales Call Script

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a self-contained, verbatim cold-call **script** (delivered by guest Marty Neumeier) that Chris Do explicitly endorses as "the most natural, true, genuine way to do a sales call I've ever heard" and tells viewers to "rewind and study." The Chris-attributed commentary distills a reusable principle set — *give value free, let the fish jump in the boat, don't push, anchor price high* — worth promoting into `wiki/topics/sales-clients/` and (dated, Chris-attributed only) `persona/beliefs.md`. ## Summary

Chris Do asks branding author **Marty Neumeier** to re-enact the cold-call sales pitch he used early in his career to land software-company clients. Marty recounts phoning a software executive, admitting up front "you don't know me," positioning himself as the specialist in software packaging, and offering to come present a free slideshow — "22 Ways to Sell More Software" — with no pressure and "no rush." The prospect books it on the spot ("Tuesday, 15 people"), the 45-minute talk runs three hours because the room wants to know everything, and Marty ends up owning the category so completely that competitors can't answer the detailed questions he's taught clients to ask. Chris Do breaks down *why* it works: it's a high-value piece of content given away free, the client does the pursuing (no selling or pushing needed), and pushing is a "tell" that signals desperation. Marty adds his pricing move — quote a price higher than you even want, to signal the work is genuinely valuable, and negotiate down later if needed.

## The cold-call script (Marty Neumeier — context / third-party)

Reconstructed from captions (some phrasing caption-garbled; sequence is faithful):

1. **Disarm + name yourself** — "Hey, um, you don't know me, I'm Marty Neumeier."
2. **Position as the category specialist** — "I do software packaging, and you're in the software business, and I'm the guy who helps shape how a software package works and [sells]."
3. **Peer framing, not pitch** — "I think we should know each other."
4. **Offer free high-value content, zero pressure** — "I have this slideshow called '22 Ways to Sell More Software'... no rush, whenever you're ready, whenever you think you're interested... I'll bring it over and share it with everybody for free, and you'll know all my secrets."
5. **Client self-qualifies / books it** — no feedback, just: "Tuesday. 15 people." (Marty expected 15, ~30 showed.)
6. **Over-deliver in the room** — the 45-minute slideshow ran ~3 hours because the audience wanted to know everything, with "rapt attention."
7. **Own the category** — by teaching clients the right questions ("what happens on the side of the box? the top of the box?"), competitors who answer "because we're experienced" get eliminated. Owning the category with no real competition lets price/profit margins rise.

### Marty Neumeier (context / third-party — self-reported)

- [2020-01-03] His cold call opened by disarming the prospect ("you don't know me") and offering a free, no-pressure slideshow ("22 Ways to Sell More Software"); prospects booked it immediately without negotiating. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-03] Once you own a category and there's nobody who can compete with you, it affects profit margins — your price goes up, because you're demonstrably valuable. The software clients' upside ran "from hardly anything to billions of dollars," so his fee was a small investment for them. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-03] "It's not magic" — building this is trial and error: figure out who you are and what you love, get it out there, and know everything there is about your niche. (paraphrase → quote)
- [2020-01-03] Price anchoring: he deliberately quoted a price *higher* than he even wanted to get, to make sure the client understood the work was "super valuable"; price can be argued down later. When a client said "you're way out of our budget," he'd ask "how much way out are we?" and negotiate — but clients usually concluded "we want the best... so we have no choice." (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-03] "We're not for everybody — that's really expensive." (used to pre-qualify / signal exclusivity) (quote)
