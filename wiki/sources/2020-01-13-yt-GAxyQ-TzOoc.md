# Most Important Sales Skills To Learn

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do teaches a self-contained mental model of the discovery
conversation built on two constraints ("it's difficult to think, it's difficult to make
a decision") plus a Socratic questioning method and the "create safe space" principle.
This is a distinct, named framing worth promoting into `wiki/topics/sales-clients/` and
`persona/beliefs.md` alongside the existing SALES / 5-never / listen-to-understand
material — it explains the *why* behind "ask questions and listen."

## Summary

Framed by Futur team host **Ricky** as advice for handling a call from a new client
(especially your first), the body is **Chris Do** teaching — from a live group/workshop
setting — how to run a discovery conversation. His core premise: two things are hard for
a client, thinking and making a decision, so the salesperson's job is to *help them think*
and *remove the fear of deciding*. He does this through Socratic questioning — short,
slightly-leading questions that break a big complex problem into smaller answerable parts,
then playing back what he heard to check it points in a logical direction. He illustrates
with a client who wants something both "epic" and "intimate" (helping them find they meant
production value, not scale), and closes on his coach's lesson that clients "are more afraid
of you than you are of them" — so you must create safe space rather than prove your
expertise. Ricky recaps six takeaways.

Speaker: **two speakers.** Ricky (Futur host) opens and delivers the closing recap;
**Chris Do** delivers all the substantive teaching in the middle (identifiable by his
extended monologue, the group/workshop context, and the citation of his own business coach).
Brief unattributed audience answers appear early (a couple of attendees respond before Chris
"jumps in") — those are not attributed to Chris and do not train the persona. An attendee
named "Moe"/"Moses" is referenced in a restaurant anecdote.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-01-13] Two things are true whenever you talk to a client: **it's difficult to think, and it's difficult to make a decision.** The whole method flows from these. (paraphrase — central premise)
- [2020-01-13] The salesperson's job is therefore twofold: **help the client think**, and **remove the fear of making a (wrong) decision.** (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-13] Don't let the client **self-diagnose** the problem; understanding the real problem and goal unlocks the conversation, but you must lead it rather than accept their stated diagnosis. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-13] You have to ask questions in a **slightly leading way** — not "what do you think of the world?" (which yields chaotic answers), but pointed questions that steer based on your knowledge and experience. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-13] **The quality of your question determines the quality of knowledge you signal** — clients can tell you're experienced by how pointed your questions are. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-13] This is the **Socratic process**: ask short questions that are easy to answer and lead somewhere; take a big complex problem and make it smaller so one part is easier to understand. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-13] You are **"excavating the information from the person's brain"** — listen, play back what you heard, and check whether it leads in a logical direction. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-13] When a client says two contradictory things (e.g. wants it "epic like a blockbuster" *and* "really intimate, one-to-one"), name the contradiction back to them and discuss it — often they simply "didn't choose the right word" because it was hard to think. In his example the client meant epic *production value*, not scale/lens-flares/explosions. (paraphrase — worked example)
- [2020-01-13] Clients are **"more afraid of you than you are of them"** — a lesson from his business coach [name uncertain — heard as "Kerr McClaren"] that he only understood years later. They fear sounding like an idiot because you're the expert whose work they studied before calling. (paraphrase, attributes lesson to his coach)
- [2020-01-13] Because of that fear, clients are reserved about giving information; you must **create safe space** for them to speak and to correct themselves if wrong. It is **not a battle to prove your might, will, and knowledge** — doing that is the wrong move. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-13] Making decisions is genuinely hard even in trivial cases — he cites watching people struggle to order at a restaurant over 48 hours together ("don't step into the line until you're ready to fire") — yet you're asking a client to decide, often on the call, about spending ~$100,000 with someone they barely know. (paraphrase, anecdote)
- [2020-01-13] The world "is full of people who are faking it," so clients can't easily tell the genuine deal from things seen/heard that don't work out — another reason the decision is frightening for them. (paraphrase)

### Ricky (host) — closing recap of the video's six takeaways

- [2020-01-13] (1) Genuinely want to help; (2) don't let the client self-diagnose; (3) build rapport with the client; (4) help the client think; (5) remove the fear of making the wrong decision; (6) do all of it by creating a safe space for the client to speak. (paraphrase — host's summary of Chris's teaching)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All quotes below are **Chris Do**:

- "It's difficult to think and it's really difficult to make a decision. Just remember those two things."
- "We have to assist the client to think, and then we have to remove the fear of making a decision."
- "The quality of the question then determines the quality of the knowledge that you have."
- "You're excavating the information from the person's brain — you're listening and you're playing back what you've heard, and you're checking to see if it's leading in some kind of logical direction."
- "I'm sorry, I'm having a hard time reconciling these two things that you want... can you?" (naming a client's contradiction back to them)
- "They are more afraid of you than you are afraid of them." (attributed to his business coach)
- "You have to create safe space for them to tell you what it is that they're thinking, and to help them correct themselves if they're wrong. It's not a battle trying to prove your might and your will and your knowledge over them — that's actually the wrong thing to do."
