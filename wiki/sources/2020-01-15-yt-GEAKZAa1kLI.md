## Summary

A short Futur panel arguing that for content creators, **quantity beats quality** — that
perfectionism is a trap that stops you from posting, and that volume plus iteration is
what actually builds skill and audience. The through-line: quality is subjective and best
left to the audience to judge; give yourself a deadline; post more, learn from the
comments, and you become a fundamentally better creator over hundreds of posts. One guest
recounts growing Instagram ~8–9k followers/week after switching from posting once every
5–7 days to twice a day, and being shocked that the content he thought was "not good" was
what took off.

**Speaker:** multi-speaker conversation. **Jonah** (host/narrator — intro and closing
recap). **Guest A** — a Photoshop tutorial creator ("my gift is teaching… I don't have to
script it out… I can just start the camera"). **Guest B** (caption garble "tree"/"true",
likely a name) — a traditionally trained creative taught "quality over quantity." An
**Instagram-growth guest** describes the 8–9k/week growth and challenging "my group" to
post twice a day. It is unclear whether the Instagram-growth guest and Guest A are the
same person. **No speaker is confirmed as Chris Do.** The famous "quantity over quality"
ceramics-class parable (from the book *Art & Fear*) is **NOT** cited here — the idea is
argued from personal experience, not attributed to any source.

## Key claims

_All claims are attributed to the panel speakers, not to Chris Do. `attribution:
uncertain` for the purpose of persona training._

### Jonah (host)

- [2020-01-15] Creativity "expands and contracts to meet deadlines" — give yourself a
  deadline, forgive yourself if you miss it, but try to hold to it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-15] If you take one thing from the conversation: "get over the idea of
  quality." (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-15] Recap takeaways: (1) set a deadline for a reasonable amount of output that
  is right for you; (2) quality is subjective — let the audience decide what's good; (3)
  you learn faster by making more, becoming better and more efficient. (paraphrase)

### Guest A (Photoshop-tutorial creator)

- [2020-01-15] Whether daily output works "totally depends on the kind of content." For
  Photoshop tutorials he doesn't need to script or do many takes — he knows the material,
  records, edits, and finishes by evening. If your content takes a while and you care
  about quality, three-a-week may suit you better than daily. (paraphrase)

### Guest B ("tree"/"true" — traditionally trained creative)

- [2020-01-15] Traditionally trained creatives are taught "the exact opposite" — quality
  over quantity — so hearing "quantity is what matters" is a radically different thought
  than what they're used to. (paraphrase)

### Instagram-growth guest

- [2020-01-15] Grew Instagram ~8–9k followers/week after changing posting cadence from
  once every 5–7 days to twice a day. (paraphrase, self-reported)
- [2020-01-15] Instagram posts used to take him ~8 hours each; he challenged himself and
  his group to hit the twice-a-day goal, failed at first, then succeeded by day two.
  (paraphrase, self-reported)
- [2020-01-15] Shock result: the content he thought was "not good" was the stuff that
  "took off" — you judge your own work by what you know and have grown into, so your
  judgment doesn't match the audience's. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-15] Posting quickly forced him to learn tricks, tips, and process, which then
  became a class he could teach — opportunities came from letting go of the idea that
  "quality trumps all." (paraphrase, self-reported)
- [2020-01-15] What made the difference was iterating, learning from your posts, reading
  the comments, and engaging with the community. (paraphrase)

### Shared / cross-panel

- [2020-01-15] "Quality is subjective; quantity is a number" — a number is what connects
  you with your audience. This is not a call to make trash: if you use your gift, it won't
  be trash, and you wouldn't post it anyway. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-15] After ~500 Instagram posts you are "a completely different" photographer/
  designer — the drastic change is in the quality of the work, regardless of follower
  count; you get better through the process. (paraphrase)
