# Imposter Syndrome: Expertise Vs. Mastery

★ **L3-candidate** — Not a tidy single framework, but this debate refines the heavily-covered expertise/imposter theme with two durable distinctions worth promoting into `wiki/topics/mindset/`: (a) **expert (eye of the beholder, a title you can accept) vs. master (a higher category others bestow, that you never claim for yourself)**, and (b) **subjective vs. objective measures of expertise**. Also a clean statement of Chris's "everyone starts as an impostor" position vs. a guest's push-back.

## Summary

An episode of Chris Do's "Thinking Critically" show, a new format in which he invites one guest to debate a point they disagree with him on. Guest **Alec Miller** (self-introduced founder of a creative company — company name garbled in captions) had responded to a Futur Pro Group call titled "What is an expert?" in which Chris said an expert is "someone who knows a little more than you or someone who can help you solve a problem." Alec argues that keeping *some* objectivity in the word "expert" is useful because it gives people a standard to work toward; he also pushes for more disclosure/transparency than Chris thinks necessary. Chris defends his position: expertise is subjective and in the eye of the beholder, everyone starts as an amateur/impostor before arriving at expertise, and the whole point is to *free* creatives to share and make content rather than freeze in imposter syndrome waiting to "arrive." They converge on: don't intentionally mislead (that's fraud), make a claim and share your journey honestly, and disclose what's *relevant* to the person deciding — without turning intros into legal disclaimers.

Speaker: **two speakers** — host **Chris Do** and guest **Alec Miller**. This is a genuine debate, so claims below are grouped by speaker; **only Chris-attributed material trains the persona.** Third parties cited (Seth Godin, Malcolm Gladwell, David C. Baker, Charles Barkley, Roberto Blake, Marques Brownlee, Will Paterson, Danny Elfman) are not present.

> Note on the title: the published title says "Expertise Vs. Mastery," but the transcript is mostly a debate on defining *expert*. The "mastery" angle appears late and briefly, as Chris's separate, higher **"masters"** category (see Chris claims below) — worth flagging so the mastery framing isn't over-read.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-01-21] An expert is "someone who knows a little more than you" or "someone who can help you solve a problem"; expertise is **in the eye of the beholder** — "just like value, beauty and fairness," other people get to decide, you don't declare it about yourself. (paraphrase + quote fragments)
- [2020-01-21] Attributes the "knows a little more than you, even if they learned it two/five minutes before" definition to **Seth Godin**. (paraphrase, third-party attribution)
- [2020-01-21] He has never (to his knowledge) called himself an expert on record — his *company's* marketing may frame him that way, which is different from him claiming it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] The reason this conversation matters: so many people suffer **imposter syndrome** and won't make content because they're waiting to "arrive" at being an expert. Following **Malcolm Gladwell's** 10,000-hours rule would exclude nearly everyone and "rob many creative people" of the ability to share and to help others. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] **Everyone starts as an impostor / a faker for a minute** — even a design-school graduate hasn't got credentials, paid clients, or income yet, so in a sense they're "faking it" until they do. This is a permission to *pursue a path*, not permission to lie. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] Cites **David C. Baker, "The Business of Expertise"**: it's important to pick a direction, claim an area, and focus; for a period "everyone is faking it for a little bit" — you say "I'm a brand strategist," put it out to the universe, then acquire the skills (books, seminars, workshops, practice, school) until you arrive. (paraphrase, third-party)
- [2020-01-21] Distinguishes **subjective vs. objective** measures of expertise: subjective = anyone can call anyone an expert (a teacher, coach, therapist — someone who helps you); objective = won a gold medal in the 100m, won a Clio or Academy Award, published, graduated with a certain distinction. Objective measures are yes/no; the rest is debatable and subjective. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] **Masters are a separate, higher category** — "there's a different category for these people... they're considered masters, and somebody else will call them that; I doubt that they would call themselves that." (This is the closest the episode comes to the "mastery" of the title.) (quote/paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] "Everybody can be your teacher" — you can learn something specific from anyone (e.g. learned to cut out hair in Photoshop from one tutorial) and "hold" them only for that; the world becomes your teacher rather than needing one anointed master. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] Big believer in the **Pareto principle**: don't design the 80% of your coaching/advice around the <20% who fake it disingenuously — that's designing "the rule for the exception." The market weeds out charlatans quickly, especially in the age of social media. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] Examples that "it's a story, not always 1000% true, and the market decides": **Apple** says "designed in Cupertino" though most manufacturing is in China because it sounds cooler; bottled water uses romantic origin stories. Not necessarily inauthentic — the market decides what it cares about. (paraphrase, third-party examples)
- [2020-01-21] Advocates **radical / transparent honesty** but says you don't need to front-load *everything* — filter for what's **relevant** to the person deciding; volunteering "the 14 times we screwed up" unprompted isn't relevant, but if asked "have you ever dropped the ball?" you answer honestly. Don't lie, don't advocate for lying. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] Turning intros into full disclaimer dumps makes them read like legal contracts — "lawyers talking to lawyers," not humans talking to humans. Disclosure that says "here's how we can work together" (e.g. "I'm colorblind, so I color by the charts") is relevant and good; a wall of caveats is not. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] Defines **imposter syndrome** as fundamentally "an inability to recognize what you're good at" — you can't accept your own achievement because of some "secret knowledge" you think invalidates it (e.g. "I'm just photo-bashing," "my dad had money"). Solution: disclose the thing you think discredits you; if people still value you, you begin to overcome it. (paraphrase; reads the dictionary definition "persistent inability to believe that one's success is deserved or has been legitimately achieved... as a result of one's own efforts or skills")
- [2020-01-21] Default script when called an expert: *"I'm not an expert at that, but I can tell you the results I've gotten, and you can discern for yourself."* Let them decide. (paraphrase/quote)
- [2020-01-21] On fame and platform: agrees with **Charles Barkley's** "I'm not a role model" — being good at something doesn't obligate you to change who you are. **But** if you *say something* from a platform, you do have a responsibility to be truthful, honest, and authentic; and it's getting harder to hide, so shortcuts only hurt yourself. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] His teaching is **reactionary** — based on what he senses the community is struggling with; his suspicion is people hold back a "dam of content" because they don't see themselves as experts, and separately some people mistakenly think they're experts without doing minimal research. His fix for both: creators, just start sharing (expertise is in the eye of the beholder); and if you're making claims, read a few books / validate against a couple of sources and course-correct on the way up. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] On the show's purpose: it's safe to disagree with him, he won't cut people off, he'll give them fair air time, and he'll replay the conversation to see what he needs to re-evaluate in himself. Frames himself as "a learning creature, evolving, changing" who will publicly say his past position was wrong. (paraphrase)

### Alec Miller (guest — does NOT train the persona)

- [2020-01-21] Believes in "brutal honesty" and isn't afraid to admit when he's wrong; some of Chris's statements "rubbed a few of us the wrong way." (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] Wants to keep **objectivity** in the word "expert" — defines it as "someone who can demonstrate a level of proficiency in a subject that is uncommon." Making expert *rare* is good because it gives people a standard/goal to work toward. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] Dislikes "fake it till you make it" "vehemently"; worries the message can be *misinterpreted* as "I don't need to know anything, I'll just give my opinion and never improve." (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] Push-back on disclosure: the natural state is misalignment between what you claim and what people know about you; the higher your position of power/authority, the **more** responsibility you have to correct people and share context ("with great power comes great responsibility"). Argues for a bit more disclosure than Chris. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] Adds to the imposter-syndrome definition: it's half not recognizing your skills, half **not correcting the misalignment** between how you perceive yourself and how others perceive you. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] Discloses he is red-green **colorblind** and tells clients before color work ("I go by the charts and graphs, I don't trust my eyes") — his example of relevant, upfront disclosure. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] His company's brand/process states upfront that it "connects clients to artists," so subcontracting isn't a hidden thing. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-21] On why he debates: he works out ideas by questioning; he made an assumption about Chris and took responsibility to ask rather than assume. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do:**

- "So many of us suffer from impostor syndrome that we're not willing to make content because we're waiting to arrive at becoming an expert."
- "The expert is in the eye of the beholder... so I'm not running around claiming that I am an expert at anything."
- "I still am adamant [that] expert or expertise is in the eye of the beholder — they get to decide, just like value, beauty and fairness."
- "Everybody starts as an impostor at some point, or a faker — everybody."
- "There's a different category for these people... they're considered masters, and somebody else will call them that; I doubt that they would call themselves that."
- "Everybody can be your teacher... in that way the world becomes my teacher."
- "I'm not an expert at that, but I can tell you the results that I've gotten, and you can discern for yourself if I'm an expert."
- "That's not humans talking to humans, that's just lawyers talking to lawyers, and I don't think that's helpful to anybody." (on over-disclaiming)
- "[Imposter syndrome is] just your inability to recognize what you're good at. Period."

**Alec Miller (guest — context only, not persona training):**

- "I believe in brutal honesty, but I'm not afraid at all to admit when I'm wrong."
- "Someone who can demonstrate a level of proficiency in a subject that is uncommon." (his definition of expert)
- "With great power comes great responsibility... the higher the position of power you are, the more responsibility you have to correct people and to make sure you're putting out the correct information."
