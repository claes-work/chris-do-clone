# Hiring Your First Employee As An Entrepreneur

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do lays out a self-contained, sequential playbook for making
the first hire and building toward world-class talent (the "intern bridge" model:
document functions → hire least-essential first → train up → promote the ones who hit
the ceiling → use the margin to buy back your own time). Worth promoting into
`wiki/topics/business/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), not just held at L2.

## Summary

In a coaching-style segment, Chris Do advises a small studio owner (Ash) who says he
wants to scale to a "multimillion dollar business." Chris tells him this requires taking
off the "Maker / Creator Hat" and putting on the "Entrepreneur Hat," because the owner's
irreplaceable job is the future-planning, vision, marketing, sales, and relationship work
that only they can do — every hour spent doing the client work is like "taking your foot
off the pedal" of the business. His concrete first-hire mechanism: write down every
function you personally perform (could be 3, could be 17), accept that one person can't
do all of them, and hire an **intern** (paid preferred, for sustainability) for the
*least essential* functions. You go through many people to learn who fits on personality,
skill, and drive; you pay entry-level rates and the exchange is knowledge/mentorship for
their labor. Keepers reveal themselves by bumping against the ceiling and asking to take
on more. The margin between what you charge and the entry-level wage funds your time to
plan, read, write, become a thought leader, and attract bigger clients — until your
rates, reputation, and portfolio align enough to afford world-class talent. He stresses
this doesn't mean never doing creative work again; it means becoming *choosy*, calling
"dibs" on the dream projects (his example: "that Calvin Klein / Mos Def project").

**Speaker:** Multi-speaker coaching video. **Ricky** hosts (intro + outro, including a
paid Epidemic Sound sponsor read). **Ash** is the studio owner being coached (one line:
confirms he wants to scale). **Chris Do** delivers the entire body of advice in first
person. All framework/claims below are **Chris-attributed** unless noted.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-01-31] To scale, you must "take off our Maker / Creator Hat and put on our
  Entrepreneur Hat" — entrepreneurs think very differently about business and design than
  creators do. (quote fragment + paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] A creative business is "no different than any other business"; the things
  that "glue all businesses together" are Marketing, Sales, Customer Service, and the
  user experience — and notice there's little room in those for the person putting their
  hands in the work. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] "Somebody out there... can do what it is that you do, but nobody can be
  you" — nobody can plan your future or has your exact chemistry and vision; that vision/
  planning is the owner's real job. (quote + paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] Every time you move away from the owner role to do the work, "it's like...
  somebody takes their foot off the pedal" and the business coasts — like turning to talk
  to someone in the back seat. (quote + paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] Most people are anxious about hiring because new hires "can't meet the
  standards they have set for themselves" — but that usually stems from hiring someone and
  paying them too little, or over-relying on local talent / people you already know.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] You don't jump from a two-person home operation to world-class talent
  overnight — "you build a bridge to it." (quote + paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] First-hire mechanism, step by step: (1) find a nearby school / community
  college / university with a relevant department; (2) bring in an **intern** — paid or
  unpaid, but he prefers **paid** "so that it's sustainable"; (3) expect to go through a
  lot of people, testing to learn who fits on personality, skill set, and drive.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] "Not so good" hires means *not a good fit* — "not that they're bad human
  beings"; you can't predict in advance which motivations are the good ones. (quote +
  paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] **Document then delegate**: write down all the functions you have to do
  ("it could be three functions, it could be 17"), accept that "I cannot hire one person
  to do all seven functions — that's a job of three people," then hire for the *least
  essential* functions first. (quote + paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] **Train them up.** The reason you train is that you're not paying market
  rate — you pay entry-level, and the exchange of value is knowledge/teaching they can't
  gain otherwise: "they exchange their labor for a little bit of money, and for you to
  teach them." Get good utility from the good ones; let the others go and move on.
  (quote + paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] A **keeper** reveals themselves by hitting the ceiling: "You know those
  three things? I've mastered those three things. I want to take on two more things." —
  that's the person to keep grooming and growing. (quote + paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] Even at modest two-person-studio rates, what you charge is still more than
  the intern's entry-level wage; **that gap funds the business** — it buys you time to do
  planning, relationship building, marketing, sales, going out to meet people, and time
  to think, read, write, become a thought leader, create content, and grow your channels
  and personal brand — which attracts bigger and bigger clients. (paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] Scaling doesn't mean never working on projects again — "actually it's the
  opposite." You get to be *choosy*, calling "dibs" on the dream jobs you must do (e.g. "I
  must do that Calvin Klein / Mos Def project! I'm not going to delegate that away") — "to
  me, that is the dream of all creative people... not to do every project, but just to do
  the ones you feel like [you must]." (quote + paraphrase)
- [2020-01-31] You repeat this loop "until your rates and your reputation and your
  portfolio are in alignment with what you need to do to be able to hire world-class
  talent. This is the process of being a creative entrepreneur." (quote)
