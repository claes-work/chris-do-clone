# How To Use Pricing Options In Your Bids & Proposals

★ **L3-candidate** — Adds concrete mechanics (naming, sequencing, and the pause-to-anchor delivery) for presenting three price options in a live bid/proposal. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/pricing/` alongside the existing three-price-options and price-bracketing pages, and into `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), not just held at L2.

## Summary

A cut-down from a Pro Group coaching call in which Chris Do coaches a member (Shia) who has been submitting a single full-branding estimate to clients. Chris's remedy is to always present three price options — one cheaper to the left, one much more expensive to the right — so the client compares you against yourself instead of shopping around. He works a specific example (a $20K full-branding offer bracketed by a ~$5K logo/strategy option and a $45K "White Glove" package built up with coaching, staff training, and months of oversight), and reinforces the anchoring effect with a wine-list analogy: reset the price ladder upward and buyers still pick the new middle. He closes with delivery mechanics: name each option, describe what's included, state the top price first, and pause to let the anchor work.

Speaker: two speakers. **Ricky** (Futur team member) delivers the intro/outro voiceover promoting the Pro Group. The coaching content — everything from the pricing framework onward — is **Chris Do** (first person, running the Pro call). Only the Chris-attributed material trains the persona; the Ricky intro is context.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-02-03] Write down your core offering (the thing you sell most) with its price, then deliberately write a cheaper option to the left and a much more expensive option to the right — this helps people make a decision. (paraphrase) — the core price-bracketing move.
- [2020-02-03] Presenting a client only one option pushes them to go shop around, compare, and mull it over; giving them your own cheaper and pricier options makes them "compare you against yourself." (paraphrase) — the genuinely-new rationale for bracketing in a proposal context.
- [2020-02-03] Worked example: if a full branding package costs ~$10K–$20K, the cheaper left option could be just a logo or a strategy session at ~$5K with no other deliverables; the pricier right option adds coaching, staff training, six months of oversight, etc. (paraphrase; caption dollar figures — the low option is stated as "five," read as ~$5K).
- [2020-02-03] The whole point of the expensive right-hand option is to make the middle option feel like the best value — "that $20,000 looks like the best value for us." (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-03] Wine-list analogy (credited to "Matthew," a Pro member then in the alt MBA program): on a list priced $20/25/30/35/40/45, most people pick the one in the middle (~$30); reset the ladder to start at $30/35/40/45/50 and buyers pick the new middle — "it feels like I deserve a little bit more but I don't need the very best." (paraphrase, third-party-sourced analogy relayed by Chris)
- [2020-02-03] Delivery script for three options: tell the client "I have three options I've crafted just for you," then name and describe each. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-03] Option 1 — the "White Glove experience" (give it a name): strategy + branding + copywriting + three months maintenance/oversight + team training + availability, priced at $45,000 — then pause and "let the price anchor do its work for you." (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-03] Option 2 — "our most popular package" at $20,000: branding, messaging, and strategy, but no oversight — "some people don't need that, don't want it, they have teams in-house." (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-03] Option 3 — the low anchor: a quick logo, three versions, "and then we're out," for $10K. (paraphrase; caption "10k")
- [2020-02-03] Naming and describing each package (not just listing prices) is part of the technique — each option is framed as "crafted just for you." (paraphrase)
