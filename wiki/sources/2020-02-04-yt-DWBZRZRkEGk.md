# Teach While You Learn w/ Melinda Livsey Ep. 21

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do offers several named, reusable frameworks here that recur across his teaching (educate = "draw out, not put in"; the audience owns the "expert/teacher" label; the *buyer* determines price/value; iterate your framework like software/"bank-robbery rigor"; be coached to teach; focus outward to beat stage fright). Worth promoting the Chris-attributed items into `wiki/topics/` (mindset, pricing, branding) and dated entries in `persona/beliefs.md`, not just held at L2.

## Summary

Melinda Livsey brings her own insecurity about pivoting into *teaching* brand strategy — worried about the proverb "those who can't do, teach" and whether she has "done enough" to charge for a course. Chris Do reframes the whole question: teaching credentials should be judged by the market and by results, not by years-served or terminal degrees; the person you teach (not you) decides whether you're an "expert" or a "teacher"; and the buyer (not the seller) decides whether your price is worth it. He redefines *educate* as drawing knowledge out of the learner rather than pushing it in, argues that being good at doing something often makes you a *worse* teacher, and prescribes concrete ways to get better at teaching (be coached yourself, teach live and read nonverbal cues, iterate the framework like software). He closes by telling Melinda to focus on the good she does and ignore criticism rooted in other people's insecurities.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-02-04] He *does* personally get the "those who can't do, teach" criticism, and thinks a level of skepticism from an audience is healthy — but you shouldn't give it credence. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] The "expert" / "teacher" label is not yours to assign — the learner assigns it. Ties it to Marty Neumeier's definition of a brand as "a person's gut feeling about your product or service": you can *influence* whether people see you as a teacher/expert, but you can't *control* it. "Everybody but you owns" your brand — your customers and audience own it. (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2020-02-04] To *educate* means "to draw out, not to put in." A lot of people think teaching is taking what you have and putting it into the student; it's the opposite — you draw it out of them through questions, examples, workshops, and short lectures. Making students memorize what you say is "not true teaching, that's called memorization." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-02-04] Criticism of 20th-century education: the "put stuff into you" model is inferior to drawing understanding out of the learner. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] Being very good at what you do often makes you a *horrible* teacher — because it's so easy and intuitive for you, your only advice becomes "just do it, just be good, just be confident," which doesn't transfer. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] Traditional schools require terminal degrees (PhD / master's) to teach, which produces impressive-on-paper faculty who may teach little of value, while a passionate, self-taught, great communicator with barely a high-school degree is blocked by "artificial barriers." Let the market decide who is most qualified to teach *this group, at this level*. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] A bad teacher self-corrects via reputation: few clients, tarnished reputation, "didn't get my money's worth," and it ends — "that's the way it should work." (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] The buyer — not the seller — determines price/value. You *set* a price, but the buyer decides "do I have a $30,000 problem to solve" and whether you're credible; if they see greater value than the price, they buy. Illustrated with video-game skins (from Jonathan Stark's newsletter): skins give zero performance advantage yet people pay real money because the buyer decides it's valuable to them. (paraphrase + quote: "it's the buyer... the buyer gets to determine the price")
- [2020-02-04] He sleeps well pricing The Futur's courses/toolkits because he believes the value delivered is ~10x what they charge; deliver greater value than the price and "you're going to be just fine." (paraphrase; self-reported on The Futur's own products)
- [2020-02-04] How long must you practice before you can teach? The general public would say "some period of time," but exact duration/heights don't matter — what matters is becoming a good teacher in the truest sense (drawing it out). He personally "worked for five years before I started teaching." (paraphrase; self-reported)
- [2020-02-04] Measure your teaching by *results and reviews*, not by self-imposed credential standards. Reviews are crowd-sourced and hard to fake at volume; it's the same signal you trust on Amazon, Rotten Tomatoes, and Yelp. It "goes back to the whole reputation / brand — what are people saying about it." (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] The power of teaching is helping someone *reframe*: "if we just move three degrees... to the right, you'll see that it's not a box, it's a pyramid," and from the new vantage point they unlock what was holding them back. (quote)
- [2020-02-04] Creative people are largely *kinesthetic learners* — they need to *do* something to experience and learn it; very few can just hear a topic sentence and "get it." (paraphrase; "anecdotally from the data collected from my students")
- [2020-02-04] Iterate your framework like software. He maps the question onto video games, an OS, and Tesla ("essentially an iPhone on wheels") — products are never perfect, get patched, gain features, and improve on feedback. Release a beta or do "running releases" and tell people the product is always improving. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] "Bank-robbery rigor": for the one-shot situations where you can't rewind (unlike software), stress-test in advance — good instructors and course designers "look for ways for it to break," imagine a better scenario, then foolproof it. (paraphrase + analogy)
- [2020-02-04] To become a better teacher/coach, *be coached yourself* and study people constantly — he's learned from a psychotherapist (how she asks questions, gives space to think, reframes), a professional business coach, workshops, seminars, and MasterClass. Cites Neil deGrasse Tyson's "imagine if..." as a powerful teaching device that lets a learner try on an idea without fully committing. Describes himself as a "super-arrow character" stealing little bits of what works. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] Teach *live* and read the room: nonverbal cues (leaning in, puzzled, disengaged) tell you what's landing. When everyone stops looking at you and writes something down, or everyone laughs / reacts emotionally, "hit that harder." Analytics (rewinds, pauses, drop-off) and even off-the-shelf eye-tracking can substitute when live feedback isn't available. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] The cure for self-consciousness / stage fright: invert the lens off yourself and onto the people you're helping. The more you focus on how you look, your hair, your voice, the bigger the problem grows; genuinely wishing to ease others' suffering and hand them tools makes you "forget that you're in the room." (paraphrase; Melinda recalls this is the advice that helped him most with public speaking)
- [2020-02-04] Keep learning to make what you offer richer — "become a monster at learning," then process and connect the dots. As he goes deeper across fields he finds story, design thinking, and branding are structurally "very similar"; finding that common thread is why he can walk into an unfamiliar situation, listen, and help. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] When we buy anything we're buying a *destination* — "we buy a car because we don't currently have the car that we want... we're buying change, we're buying a destination"; the job (in business, coaching, or strategy) is to find where someone wants to go and help them get there. (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2020-02-04] On handling criticism: focus on the good you do; there will always be people who doubt your intentions. Distinguish *valid criticism* from "criticism based on other people's insecurities projecting themselves onto you." (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-04] "Purple donkeys eat carrots" test for criticism: if a statement doesn't describe you, it can't penetrate — ignore it the way you'd ignore being called a purple donkey. (paraphrase; a prior exercise he did with Melinda)

### Melinda Livsey (context — does NOT train the persona)

- [2020-02-04] She switched from design to brand strategy ~2.5 years ago after meeting Chris, and over the past year has been pivoting into *teaching* designers how to become brand strategists. (context)
- [2020-02-04] Every pivot makes her feel insecure ("what if I don't know enough"), which makes proverbs like "those who can't do, teach" feel aimed at her even when they aren't. (context)
- [2020-02-04] Quotes a Twitter post she saw: the only paid course worth taking is from someone who got successful in their domain first and only later offered a course — "rare these days where we have more [teachers/'growers'] than practitioners." (context; third-party quote she relays)
- [2020-02-04] Draws a teacher-vs-coach distinction: a teacher transfers/breaks down knowledge; a coach helps you *implement* what you already know, reading you and the situation. (context)
- [2020-02-04] Her college design professors who went straight into teaching (no industry experience) prepared her worst; the ones with real-world experience were much better. (context, anecdotal)
- [2020-02-04] Nervous about teaching an *updated* brand-strategy framework she hasn't yet tested with a live client. (context — this is the question Chris answers with the "software update / bank-robbery" reframe)
