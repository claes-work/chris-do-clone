> design) is **Paula Scher's**, not Chris Do's, and is filed here as **context / guest

> trains the persona. Paula Scher's claims are marked `[guest — context, do NOT train persona]`.

## Summary

Chris Do introduces a clip of "design legend" Paula Scher on how to align with a client's
interests before designing a logo. Scher's core method: alignment happens *before* you begin
designing, through conversation. Her signature technique at Pentagram is a book of ~500
trademarks the firm has produced — she hands each client (especially when there are 3+
decision-makers) a copy and has them flag the marks they love in one color and the ones they
hate in another. This reveals each person's taste (modernist vs. ornamental) and, critically,
whether a multi-person client group agrees or diverges — if they pick differently, "you're in
trouble." She adds: ask what brands they like (past the obvious Nike/Apple), ask about
competitors to learn how much they want to resemble vs. differ from them, and treat designing
for someone as shopping for someone else — study what they always wear, always buy, never buy.
The reliable signal you're on track is watching faces for a positive read as you play back
descriptions of possible directions. Chris Do's on-camera contribution is limited to the intro
framing and one follow-up question about phrasing the competitor question.

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

  what they want "even when they don't know how to communicate it." (paraphrase — endorsement /

  like everybody else"? (paraphrase — observation about client self-contradiction)

### Paula Scher `[guest — context, do NOT train persona]`

- [2020-02-06] Aligning with a client "starts before you begin designing" — it's about the
  conversation, not the drawing. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-06] At Pentagram she uses a book of ~500 trademarks the firm has done; she gives each
  client a copy and a sticky pad and has them mark loved marks in one color, hated ones in
  another — so she can read their taste (modernist vs. "a lot of curlicues"). (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-06] When there are three or more client participants: if they all flag the same marks,
  "that's fantastic"; if they diverge, "you're in trouble" because different tastes will be
  making decisions around the table. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-06] Ask what brands they like; get past the obvious answers (Nike, Apple) to reach the
  real information you need — including their competing businesses. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-06] If they name competitors, find out how much they want to be *like* them and how
  much they want to be *different* — "that's very revealing." (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-06] A client saying they don't want to be like a competitor they compete with actually
  means they want to be like them "a little bit… in the good ways"; the envy may not even be
  about graphic design — it can be org structure, media coverage, or something else. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-06] Designing for someone else is like shopping for someone else: study everything
  they wore, what they always bought and never bought, then you know how to pick. (paraphrase —
  she calls it "a girly answer")
- [2020-02-06] People have fixed taste and you can only stretch it so far — you can't put someone
  who always wears black in a pink party dress, but you can add "a terrific red belt." Meet the
  client where they are and push only within their range. (paraphrase — analogy)
- [2020-02-06] To check you're on the right track, play back a description of a possible direction
  and read their faces for a positive signal (nodding); use examples ("should it be like
  such-and-such?"). When they start saying they like or dislike something, listen very carefully.
  (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Paula Scher (guest — context):**

- "A lot of it starts before you begin designing because it has to do with your conversation with
  them."
- "I give them each a book and I tell them to take… a sticky note and mark the ones they love in
  one color and the ones they hate in the next color, so that way I can tell what their tastes
  are."
- "If they come out all different you're in trouble, because you're gonna have different tastes
  making decisions around the table."
- "If a client tells you that they don't want to be like someone but they're competing with them,
  it means they want to be like them a little bit — not just in the bad ways, in the good ways."
- "You can't make somebody who always wears black put on a pink party dress… you can make them
  wear a red belt on the black dress… just make it a terrific red belt."
- "When they start to tell you that they like something or don't like something, you should
  listen very carefully when they do."
