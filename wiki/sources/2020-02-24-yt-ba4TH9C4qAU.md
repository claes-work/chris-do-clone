# Be The First To Raise Client Objections

★ **L3-candidate** — This is a named, self-contained sales technique (proactively voicing the client's objections before they can). The **core teaching is confidently Chris-Do first-person** and worth promoting into `wiki/topics/sales-clients/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated). Gate on attribution: the host framing (Ricky, intro/outro) is Futur context, do-not-train — see below.

## Summary

Excerpt from a Futur Pro Group coaching call, packaged as a short. Chris Do teaches that when a client is combative or fearful about your capability, you should be the first to name their objections out loud — pre-empting "too expensive," "not enough experience," "no guarantees" — so those objections are "gone" and a real conversation can begin. He walks through a ~$700K video-project example where he told the prospect that if technical specs (technical directors, software licenses, render farm) matter most to them they should hire someone else, then reframed the choice as "technical specs vs. artistry." He layers in a conditional/hypothetical rhetorical device ("if X is important to you, you probably shouldn't hire us") that makes prospects question whether the trait they claimed to want actually matters — a "U-turn" that turns their stated criteria against themselves.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (first person — trains persona)

- [2020-02-24] When a client opens combatively ("you're not right, you don't have the team, you don't have the expertise"), you can still win the project by **fully embracing all their objections and not fighting them at all**. He describes taking one such prospect "from a no, to maybe, to a yes" over a 90-minute call. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-24] References **Chris Voss's book *Never Split the Difference*** and its "accusation audit" (caption garbles it as "Chris boss / argument audit"): list every argument or complaint a prospect could bring up against you. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-24] Anyone who has done business for any period of time should be able to write down the **three complaints prospects always raise** — his set: **too expensive, not enough experience, no guarantees.** Then prepare what you'll say to each. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-24] The move: at the very start of a call, say them yourself first — "I'm probably going to be too expensive, I probably don't have the kind of experience you're looking for, and I can't guarantee the results." Once vocalized, "they're gone... now we can have a real conversation." (paraphrase; near-verbatim below)
- [2020-02-24] In the ~$700K project example, he told the prospect that if their primary concern is that "we don't have technical directors... software licenses... a render farm is inadequate... we may not have the right experience," they should hire someone else — then reframed the decision as **"do you care more about technical specs or artistry?"** (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-24] He disarms the "no experience" objection by conceding it and adding social proof: **"every project we've done was exactly the first time we did that kind of project... I've been in business now for probably 19 years and I haven't dropped the ball too many times. You get to decide."** (paraphrase/quote)
- [2020-02-24] Powerful rhetorical device — the **conditional/hypothetical**: phrase it as "IF this is important to you, then you probably shouldn't hire us." This forces the prospect to decide whether the criterion is actually important, and often they conclude it isn't. (paraphrase)
- [2020-02-24] Calls the maneuver a **"U-turn"**: you take a trait the prospect said they wanted and make them turn against it themselves — "because it's hard to think and it's hard to make decisions, so they think this is what they want but truthfully they haven't really thought it through." (paraphrase/quote)
- [2020-02-24] Analogizes to dating: "if good looks matter to you, if six-pack abs matter to you, then you probably don't want to hang out with me — if you want a future." (paraphrase; the "future" line is a plug/pun on The Futur) (quote)
- [2020-02-24] On mastery: "I can do this because I've had a lot of practice — 20-plus years of practice will do that to you," and he frames the Pro Group as condensing that so members learn it in "maybe two [weeks]." (paraphrase/quote)

### Ricky (host — Futur context, DO-NOT-TRAIN)

- [2020-02-24] Frames the episode as "what to do when a client calls and you're feeling inadequate... the elephant in the room," and closes by comparing Chris's technique to Eminem's *8 Mile* battle against Papa Doc (address every attack yourself first and there's nothing left to say). Narrator commentary — not Chris. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do:**

- "The way that you do that is you fully embrace all their objections — you don't fight it at all."
- "They always say the same three things: too expensive, and you don't have enough experience, no guarantees."
- "Right at the beginning of a call, before we go any further... I'm probably gonna be too expensive, I probably don't have the kind of experience you are looking for, and I can't guarantee the results. All those things are gone now... and now we can have a real conversation."
- "Do you care more about technical specs or artistry?"
- "You don't have to take my word for it, but I've been in business now for... probably 19 years, and I haven't dropped the ball too many times. You get to decide."
- "If this is important to you, X Y and Z, then you probably shouldn't hire us — and then it makes them think, well, what's the alternative to that?"
- "You make them turn against themselves, because... it's hard to think and it's hard to make decisions. So they think this is what they want, but truthfully they haven't really thought it through."
- "If good looks matter to you, if six-pack abs matter to you, then you probably don't want to hang out with me — if you want a future."

**Ricky (host — do-not-train):**

- "If you address the elephant in the room, you can move past it."
