# Specialize or Generalize - Niche or Broad

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a named, self-contained resolution to the specialist-vs-generalist debate: **"specialize externally while remaining a generalist internally."** This reframes/extends the existing corpus (inch-wide-mile-deep, tip-of-the-spear niching) by explicitly reconciling the tension with personal fulfillment, and introduces the **halo bias** as the mechanism that makes external specialization pay off. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/branding/` (positioning) and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated).

## Summary

Answering a student's anxiety about feeling pressured to specialize (narrow from "visual artist" to "typographer") while having many passions, Chris Do resolves the specialist-vs-generalist debate with a single reframe: **specialize externally, stay a generalist internally.** Show the world one thing you go deep on so you become the known expert; keep pursuing all your other interests privately. The mechanism that makes this profitable is the **halo bias** — if you're seen as great at one thing, people assume you're great at everything, so mastery in one visible domain opens doors in adjacent ones. He grounds the "pick a lane" discipline in Blair Enns (creatives are addicted to novelty, which works against marketing/expertise/value) and Jordan Peterson (creatives are divergent thinkers; a little convergent discipline pays off). He closes with portfolio advice: edit ruthlessly, show only 3–4 of your best pieces, because reviewers judge you by your worst piece.

Speaker: single-speaker classroom Q&A. An **unnamed student** asks the opening question and **Diego** (a student in the room) is used as a live example; all substantive content is **Chris Do** answering in first person ("I read faces for a living," "internally I'm studying philosophy," "I got my first job in advertising by sending four pieces"). No co-host or guest speaks. Blair Enns and Jordan Peterson are cited third parties, not present. Setting appears to be a talk at LMU (Loyola Marymount University).

## The framework

**Specialize externally, generalize internally.**
- **Externally** (what the world sees): pick one lane, go deep, become the known expert at that one thing. This is what earns money, work, and opportunities.
- **Internally** (what you keep doing privately): keep all your other passions — Chris cites his own study of philosophy, psychotherapy, typography. This keeps you *rich internally* (fulfilled) while the external focus makes you *rich externally* (money in the bank).
- **The engine: halo bias.** If you're good at one visible thing, people assume you're good at everything — so adjacent opportunities open up once you've established "superstar/all-star" status. (It cuts both ways: if your one visible thing looks bad, they assume you're bad at everything.)

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-03-19] The generalist knows many things (goes wide); the specialist focuses on one thing and goes deep. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-19] When hiring someone to solve a problem, people want depth in the one relevant thing, not surface-level breadth — e.g. for a math problem you hire the math genius who has "seen this problem probably 1,000 times," not the generalist tutor who has seen it once. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-19] You should **specialize externally while you remain a generalist internally** — this is "the critical difference." Put one thing into the world (what they know you for); keep composing music, studying photography, writing poetry, etc. privately. (paraphrase — core claim)
- [2020-03-19] The **halo bias**: if you're good at one thing, people assume you're good at everything. Showing the world one thing you're excellent at makes them assume you're excellent broadly, opening adjacent opportunities once you reach superstar/expert/credibility status. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-19] Being an internal generalist "makes me richer internally"; being an external specialist "makes me rich externally, meaning money in the bank." You can still love all the things you do — just pick a lane, go deep, and put that face into the world. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-19] Cites **Blair Enns**: creative people are addicted to the new and the novel; they tire of doing the same thing repeatedly. This addiction works *against* our ability to market ourselves, develop expertise, and command the greatest value — so we have to get rid of the desire to do so much. (paraphrase, third-party attribution)
- [2020-03-19] Cites **Jordan Peterson**: creatives create immeasurable value (examples: the Sistine Chapel's commerce; the Eiffel Tower reportedly generating over a billion dollars in tourism/merch for France), but we are by nature divergent thinkers rather than convergent; a little discipline to "pick a lane" lets us do really well. (paraphrase, third-party attribution — figures unverified)
- [2020-03-19] Portfolio rule: edit out all the garbage — you need only **three** strong pieces, not fourteen. Reviewers look at your *worst* piece and imagine the rest of your work is like that. Be merciless and vigilant about what you keep. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-19] Self-report: he got his first advertising job by sending **four** conceptual pieces in a FedEx box, even though his portfolio was not advertising-based — he picked pieces that "might feel like advertising" and got hired because of that. (paraphrase, self-reported)
- [2020-03-19] Tells the student they think they're specializing now (at LMU) but "you're not, not even close" — formal programs are broad, not focused specialization. (paraphrase)
