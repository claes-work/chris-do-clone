# How To Double Your Income As A Solopreneur

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a named, self-contained pricing method (Chris Do's "price the job as if you had to hire someone at or above your level" bid-buildup). Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/pricing/` and `wiki/topics/business/` (scaling from solo to team), dated, rather than held only at L2.

## Summary

Chris Do teaches solopreneurs how to raise their rates and transition into being business owners. The core move: research what it would cost to hire someone at or above your own level to do your work (get 3+ data points, average them), then price every future bid *as if* you had to hire that person — and layer in the cost of a salesperson, production manager, and project coordinator, plus 20–30% profit and padding for things that go wrong. Doing this reveals that most solopreneurs are charging below market value; it also gives you the *option* to hire help (whether you exercise it or not), which is how you scale from a solo operation to a multi-person team via freelancers you eventually convert to full-time.

Speaker: single-speaker monologue by **Chris Do** (first person throughout — "I'm gonna teach you," "if I bring on somebody," "I want you to think of yourself as an investor"). No co-host or guest speaks. Grant Cardone is cited as a third-party sales anecdote, not present.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-03-25] To find your true market value, research what it costs to hire someone at or above your level to do the work you do — dig around, make calls, even send "fake" inquiry emails posing as a prospective client — and once you have 3+ data points, average them. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-25] Most solopreneurs discover from this exercise that they're charging **less than market value** for what they do. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-25] On your next bid, act as if you had to hire that at-or-above-level person, manage them, and manage the risk if they don't work out — then charge accordingly. Example: if someone charges $1,000/day and you estimate 5 days, bidding a flat $5,000 loses you money, because if it takes them twice as long you're "done for." (paraphrase; dollar figures $1,000/day and $5,000 stated in transcript)
- [2020-03-25] A real bid must also account for the roles a business fills: you brought in the project (sales), you manage the project (production manager), and you coordinate it (project coordinator) — each is a distinct role that costs money — and then you add profit on top. Add roughly **20–30% profit**, plus padding "because things never go the way you think." That's how you run a business. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-25] Once you've priced it properly, you get to *choose* whether to do the work yourself or hire someone to run the project — that optionality is the point. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-25] Cites **Grant Cardone**: Cardone buys apartment complexes of ~200 units or more, because at that size the units generate enough to pay for a manager — he doesn't want to *become* a building manager, since managing it himself isn't his job. (paraphrase, third-party anecdote)
- [2020-03-25] Think of yourself as an investor whose money makes money — you're investing in a creative team, and you play those hands-on roles only if you *choose* to, not because you *have* to. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-25] Most solopreneurs can't afford to hire others because they **bid too low**; they'd lose money bringing anyone on. The remedy is to find out what the work actually costs and give yourself the *luxury/option* of hiring — whether you use it or not doesn't matter, because now you've found true market value. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-25] Scaling method: work with freelancers; the freelancers you "fall in love with," book more often and extend their bookings so you don't lose them; eventually offer them a full-time opportunity — but **wait** and make sure they're the right person and fully committed before hiring. (paraphrase)
- [2020-03-25] Don't be quick to hire, "because like most creatives we find it very hard to fire people, myself included." (paraphrase, self-referential)
