# Make A Living Doing What You Love—Practical Advice

★ **L3-candidate** — This is a self-contained monetization playbook: Chris Do walks a
creator through building **multiple parallel income streams** ("little buckets that
collect a little bit of rain") around a passion, with concrete pricing/bundling
mechanics and rare specific Futur financials. Worth promoting into
`wiki/topics/business/` and `wiki/topics/content-strategy/`, and into
`persona/beliefs.md` (audience-first monetization, fame-vs-price), not just held at L2.

## Summary

A recorded coaching call. **Chris Do** advises a professional Los Angeles session
drummer (addressed as "Blair" in the transcript) who runs an educational site ("Sticks
and Wires") and wants to make money teaching drum recording. The drummer's goal is
modest — $10,000 by year's end — and Chris argues there are many ways to blow past it.
He lays out The Futur's own revenue mix as a template (courses, a subscription coaching
group, YouTube ad revenue, sponsorships, affiliate deals, donations), gives concrete
course-pricing and bundling tactics, and repeatedly reframes the real asset as the
**audience**, not the product. He closes with a "garage sale" idea — packaging digital
assets you already own — and encourages starting now with an email list.

Speaker: **two speakers.** (1) **Chris Do** — the coach, first person, gives all the
advice; identifiable by references to "the future" company, its $3.1M year, 12 staff,
his book Kickstarter, and the coaching group. (2) **"Blair," the drummer/guest** — the
coachee, describes his situation and asks questions. Only Chris-attributed material
trains the persona; the drummer's lines are context. Dustin Lee (RetroSupply), Ron
Segal, and the authors of *Rework* / Basecamp (37signals) are named as third-party
examples, not present.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

**On multiple income streams (The Futur's own mix, as a template)**
- [2020-04-08] The Futur makes money "doing a lot of different things," and the biggest
  revenue driver is **course sales**; courses range from **$49 up to ~$500**, plus a
  bigger package around **$5,000**. A mix of products and price points helps because
  people won't make a giant purchase before they know you and your quality. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] They run a **coaching/subscription group** (~300 people) that pays to
  join for weekly coaching calls plus a video archive; the annual rate is discounted to
  ~10 months' cost vs 12. This recurring revenue "adds up really fast" — on the order of
  **~$30k/month** (caption garble corrected). (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] **YouTube ad revenue** was averaging about **$10,000–$12,000/month** for
  the channel at that time. The key is lots of views. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] YouTube ad *rates* depend on your audience/demo: a design channel draws
  advertisers like Wix and Vimeo (lower bids for that demo), whereas content that tech
  companies or big manufacturers want to advertise on commands a much higher rate. Some
  people do $100k/month on ad revenue alone but have "ginormous" views. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] They also make money via **sponsorships** — companies pay to be mentioned
  on the podcast or a YouTube episode, ranging from a few thousand to **over $10,000 per
  episode** (podcast rates lower than YouTube). (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] And via **affiliate deals** — you promote products you genuinely use and
  earn a percentage of each sale you send, ranging **10% to as much as 50%**; you act as
  the "organic trusted expert." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] They also take **donations** — no Patreon; people "just give us money."
  He's experimenting with posting his Venmo and asking viewers to chip in, and people do,
  sometimes ~$500. It works because they've "built up enough goodwill." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] Framing metaphor: it's "a lot of little buckets that collect a little bit
  of rain and they'll all equal something." (quote/paraphrase)

**On course pricing and bundling**
- [2020-04-08] Structure a big course as **mini-courses** at a low, low-risk price
  (~$50–$100 sells well as "disposable income"). Then **bundle** ~8–12 modules into a
  master class and price the bundle **below the sum of the parts** so buyers feel they're
  already saving. E.g. twelve $100 courses "should sell for $1,200," but you discount it —
  people see each part's value and feel the bundle is worth even more. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] "Outrageous" bundles work: total listed value ~$3,700 sold for ~$499 is "a
  trigger for a lot of people," while the risk-averse can just try one mini-course.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] Offer a **try-then-credit** path: let them buy one course, and if they like
  it, credit that toward the full bundle purchase. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] **Bake the value into the name/positioning**: cites friend Ron Segal, who
  sells a "how to build a $10,000 website" class for ~$400 — "the value is baked in
  already without even having to market" (like calling it a "$7 burger for $4.99").
  (paraphrase, third-party example)

**On the fame-vs-price principle**
- [2020-04-08] "The more well-known you are the less price matters, and the less
  well-known you are the more price matters." So **get more well-known first** and the
  price will sort itself out. (paraphrase → see quote)
- [2020-04-08] What people find valuable is what they believe makes them money: "if you
  sell me a product I know I can make money on, I will pay you all kinds of money for it."
  Frame outcomes ("how to get your first paid gig," "how I made X selling loops"). People
  think short-term, so a clear near-term financial benefit loosens their wallet.
  (paraphrase)

**On audience being the real asset**
- [2020-04-08] "The audience is everything." Once your audience "falls in love with you,
  they'll buy anything that you make just to support you." Build the audience first — it
  takes time. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] The product can be a decoy for the real goal: "sometimes the course is the
  distraction — it's actually the audience that is the core goal," and the course becomes
  a vehicle for fans to say thank you. (paraphrase → see quote)
- [2020-04-08] Proof from his own book: he never saw himself as a writer but got repeated
  requests, so when he ran the Kickstarter he told his team he wasn't interested unless it
  raised **$20,000** — they "blew past" it and landed **north of $70,000**. (paraphrase)
  [The Futur book campaign, ~2019.]

**On sequencing the sale (relationship before ask)**
- [2020-04-08] Use **microtransactions / a ladder of commitment**: watch a video →
  subscribe → buy the course; each step is a deeper commitment, a better path than
  "here's a video, go buy this." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] "Don't crowd and close on the first date" — at minimum get them on your
  **email list** first; the newsletter doesn't need to be weekly (bi-weekly/monthly is
  fine) and shouldn't sell. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] Make the newsletter **story-driven** and personal (mine your archives —
  the fired-by-a-band stories, dumb/smart decisions); **you** have to be genuinely
  interested in it, or no one else will be. If the newsletter gets good open rates, tell
  those same stories on camera. (paraphrase)

**On the "garage sale" — sell what you already own**
- [2020-04-08] Cites the book *Rework* (37signals / Basecamp founders): "have a garage
  sale" — look at the digital assets already sitting on your hard drive and sell them.
  For a designer that's textures, brushes, templates, presentations, case studies; for a
  drummer, drum loops/samples from past sessions. It's **lower-hanging fruit than writing
  a course** and gets people used to pulling out a credit card. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] The Futur does exactly this: packages **case studies** (because people
  rarely see work at that level) and cleaned-up, templatized **legal/contract forms** in
  Word so buyers don't pay an attorney "$400/hour" — it makes money and builds goodwill.
  (paraphrase)

**On passive income and just starting**
- [2020-04-08] Passive income means revenue "comes in whether or not we show up." Cites
  Dustin Lee (texture packs / illustration kits) who was struck when he "walked out of
  Target having made more money than he spent." Chris says checking the daily revenue
  report now feels the same. (paraphrase, third-party example)
- [2020-04-08] You can hit a $10k goal in a single course launch "in a matter of a couple
  days," not just spread across the year. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-08] Just start with a plan: a simple page collecting emails, a face to the
  name. He's giving "the shortcut" past the trials he took years to learn. (paraphrase)
