## Summary

Design-business consultant Emily Cohen gives blunt advice on spotting and pricing "bad
clients." Her signature rule: count the red flags at the start of an engagement and raise
your fee ~10% per red flag. Red flags she names include clients who keep you on the phone
for hours, don't return emails, bring unexpected stakeholders to meetings, or aren't the
real decision-maker — with the caveat that everyone's red flags are personal (hers is
people who talk slowly and never get to the point). Her closing theme is learning to say
"no": she recommends firing roughly one client a year, and coaches how to end a
relationship diplomatically and honestly (e.g. "we've outgrown each other" or "I'm too
expensive"), rather than hiding behind "I'm too busy." She tells a story of firing an
alcoholic client who years later returned after going to Alcoholics Anonymous. Chris Do
hosts, reacting and running a short role-play. The clip ends with a Vexels sponsor read.

Speaker: **two speakers, unlabeled.** Guest **Emily Cohen** carries essentially all the

role-play ("Emily, can you fire me please?"). Caption garble means some short lines cannot

## Key claims

### Emily Cohen (guest — context only, does NOT train persona)

- [2020-04-14] Rule of thumb: **for every red flag, raise your fee ~10%.** Creatives get
  so involved in and fond of their clients that they wear "rose-tinted glasses" and ignore
  huge red flags staring them in the face at the start of a relationship; instead, count
  the red flags and increase the fee accordingly. (paraphrase; confirmed as Emily Cohen's
  signature rule)
- [2020-04-14] **Red flags are personal / vary by person** — you may be patient with some
  behaviors and impatient with others. Her own red flag: clients who speak really slowly
  and never get to the point; she charges them more because she lacks the patience/time to
  spend hours on the phone on something that could have been said in ten minutes.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-14] **Common red flags:** too many stakeholders, and dealing with someone who
  is not the main decision-maker. Designers should be more aware of these before the
  relationship starts, because you can detect them and protect yourself via the scope of
  work and your fees. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-14] **Learn to say "no."** Saying no "feels so good," and the word has power —
  it's not a bad word. People often soften it ("no, sorry" / "no, because…"); better to
  "just say no, then breathe, then explain." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-14] **Fire roughly one client a year.** If you're doing your positioning job
  well, you don't get too many bad clients in the first place. (paraphrase; the
  "positioning" remark's speaker is uncertain — see below)
- [2020-04-14] **How to fire a client diplomatically:** the best framing is "I think we've
  outgrown each other / I might be too expensive for you — here's someone else." For a
  misbehaving client mid-project: "I want to finish this project, but after this I think
  our relationship is at its end, because I don't work with clients who behave like this."
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-14] **Firing should never be a surprise** — communicate problems throughout so
  the client already knows they're misbehaving; if they only find out at the end, you
  failed to communicate. Typically she waits until the client comes back for the next
  project, then declines. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-14] **Be honest, don't use excuses.** Avoid "I'm too busy" — they'll believe it
  and tell everyone you're too busy. Prefer honesty: "I really loved working with you
  initially, but the relationship deteriorated over these specific things, and I'm not sure
  I'm the best firm for you." Call them on their behavior and recommend they move on.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-14] She'll say **"I'm too expensive," even when it isn't strictly true** — with
  a caveat: if a client pays huge money (e.g. "$200,000 for a logo") but is still a bad
  client, quoting more might get you $300,000, at which point it may be worth working with
  them. So firing a client sometimes yields more money, and sometimes the client comes
  back. (paraphrase; the "$200k logo" figure is a hypothetical, caption-garbled)
- [2020-04-14] **The alcoholic-client story:** she fired a client by telling him "I think
  alcohol's a big influence on your culture… when that's no longer such an influence, I'd
  love to work with you again." (The client's project manager was his ex-bartender and they
  held happy hour at lunch.) He came back two years later, said the comment was so mean and
  made him angry that it pushed him to go to Alcoholics Anonymous, and they then worked
  together well for a long time. (paraphrase; caption garble around names)
