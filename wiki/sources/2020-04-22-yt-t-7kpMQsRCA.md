# Why Your Instagram Carousels Are Being Ignored— Use AIDA technique

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do applies the classic **AIDA** copywriting model (Attention, Interest, Desire→"Details", Action) to Instagram carousels and adds two of his own structural rules ("get them past slide 3", the "magic 8 slides", the breath/comma metaphor for slide breaks). This is a self-contained, named, Chris-attributed framework worth promoting into `wiki/topics/content-strategy/` and (dated) `persona/beliefs.md`. Gate: confirmed Chris-taught (see attribution below), so promotion is clear once synthesized.

## Summary

Chris Do teaches how to structure an Instagram carousel so it doesn't get ignored by the algorithm, using the **AIDA** copywriting framework — **A**ttention, **I**nterest, **D**esire, **A**ction — with one modification: he renames/reworks "Desire" into "give the details." Slide 1 is the magnet/hook (the "clickbait"), built from a formula of type + image; the sub-headline and body build **interest**; the middle slides deliver the **details/value**; and the final slide is a single **action** (a CTA). Two structural rules of his own: you must get viewers **past slide 3** (if momentum breaks there, the algorithm reads it as clickbait and suppresses the post), and you effectively have a **"magic 8" slides** (of a max 10) because the first and last are consumed by hook and CTA. He warns against both extremes — dumping the densest value too early (once the reader has what they need, they leave) and saying nothing at all ("cotton candy"). His slide-breaking tip: use a speaking metaphor — every breath / comma = a new slide — which also multiplies your content ("this is how you can do two [posts] a day").

Speaker: **Chris Do** teaches the full body of the video (first person — "my pro community," "when I used to teach sequential design," "my wife tells me"). The opening ~7 seconds is a framing voiceover by **Ricky** (Futur team member): "Ricky here, back with another video in part three of our four-part video series on Instagram." Ricky's intro is Futur-context framing only and does NOT train the persona; all teaching content is Chris-attributed and confident. **Marty Neumeier** is referenced in a third-party anecdote (not present).

## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona-training)

- [2020-04-22] Structure a carousel on **AIDA**: Attention, Interest, Desire, Action — "otherwise it's AIDA just like it said here." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] **Attention** = grab attention and tell them why they should care; the reason to care is a **benefit**, and "this is about them, not you." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] Anecdote on writing for the reader: he tweeted he wanted to write a book titled *"100 Books That Changed My Life"*; Marty [Neumeier] replied to change it to *"100 Books That'll Change **Your** Life"* — shifting it from being about the author to about the reader. He calls the edit "genius." (paraphrase, third-party anecdote)
- [2020-04-22] For effective headlines, "do a quick search like 'the most effective headlines'" — they involve words like "how to." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] **Interest**: the headline captures attention, the sub-headline and body copy build interest; if you don't deliver more, "I'm out." You have (on Instagram) the slides to keep building on it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] He reworks **Desire** into "**give the details**" — "that's the only difference. Otherwise it's AIDA." (In classic AIDA, Desire = making them imagine their life transformed.) (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] **Action**: give a single, clear thing to do; ideally step-by-step instructions (he uses a direct-mail example: pull it out, open the envelope, put five dollars in, stamp it, drop it in the mailbox by Tuesday — "this is effective direct marketing"). (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] **Slide 1 is the magnet / clickbait** — whatever it takes to pull you in — and it's built from a **formula of type + image**. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] Self-deprecating example: he was "a little lazy" and used all-type slides; his wife told him she couldn't tell what it was, so he added an image — a woman's lip breaking through a piece of paper, all pink ("pink on pink," monotone/duotone), which became his **highest-performing post** because it's provocative and mysterious. The image-to-type combination "really works." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] Designers "are really good at doing [this] for other people [but] don't do a good job for ourselves" — i.e., they fail to sell their own benefit. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] **Rule: get them past slide 3.** "There's momentum being built here. If they can't get beyond slide 3, if it breaks here, I don't think your post is going to perform well. The algorithm will think 'now clickbait,' lost me, out." (paraphrase + quote) He credits discussing this with his Pro community.
- [2020-04-22] Two ways to lose the viewer: (1) you don't tell them anything new, or (2) you tell them **too much** and they get "stuck" on a slide — on Instagram people swipe fast ("1001, 1002"), so a dense slide stalls the flow. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] Mistake he made: putting the densest, most value-packed slide too early — "if they read it all, we're done. You gave me all that I needed to know." Put the juice in the middle and go deep there. (paraphrase, self-critical)
- [2020-04-22] In the value/middle slides you either **educate** (tell me something I didn't know) or **entertain** (make it funny / tell a joke). (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] The Action slide should give people a reason to **engage** — e.g., "you have a question, for the next two hours I'll answer them," which also **generates your next post**. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] **The "magic 8":** you have max 10 slides but effectively lose two (first = hook, last = CTA), so focus on the middle 8. "Think eight. Eight is great." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] **Slide-breaking tip:** use a speaking metaphor from storyboarding — "every time you take a breath, every time you put a comma, new slide." His early posts were "so dense"; one dense slide should have been broken into four. (paraphrase) He ties this to his background: "when I used to teach sequential design."
- [2020-04-22] Breaking dense slides apart is "good news" because it gives you **way more content to share** — "this is how you can do two a day. Don't try to put too much in." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-22] The opposite failure is content that's "like cotton candy… it feels like I'm eating something but there's nothing there." (quote/paraphrase)
