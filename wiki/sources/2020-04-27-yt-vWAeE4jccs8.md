## Summary

A Q&A-style interview (recorded during the early-2020 COVID downturn) in which Chris Do
puts small-creative-firm questions — several submitted by the audience — to **David C.
Baker**, author of *The Business of Expertise* and a longtime consultant to creative firms
(already logged in the repo as an influence). Baker argues that the right size for a firm
is "always smaller than your opportunity" (so you retain the ability to say no and protect
pricing), that in a downturn you should cut the middle-management layer and keep/flex the
core creative "skill players" rather than the reverse, and that selling should be reframed
as *helping* clients — approaching prospects as if you don't need the work, because "whoever
wants it worst is the loser." Chris mostly frames and probes, and admits The Futur itself
fell into the over-hiring "feeding the machine" trap.

**Speaker:** Host **Chris Do** (subject) interviewing guest **David C. Baker (context)**.
Baker does most of the talking; Chris asks the questions and reads audience questions
(e.g., from "Daniel"). Third parties referenced: **Blair Enns** (Baker's *2Bobs* podcast
co-host, whose selling philosophy Baker explicitly channels).

## Key claims

### David C. Baker (context — NOT persona training data)

- [2020-04-27] The right size for a creative practice is a balance between "not too much
  doing and not too much managing." If you want to keep *doing* the creative work, growing
  is a bad idea because it necessarily pushes you toward managing; if you find you enjoy
  managing (and people usually get better at it with practice), then growing is smart.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-27] A firm should always be "smaller than your opportunity" — you need more
  opportunities to say yes than you have capacity to say yes to, because that gap is what
  lets you say no, push back, and retain the integrity (i.e., keep high) of your pricing.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-27] The failure mode: as demand rises, owners hire and expand capacity quickly,
  then get caught in a negative cycle of saying yes to more jobs just to keep people busy —
  Baker calls this "feeding the machine." (paraphrase / named concept)
- [2020-04-27] On downturn layoffs / right-sizing: the instinct is to cut account managers
  and project managers (seen as "not essential") and keep the core creatives — but you
  should do the opposite. Keep the **core role players** and **flex with the skill players**
  (writers, designers, UX people, coders). (paraphrase)
  > Note: transcript wording is slightly tangled here; Baker's larger point is that you
  > protect the roles clients actually buy and treat specialist skill capacity as flexible.
- [2020-04-27] When you grow to five or six people and then add a middle layer, that middle
  layer is the people you're closest to emotionally (they freed you from work you didn't
  enjoy), so it's easy to protect them — but you should "reverse build the pyramid" and pull
  from the bottom, or you end up top-heavy. Cutting managers may mean the owner does more of
  the work again for a time. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-27] On selling in an uncertain environment (explicitly crediting Blair Enns's
  thinking): if you understand selling not as convincing anyone of anything but as
  "predominantly helping your prospective clients get what they need to accomplish," then
  continuing to sell during a crisis doesn't feel rude or tone-deaf — you're just continuing
  to help. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-27] A practical test before writing to or calling a prospect: put an artificial
  constraint on yourself — "I am no longer taking any clients. What would I say if I did NOT
  want this client to hire me?" That leads you down the right path, because the prospect
  hears you instead of smelling desperation, and is actually *more* likely to hire you when
  you signal you don't need to be hired. (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-27] "The more you want something the less you get it" — echoing an idea from a
  Blair-led podcast episode: "whoever wants it worst is the loser." If you want the deal more
  than the prospect does, you have very little leverage. (paraphrase + quote fragment)
- [2020-04-27] On personally coping with fear (e.g., only two months of runway left): Baker's
  own method is to ask "what's the worst that could happen?" — go out of business and get a
  job doing something else — and if you can face that, tell yourself not to worry. If closing
  the business terrifies you, you'll hold on too tightly. He notes he's "not convinced
  everybody should do that." (paraphrase, self-reported personal approach)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**David C. Baker (context):**

- "The right size is a mix between not too much doing and not too much managing."
- "The answer is always smaller than your opportunity... you have to have more opportunities
  to say yes than you have capacity to say yes, because that gap between those two represents
  your ability to say no and to push back and to retain the integrity of your pricing."
- "That's what I just call feeding the machine."
- "You want to keep the core role players and then flex with the skill players."
- "If you understand selling as predominantly helping your prospective clients get what they
  need to accomplish... it doesn't seem so rude and tone-deaf to keep selling."
- "I am no longer taking any clients — what would I say if I did not want this client to hire
  me at this point?"
- "They won't smell this desperation and they're actually more likely to hire you if you give
  off this sense that you don't need to be hired."
- "Whoever wants it worst is the loser."
- "What's the worst that could happen? Go out of business and I go get a job doing something
  else. Could I face that? And if the answer is yes, then I just tell myself don't worry
