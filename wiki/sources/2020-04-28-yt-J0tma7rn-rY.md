## Summary

The positioning/specialization segment of Chris Do's early-2020 interview with **David C.
Baker**. Baker walks through a graphic from his book: creatives start on the
"undifferentiated" end and must walk a path toward more specialization until they reach a
"green zone" — an optimum defined by a certain number of prospects and competitors; walk
too far and you run out of opportunity, not far enough and you stay interchangeable. His
sharpest test: count how many people claim to do the same thing you do — the sweet spot is
roughly **10 to at most 200**, and if everyone in a room of a thousand peers would raise
their hand at your positioning, you've failed. To narrow down, a firm chooses a **vertical**
(an industry) or a **horizontal** (a demographic/target). He adds the "Google-ized"
marketplace point: since geography no longer protects firms, a good specialization signal
is that clients travel to you — of ~20 clients, ideally only 6–7 are local. Chris frames
the questions and closes by summarizing for the audience that willingness to travel to work
with you signals you're specialized enough.

**Speaker:** Host **Chris Do** (subject) interviewing guest **David C. Baker** (context).
Baker does nearly all the substantive talking; Chris sets up the graphic, reads/poses the
tests, and delivers the closing audience takeaway. A hypothetical audience member "Linda"
is used as an example (not a real speaker).

## Key claims

### David C. Baker (context — NOT persona training data)

- [2020-04-28] Most creatives, especially those who start young, begin on the
  "undifferentiated" end. The task is to walk a path toward a more specialized position; it
  "takes courage" and "takes focus." (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-28] There's an optimum: keep walking until you reach the point of the optimum
  number of competitors and prospects. Walk too far and "you run out of opportunity"; don't
  walk far enough and "you're too easily interchangeable." He visualizes it as a light that
  turns green in the sweet-spot zone and red again if you over-specialize. (paraphrase /
  named model — the book gives specific numbers)
- [2020-04-28] **The competitor-count test:** count how many other people say they do the
  same thing you do. If, in a room of a thousand peers, almost everyone would raise their
  hand at your stated positioning, "it failed the test." The target is to have only about
  **ten, at most 200** people who claim the same thing — that makes you "less
  interchangeable." (paraphrase / named test)
- [2020-04-28] Claiming to be a "branding specialist" — or the newer version, a
  "storytelling specialist" — is the classic mistake: thousands would say the same. The
  *category* (design firms, creatives) is not the positioning; **who you do it for** is.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-04-28] **Two axes to narrow down:** a firm chooses either a **vertical** (focus on a
  particular industry) or a **horizontal** (focus on a particular demographic/target).
  Examples: branding for challenger brands climbing quickly toward the top (but not yet at
  the top) = one option; branding for firms that reach the elderly or Millennials = a
  horizontal. (paraphrase / named framework)
- [2020-04-28] **The "Google-ized" marketplace / travel signal:** before Google (late '90s)
  you had a "protected marketplace" — an LA firm didn't fear Phoenix firms. Now the market is
  "Google-ized," so distant firms compete for your clients (and you for theirs). Expertise
  usually lives "a little bit afar," so someone travels in a specialized relationship. Of ~20
  clients, ideally only **6 or 7** are local (reachable in person within ~an hour); the rest
  being far away signals that your expertise is "so essential and irreplaceable that it's
  worth bridging the distance." (paraphrase / named test — the "30-mile test" per Chris's
  setup)

### Chris Do (subject — persona-eligible)

- [2020-04-28] Framing observation: "there's something intrinsic about creative people" that
  makes them resist specialization/narrowing "with all their might," and many who *think*
  they've narrowed down "actually are really far from it." (paraphrase — posed as interview
  framing, but a Chris-attributed view)
- [2020-04-28] Audience summary of Baker's travel point: if someone is willing to go out of
  their way to work with you, it's a good signal you're specialized enough — so if you only
  work with clients within a certain radius of yourself, you may want to ask whether you're
  specialized enough. (paraphrase — Chris restating/endorsing the guest's idea)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**David C. Baker (context):**

- "You want to keep walking until you get to the point where you have the optimum number of
  competitors and prospects. If you keep walking too far you run out of opportunity, but if
  you don't walk far enough then you're too easily interchangeable with other firms."
- "Normally you want to have only ten, maybe at the most 200, people who say they do the same
  thing — that makes you less interchangeable."
- "The category — so many of your listeners would be design firms, creatives — that's the
  category. But who do you do it for? That's what we're talking about."
- "She can either choose to specialize in a vertical — that means she might focus on a
  particular industry — or she might focus horizontally, which means that she focuses on a
  particular demographic or target."
- "Before Google came around in the late '90s you had a protected marketplace... now the world
  became Google-ized, and now Phoenix firms can do work for LA clients."
- "Ideally only six or seven of those clients will be local... their expertise is so essential
  and irreplaceable that it's worth bridging this distance to do it."

**Chris Do (subject):**

- "There's something intrinsic about creative people that goes against their core fiber — they
  resist with all their might — and a lot of people who think they have narrowed down actually
  are really far from it."
- "Did you guys hear that? If somebody's willing to go out of their way to work with you, it's
  a good signal that you are specialized enough... so if you're only working with clients
  within a certain radius of yourself, you may start to think: am I specialized enough?"
