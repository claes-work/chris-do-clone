# Managing Your Team – Should You Let Them Fail?

## Summary

A one-on-one leadership coaching session (inside a small group) in which a Futur coach helps a team leader named **Matthew** who over-functions — he feels "immensely responsible" for his team's outcomes, so he swoops in to save every project and never lets people fail. The coach reframes failure as feedback and the primary way people learn (the "touch the stove" metaphor), then gives a practical de-risking method: hand over only a *portion* of a project, give full context and expected roadblocks, agree on expectations, evaluate afterward, and "expand the rubber band" a little more each time. He distinguishes healthy leader **responsibility** from **over-responsibility** (often rooted in upbringing) that must be released, and redefines responsibility as "our ability to respond." Closes with a pattern-interrupt tactic (pause and ask a second person for their lens) and Greg Gunn's podcast plug.

## Key claims

### Coach (likely Chris Do — attribution uncertain)

- [2020-05-04] Failure is how people learn — it is feedback; if a leader always "saves the day" and removes every roadblock, the team never learns. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] "Touch the stove" metaphor: you can tell someone 500 times not to touch the stove, but they won't understand *why* until they touch it — then they'll never touch it again. Lived consequence teaches what instruction can't. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Leaders carry responsibility in two directions at once — up to the organization and the person above them, and down to the people below — which is why letting someone fail feels so hard. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] De-risking method for delegating: don't throw someone in the deep end; hand over a *portion* of a larger project, give full context, tell them what roadblocks might show up, agree on expectations, and set the rule that if something you left out appears, they come to you and you talk it through. You mitigate the risk rather than eliminate it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Think of delegation as **expansion** — like stretching a rubber band a little more each time; give them a bit more runway and leeway with each pass. It serves them (they grow) and serves you (you can step farther away and focus on the other "plates" you're keeping in the air). (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Managing the team is only *one part* of a leader's job. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Beware black-and-white / extreme thinking ("I'm off the project, it's all you" vs. total hand-holding) — polarized thinking inhibits your brain's ability to generate multiple options. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Pattern interrupt for the urge to swoop in: pause and ask "is there a way to do this differently?" — and if you're stuck, ask someone else for their view. Someone outside your world has a different lens/filter and can see it more clearly. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Distinguish **responsibility** from **over-responsibility**: being responsible as a leader is essential, but an inflated sense of over-responsibility (often shaped by upbringing) is what should be released. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Taking responsibility as a leader means owning mistakes out loud — "I messed up, I want to do that differently next time" — which *models* that behavior for staff. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Redefines the word responsibility as "our ability to respond" — we control our own response and no one else's; we always have control over that. (paraphrase, likely borrowed frame)
- [2020-05-04] Let the team experiment, be challenged, make mistakes, and fail — but at a *manageable scale* ("at a level that I want to start the business... don't close the doors"), i.e. fail small, not fatally. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Growth requires getting "comfortable with that discomfort." (paraphrase)

### Matthew (coachee — context only, NOT the subject)

- [2020-05-04] Feels immensely responsible for the team and its outcomes; if someone performs poorly, "that's on me" and he has to report it upward to "the big guy." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Defensively takes on more work so his people can't fail — acts as a "savior" who swoops in to fill any gap. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Wants his team "to be the best version of themselves." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-04] Take-away he commits to: empower people to take more responsibility and be okay with them failing, without feeling guilty or over-responsible for it. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Coach (likely Chris Do — attribution uncertain):**

- "When is it okay to let someone fall and fail? Because that's how we learn. That's feedback."

- "You think about it as expansion — it's like, how much can you expand that rubber band a little bit more each time? Give them a little more runway, a little more leeway."
