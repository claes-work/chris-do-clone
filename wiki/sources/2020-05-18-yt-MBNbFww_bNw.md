## Summary

Guest presentation on how to "create" (train) your ideal client during the gap between
signing and the first real working session. The speaker argues that unprepared clients on
kickoff calls create wasted time, scope creep, and lost profitability, and that this gap
should be filled with (1) automated training assets — one or a few videos teaching the
client how to work with you, what to expect, and how to avoid slowdowns; (2) warm-up
assignments/checklists so the client does real prep work before any call ("on the same page,
not just the same book"); and (3) live coaching/consulting for the customization that
training can't cover. He frames these as three ingredients whose proportions ("the pie")
shift over time as recurring questions get productized into new training. Closes with a plug
for The Futur's project-management course and pitch kit.

## Key claims

### Greg Hickman (context — guest) — `attribution: uncertain`

- [2020-05-18] You can deliberately "create great clients" — training clients how to be good
  clients is a real, teachable thing. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-18] The costly inefficiency lives in the gap between when a client pays and when
  meaningful work starts; unprepared clients turn kickoff into you dictating what you need,
  sometimes across two or three calls — wasted time that costs money and invites scope creep.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-18] Automate the onboarding with reusable training assets (one or a few videos)
  covering how you work, what to expect, and what slows down turnaround — rather than
  repeating it live on every call, which wastes both parties' time. Analogy: airline safety
  briefings are recorded/automated so the same message runs reliably on every flight.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-18] Automating the process is not about removing humans; it's about making the
  human touchpoints more meaningful and intimate, and turning staff into "super employees"
  who focus on a few things instead of everything. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-18] Warm the client up with a pre-call assignment or checklist so they arrive with
  momentum — e.g., a buyer-persona worksheet for a branding engagement. Even if you scrap all
  of their work, they arrive a better, more prepared client. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-18] Cites a client, "Ilsa," with a web design firm who used this onboarding process
  and cut fulfillment from a three-week turnaround to a three-day turnaround, purely from work
  the client did up front. (paraphrase; name spelling uncertain from caption — "Ilsa")
- [2020-05-18] References a mentor, "Brad Martin," whose firm brings clients to the office for
  a two-day intensive and uses an almost vlog-style video to set expectations (where to go,
  food laid out, kickoff time) — a "virtual experience" so clients know what to expect.
  (paraphrase; both name and details caption-garbled/uncertain)
- [2020-05-18] Three ingredients move a client to the end result: (1) training (linear,
  sequential journey to a clear destination), (2) modular training plus live prescription/
  coaching for their specific situation, and (3) initial done-for-you implementation. The
  training should be a clear, end-to-end, sequential path. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-18] Don't spend seven months mapping a program before selling it — sell it to a
  small group at a reasonable fee as an alpha/beta, and they'll tell you exactly what the next
  version needs. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-18] Generic "just slam it into a six-week course" models break the moment an agency
  owner has a question about their unique situation — which is why consultation matters and why
  the ratio of the three ingredients ("the pie") differs per person and shifts over iterations.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-18] When you answer the same question repeatedly on coaching calls, productize it:
  make a video and send the link first. Over time this shrinks the coaching "slice," and you
  choose how involved you stay — some clients like being in the process. (paraphrase)
