# How To Give Feedback To Teams That Empower & Engage Creatives

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do lays out a named, self-contained, repeatable feedback protocol ("feel → why → what specifically → open discussion → recommendation → negotiate → confirm next actions") plus a silence-training drill. This is a distinct creative-direction framework worth promoting into `wiki/topics/design-craft/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated). Attribution is clean first-person Chris, so the gate is met.

## Summary

Chris Do explains how he gives feedback to his creative team in a way that develops them instead of overriding them. Rather than dictating the fix, he names how the work makes him *feel*, diagnoses what specifically is causing that feeling, then hands the problem back to the designer to solve — only making his own recommendation if they hit a wall, and even then leaving room for negotiation. He ties this to a discipline of silence (asking a question then staying quiet for 3–5+ seconds to give people time to think) and to scaling: if you insist on being the only creative genius in the room, you can't grow beyond your own hours and abilities.

Speaker: single-speaker monologue by **Chris Do** (first person — "when I'm working with my team and I review the work," addresses his designer "Ben," "I have two resources I made"). No guest or co-host speaks. Attribution is unambiguous.

## Key claims

All claims are **Chris Do**, single speaker.

- [2020-05-22] His feedback sequence: (1) express how he *feels* about the work ("I'm feeling a little bit confused"); (2) identify what *specifically* is causing that feeling (e.g. the information hierarchy doesn't line up); (3) ask the designer what *they* think and how they'd fix it, before he offers his own view; (4) only if they hit a wall, make a recommendation; (5) leave room for discussion/negotiation since it can be subjective; (6) confirm the next actions. (paraphrase — the core framework)
- [2020-05-22] He leads with feeling, not verdict — "first I express how I feel... why am I feeling the way that I'm feeling" — because the felt reaction is the honest data point, and the diagnosis follows from it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-22] He opens the fix to the designer first specifically "because I don't want to rob them of their creativity... their problem-solving abilities." His job is to *probe* so they see their own work from a different angle; more angles yield more creative outputs. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-22] The director's role is to "facilitate better ideas," not supply them. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-22] Even his own recommendation is offered as a proposal open to challenge ("what do you think about that, Ben?") — he "always leaves space" and tries not to talk back-to-back with no room for someone to interject. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-22] Silence is a tool: ask a question, then stay silent, because for some people it takes an extra five seconds to complete a thought before they'll say it out loud. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-22] Silence drill for people who compulsively fill dead air: after you ask a question, stay quiet for 3 seconds, then practice 5 seconds, then longer — get progressively more comfortable with silence. (paraphrase — actionable exercise)
- [2020-05-22] Giving people (especially when you're the intimidating person, or when it's a client) the space and time to process makes them feel safe to jump in; without it they won't speak "out of turn" for fear of sounding dumb. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-22] You can explicitly name the pause to reduce pressure — e.g. "I know I just asked you something pretty challenging and I can see you thinking, that's okay, you can take some time... if we can't figure it out now, we can come back later." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-22] The scaling rationale: "you can't be the only creative genius in the room, because... you don't scale, you can't grow beyond yourself" — you're limited to your own hours, days, and abilities. Teaching the team to problem-solve "multiplies your team" and "multiplies your efforts." (paraphrase — the WHY behind the method)
- [2020-05-22] Good direction (asking good questions + leaving space) also "removes the friction that normally happens in communication and relationships between clients and your team." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-22] Discussion of the fix should be anchored "relative to the brief and the goals," not to personal taste alone. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All quotes are **Chris Do**:

- "So first I express how I feel... why am I feeling the way that I'm feeling. I'm feeling confused because the hierarchy of the information doesn't line up."
- "Before I make the recommendation of what I would do, I would like to open it up to challenge their thinking."
- "I don't want to rob them of their creativity, I don't want to rob them of their problem-solving abilities. All I want to do is probe them so that they can look at their own work from a different angle."
- "All I'm doing there is trying to facilitate better ideas."
- "I always leave space and I try not to talk back to back where there's no room for somebody to interject. I ask a question and then I stay silent."
- "Every time you say something or ask a question, stop and be silent for three seconds, then try and be quiet for five seconds and then even longer."
- "You can't be the only creative genius in the room, because guess what — you don't scale, you can't grow beyond yourself and you are limited to the hours and the days and your abilities."
- "If you can learn to ask good questions, to give good direction, and then leave space for others so that they can practice and flex their creative problem-solving muscles... you're multiplying your team, you're multiplying your efforts."
