# Original Is Just A Remix of Old Ideas Presented In A New Way

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do teaches a self-contained, repeatable creative method here ("new = old, shown in a new way"; cross-pollinate from an adjacent field; the "big small" reframe trick; the macro-lens assignment). It sharpens his signature anti-originality stance into concrete generative techniques and a redefinition of the creative goal ("new to *this audience*"). Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/design-craft/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated), pending it's clearly Chris-attributed — which it is.

## Summary

Chris Do reframes "new" as a trap and offers a more useful, hittable target: work that is **new to a specific audience**, not never-before-seen. Almost nothing is truly original — everything gets remixed, and technology just makes remixing easier and more creative. He argues creators should stop chasing originality and instead (1) understand who the audience is, (2) go to an adjacent field where that audience isn't looking and bring an idea back ("the secret to originality is to hide your sources"), and (3) apply reframing tricks that force people to notice the overlooked. He illustrates with story archetypes (Romeo and Juliet retold endlessly with the variables changed), the "big small" scale-flip trick, and a macro-lens assignment he gave his students.

Speaker: teaching monologue by **Chris Do** (first person — he is the "somebody like me comes along and says… I've seen this already," references "one of the assignments I would give my students"). A student/interlocutor voices short questions and pushback ("we are people who study design… you have more exposure than we do"), but no substantive claim in the persona-training material comes from anyone but Chris. ## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-05-25] "New" is relative to the viewer: something feels new only because *you* haven't seen it. A more experienced observer has already seen it, so "never-before-seen" always carries a hidden asterisk — "the asterisk is *to you*." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-25] The realistic, hittable creative goal is not "new" but "**new to the audience**" — which makes the target (the bull's-eye) much clearer, because you first have to understand who that audience is and what they're looking at all the time. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-25] Very few things are truly never-before-seen; "everything just gets remixed," and technology lets us remix more creatively — "a new application of an old idea" is new enough for its audience. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-25] His own "new" ideas aren't original either: "everything I know is from a book or a thing I read or watched or somebody else coached me on, and most of my new ideas are terrible." All he's doing is presenting information the listener believes to be new. (quote / self-critical)
- [2020-05-25] Method — cross-pollinate from an adjacent field: audiences gather at a "creative watering hole" all looking at the same "old" stuff; go somewhere else, bring an outside idea back, and to them it reads as new. "If you're in fashion, look at architecture; if you're in photography, look at cinema; if you're in cinema, look at musicals or plays. All you do is go outside." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-05-25] Cites an unattributed quote: "the secret to originality is to hide your sources." His gloss: "if you want to do something new, go where nobody's looking, bring it back to this world, they get excited." (quote + paraphrase)
- [2020-05-25] Audiences aren't actually looking for new — "we're looking for familiar but presented differently." Proven story ideas get repeated because we've figured out what works; Romeo and Juliet is retold endlessly with "the variables changed" (jock vs. artist, poor vs. rich, rival businesses). (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-25] Reframing trick — "the big small": take something big and make it small (miniature sets that fit in a hand) or something small and make it big (human-sized sculptures of tiny objects). It "forces us to look at something in a way we've never seen before." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-25] We move through the world largely oblivious because processing every detail would be too taxing on the brain — you'd "fall over in fatigue." Creative work reveals what people habitually miss (the crack in the sidewalk that looks like a face). (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-25] Student assignment he used to give: get a macro lens and shoot something nobody pays attention to — the backside of a keyboard, cracks between things, the printing on the bottom of a package. "That's how you discover the new in the old." (paraphrase + quote)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All quotes are **Chris Do**:

- "Everything I know is from a book or a thing I read or watched or somebody else coached me on, and most of my new ideas are terrible. All I'm doing is presenting to you information that you believe to be new."
- "There's very few things that are never-before-seen because everything just gets remixed. It really does — and technology allows us to remix more creatively now."
- "If you're in fashion, look at architecture. If you're in photography, look at cinema. If you're in cinema, look at musicals or plays. All you do is go outside."
- "There's a quote — I don't know who said this — but the secret to originality is to hide your sources. That's all it is. So if you want to do something new, go where nobody's looking, bring it back to this world, they get excited."
- "This idea of new is just showing me old… show me something old in a new way. That's all it is, man."
- "We're not really looking for new, we're looking for familiar but presented differently."
- "[Romeo and Juliet] just told over and over again… they just changed the variables."
- "Show me something that I don't think about — and that's how you discover the new in the old."
