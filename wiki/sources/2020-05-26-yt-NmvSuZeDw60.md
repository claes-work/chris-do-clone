## Summary

A first-person guide by Matthew Encina on speaking more confidently in client meetings
and conversations, aimed at people who freeze up or feel they must "sound smart." He
frames three lessons: (1) slow down and sync your speech to your thinking, using
deliberate pauses and 3+ seconds of silence before responding; (2) you don't need to
have all the answers — it's liberating to say "I don't know" or "I'd like to collect my
thoughts"; (3) be genuinely curious and ask more questions rather than trying to
perform. He recommends the book *The Coaching Habit* (Michael Bungay Stanier) and
Celeste Headlee's TED talk on how to have a good conversation.

## Key claims

### Matthew Encina (NOT the subject — context only)

- [2020-05-26] Early in client work he felt he was "searching for the right words to
  sound smart," which had the opposite effect; he was confident in his design craft but
  struggled to articulate thoughts in real-time conversation. (paraphrase, biography of Encina)
- [2020-05-26] Lesson 1 — Slow down / get in sync: the mouth doesn't work as fast as a
  racing brain; he deliberately slowed his cadence, speaks in small bursts without
  fillers, and uses pauses to collect thoughts and breathe. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-26] Allow silence: resist responding right away; count "a full three seconds
  or more" before responding, which lets conversations go deeper as everyone completes
  their thinking. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-26] Lesson 2 — You don't need all the answers: saying "I don't know" was one
  of the most liberating things he learned; the pressure to always respond is
  self-imposed. Any job requiring critical thinking takes time to process. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-26] Practical move: say "I'm still not quite sure yet, I'd like to collect my
  thoughts," sit in silence, then re-enter with "I think I've got my thoughts together,
  I'd love to share them." Transparency about processing in real time creates a safe
  space for others. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-26] Bigger jobs bring more complex problems; no one expects an on-the-spot
  solution, because "if the answers were that easy the problem would have been solved and
  they probably wouldn't need your help." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-26] Lesson 3 — Be genuinely curious, ask more questions: he was a
  "wallflower" who felt others were better at talking; the shift was to actively listen
  and ask rather than answer, removing the pressure to sound smart. (paraphrase, biography of Encina)
- [2020-05-26] Asking "why, how, and what" questions invites the other person to explore
  possibilities; helping people arrive at their own conclusions and find clarity is "the
  most valuable thing you can do for anyone you're helping." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-26] Practice: when you feel the urge to answer, respond with a question that
  digs deeper into what they just said; drop your agenda, be generous and curious. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-26] Recommends *The Coaching Habit* by Michael Bungay Stanier (a recent Futur
  podcast guest) and Celeste Headlee's TED talk "How to have a good conversation" (her 10
  rules). (paraphrase, resource recommendation)
