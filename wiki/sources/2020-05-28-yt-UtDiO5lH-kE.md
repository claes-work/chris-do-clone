# Why Working Harder Isn't Getting You Closer To Your Goals

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do delivers a self-contained mindset framework here: **effort should be judged by results, not by hours; extra time that produces no measurable difference in outcome is time spent foolishly.** Bundles several linked ideas (opportunity cost of commitment, Parkinson's Law, "two intentions" choice, memento mori) that extend his work-on-yourself / leverage material. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/mindset/` and dated entries in `persona/beliefs.md` — gated on attribution, which is clear here (Chris is the coach).

## Summary

A coaching conversation in which Chris Do challenges a team member (Ricky, a designer) who believes more time and more effort make his work more valuable. Chris's core argument: if two outputs produce the **same result** — same views, same sales, same praise — the one that took less time is the better choice, and pouring extra hours into something whose outcome is unchanged is "spending your time foolishly." He reframes work around intentionality (every "yes" is also a "no" to something else), warns that giving people more time than a task needs only makes them fill it (Parkinson's Law), and grounds it all in a memento-mori note about finite time. He distinguishes doing work to **get the job done** from doing it to **feel good / be right**, and insists results are what count.

Speaker: **multi-speaker coaching dialogue.** The teaching voice throughout — the mentor delivering the lessons ("we have to be more intentional with how we live our life"; "this is a genius thing, Ricky") — is **Chris Do** (first person, addressing his team). The person being coached is **Ricky**, a designer on the team who defends the value of extra effort/time. A third team member, **Jonas** (caption spelling; likely "Jonah"), is referenced in an example but does not speak. Only Chris-attributed material trains the persona; Ricky's lines are captured as the counterpoint, not as Chris's views.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (coach)

- [2020-05-28] Every commitment carries an opportunity cost: "everything you agreed to do means you're also agreeing not to do something else." Getting clear on that changes your life. (paraphrase / near-quote)
- [2020-05-28] The beauty and production value of a piece has very little correlation with how well it performs — a gorgeous piece on an abstract subject nobody searches for won't get watched. (paraphrase — re: YouTube/content)
- [2020-05-28] Ideal is "a lot of effort and a lot of result" — he doesn't mind maximum effort for maximum result. But when results are identical, the smaller effort is the better choice. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-28] If two outputs earn the exact same outcome — same sales, same praise — but one took a minute and one took ten years, choosing the ten-year approach is the wrong call; you should choose the faster one "every time." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-28] Making the extra effort when the result is the same is "self-indulgent" — it's for yourself, not the audience, "because nobody else cares." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-28] Living intentionally means being deliberate with limited time; "we have to be more intentional with how we live our life." (quote fragment / paraphrase)
- [2020-05-28] Parkinson's Law, in his words: never give someone more time than they need, "because you'll take up all the time to do the same thing." Illustrated with two team members told to make a video that looks a certain way and hits 10,000 views — Jonas finishes in two days and frees time for personal projects, while the slower person grinds two months on the identical result. (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-28] Spending extra time on the "same thing" instead of improving yourself (gym, reading the book he recommended) means you "didn't do anything to improve your status in your own personal or professional life" — because it "felt right." Acting on feelings over outcomes is the wrong operating mode. (paraphrase, coaching-critical)
- [2020-05-28] There are two intentions behind any work: (1) get the job done, or (2) to feel good — "to be right, to be smart, to be popular, to be comfortable." Choose one. He almost always chooses get-the-job-done; "anything less is insane to me." (paraphrase, near-quote list)
- [2020-05-28] Memento mori: we have a finite number of breaths and an expiration date set ahead of time; "the minute you were born your body was beginning to die." Don't tell yourself you have tomorrow, next year, to learn these lessons — that's "pissing away good time." (paraphrase, quote fragments)
- [2020-05-28] Restates his results principle ("seven and eight, the results, [is] what counts"): putting extra time into something that has **no measurable result** in terms of the outcome is spending your time foolishly. (paraphrase)

### Ricky (being coached — NOT persona training data)

- [2020-05-28] Believes he'd "accept the work more" / value it more knowing it took more effort and time. (paraphrase — the position Chris pushes back on)
- [2020-05-28] Concedes the extra effort is "for me," "probably" self-indulgent, and that "the only thing that matters [is] results." (paraphrase)
- [2020-05-28] Argues people inherently take all available time regardless, and that he'd happily stay late to finish. (paraphrase)
