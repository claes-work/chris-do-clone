# Make A Case Study That Gets Client Work

★ **L3-candidate** — This is a named, self-contained framework (Chris Do's case-study "secret formula": title → visuals → summary → body, plus the StoryBrand hero/guide framing applied to case-study storytelling). Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/content-strategy/` or `wiki/topics/design-craft/` and into `persona/beliefs.md` (dated). Attribution is clean first-person Chris, so it is safe to promote.

## Summary

An excerpt from The Futur's "Show Your Work" webinar. Chris Do teaches how designers should build case studies (and portfolios) that actually win client work or jobs. He defines a case study as a detailed examination of the creative process and results that shows a **causal relationship between effort and impact** and tells a story. He gives a four-part "secret formula" — title, visuals, summary, body — and stresses that the **results/impact** section matters most, because clients don't always have good taste and you don't want to be judged on aesthetics alone. He applies Donald Miller's *StoryBrand*: the **client is the hero, you are the guide** (Obi-Wan, not Luke). He closes by urging designers to capture process visuals (people in action, sketches, wireframes, post-its, whiteboards) so the case study has a middle, not just a before-and-after.

Speaker: single-speaker monologue by **Chris Do** (first person — "early on my career," "one of my old clients," "we spent maybe 30 seconds reviewing the portfolios of the people that applied for design positions at blind"). Blind is Chris Do's studio. No co-host or guest speaks. Audience members named in the webinar chat (e.g., "Jamie") are quoted only as third-party chat responses, not persona material.

## The case-study "secret formula" (framework)

Structure every case study as: **Title → Visuals → Summary → Body.**

1. **Title** — hook the reader; don't just name the client (your clients aren't as famous as Coca-Cola or MGM). Ignite curiosity while giving enough info to decide if it's worth a click. Example: instead of "Packaging for Beauty by Earth," use "How we helped a beauty brand grow from the garage to \$10M+ in sales." Like YouTube clickbait, "except on the other side there's actual value."
2. **Summary** — written for busy people (reviewers spend ~30 seconds to a few minutes). Split into three parts:
   - **Mission / challenge** — framed from the *client's* point of view (they want to increase revenue / launch a brand / improve click-through), not "the client asked me to build a logo."
   - **Outcome** — what you actually did (designed a website, built a logo, wrote a campaign).
   - **Results / impact** — the most important part: the measurable effect on the client's business.
3. **Body** — should read like a story (StoryBrand: client = hero, you = guide). This is where designers struggle most.
4. **Visuals** — must be "badass," but critically must also document the **process/middle** (see below), not just the final deliverable.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-06-08] A case study is "a detailed examination of the creative process and the results of a specific project"; what makes it different from portfolio work is that it shows a **causal relationship between effort and impact** and it tells a story. (paraphrase; "causal relationship between effort and impact" is a near-quote)
- [2020-06-08] Case studies should live primarily on **your website**, and also in a **capabilities deck**. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-08] A **capabilities deck** is a document with a couple of case studies plus an outline of your services — something meaningful to send a prospect who is "fishing for information" and asking for a proposal before you've even discussed the work, needs, or budget. It's not a full-blown proposal. (paraphrase) — *NEW artifact/concept for the corpus.*
- [2020-06-08] People read case studies to see results, what happened during the job, and — most importantly — the **outcomes / impact on the client's business**; they also want to verify your **expertise**, which helps **de-risk the project** for them. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-08] Big agencies' case studies all follow the same title/visuals/summary/body formula. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-08] Titling a case study by the client's name is weak because your clients usually aren't famous; the title should hook and create curiosity, like clickbait but with real value behind it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-08] Write the summary for busy people: reviewers spend ~30 seconds skimming portfolios (he cites reviewing applicants for design positions at Blind), so front-load the content browsers need. (paraphrase; self/company-reported)
- [2020-06-08] Frame the mission/challenge from the **client's point of view** (increase revenue, launch a brand, improve click-through), going deeper than "the client asked me to build a logo." (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-08] The **results/impact** section is the most important; without a measurable result, the case study gets judged only on aesthetics — and "clients don't always have the best taste," which is bad news. (paraphrase; "clients don't always have the best taste" is a near-quote)
- [2020-06-08] Applies Donald Miller's *StoryBrand*: **your client is the hero, you are the guide.** In the Star Wars analogy, the client is Luke Skywalker and the designer is Obi-Wan Kenobi. The point of the project's "saga" isn't finishing the project correctly — it's helping the client get where they want to go. (paraphrase; explicit attribution to *StoryBrand* by Donald Miller) — *NEW named source/framework for the corpus.*
- [2020-06-08] Audiences don't care whether Luke masters a specific Force trick or uses the right lightsaber — they care whether he wins the quest; likewise, focus the case-study story on the impact, not the craft minutiae. "There's nothing worse than reading a case study about kerning." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-06-08] Most case studies jump from challenge straight to outcome/impact and skip the **middle** — the process of working with the client. Capture process visuals so the story has a middle. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-08] Process-visual ideas: shoot people in action (take photos even in a discovery meeting / on Zoom — "you never know, you might use them later"); save all sketches, wireframes, notes, post-its, whiteboards, and reference materials ("these are all golden"). (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-08] Final-deliverable visuals: show logos **in use** (large and small scale, animated), UI/UX graphics, video walkthroughs for interior design, packaging in use — and also show the design **isolated** on a plain white or black background so its beauty is visible. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-08] Early in his career he responded to a freelance opportunity by sending a PDF of logos with no explanations, and felt lost ("I don't know what I'm doing man") — the origin motivation for teaching case studies. (paraphrase; self-reported biographical anecdote)
- [2020-06-08] The full "Show Your Work" webinar (in which he reviewed student case studies) is sold as "The Complete Case Study" on The Futur's website; a free version was promised "in the next few weeks." (paraphrase; product mention)
