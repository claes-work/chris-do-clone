## Summary

A whiteboard teaching session (filmed in-studio during COVID, June 2020) in which Chris Do teaches "how to learn anything, for creatives." The format is experimental: four Futur team members plus a live Zoom audience participate, making it an interactive virtual workshop. Chris develops the session in two halves. The first half is theory: framed around an Alvin Toffler quote (learn / unlearn / relearn), he lays out three essential skills for 21st-century education — self-learning, critical thinking, and articulation — and argues that traditional four-year brick-and-mortar education may be less essential than assumed for creatives. He then introduces a model distinguishing **input (consumption)** from **output (implementation)**, arguing output matters more, and breaks output into a cycle: high-quality input → reflect → implement → share (teach). The second half is applied: using his 14-year-old son's art-school portfolio (Basquiat-, Lichtenstein-, and other-influenced pieces) as the case study, Chris teaches the **"Five Ingredients"** method — deconstruct any work you want to learn to reproduce into ~five discrete, observable, *repeatable* attributes that sit in the sweet spot between "too specific" and "too broad." The live audience practices on reference images (a Basquiat, a Star Wars Star Destroyer render, an illustration, a Blade Runner 2049 frame), and Chris models how to translate observations into precise, reproducible creative direction (light quality from shadows, color grading, composition).

## Key claims

All claims below are paraphrased from Chris Do (host, solo teaching), dated 2020-06-09, unless attributed otherwise.

- **The illiterate of the 21st century are those who cannot learn, unlearn, and relearn.** Chris quotes Alvin Toffler and builds the whole session on this cycle: learning should never stop; it runs in a loop. (2020-06-09) [Toffler quote, attributed]
- **We live in a time that is "information rich and time poor."** Information has been democratized and is mostly free; your ability to gather, process, and learn determines how far you go. (2020-06-09)
- **Don't get stuck on the first thing you learn.** When a new source conflicts with an earlier one, resolve it by forming an informed opinion — compare, contrast, then relearn and keep testing the hypothesis. (2020-06-09)
- **The three essential skills for 21st-century education are: (1) self-learning, (2) critical thinking, (3) articulation.** Chris presents these as an alternative blueprint to expensive traditional tuition, especially relevant to homeschooling and the COVID shift to distance learning. (2020-06-09)
- **Self-learning sources exist everywhere:** books, YouTube, courses, niche blogs/websites, Reddit (flagged as "a dangerous place to learn"), Instagram, podcasts (valued because passive/multitaskable), trial and error, libraries. Mentorship is deliberately excluded here because it's not self-directed and not everyone can afford it. (2020-06-09)
- **Asking the right question is ~90% of the work.** In the "search age" you must learn to phrase questions well: wrong question → wrong answers → wrong conclusion. Curiosity is the starting point of all learning. (2020-06-09)
- **Critical thinking = "syntopical" reading** (he cautiously names it, admitting he's still learning the term): read many sources on the same subject, compare and contrast, debate and reason, look for evidence and correlations, and search for verifiable objective truth. Intelligence is pattern-matching / connecting dots. (2020-06-09)
- **Articulation need not be verbal.** It's expressing an idea in some form (spoken, written, design, animation, pottery, dance, song); words and writing are just the most natural. He cites a writer's line "I write entirely to figure out what it is that I know" — you don't know what you know until you express it. (2020-06-09) [writer quote, attribution uncertain — Chris says the editing team will identify the source]
- **Wisdom = equal parts experience + reflection.** Chris attributes this to Aristotle (via Brian Tracy's *The Power of Self-Confidence*), hedging on the exact attribution. Without trying things you have no experience to reflect on. "The more you try, the less you have to fear." (2020-06-09) [attributed to Aristotle/Brian Tracy, attribution uncertain by Chris's own admission]
- **Output matters more than input.** Consumption (reading, watching, listening) is "input"; implementation (making something) is "output." Learning without application is "the illusion of knowledge" / "intellectually self-gratifying." Analogy: he watches many cooking shows but can't cook a single dish. (2020-06-09)
- **The output cycle has components: high-quality input → reflect → implement → share.** (2020-06-09)
  - **Quality of input matters:** single-task, remove distractions (phone notifications, multiple books, background TV/music). Focus raises input quality.
  - **Reflect** by asking: "What was most useful to me? How might I apply this in my life and work? Where else have I seen this idea?" Write it down.
  - **Implement:** turn thought into action; you can't learn by watching alone. This cements the knowledge.
  - **Share/teach:** being forced to articulate to someone else drives learning deepest ("goes deep down the learning pyramid"). He swaps the intimidating word "teaching" for "share."
- **"When one teaches, two learn"** — the teacher gains clarity through articulation and the student/audience learns. Sharing formats scale from a tweet → blog/Medium/LinkedIn article → ebook → book → encyclopedia, or a one-minute video upward. Condensing to a tweet tests real understanding: "if you can't explain it simply, you just don't know it." (2020-06-09)
- **Love being wrong.** Putting out an idea and later being corrected is a timestamp of what you knew then; fear of looking dumb stops creatives from sharing. Chris references Gary Vaynerchuk's harsher version ("fall in love with sucking / effing up"). (2020-06-09) [Gary Vaynerchuk attributed]
- **The Five Ingredients method:** to learn to reproduce any work, deconstruct it into ~five discrete, observable attributes. Zoom in until you can isolate one thing at a time; find the balance between "too specific" (e.g. "a skull with horns") and "too broad" (e.g. "it has words"). The sweet spot is the middle. He teaches his son this instead of answering "what should I do?" — "go back, do your five ingredients." (2020-06-09)
- **"You can't draw what you don't know / you don't know what you haven't seen" — learning is learning to see.** Chris attributes this to Niels Lindstrom (his former lettering teacher, who taught a lettering class for The Futur). He connects it to Seth Godin's *This Is Marketing*: "in order to be seen you must first learn to see," and argues it applies well beyond art — e.g. UX design requires seeing/knowing the user. (2020-06-09) [Niels Lindstrom and Seth Godin attributed]
- **Good observations must be repeatable and extrapolatable.** A useful deconstruction lets you predict/create the *next* frame in the same universe, not just describe the exact image. Test observations by asking whether you could draw/paint/shoot a new scene from them. This is the skill an art/creative director needs to lead a team. (2020-06-09)
- **Reading shadows reveals the light** (taught via the Star Destroyer example): hard-edged shadows = linear/hard light (like the sun); soft/diffused = area/spot light; multiple shadows = multiple sources; color in the shadow = color in the light — unless heavily retouched. Once you train your eye you can diagram a photo's lighting. (2020-06-09)
- **Precise language enables reproduction** (Blade Runner 2049 color example): describing a frame precisely — "monochromatic, warm tobacco tint in the highlights, cyan in the crushed mid-tones and shadows" — lets a colorist reproduce the look "in the blind" and save it as a preset. Precision of language is a filter you can apply to new work. (2020-06-09)
- **"What you see is good enough for now."** For parents and beginners learning a new art form, you don't need art-historian precision; you're approaching the master, not claiming to be the master. Give yourself permission to fail. (2020-06-09)
- **Objective truth is a direction, not a day-one requirement.** (Answering audience member Adelie/"Emily" Hanson on how many sources before reaching objective truth.) Don't wait to reach objective truth before entertaining ideas; learn, and when a source challenges you, decide whether to unlearn/relearn, keep going until you feel you've exhausted it, but never stop sharing and reflecting. Go at least deep enough on one source — read the whole book / watch the full video, take notes, reflect — not just the cover and table of contents. (2020-06-09)

Audience-contributed points Chris endorsed (not persona voice):
- **Matthew** proposed that failure/new data fits all three education pillars — new information that supersedes the old means you must let go of the old idea (as scientists do). Chris affirmed and built on it. (attribution: Matthew, audience)
- **Alec** raised trial and error; his group deconstructed an Ash Thorpe Star Destroyer render down to lighting/shadow angles. (attribution: Alec, audience)
- **Cara/Kara** deconstructed a Moxie Sozo illustrator's cross-hatched black-and-white work by zooming into small details. (attribution: Cara, audience)
- An audience member noted teaching macro-photography students to "describe things as if they were visually blind" as a deconstruction technique. (attribution: uncertain, audience)
- **Austin's** group deconstructed a Blade Runner 2049 frame into: futurism, barren, loneliness, desaturated, seeking. (attribution: Austin, audience)
