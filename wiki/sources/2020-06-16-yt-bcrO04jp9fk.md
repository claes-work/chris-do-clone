## Summary

Matthew Encina explains, from the perspective of someone who *hires* freelancers at
Blind/The Futur, how freelancers should think about what to charge. He walks through three
billing structures (hourly, day rate, per-project), then a talent/rate ladder (junior →
mid → senior), a supply-and-demand argument for specializing over generalizing (specialists
face little competition and can command high rates; generalists on global marketplaces like
Fiverr are in a "race to the bottom"), and finally a hiring-priorities framework lifted from
Joey Korenman's book: reliability and talent+personality matter most, and **rate is the
least important factor** to him when deciding whom to re-hire.

Speaker: single-speaker monologue by **Matthew Encina** (first person — "the rates that I
pay freelancers," "my expectations," "the people I keep bringing back"). Chris Do is not
present. Joey Korenman ("Joey Corman" in the captions) is cited as the source of the
reliability/talent/personality slide.

## Key claims

### Matthew Encina (host — NOT the subject; does not train persona)

**Three ways to charge:**
- [2020-06-16] **Hourly** is useful when you don't know how long a task will take, but it is
  "not really the preferred way to go" — it's just a way to start. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-16] **Day rate** is what he did in the motion-design industry and is very common;
  the upside is a guaranteed amount for every booked day. His example: booked for a week
  (five days) = **$3,000** guaranteed (i.e. ~$600/day). The downside: if you're not careful
  you end up doing "a lot of unpaid overtime." (paraphrase; caption dollar figure $3,000/week)
- [2020-06-16] **Per-project (fixed fee)** is "fantastic" because the fee is fixed to
  deliverables and a set number of revision rounds — flexible for the freelancer (a logo that
  takes an hour still collects the full fee) and predictable for the client. The danger: if
  you're not careful you fall into "unlimited revision cycles." (paraphrase)

**Talent/rate ladder (what he expects at each level):**
- [2020-06-16] **Junior:** knows the software very well; strong portfolio with at least five
  examples showing consistency. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-16] **Mid-range:** everything above, plus very fast execution and iteration —
  told something once at the start of the day, it's done by end of day — and always hits
  deadlines. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-16] **Senior:** everything above, plus an excellent problem solver who can
  troubleshoot technical issues. (paraphrase)

**Supply & demand / specialize vs. generalize:**
- [2020-06-16] Talent is globally abundant; the rate you can command is set by *demand meeting
  talent*, not talent alone. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-16] Competing on a global scale (e.g. Fiverr, against Western, Southeast Asian,
  African and Eastern European markets) forces a very low rate — "a race to the bottom." (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-16] To command much higher fees, enter markets with high demand and little
  competition. **Generalists** ("a little bit of everything") face heavy competition;
  **specialists** — "one of three people that does this in the LA market or whatever market
  you're in" — face little competition and can charge a lot. (paraphrase)

**Hiring priorities (attributed to Joey Korenman's book):**
- [2020-06-16] The single most important thing when freelancing is **100% reliability** — the
  people he keeps bringing back are the reliable, trustworthy ones who deliver when they say
  yes. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-16] **Talent and personality are equally weighted** — you have to be as good a
  person as you are talented; talented-but-not-a-good-person doesn't get hired. (paraphrase;
  note the caption garble "if you're talented and person I'm not gonna hire you" appears to
  mean the opposite of talent-without-personality — treat wording as unreliable.)
- [2020-06-16] **Rate is the least important factor** to him; reliability, talent, and being
  a good person to work with rank above price. (paraphrase)

- "I am your host Matthew Encina, I'm a creative director here at Blind and the Futur."
