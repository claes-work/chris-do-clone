## Summary

An interview excerpt on the psychology of pricing and self-worth when facing a prospective
client. The guest advisor reframes the sales dynamic: many people enter a client
conversation "already defeated," having talked themselves into a story that their bid is
too high or that someone else could do the job better. The core reframe — "it's almost like
we believe that they're choosing us, but really you're choosing them" and "you are actually
the prize" — is the thesis the video title comes from. The guest then offers a practical
emotional technique drawn from Buddhist philosophy: notice the self-talk, name it as a
"story," drop the story, and focus instead on the feeling you want (confident, capable, of
service), then let every decision move you toward that feeling. The host amplifies the
reframe (the prize is your gift/talent, not their money). Matthew Encina delivers the outro.

## Key claims

### Guest advisor (unnamed)

- [2020-06-18] Many people enter a conversation with a prospective client "already defeated"
  — a form of self-sabotage rooted in negative self-talk about the client, the work, and
  themselves. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-18] The fix starts with paying attention to *how you talk to yourself* — e.g.
  "am I telling myself there's someone out there who can do this job better than I can?" —
  and then testing whether that story is actually true. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-18] "Is it true that your bid is too high, or is that just a belief that you
  have?" It comes down to value: how you value yourself, your work, and your belief in what
  you're doing. (paraphrase / partial quote)
- [2020-06-18] The central reframe: "it's almost like we believe that they're choosing us,
  but really you're choosing them" — and "you are actually the prize." (quote fragments)
- [2020-06-18] Technique: **drop the story, focus on the feeling.** When you notice you're
  telling yourself a story ("I need them," "last time I put something out there it wasn't
  received"), label it — "there's a story; the story is trying to pull me away from the
  feeling I want." (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-18] Name the current feeling honestly (lack, not-enough, not-good-enough), then
  ask what you *want* to feel instead (confident, capable, of service), then ask what you
  can do in this moment to feel that way. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-18] Use the target feeling as a decision filter: for each belief/story, ask "is
  this moving me toward feeling confident, capable, of service?" If not, thank the story
  ("I see you, thank you for sharing… you've helped me and I've needed you") and let it go.
  (paraphrase — the "thank the story and say goodbye" move)
