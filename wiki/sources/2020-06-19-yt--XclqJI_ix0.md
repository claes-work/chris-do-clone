# How To Talk To Clients That Use Abstract & Unclear Language

> ⚠️ ATTRIBUTION: The teacher/interviewer in this clip is **Matthew Encina** (The Futur
> creative director), **not Chris Do**. The demonstration is confirmed by the outro plug
> for "the practical project management course and the pitch kit" (both Matthew Encina
> Futur products) and by the on-screen framing "Matthew showcases how to break down one
> of these bait words." The interviewee ("Ben") is a designer being questioned. **No
> Chris-Do first-person material is present**, so nothing here should train the persona.
> Filed at L2 as corpus context (Futur team teaching consistent with Chris Do's discovery
> philosophy), not as persona training data.

## Summary

Matthew Encina demonstrates how to handle clients who describe what they want in vague,
abstract "coded language" — words like *exciting, clean, bold, minimal*. Live on camera
he interrogates one such word ("minimal") with a colleague, Ben, asking one attribute at
a time (colors, typography, white space vs. negative space, textures, contrast) to
convert the abstract word into concrete, shared meaning. The core lesson: clarifying the
client's language *up front* — including with mood boards / style-scapes as a gut check —
is far cheaper than spending three weeks building the wrong thing because you misread
their words.

Speaker: **Matthew Encina** (Futur team; NOT Chris Do) — first-person interviewer/teacher.
Second speaker **Ben** (a designer, being questioned as the demo "client"). Attributed as
team content, not subject content.

## Key claims

### Matthew Encina

- [2020-06-19] Clients often describe what they want in "coded language" / "bait words"
  — *exciting, clean, bold, minimal* — that mean different things to different people, so
  you can't assume you share a definition. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-19] The way to decode a vague word is to ask about it one concrete attribute
  at a time: what colors come to mind, what typography, what does "white space" mean (the
  color white vs. negative space), what textures/materials, how much contrast. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-19] Deliberately probe attributes the client did NOT mention (color, materials,
  typefaces) — that surfaces hidden assumptions and tests whether you're actually aligned.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-19] Even when you *feel* you already agree, don't just nod along assuming the
  client will "give you everything" — the questioning is how you verify the agreement is
  real. (paraphrase, self-aware about his own assumption)
- [2020-06-19] Clients may not have precise design language; you may be talking to someone
  with very limited vocabulary, so use popular references or pull a few reference images to
  confirm you're on the same page. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-19] A fast alignment tactic: put together a style-scape / mood board of ~5 images
  ("you said minimal and we were having a hard time articulating it — do any of these look
  right?") and use it as a gut check on what does and doesn't match their intent. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-19] Heavy questioning can feel like "pestering," but it isn't — it shows the
  client you're listening, that you understand, and that you're doing the hard lifting
  before spending their money. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-19] The most expensive mistake is spending three weeks building something when
  you were wrong from the start because you misinterpreted the client's words; spending time
  up front to clarify language is far cheaper. (paraphrase — stated at both open and close)

### Ben (interviewee, demo "client")

- [2020-06-19] To Ben, "minimal" means: clean, lots of white space, very organized, visual
  language almost stark; colors white and black; sans-serif type with no flourishes
  (Helvetica or Univers), spaced/tracked out rather than tight; "white space" meaning
  negative space / air between elements; low contrast; at most a simple linen or cotton
  texture. (paraphrase — used only to illustrate how one word unpacks)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All quotes are **Matthew Encina** (NOT Chris Do — do not use for voice training):

- "When you ask clients what they want something to look like and they give you this coded
  language — words like exciting, clean and bold — what the hell does it even mean?"
- "I'm trying to ask one thing at a time. He said 'stark' — so what does that mean? ...
  And I started asking about other things that he didn't mention at all, like color,
  materials, typefaces."
- "You might be working with somebody with very limited language, and you might need to use
  things like popular reference, or go back to the drawing board and pull a few references
  to confirm if you guys are on the same page."
- "I'll just pull together like five images — 'on the call you said minimal and we were
  having a hard time articulating what it means; I pulled a few of these together, do any of
  these look right?'"
- "It sounds like it could be a little pestering, but it's really not, because you're trying
  to show that you're listening, you understand, and you're doing all the hard heavy lifting
  before you spend all of their money making something."
- "The most expensive way this is going to get is if you spend three weeks on something and
  you are wrong to begin with — if you didn't understand the words that they used to begin
  with, if you've interpreted it wrong. That is way more expensive than just spending the
  time up front to clarify language, ask questions until it's clear."
