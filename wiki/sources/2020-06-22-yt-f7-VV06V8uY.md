## Summary

Guest Cynthia Kane teaches how to raise a difficult money/scope issue with a client
(or partner) *early and small* instead of suppressing it until it explodes. Her core
moves: (1) notice the inner voice that says "I should probably say something" and
trust it rather than the voice that rationalizes staying quiet; (2) practice trusting
that instinct in low-stakes daily moments so you can act on it in high-stakes ones;
(3) reframe attention away from the worst-case reaction toward *why* speaking up could
help both you and the client. She frames unspoken issues with a **laundry metaphor** —
let small things pile up and you eventually face an unmanageable load (a blown budget,
an angry client) instead of a one-sock-early quick word. The practical script: tell
the client proactively "before this gets out of hand, we're already over by this much
— are you fine with that, can we keep going, or do we need to scale back?"

## Key claims

### Cynthia Kane (context — does NOT train the persona)

- [2020-06-22] Have the small interactions early so you prevent the bigger discussions
  and meltdowns; when the budget conversation surfaces, or you feel a "mental note" that
  you should say something, that is the voice to listen to — not the voice that
  rationalizes your way out of it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-22] Practice trusting that inner voice in everyday low-stakes moments (e.g.
  the nudge to "stop into this store" or "go left here") so that when a real issue pops
  up you can recognize when you're talking yourself out of addressing it and instead act.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-22] **Laundry metaphor:** unspoken issues are like letting laundry pile up —
  it gets heavier and heavier until you have no clean clothes and a huge load; likewise a
  suppressed budget issue keeps piling until something blows up (a client "yelling at you
  on the phone for going over budget") when a word "back when you had one pair of socks"
  would have handled it. (paraphrase; she notes she forgets who originated the metaphor)
- [2020-06-22] The mindset trap is telling yourself "I don't want to be petty / nitpicky,
  just this one time" — but "274 changes later the laundry room is about to explode." The
  fix: recognize that judgment/evaluation of whether the issue is worth raising "isn't
  helpful"; the moment you notice yourself talking yourself out of speaking is your cue to
  do something different. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-22] Shift your attention from the worst-case scenario (how small it seems, how
  it might waste the client's time) to *why saying something would be the best
  possibility* — how it helps you and how it helps the client. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-22] Suggested proactive script to the client: "I wanted to let you know before
  this gets out of hand that we're already over by this much — so if you could let me
  know, are you fine with that, can we keep going, or do we need to scale back?"
  (near-verbatim)
