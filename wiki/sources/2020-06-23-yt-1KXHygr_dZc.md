## Summary

A single-speaker monologue on how a creative director (or solo creative) should translate a vague client request into an actionable design brief. The core framework is three things you must clearly understand from the client before any work begins: (1) the **goal** — the one thing the deliverable is supposed to do; (2) the **context** — where it lives, format/platform, audience, and the action you want the audience to take; (3) the **parameters** (constraints) — the "sandbox," rules of the game, brand guidelines, must-dos and must-avoids. The speaker refuses the "you're the creative one, make it up" open brief because "if it could be anything, it could be everything." A second technique covers decoding vague "coded language" (e.g. "energetic and exciting") by asking clarifying questions until you reach a concrete example. The back half covers delegating the decoded brief to a design team with clear roles, responsibilities, and agreed-upon deadlines — giving each designer space to push back and say a deadline isn't reasonable.

## Key claims

_All claims below are from the **unidentified speaker** (attribution: uncertain) — not confirmed Chris Do._

- [2020-06-23] Before you can deliver anything to your team, you must first clearly understand the request from the client — this applies whether talking to a client or to your own team. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-23] The three things to nail down are the **goal**, the **context**, and the **parameters** — in that order. (paraphrase — the framework)
- [2020-06-23] **Goal (1):** the single most important question — what is the purpose of the thing, the one thing it's supposed to do. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-23] **Context (2):** where the deliverable lives — the format, platform, length, whether it's moving or static — plus the audience: who they are, what they care about, what they need to see, and the action you want them to take after seeing it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-23] **Parameters / constraints (3):** the "sandbox" and "rules of the game" — brand guidelines to follow, things it must do, and things it absolutely shouldn't do. Define the rules clearly before starting. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-23] Rejects the open-ended "I don't know, you're the creative one, make it up" brief: "if it could be anything, that means it could be everything," and chasing everything wastes both your time and the client's unless they have unlimited time and money. (paraphrase / partial quote)
- [2020-06-23] Guessing at an undefined target is "throwing a dart in the dark" — you don't want to be guessing in this game. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-23] "Coded language" technique: when a client uses vague shorthand ("energetic and exciting"), don't accept it — ask "what does that look like to you?" and keep asking clarifying questions until you reach a concrete example (e.g. a specific Michael Bay rotating-hero shot from a Transformers movie). (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-23] You don't need one hyper-specific answer — identifying a few examples or a range is enough to define the space you'll work in; the point is to avoid re-interpreting vague notes later and being "left guessing again." (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-23] Once goal, context, and parameters are set, translate them into actionable steps for the team: assign different parameters to different designers, optionally give one a "wild card" to play slightly outside the box, and set a shared review deadline. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-23] When briefing the team, give clear roles, responsibilities, and agreed-upon deadlines — and leave space for a designer to say "no" or that a deadline isn't reasonable, so they're confident they can deliver rather than failing silently at the deadline. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-23] If designers ask good questions the director didn't ask, the director goes back to the client to clarify. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-23] Summary of the two halves: communicating ideas = (a) extraction and translation from the client, then (b) delivery to the team — understanding the goal, context, parameters, plus clear roles and responsibilities. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

- "what is the goal, what is this thing supposed to do... what is the one thing it's supposed to do, that's the most important."
