# Justin Trudeau's 21 Seconds Of Silence Is Powerful—w/ Author Cynthia Kane

## Summary

Cynthia Kane, in conversation with the Futur host (Matthew Encina), argues that silence is more powerful than speaking. She frames the pause as a tool for grounding yourself in the moment, creating intimacy and space in a conversation, and noticing the urge to interrupt, fix, or give advice before the other person has been fully heard. She contrasts healthy silence (grounding, listening) with weaponized silence (power-over, punishment, blame), ties the compulsion to respond to a lifetime of being trained to react rather than converse, and offers a practical experiment: try not to start your responses with "I," "me," or "my." She closes by citing Canadian Prime Minister Justin Trudeau's ~21-second pause before answering a June 2020 press question about the U.S. teargassing of protesters for a presidential photo-op as a public example of grounding silence.

## Key claims

### Cynthia Kane (context — guest, does NOT train persona)

- [2020-06-24] Silence is more powerful than speaking and is a way to create more intimacy in a conversation. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-24] There are two kinds of silence: weaponized silence used as "power over," punishment, or blame; and grounding silence used to root yourself in the present moment so you can choose how you want to interact. She advocates the second. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-24] Allowing space, pauses, and breath in a conversation gives way to more creativity and more openness. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-24] Silence lets you notice the impulse to interrupt, fix, solve, or keep speaking — when most of the time people don't need that; they need to be listened to first, after which you can ask whether they want suggestions. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-24] Silence is very uncomfortable when you start practicing it; much of the practice is becoming comfortable with discomfort — reminding yourself to "stay open" and let the other person finish. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-24] People feel compelled to respond instantly out of excitement and a fear the thought will slip away, and because we are trained to react — "the art of conversation is not something that we learn." We take in what someone says, relate it to ourselves immediately, and blurt out whatever comes up. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-24] A practical experiment: try not to begin responses in a conversation with "I," "me," or "my," to notice how often you interact only from your own perspective/lens. It's less a rule than an attention exercise. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-24] Cites Justin Trudeau's ~21-second pause before responding to a reporter's question about U.S. protesters being teargassed for a presidential photo-op as a powerful public example of grounding into the moment to find the best way forward. (paraphrase, third-party example)

### Host — Matthew Encina (attribution: uncertain; NOT the subject, does NOT train persona)

- [2020-06-24] We feel compelled to give in to our "advice monster" and start telling clients the problem and the fix (e.g., "a new website will solve this") instead of staying quiet and understanding what they actually need. (paraphrase; "advice monster" attributed to Michael Bungay Stanier)
- [2020-06-24] The reason we rush to give advice: we've spent a lifetime being rewarded for "having the answer" — school, high school, university, and early career all condition us that knowing the answer equals succeeding. (paraphrase, attributed to a conversation with Michael Bungay Stanier)
- [2020-06-24] To be a good strategic partner to clients, we have to learn to be comfortable in the silence, which is more powerful than speaking. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes
