# How To Raise Your Rates When Someone Says "I Thought You Were Cheaper"

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a genuinely-new, reusable script for the "but Bob told me you did it for $200" objection (the *favor-price / one-time price* reframe), plus a lead-generation angle (referrals cluster at similar budgets → escape by building expensive-looking work). Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/pricing/` and `wiki/topics/marketing/` if a synthesis pass confirms the favor-price script isn't already captured. Gate: attribution is clean (single-speaker Chris Do), so no attribution block on promotion.

## Summary

Chris Do coaches a group member who observes that their projects mostly come from referrals with budgets similar to past projects, and asks how to find higher-budget clients without existing connections. Chris explains "birds of a feather flock together" — referrals cluster around the same budget, so a stream of low-budget leads has to be actively changed. He gives a script for when a referred lead expects your old, lower price ("Bob told me you did it for $200"): frame that price as a one-time relationship favor at a past point in time, and explain you can't run an equitable, sustainable business at that rate. He then pivots to lead generation: build beautiful case studies and mock-ups on Behance, translate them to Instagram with links back, and "close the gap" between your presentation and top-tier work (e.g. Pentagram) to command $1k–$100k for a mark. He closes on a self-reliance theme — be a "life learner," look objectively and self-critically at your own work, and don't wait for "some fool like myself" to tell you it's good enough.

Speaker: single-speaker coaching monologue by **Chris Do** (first person — "the best way that I know how," "some fool like myself," "I helped produce"). The group member's question is paraphrased/quoted at the very start; the clip is a cut-down from a larger video (Chris signs off asking viewers to go watch the full video). ## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-06-25] "Birds of a feather flock together" — leads that come through referrals tend to be very similar to each other (including budget); if the leads aren't good, you have to actively start changing the source. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-25] Referral budgets anchor to your previous projects: people with similar budgets to past clients refer people with similar budgets, so you stay stuck at that price band unless you deliberately break out. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-25] When you know the referral source and the project sounds too small, qualify budget early and directly — before going deep — so you don't waste time: ask "can you afford X?" (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-25] The **favor-price / one-time-price reframe** for "but Bob told me you did it for $200": explain you had a relationship with Bob, that price was for a specific point in time, it was a one-time favor price, and you can't run an equitable, sustainable business at that rate — "I might not be here tomorrow if I keep charging that price." (paraphrase — core new script) *(caption garble: transcript renders the number both as "200" and "$200"; treat as an illustrative low figure.)*
- [2020-06-25] To generate your own leads (not rely on referrals), the best method Chris knows: develop really beautiful case studies and mock-ups on Behance, then translate that to Instagram with links back to the Behance site. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-25] "Close the gap" between your work-and-presentation and top-tier work (names Pentagram): the more you close that gap, the more likely you are to command $1,000 / $5k / $10k / $20k / $100k for a mark. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-25] Presentation is a value multiplier — spend the time, money, and effort to build beautiful mock-ups and case studies "so that the work looks super expensive." (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-25] Be a "life learner": learn to look objectively and self-critically at your own work and judge it yourself, rather than waiting for "some fool like myself to tell you it's good enough." Waiting for external validation just leaves you dependent on the next person's approval. (paraphrase)
- [2020-06-25] You already have all the tools and resources to close the gap — what you're missing is "the will and the eye to see things for what they are." (paraphrase)
