# Bad Bosses—How To Say No To Unpaid Overtime (Role-play)

★ **L3-candidate** (gated on attribution — clears the gate) — Chris Do demonstrates a full, named negotiation technique stack (smiling/disarming, agreeing-then-finding-the-gap, consistency principle, "symmetry of logic," forcing an acknowledgment question) applied to defending your own time. The debrief is unambiguously his real coaching. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/sales-clients/` (negotiation tactics) and `persona/beliefs.md` (boundaries / say-no), dated — it extends his boundary material from the sales context into the employee/overtime context.

## Summary

Chris Do stages a role-play of an employee being pressured by a toxic boss to stay late and work unpaid overtime, then pauses repeatedly to narrate the negotiation techniques he is deploying. He plays the "Chris" character who is leaving at 8pm after a nine-hour day on an eight-hour agreement, defends his boundary without becoming confrontational, and closes by forcing the boss to admit the work is good — leaving the boss "flummoxed" with nothing left to argue. The through-line: an agreement is a bond both sides made; protecting your own time is legitimate regardless of whether your plans are "important," and you can hold the line while staying non-aggressive and respectful.

Speaker: **single presenter, Chris Do, performing a two-hander role-play.** Chris voices BOTH characters — the employee "Chris" (himself) AND the toxic boss "Greg" — and steps out of the scene to deliver coaching commentary (timestamped in the transcript as "215," "245," meaning ~2:15, ~2:45). The in-character lines (both the employee's pushback AND the boss's pressure tactics) are **illustrative role-play**, not statements of Chris's belief; the boss's lines especially are a caricature he is teaching against. Only the **debrief/coaching commentary** is Chris's real position and trains the persona. Attribution is clear (not uncertain).

## Key claims

### Coaching / debrief — Chris Do's real position (trains persona)

- [2020-07-30] Saying no to a boss pressuring you to stay late is genuinely hard because you want to prove you're pulling your weight, and because your financial health depends on staying employed and on that person not badmouthing you. (paraphrase)
- [2020-07-30] Open with a smile as a disarming technique — it changes the tone of your voice and makes what you say sound less confrontational. The moment you say something aggressively, the other person has no choice but to respond aggressively, and in that fight the boss has far more leverage, so you'll likely lose. (paraphrase)
- [2020-07-30] Tactic: **agree first, then find the gap.** Mostly agree with the other side ("yeah, let's get this done"), which brings them to your side; there's usually a gap in their logic you can then work. He is "looking for something for us to disagree on" only after establishing agreement. (paraphrase)
- [2020-07-30] Slow the conversation down and speak in near-whispering tones; ask to take it aside privately. Doing so signals respect and submission — "you are in control, I'm not trying to exert myself over you" — which lowers the temperature. (paraphrase)
- [2020-07-30] Repeatedly saying "I know, I know / I'm in agreement" keeps you out of a fight until the boss changes the tune (e.g., "everyone's working till 10 or 11"), which is the point where you finally disagree. (paraphrase)
- [2020-07-30] **The consistency principle:** one of the most powerful forces in people is the need to be consistent. If they agreed to an eight-hour day, it's very hard for them to now decide it's a ten-hour day — so restate the original terms and ask "are you going back on your word?" (paraphrase)
- [2020-07-30] Frame the ask as: "I gave you a solid nine-hour day for an eight-hour quote"; if you want more I can *possibly* stay, but I'll charge you more — are you okay with that? Overruns caused by how the company bid/estimated the project are not the worker's mistake to pay for. (paraphrase, role-play framing he endorses)
- [2020-07-30] Recognize implied threats. "If you want to be part of the team, it's important you stick around" is an implied (not explicit) threat that if you don't comply, you're not a team player. (paraphrase)
- [2020-07-30] **Force an acknowledgment with a question you already know the answer to.** Rather than asserting your value, ask "Do you like the work I'm doing?" You already know the answer is yes — because if they hated your work the conversation would be about performance, not about staying late. Once they admit the work is good, they have to capitulate: you ARE doing the work and being a team member; more than that costs extra. (paraphrase — a distinct tactic)
- [2020-07-30] "Symmetry of logic" (a framework he references as previously discussed): if you want to work for free that's your prerogative, and if the whole team wants to work for free that's theirs — but "I just don't choose that, and that should be my right to choose." Let people make their choice while asserting your equal right to make a different one. (paraphrase; "symmetry of logic" = his term)
- [2020-07-30] The boss is "making their problem my problem." You're a human being with other obligations (dog sitter, babysitter, dinner with family) and you plan your life accordingly; you don't live your whole life on-call for undisclosed amounts of work. (paraphrase)
- [2020-07-30] This is not a nurse or doctor on call where lives depend on you showing up — it's just a boss trying to extract more work, so there's a financial and personal price for it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-07-30] The whole video is about **managing conflict** and, specifically, protecting your own time — which matters "regardless if you have legitimate plans or not." You made an agreement; the company agreed to it, and that bond should hold you together. (paraphrase)
- [2020-07-30] Putting in extra from time to time can be fine if you agree to it, but it should not be **company policy / the default expectation.** In a toxic environment, use these strategies to move the conversation away from a negative, confrontational place toward agreement on basic principles and values. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do (coaching/debrief — real position):**
