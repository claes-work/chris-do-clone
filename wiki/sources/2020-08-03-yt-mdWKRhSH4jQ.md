# How To Get Your First Design Client

## Summary

A Q&A-style panel answers the recurring question "how do I get my first design client?" The opening answer (a Futur team member, identity uncertain) lays out four low-stakes approaches — networking, publishing self-directed work, approaching local businesses you think you can help, and just getting out and trying things rather than perfecting process/pricing/proposals first. Chris Do then takes over with the sharper, named material: if you're leaving a job, "plan your escape" like Alcatraz — build client and internal relationships and sit in on new-business meetings *before* you jump; if you're fresh out of school, call your professors for referrals; and start any relationship *before* you need something from it. The clip closes with a live-question answer where Chris reframes "how do I find a mentor?" into "you don't need a mentor, you need heroes."

## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- [2020-08-03] The path to a first client differs sharply by situation: leaving a studio after years vs. just graduating are "vastly different" starting points. (paraphrase)
- [2020-08-03] If you're still employed and planning to go independent, treat it like planning an escape from Alcatraz: build relationships with clients and with people at the office, and sit in on important new-business meetings so you get a firsthand education in how new business is *won and subsequently lost*. Set up your "side game" and prepare before you jump — "to jump without a plan… you might get out but you're not going to last very long." (paraphrase + partial quote)
- [2020-08-03] Fresh out of school with no experience: call up your professors — all of them — and ask if there are clients or people they can recommend you talk to; if you did good work in class there's a good chance they'll refer you. (paraphrase)
- [2020-08-03] Design-school professors are undervalued as a referral source: they're often working professionals or well-connected, because big companies "like Google and Apple and Microsoft" call them for referrals — so they carry clout. (paraphrase)
- [2020-08-03] Start a relationship *before* you actually need something from it — don't open a brand-new relationship with an immediate ask. (paraphrase)
- [2020-08-03] Flattery-then-pitch fails: he gets "hammered" online with "hey Chris, love the show… here's what I do, can you hire me," and the flattery "doesn't mean anything" and "won't get you anywhere" when a request follows immediately. (paraphrase + partial quote)
- [2020-08-03] On finding a mentor (answering viewer "Dan Nad"): "I don't think you need a mentor — what you need are heroes." Heroes are plentiful; mentors are in limited supply. Credits the idea to Seth Godin [transcribed "south golden" — caption garble]. Before seeking a mentor, exhaust the free available resources. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-08-03] Cites Michael Bierut: "you can hijack your mentor" — they don't even need to know you exist (i.e., learn from someone at a distance without a formal relationship). (paraphrase, third-party attribution)

### Futur team member — `attribution: uncertain` (NOT for persona)

- [2020-08-03] There are several effective ways to get a first client: (a) networking — meet people who might need what you offer; (b) publish self-directed work — be your own client, make the work you want to be hired for, and put it in front of people who might need it; (c) community — look at local businesses you genuinely think you could help and approach them with "here's how I think I can help you, what do you think?"; (d) just get out and try things. (paraphrase)
- [2020-08-03] Chasing your first client, don't worry about pricing, a perfect process, or a beautiful proposal — worry about getting the work; that will pull the other questions into view as they arise. "Don't let the great be the enemy of the good." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-08-03] The real question is usually about *getting started*, not about who the specific first client is. (paraphrase)
- [2020-08-03] Contrast to the recommended approach: the passive "rain catcher" ("I'll take whatever comes my way") is fine but has problems. (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do:**

- "You're in Alcatraz, you want to get out of the big house, so you should be building relationships with clients, with people at the office. You should be sitting in on those very important new-business meetings so you get a firsthand education as to how new business is won and subsequently lost."
- "To jump without a plan, it's just like trying to escape out of prison without a plan — you might get out but you're not going to last very long."
- "Call up one of your professors, call up all of them, and say: look, I got out of school and I'm looking to set up shop — are there clients or people that you can recommend that I can talk to?"
- "Guess who calls them for referrals — all these big companies like Google and Apple and Microsoft. So they have clout."
- "Start the conversation before you actually need something."
- "I get hammered with this all over the internet: 'Hey Chris, love the show, by the way here's what I do, can you hire me.'… You're trying to flatter me, which won't get you anywhere, and then you're asking for something right away."
- "I don't think you need a mentor. What you need are heroes — and heroes are plentiful, whereas mentors have a limited supply." [idea credited to Seth Godin]
- "[Michael Bierut says] you can hijack your mentor — they don't even need to know that you exist."

**Futur team member (uncertain attribution):**

- "Don't let the great be the enemy of the good here."
- "You do… you're your own client and you make the type of work that you want to be doing for other people, and showcase that and share it with people and put it in front of people who might actually need it."
