# Use This Sales Technique The Next Time You Pitch

★ L3-candidate — the "Eight Mile Principle" (name the client's objection before they do) is a distinct, reusable sales/pitching technique that belongs in `topics/sales-clients`.

## Summary

Solo Chris Do video (host and sole narrator) breaking down two competing pitch scenes from the AMC show *Better Call Saul* — framed as a "David vs. Goliath" contest where a small firm and a big firm each pitch the same client (Mesa Verde bank) for its expansion work. Chris uses the clips to teach one central sales idea: **know what your client is thinking, and say it first.** He calls this the "Eight Mile Principle" (after the Eminem film): in a rap battle you win by taking away your opponent's ammunition — voicing their attacks before they can, leaving them speechless. Chris applies the same logic to competitive pitching: preempt the client's objections and your competitor's likely attacks by naming them yourself.

He narrates two pitches. First, Kim Wexler (the small/solo firm) opens by admitting she's "not the safe choice… but the right choice," then uses a tailored-suit/bespoke metaphor to reframe her small size as an advantage (custom, undivided attention, "you are my sole focus"). Chris praises her positioning given limited resources. Then Chuck McGill (the big firm, HHM) pitches — and out-maneuvers her by anticipating both what the client is thinking and what Kim already said. Chuck disarms up front ("I'm not here to give you the hard sell"), appears to praise Kim ("she's the future… we're the past"), then pivots: he plants seeds of doubt about the risks of trusting one young, inexperienced person with high-stakes, detail-heavy banking-compliance work, positioning decades of experience and deep fed networks as the safe choice. Chuck wins the business. Chris's closing lesson: Kim's critical mistake was not anticipating what the bigger, more established competitor would bring up; the client ultimately wants the **safe** choice, and Chuck presented himself as the safest. Chris ties it to his "How to Negotiate" course and its "Socratic Six" questions.

## Key claims

All claims below are **paraphrased** unless in the quotes section. Dated **2020-08-19**. Speaker: **Chris Do** (host/narrator) unless noted. Statements by Kim / Chuck / Howard are **fictional TV characters** quoted as teaching examples, not persona material.

- **Chris (2020-08-19):** The single most important pitch technique is to know what your client is thinking and say it first — voice their concerns before they can. *(central thesis / paraphrase)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** He names this the "Eight Mile Principle," after the Eminem film — in a freestyle battle you win by humiliating your opponent, and one very effective strategy is to disarm them by being the first to say any attack they could throw at you, leaving them with nothing. *(paraphrase — named framework)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** When you know you are NOT the safe/obvious choice, you must bring that up front yourself (as Kim did: "safe choice → go with HHM; right choice → go with us"). *(paraphrase)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** Asking pointed questions (e.g. "Did you buy that suit off the rack?") sets the groundwork for a "trap" — it lets you steer the prospect into a spot where your strength becomes the obvious answer. *(paraphrase)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** Tap into something you specifically know about this client and play to your genuine strengths; a small firm's strength is a bespoke, custom, non-"cookie-cutter" solution that a bigger firm can't match. *(paraphrase)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** Big, important clients don't want to "fit the suit" — they want the solution made for them (bespoke), and they want to be the provider's sole focus. *(paraphrase)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** Overcome each objection one at a time; the "magic word" is **you** — make the client the sole focus of the answer. *(paraphrase)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** Chuck's masterful move: disarm the client up front by naming what they're already thinking ("you've already suffered the hard sell; we've made up our minds to go with Kim"), which builds trust before the pivot. *(paraphrase of technique)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** The "pivot" technique — plant an innocent seed the client might already half-believe (e.g. "banking law needs young, fresh energy"), then stretch it to its extreme so it starts to cause concern, even though neither of you truly believes it. *(paraphrase — named "masterful pivot")*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** Sequence your attacks like a boxer: a couple of light "jabs" (planting doubt about penalties, mistakes, missed boring details) before the "killer hook / KO." *(paraphrase — boxing metaphor)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** Reframe your own apparent weakness as a strength — Chuck turns "old / stale / long-winded" into "decades of experience, deep fed networks, catches the boring details that save hundreds of thousands of dollars." *(paraphrase)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** When the client signals they've chosen the competitor, don't fight it — agree, then establish your power structure over the competitor ("she learned everything from us; we trained her — do you want the student or the masters?"). *(paraphrase)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** Kim's critical mistake: she didn't think enough about what the bigger, more established competitor would bring up; she was "outgunned" by a seasoned pro who could raise many objections and speak to them. *(paraphrase — the lesson)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19):** At the end of the day the client really wants the **safe choice**, and the winner is whoever presents themselves as the safest. *(paraphrase — closing takeaway)*
- **Chris (2020-08-19) — CTA / homework:** Sit down and write a list of objections or accusations a prospective client might raise, and make sure you bring those up first. He promotes his "How to Negotiate" course, which includes his "Socratic Six" — six questions to overcome almost any objection — to help viewers close more jobs and earn more. *(paraphrase)*
