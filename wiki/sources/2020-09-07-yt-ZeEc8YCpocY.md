## Summary

Chris Do argues that creatives tend to do what they *like* rather than what they *have* to do to run a successful business, and that books like Steve Blank's *The Four Steps to the Epiphany* explain how to find product-market fit but not what to do "day in and day out" to run a company. He presents eight core concepts present in every business — Leadership, Finance, Operations, Growth, Product, Service, Sales, Marketing — and walks through what each means, a priority order for them, and the team roles that back each one. His central structuring idea is "inheritance" (borrowed from computer science): each concept embeds the ones above it, so the highest concept (Leadership) is present in all the others, and the last (Marketing) is the most comprehensive. He splits the eight into "inside the building" (Leadership, Finance, Operations, Growth) vs. "outside the building"/customer-facing (Product, Service, Sales, Marketing), stresses that internal order must come before customer delivery, and closes by plugging his book *Create & Orchestrate*.

## The 8 core concepts (framework)

Priority order (highest first). "Inside the building" = internal/team-facing; "outside the building" = customer-facing.

1. **Leadership** (inside) — creating a culture in which a business can thrive: vision, values, management, communication. Communication is "the number one role of leadership." Applies whether you're a one-person or ten-thousand-person business.
2. **Finance** (inside) — ensuring the sustainability of the business: revenue, expenses, profit, assets, liabilities, capital, taxes. "If you don't make a profit you don't have a business, you have a front piece of art."
3. **Operations** (inside) — minimizing risk: company form (sole proprietor / LLC / corporation), insurance, contracts, human resources, process, technology, measurement.
4. **Growth** (inside) — optimizing the business for a dynamic (not static) market: reinvestment, developing a point of view, innovation; drive change rather than be impacted by it. Growth doesn't mean "make more money all the time."
5. **Product** (outside) — "a predictable unit of value"; the discipline covers research, development, design, production, quality control, pricing, and more.
6. **Service** (outside) — maximizing customer satisfaction; treating the customer as a relationship to be invested in; standing behind a guarantee and fixing what goes wrong.
7. **Sales** (outside) — generating revenue: point of sale, method of sale, model for selling. "This should be the simplest thing ever."
8. **Marketing** (outside) — identifying, understanding, and generating demand: brand, reach, content, channels. His favorite; the most comprehensive concept.

- [2020-09-07] Books like Steve Blank's *The Four Steps to the Epiphany* (customer development) are excellent for finding product-market fit and thinking a business through, but they don't tell you what you have to do day-to-day to run the business. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-07] Every single business contains these eight core concepts — Leadership, Finance, Operations, Growth, Product, Service, Sales, Marketing — and every successful business is led by a team that understands them. (paraphrase)

- [2020-09-07] Finance ensures the sustainability of the business; without profit you don't have a business, "you have a front piece of art." Taxes are a distinct thing — misunderstand them and you can do everything else right and still go out of business. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-09-07] Operations is ultimately about minimizing risk (company form, insurance, contracts, HR, process, technology, measurement); employing people brings "a lot of risk." (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-07] Growth is about optimizing for a market that is dynamic, not static — what works today can be a saturated market full of copycats in two months — so you can never get lazy; think about reinvestment, point of view, innovation, and driving change rather than being impacted by it. - [2020-09-07] Product is "quite simply a predictable unit of value" — the thing the customer understands they're getting and will exchange money for. Silicon Valley has overloaded the word "product" beyond its intended meaning. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-09-07] A consulting/"service-based" business is a misnomer: people who think they sell a service miss that they still have to deliver a predictable unit of value. No matter the business, you must deliver service because things go wrong; a customer is a relationship you invest in like one with a loved one. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-07] Sales is simply generating revenue — your point of sale, method of sale, and selling model. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-07] Marketing (his favorite) is about identifying, understanding, and generating demand: brand, reach, content, channels. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-07] The concepts follow an "inheritance" model (from computer science): the lower the priority, the more comprehensive the concept, and each embeds the ones above it — Leadership is found in all the others; Finance includes Leadership; Operations includes Leadership and Finance; and so on. Marketing, being last, is the most comprehensive. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-07] Finance is the second most important concept and where creatives most get it wrong — they love the idea and the customer but ignore their P&L and taxes, then "look up in two years" broke. (paraphrase, framed via "we"/creatives)
- [2020-09-07] Internal concepts (Leadership, Finance, Operations, Growth) take precedence over customer-facing ones because you can't deliver to the customer if "your house isn't in order." (paraphrase + quote)

(paraphrase, self-critical, first-person biography)
- [2020-09-07] Marketing officers are rising to the top of leading companies (next to the CEO) because marketing requires understanding every aspect of the business — you're in the market communicating with prospects before they become customers, and must represent every aspect authentically so you don't oversell and reality meets or exceeds expectations. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-07] He wrote about the core concepts in his book *Create and Orchestrate*, an Amazon #1 bestseller; the book is mostly narrative about his failures, but its spine (no narrative) is a full explanation of the core concepts. (paraphrase, self-reported)
