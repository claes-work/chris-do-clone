# How To Talk To Clients & Get To Yes: Role-play w/ Melinda Livsey

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains several named, reusable Chris Do techniques (the "dream date" discovery frame, "robot brain vs monkey brain," mirror-the-client's-exact-language, "what would make this a no-brainer for you?", and collaborative budget-anchored pricing). Worth promoting the Chris-attributed coaching into `wiki/topics/sales-clients/` and dated `persona/beliefs.md` entries — not just held at L2.

## Summary

A live sales-call role-play plus debrief. **Chris Do plays the vendor/salesperson**; **Melinda Livsey plays the prospective client** who wants help turning her long-form content into bite-sized micro-content for Instagram and LinkedIn. Chris runs a full discovery-to-close: he sits in the client's problem before ever discussing price, mirrors her exact language back to her, surfaces a hidden contradiction (she says she cares about reach/click-throughs but prescribes "five cuts a week"), anchors his pricing collaboratively against her budget, and deliberately scales the scope *down* (from 5 videos/week to 2–3) so he can add value and stay engaged with her real goal. In the debrief he coaches a third participant, **Mo** (a student who did an earlier "old you" version of the same call), on the underlying *mindset*: be present to the human in front of you ("monkey brain"), not reading an invisible script ("robot brain"), and simply ask "what would make this person happy?"

**Speaker / role-play note:** This is a role-play, so many lines are *illustrative in-character* rather than Chris's real assertions. The opening caption ("role play between trish and melinda") is a caption garble — the actual role-play is Chris (vendor) and Melinda (client). Only Chris's real **debrief/coaching** commentary (the "here's how I handled that / here's the mindset" sections) trains the persona; Melinda's and Mo's lines are context. Attribution below separates the two.

## Key claims

### Chris Do — real coaching / debrief (persona-training)

- [2020-09-09] Sit in the client's problem and "milk it" to fully understand *why* they need the thing before you ever start talking price. (paraphrase; confirmed by Mo's observation and Chris's agreement)
- [2020-09-09] **Mirror the client's exact language.** Chris says he wrote down and mirrored back her own words — "you want to be hands off, you want to ramp up, you want to have output, you want to convert" — so she knew he was actually listening. (paraphrase + partial quote)
- [2020-09-09] **Clients prescribe a solution but care about one result.** Melinda said she cared about reach and click-throughs, then immediately prescribed "volume volume volume" (five cuts a week). "A lot of your clients... may say make us 45 things, make us 200 logo sketches, but at the end of the day they care about one result." Spend the beginning of the call finding that one result. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-09-09] **Surface contradictions subtly rather than arguing.** When her prescription (volume) contradicted her stated goal (engagement/results), Chris didn't argue — he reflected it back ("you just need a person to edit and cut it down, right?") as a "very subtle way to call into contradiction" so she resolves it herself. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-09-09] **"Robot brain vs monkey brain."** Mo defaulted to "some kind of invisible script" and wasn't present to the person in front of him — that's robot brain. "A child could read a script, call centers read scripts, and they're not paid a whole lot." Chris wants people to be human and present ("monkey brain"). (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-09-09] The mindset that produces the right words: "In my mind I'm just sitting here thinking, what would make Melinda really really happy... what does she want from this call?" The words can come out weird or out of order — it doesn't matter, they'll be genuine, because the intention is right. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-09-09] Ask outcome-eliciting questions: "what matters to you," "what's a good price," "what do you hope to achieve," "what's a home run look like," "how do I make this a no-brainer for you?" — the goal is to **remove barriers to her saying yes.** (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-09-09] **The "dream date" frame** — let the client describe *their* ideal outcome; don't project your vision onto them. If you just met someone and pitched your fantasy date (expensive wine, ribeye), you'd find out too late "I'm a vegan" / "there's alcoholism in my family" — "I made it all about me." Instead: "tell me what your ideal date looks like," then design the engagement around exactly that. (paraphrase + quote, extended analogy)
- [2020-09-09] Where you hear conflict, try to resolve it — but the *initial intention* is only to find out what makes them happy, then design the conversation around giving them that. (paraphrase)

### Chris Do — in-character role-play lines (illustrative, NOT literal doctrine but demonstrate technique)

- [2020-09-09] Opens by building rapport / making the client feel special before scoping ("you seem like a really decent human being... somebody I think we could do something good together"). (illustrative quote)
- [2020-09-09] Softens his questioning: "I don't want to get into the weeds... I don't want to kill you with all my questions, I hope you don't mind." (illustrative quote)
- [2020-09-09] Pushes past vanity metrics to a metric he can actually control: sales → email subscribers → clicks → "since I don't have control over those parts, what's the best thing the video can do for you? Reach." (illustrative paraphrase)
- [2020-09-09] **Pricing, stated in-character:** "My minimum level engagement is $2,500 a month to work with me... and it can go up from there to twelve thousand. I have a client that pays me two thousand dollars a month." Then: "figure out together what number feels comfortable and then design a package that will meet your needs based on your budget — where are you in that spectrum?" (illustrative quotes — demonstrates collaborative, budget-anchored pricing)
- [2020-09-09] Holds the price without desperation: when she offers $2,000, "it's hard for me to take on clients that spend less than that, unfortunately." (illustrative quote)
- [2020-09-09] The value framing: "if you spent $2,500 with anybody... and got something that exceeded your expectations, then $2,500 would be a bargain; but if it's less than you'd hoped, you'd feel it's wasting money — so you tell me what results you want" and set them as benchmarks. (illustrative paraphrase)
- [2020-09-09] **"Carrot and stick" / pull-back move** (named by Mo in debrief): when he described the extra value he'd add (titles, graphics, pulled quotes, recontextualizing, a clear CTA) and then said "but it doesn't sound like you need that," the client pulled *toward* him ("wait, but I do want that"). (illustrative, technique named by Mo)
- [2020-09-09] **Scopes down to serve the goal:** rather than 5 cheap cuts/week, proposes 2–3 videos/week so he can add real value, reflect on engagement, and respond to the audience — reframing the client from "hiring an editor for straight editing" to a partner working toward her goal. (illustrative)
- [2020-09-09] Closes with a low-risk trial: "three videos a week at $2,500 a month, try it for 30 days," honest about a learning curve ("probably not going to happen until the midway point"), with a bi-weekly report and course-correction. Ends with a one-page deal memo + invoice. (illustrative)

### Melinda Livsey (context — client role + observation)

- [2020-09-09] As the client, states her real goal is to be *hands off* and expand her audience; her success metric ladders sales ← email subscribers ← reach. (context, in-character)
- [2020-09-09] Observation on the debrief: Chris "only gave the information as it pertained to what I had just said"; he wasn't leading her, he was "walking with me to see where I was going," not dumping options — unlike Mo. (context)

### Mo (context — student)

- [2020-09-09] Observes Chris "stayed in the problem before even talking numbers," gave the client the power via "what would make this a no-brainer for you?", and didn't sound desperate/eager on price. (context)
