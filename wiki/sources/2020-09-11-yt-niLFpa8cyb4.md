# Don't Take Your Clients For Granted

★ **L3-candidate** — Contains a distinctive, quotable retention thesis ("if you're just hands, a competitor who cares can steal your client and charge more") plus a memorable romantic-relationship analogy for client neglect. Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/sales-clients/` and `persona/beliefs.md` (dated) if not already covered by existing client-relationship material. Gate: substantive material is clearly Chris Do first-person; framing is by team members.

## Summary

A team-hosted Q&A clip. Editors Jayden (host) and Jonah pose a question to Chris Do: if a client shows up and asks you to just be "hands" (do exactly what they say, "the monkey" / production robot), should you take it and take the money, or do more? Chris argues that treating the engagement as minimum-effort "hands" work is dehumanizing and, more importantly, strategically dangerous: it leaves the client relationship weak, and a competitor who is genuinely interested in the client and talks to them the way they want to be talked to can walk in, take the client, and charge more. He reframes every client engagement as a relationship (platonic, but still a relationship) in which the client needs to feel cared about, and demonstrates his own approach — designing the conversation entirely around discovering and delivering what makes the client happy.

Speaker: primarily a **Chris Do** monologue answering a question, with framing/questions from **Jayden** (Jaiden, the on-camera host/editor) and a relayed question from **Jonah** (another editor). The substantive claims are all Chris Do, first person ("if I really believe in the things that I'm doing," "what would make Melinda really happy"). "Melinda" is used as a hypothetical/role-play client name. Attribution for persona-training material is clear.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-09-11] Sometimes a client genuinely just needs "hands" — cookie-cutter execution you crank out — but Chris personally finds being reduced to a "production robot" dehumanizing when he believes in the work. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-11] He aims to build a relationship where he contributes more value than he's paid for; if he does the minimum, he'll be paid the minimum "the rest of my life." (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-11] Cites Zig Ziglar: "if you do more than what you're paid to do, eventually you'll be paid more than what you do." (quote, attributed to Zig Ziglar)
- [2020-09-11] Showing up intending to do the minimum means you've already decided to self-sabotage the relationship. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-11] Core thesis: if a designer just takes the money and cranks edits in the back room without deepening the relationship, an "uppity" competitor can approach the client — "I noticed you've been doing videos and I think there's a lot of opportunity... it doesn't look as engaging as it could" — and steal the client. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-11] A client engagement IS a relationship — platonic, but still a relationship — and people need to feel you care about them and have their best interests at heart. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-11] Analogy: an attractive person whose partner takes them for granted (makes them wait, belittles them, isn't supportive) leaves the "door open" for someone else to walk in kindly — a neglected client relationship works the same way. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-11] His entire mental focus on a client call is "what would make [the client] happy / what does she want from this call," then designing the conversation to give her exactly that (resolving conflict where it arises). (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-11] Sample discovery questions he asks: what matters to you, what's a good price, what do you hope to achieve/accomplish, what's a home run look like, how do I make this a no-brainer so you could sign tomorrow. Making it easy is "what you're paying me for." (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-11] Competitive reality: if he had a rival's client list (easily found on their website), he could call those clients; unless the rival has "a great relationship, an unbreakable relationship, an unfair competitive lock," someone who's genuinely interested and talks to the client the right way "could probably steal your client and charge more." (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-11] Defense: strengthen your client relationships, learn to talk about their business, and develop rapport — that's the "unfair competitive lock." (paraphrase)

### Jayden / Jonah (framing — not persona-training)

## Notable verbatim quotes

- "If I just do the minimum, I'll get paid that... the rest of my life."
