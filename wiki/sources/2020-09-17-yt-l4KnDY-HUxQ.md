# How To Copy Without Stealing (5 Ingredients Technique)

Worth promoting into `wiki/topics/design-craft/` and `wiki/topics/mindset/` (learning method) and into `persona/beliefs.md`/`voice.md` (dated), not just held at L2. Capture the method below intact.

## The 5 Ingredients technique (framework)

A method for learning anything by deconstruction: reduce a thing you admire to ~five core "ingredients" (like reverse-engineering a dish you love), so you understand *why* it works — then, with experimentation, you can make your own version. Copy *how it's made*, not the thing itself → "copy without stealing."

Chris demonstrates it live by breaking down a Jean-Michel Basquiat painting. The loop:

1. **Observe objectively.** Describe only what you literally see; withhold judgment and interpretation ("keep it objective," "try to stick with what you see"). Interpretations like "self-portrait" or "trying to tell a story" get pushed back on — those aren't observations.
2. **Push vague words toward specific craft language.** "Not realistic" → "not *rendered*"; "no shading" → "no *value*"; "colorful" → "minimal colors that feel dirty." The vocabulary must be specific enough to act on.
3. **Isolate ONE element per box and try to reproduce it** with a blunt instrument, writing the words that describe it (word-dominated people write first, then draw).
4. **Distill each observation into a named ingredient with attributes.** In the Basquiat breakdown the ingredients land as:
   - **Line quality** — irregular, inconsistent, rough (looks drawn with the non-dominant hand).
   - **Figures** — distorted proportions, flat (no perspective), face the camera.
   - **Colors** — minimal, dirty, lots of black.
   - **Tools** — thick brushes, oil pastel, canvas, paint (tools influence the work).
   - **Words** — misspelled on purpose, crossed out, scribbled over, short/incomplete phrases, copyright/™ symbols; stream-of-consciousness.
5. **Test the ingredients by trying to make the thing.** If your recreation fails, your ingredients were too broad or too specific — the error shows up in the work. "If you couldn't see this, you couldn't make it."

Gating rule for the method: an ingredient must be neither too broad nor too specific; getting it wrong is diagnosable by attempting the reproduction.

## Summary

Chris Do releases a segment cut from a "How To Learn Anything" whiteboard session and applies his "five ingredients" method to a Jean-Michel Basquiat painting with the in-studio crew. He drills the crew to describe only what they objectively see, then converts loose language into precise design vocabulary, isolates single elements, and distills the work into five reproducible ingredients (line quality, figures, colors, tools, words). He frames the whole method as how he teaches his 14-year-old son to teach himself rather than be told the answer, and closes by grading his son's Basquiat attempt "as a professor of design" — a C-minus.

Speaker: single-speaker teaching monologue/dialogue led by **Chris Do** (first person — "my son," "I teach him," "as a professor of design," references his own whiteboard session and channel). ## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-09-17] The "five ingredients" is a simple way to break anything you want to learn into understandable chunks so you can decipher *why* it works — analogous to breaking a dish you love into its core ingredients so you can, with a little experimentation, make it yourself. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-17] When deconstructing, describe only what you objectively *see*; keep judgment and interpretation out ("self-portrait," "telling a story," "violence" are interpretations, not observations). (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-17] Vague descriptions must be pushed to specific craft language to be usable: "not realistic" → "not rendered," "no shading" → "no value." (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-17] A viewer's felt reaction (e.g. a piece feeling "violent") often traces back to a concrete formal property — here, the rough, jagged, fast **line quality** / "the way the mark was made" — not the subject matter. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-17] Tools influence the work; you can partly reverse-engineer which tools were used from the result (thick brushes vs. a fine sable brush give totally different quality; oil pastel, canvas, paint). (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-17] He uses the method to teach his son to teach himself: when his son asks "Dad, tell me what it is," Chris refuses and sends him back to "do your five ingredients." (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-17] Being stuck usually means an ingredient is too specific or too broad, and the error will be visible in the work — "if you couldn't see this, you couldn't make it." (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-17] Cites his former lettering teacher Niels Lindstrom: "you can't draw what you don't know, and what you don't know is what you haven't seen." (paraphrase, attributed to Lindstrom)
- [2020-09-17] Earlier in the session he deliberately created an "open loop" (a story with no ending) by naming the five ingredients but not explaining them, then "closed the loop" later — an intentional teaching/attention device. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-17] Grading his son's attempt "as a professor of design, not as a father": son got the colors roughly right but was afraid to let them bleed/feel dirty, the subject isn't truly facing the camera (turned via the eyes), and he didn't use the oil pastel — "a solid C-minus." (paraphrase)
