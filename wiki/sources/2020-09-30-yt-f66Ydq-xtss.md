# When To Send A Proposal?

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do walks the full sales timeline (inquiry → qualify → confirm budget → proposal → signed contract → project) and gives a detailed proposal-vs-contract breakdown. The "confirm the price before you write a proposal" principle already lives in the corpus, but the sales-timeline map, the four lead-qualification criteria, and the proposal-vs-contract contents list are worth promoting into `wiki/topics/sales-clients/` and `pricing`. Gate: attribution is clean (single-speaker Chris Do), so promotable.

## Summary

Chris Do maps the sales timeline from first inquiry to the start of a project and answers the titular question: send the proposal only *after* the lead is qualified and after the client has verbally agreed to at least a price range on the phone — never before. He details the four things that qualify a lead (a problem you can solve, budget/affordability, a timeline of need, and mutual desire to work together), stresses that the entire sales cycle expands in proportion to budget, and then contrasts a proposal (a sales/negotiating tool meant to impress) with a contract (a legally binding agreement covering rights, IP, liability, revisions, and scope). He closes with a plug for The Futur's "Perfect Proposal" product.

Speaker: single-speaker monologue by **Chris Do** (first person throughout — "we've qualified leads," "I've nurtured a relationship," "in my opinion," "I strongly recommend," "the $250,000 logo job that we did in 2017"). No co-host or guest speaks. Attribution is clear; not uncertain.

## Key claims

### Chris Do

- [2020-09-30] Proposing too early "can wind up in disaster," and you also don't want to propose too late — timing the proposal is the whole point. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] The sales timeline runs: **inquiry → qualify the lead → confirm budget → send proposal → signed contract → start the project.** (paraphrase, framework)
- [2020-09-30] An inquiry can originate on either side — the client reaches out (e.g., website contact form) or you reach out (e.g., meeting at a trade show); either way the two companies connect. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] A lead is qualified when **four things** are in place: (1) they have a problem you can solve, (2) they can afford your services (budget), (3) they have a timeline of need / a deadline, and (4) there is a mutual desire to work together (both ethically and personally). (paraphrase, framework)
- [2020-09-30] A vague timeline is a red flag: a lead that wants a new website, rebrand, and campaign but is unsure when ("maybe next quarter") should make you "run," or get them nailed down. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] Desire to work together goes both ways — if a client is already a pain before any money changes hands, ask whether you want to continue that in a paid relationship. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] How long qualification takes varies wildly: some leads qualify on the first call or on the spot at a trade show; others took 10–15 calls, meetings, and lunches before becoming qualified. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] The entire sales process scales in proportion to the budget: a bigger budget means a longer sales cycle. The $250,000 logo job The Futur did in 2017 took six months to close. (paraphrase, dated example)
- [2020-09-30] **Talk about price before you put together a proposal** — on the phone, get the client to agree to at least a price range, even if they won't yet commit to the project. Otherwise you're just guessing, and you don't want to guess. (paraphrase, core principle)
- [2020-09-30] If talking about money is uncomfortable, practice saying the words until it becomes muscle memory (practice with your mom, spouse, or kids). A working script: "typically in the past I've seen these projects between [X] and [Y] — does that range work for you?" — which lets you measure the client's budget. (paraphrase; note caption garble: the transcript reads "between twenty thousand and five thousand," likely meant to be a range such as $5,000–$20,000.)
- [2020-09-30] Only once the lead is qualified *and* you have a verbal agreement on budget are you ready to send the proposal. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] A step many people forget sits between proposal and project start: **a signed contract.** Agreeing to a proposal is like agreeing to marry — it doesn't make you married; the license still has to be signed. (paraphrase, analogy)
- [2020-09-30] Because of this, The Futur's proposal template has nothing for the client to sign — you establish the relationship (via contract) before getting to work. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] **Proposal vs. contract:** proposals are for sales and negotiating; contracts are legally binding agreements. He recommends a contract for every big project. (paraphrase, distinction)
- [2020-09-30] A **proposal** contains: basic company info, a problem summary, project cost, a project timeline, and a process snapshot — it is polished, meant to impress, a sales tool. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] A **contract** contains: detailed company info, an offer term (how long the offer is good for), a change policy, client responsibilities, right to reproduce (permission to show the work in your portfolio — "massive," especially with bigger clients), warranties, liability, dispute resolution, a termination clause, intellectual property (who owns the work / whether it's licensed), and the nitty-gritty: scope of work, fees and expenses, and payment terms. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] Right to reproduce and intellectual property are the items creatives skip "every single time" and both are massive; a good contract covers them. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] A contract is enforceable, but you probably won't take a client to court — its real value is forcing the conversation up front on things like number of revision rounds and who owns what when the work is done. Without a contract, there's no natural moment to settle those. (paraphrase)
- [2020-09-30] If money is tight (especially "with everything going on in the world right now" — a COVID-era reference), he recommends building proposals on your own; otherwise he invites viewers to The Futur's "Perfect Proposal" (recently redesigned, with bonuses like written copy and a one-page proposal template). (paraphrase, product plug)
