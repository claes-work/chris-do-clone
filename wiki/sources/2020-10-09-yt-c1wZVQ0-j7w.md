## Summary

An unidentified female speaker explains her method for building new habits: because the
subconscious runs on familiar "programs," any new behavior feels uncomfortable, so the
key is to start with one tiny, almost laughable promise (her recurring example: a
community member "Ally"/"Ali" who committed to drinking one glass of water on waking) and
keep it consistently, day after day, rather than overhauling five life areas at once. She
frames the payoff as psychological, not physical: keeping a promise to yourself — however
small — is "personal empowerment" and rewrites the story you tell about what you're
capable of. She closes by arguing you cannot force another person to change; healing is a
personally chosen journey, though focusing on your own change can either dissolve the
issue or inspire others.

### Unidentified guest speaker

- [2020-10-09] The subconscious creates behavioral "programs," so anything new feels
  unfamiliar and uncomfortable by definition; people scurry back to the comfort zone. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-09] Don't try to change your life "top to bottom in one fell swoop" — changing
  five areas overnight overwhelms the subconscious. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-09] Habits are not built by "white-knuckling" five new things for five days;
  they are built by doing one small thing many days in a row — consistency is the key
  word. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-09] Pick one area so small you could "roll your eyes" at it (e.g., one glass of
  water on waking) and hold yourself accountable to that single promise. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-09] The content of the promise doesn't matter — what matters is the act of
  keeping it; for many people it's the first experience of keeping their word to
  themselves, which she calls "personal empowerment." (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-09] She calls living stuck on autopilot in an unwanted life "habit
  self-betrayal" — a personal "Groundhog Day"; not keeping promises to herself made her
  feel shameful ("what's wrong with me"). (paraphrase; her coined term)
- [2020-10-09] As she kept more promises she "internalized the belief" she was capable of
  more than she thought, which broke down the old self-instructions — the "chip wasn't
  controlling me anymore, I was controlling me." (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-09] You cannot make another person (partner, sister, mom) change; healing is a
  personally chosen journey requiring personal commitment. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-09] Focusing internally on your own healing does one of two things: the issue
  that made you want them to change becomes less problematic because you now show up
  differently, and/or seeing you change can inspire them to want to change themselves. (paraphrase)
