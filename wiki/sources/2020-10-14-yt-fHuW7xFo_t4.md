# How To Be A Better Communicator: Acknowledge & Hold Space

## Summary

A short clip on handling hard conversations without absorbing the other person's
emotional reaction. The framework: when your words provoke anger or upset, don't
empathize (which makes you absorb their feeling) — *acknowledge* it and "hold space"
for the reaction, staying open and present rather than lashing out, defending, or going
passive-aggressive. The through-line is "responding versus reacting" and "the less you
speak, the more effective you are." **The framework is delivered by the guest, not by
Chris Do** — Chris only asks the questions.

**Speaker:** Two speakers. The **guest** (a communication coach) delivers the entire
framework — she says "I tell students it's not about empathizing, it's about
acknowledging" and Chris references "your site about responding versus reacting." The
theme (acknowledge vs. empathize, hold space, responding vs. reacting, mindful
communication) strongly matches recurring Futur guest **Cynthia Kane**, but her name is
not spoken in this clip, so `attribution: uncertain` — treated as CONTEXT. **Chris Do**
is the interviewer; his only contributions are the framing questions. No Chris-attributed
framework material here.

## Key claims

### Guest (communication coach — attribution: uncertain, likely Cynthia Kane) — CONTEXT, does NOT train persona

- [2020-10-14] You're not responsible for how other people react; if your intention was
  good — to be helpful, to lessen others' suffering, to be of service and not
  self-serving — you step back and allow people to have the reaction they need to have.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-14] Hard conversations happen *by* allowing people to be angry, sad, upset,
  and uncomfortable, and holding the space for that. You can share what is true for you,
  and it's okay if others can't hold that, because you can hold the discomfort for them.
  (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-14] When someone reacts angrily, the best action is to **acknowledge** it and
  "match" them — not empathize. The moment you empathize is the moment you absorb and
  take on someone else's fear/joy/sadness, which isn't yours. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-14] Acknowledging can be as simple as "wow, I see that what I said really upset
  you" — that's all you have to say; just acknowledge that your words affected the person
  the way they did. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-14] Less is more in communication: the less you speak, the more effective you
  are, because you choose your words wisely. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-14] In heated moments you *can* stay open: notice the emotion welling up in the
  body that wants to lash out / go passive-aggressive / defend / go quiet, take a breath,
  move the emotion aside, return to the present, and see the person as human — someone you
  respect and support even if you disagree — then ask how you can be helpful to them.
  (paraphrase)

### Chris Do (interviewer)

- [2020-10-14] Framed the topic by referencing the guest's teaching on "responding versus
  reacting," and asked whether the best reaction to someone who took your words the wrong
  way and got angry is to process it rather than respond. (paraphrase — question only, no
  claim of his own)

## Notable verbatim quotes

All framework quotes are the **guest** (attribution uncertain — CONTEXT):

- "It's not about empathizing, it's about acknowledging, because the moment we empathize
  is the moment we absorb and we take on somebody else's fear, joy, sadness."
- "You have to step back and allow people to have the reactions that they need to have."
- "That's how hard conversations happen — is by allowing people to be angry and sad and
  upset and uncomfortable, and holding the space for that to happen."
- "It really looks like: 'wow, I see that what I said really upset you' — and that's it,
  that's all you actually have to say."
- "The less you speak, the more effective you are, because you're choosing your words very
  wisely."
- "See the person in front of me as somebody who is human, who I respect, who I support,
  who I may not agree with, but I still am here for."
