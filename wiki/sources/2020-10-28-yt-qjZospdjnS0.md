# Improve Your Instagram Design: Critiques, Tips & Advice PT 1

★ **L3-candidate** — Chris Do opens with a named, reusable **5-criteria rubric for evaluating Instagram carousels** (clarity, fulfillment, typesetting, concept support, X-factor) and then applies a large set of consistent design-critique principles (proximity, restraint, template discipline, color-palette limits, value-before-pitch, upper/lowercase body copy, breaking content across frames). ## Summary

Chris Do runs part one of his Instagram carousel critique clinic, reviewing six viewer submissions (Daniel, Keith, "Melissa," Goran, Lisa, Amy, Sarah) live and narrating what works and what to fix. He first states the five criteria he judges by, then applies them to each carousel, teaching typography, layout, color, personal branding, and content-strategy lessons as he goes. Recurring themes: break dense content across multiple slides, cut filler, keep a tight consistent color palette when starting out, respect typographic details (real apostrophes vs. prime marks, upper/lowercase body copy, spacing/proximity), make every call-to-action ask for one thing, and lead with value rather than pitching early.

## Chris Do's 5 carousel-critique criteria (framework)

1. **Clear or confusing?** Can I tell what's going on? Is there too much happening on the screen?
2. **Empty or fulfilling?** Are you giving me something, or taking away my time? ("This is critical.")
3. **Typesetting no-nos** — broken typography rules, typos, grammar.
4. **Do the design decisions support the concept?** (e.g. an image of a person screaming under a message about calming down is a disconnect.)
5. **The X factor / special sauce** — what makes it uniquely your own: colors, typography, illustration, or neat inter-frame transitions that link frames into a seamless carousel.

## Key claims

All claims below are **Chris Do** unless noted.

### Content strategy & audience

- [2020-10-28] Read your own carousels out loud, because the way you write and the way you speak differ; you want copy to sound natural when spoken. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Don't pad a carousel with filler to fill 10 slides; if the content doesn't fill the slots, break one dense idea across several frames instead. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] People are not on Instagram to read long blocks of copy — for that they have other places. Break complex ideas across multiple slides; one slide crammed with the "meat" makes readers stop and skip it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] "I want a satisfying but light meal. I don't want a full three-course meal and be very heavy and fall asleep" — a carousel should feel light to consume, not overwhelming. (quote)
- [2020-10-28] Every call-to-action slide should ask the audience to do **one** thing (comment, or save, or like) — not a pile of asks at once. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Don't hard-pitch in a carousel too early; first create value and build an audience, then eventually they figure out they need you and hire you. A pitch is fine at a good ratio (e.g. 12–15 pieces of value to one pitch), but if every carousel is a hard pitch to DM/hire you, that's off. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Forcing people to call/DM you just to learn what a framework means is a weak strategy — teach the thing in the slides and build value that way. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Don't include links in an Instagram carousel — people can't click them there, so there's no point; credit the source briefly (author, year, book) instead of a URL. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] On brand loyalty (disagreeing with a submitter): people are **not** loyal to brands because of mission/vision statements — most people can't recall Apple's or Nike's mission statement. We buy because of how products/services make us feel about who we are and the tribes we're joining. Values must be **lived** and expressed consistently across every touchpoint, not read. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] We don't always buy what we need; we buy things because we want them / because of how they make us feel — purchases are emotional. (paraphrase)

### Personal brand

- [2020-10-28] Your CTA/bio photo should capture your personal brand — a pose or accessory that instantly conveys who you are makes you more memorable. (Chris cites his own hat as signaling a connection "between God and design.") (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] A great personality-rich portrait (good cropping, visual tension, white space) can make you the hero of the final slide; strip everything else back so you're not competing with clutter. (paraphrase)

### Typography & typesetting

- [2020-10-28] **Proximity tells us relationship** — the closer elements sit together, the more you signal to the reader that they're connected and important; watch indentation off a numbered list so the number stays visually tied to its item. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Use a real apostrophe/quote mark, not a **prime mark** (the vertical stick) — real apostrophes and quotes have an angle to them. Chris flags this as a repeated pet peeve across submissions. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Body copy should be upper-and-lowercase, not all caps — all caps is harder to read, especially combined with multiple point sizes and typefaces. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] On a summary/takeaway slide, simplify: fewer point sizes, fewer colors, ideally one typeface, and align left / rag right rather than centered. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Tighten interline spacing (leading — caption garble "letting") and paragraph spacing so a bullet list reads as one cohesive group; spacing elements far apart makes them feel like separate items instead of a group. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Futura is great for bold headlines but (personal preference) not for body copy; pair it with a legible secondary typeface for longer text. (paraphrase, self-flagged opinion)
- [2020-10-28] Choose a simple, contrasting font (Futura, Helvetica, DIN) to pair with a decorative script/calligraphy so legibility of the message isn't compromised. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Believes there's a typographic rule to put a space before an ellipsis, with consistent spacing between the dots — but hedges and openly asks typographers to correct him. (paraphrase, uncertain)
- [2020-10-28] "English is a second language for me, so I'm no grammar Nazi" — self-report while still flagging spelling/grammar to check against sources. (quote / self-report)

### Layout, color & production discipline

- [2020-10-28] Use a template with **master pages** so recurring elements (footer type, logo) never accidentally shift from frame to frame. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Keep icon/color treatment consistent frame-to-frame — when colors are close but not identical, they must match exactly; slightly-off tints "throw off" the viewer ("that little annoying OCD thing that designers do"). (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Footer detail (logo, source type) should be simple and small so it doesn't pull attention from the message; if a logo isn't adding value, just typeset it. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] When starting out, stick to a tight palette — roughly **three colors** plus tints/shades and black/white — applied consistently so people get to know you before you experiment. Chris admits his own feed is "all over the place" but says he can get away with it now given his following. (paraphrase, self-aware)
- [2020-10-28] Don't let a stock photo dictate your design — if an image arrives on an off-brand background (e.g. turquoise), cut it out and recolor it to match your palette rather than building the whole design around the stock color. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Design decisions must support the concept and imagery must match the words — e.g. social-media like/heart icons don't illustrate a line about "brands you purchase from," creating a concept/image disconnect. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] **Restraint / hierarchy:** "if everything stands out, nothing stands out" — use grids, minimal colors, negative space, and simple typefaces to spotlight the subject. (endorsing a submitter's line, which Chris says he believes) (paraphrase + quote)
- [2020-10-28] Linking slides ("inter-frame" moves) — carrying a shape across a frame edge (e.g. an elephant's back legs) creates seamless carousels, but don't let the desire to connect two slides force a design compromise or introduce a third clashing design language. (paraphrase)
- [2020-10-28] Get all design components to harmonize as if commissioned/crafted together, rather than feeling like separate elements pasted in. (paraphrase)
