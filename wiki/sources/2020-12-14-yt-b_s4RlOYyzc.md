# Improve Your Instagram Carousel Design: Content Marketing Advice

## Summary
Part two of Chris Do's Instagram Carousel Clinic critique series (solo, first person throughout). Chris reviews 13 student carousel submissions (square vs. rectangular format), scoring each against a five-point rubric and a "gold standard" storytelling frame. This is a direct extension of the Instagram-playbook cluster — it names and applies the ROT hook formula, the "slippery slide"/flow concept, and the "light but satisfying" balance already queued for synthesis; it also adds the "home stretch" summary-slide convention and a strong headshot/call-to-action discipline. Fully Chris-Do-attributed — trains the persona.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (critiquing named students' work; only Chris's commentary is training material)

## Key claims
[2020-12-14, all paraphrase unless quoted]

- The carousel critique rubric has five criteria [paraphrase]: (1) **Is there a hook/headline** compelling enough to dig deeper; (2) **flow / the "slippery slide"** — do slides 1–10 flow effortlessly; (3) **light but satisfying** — not too dense (head hurts) nor too frothy (no value); (4) **contrast** in every sense (negative space, color, legibility, busyness); (5) **typesetting**, including grammar and typos ("grammar matters and typos do matter... proofread everything").
- The **headline should use the "RO / ROT" formula** — Results (what the audience wants), Objections (too expensive, too time-consuming, too difficult), and Time (how quickly they can learn/do/achieve). "This formula... is from advertising." Any combination of the three yields a stronger hook. [paraphrase; note: he says "RO" and "ROT" interchangeably in the caption — the framework is Results/Objections/Time]
- **Fix a weak headline by flipping the frame toward a desired result.** Example given: "Why you find writing painful" (an unwanted frame) → "Writing doesn't have to be painful" (promises a result / addresses an objection). [paraphrase]
- **Write 10 headlines and pick the best one.** "Sit down and write 10 headlines... get through the bad ones, the obvious ones, and maybe you'll find one that's really good." [paraphrase] — repeated advice given to multiple students.
- **The "gold standard" of content: tell a personal story that evokes an emotional response** (angry, sad, anxious) **and delivers one clear takeaway.** Chris admits he doesn't always hit his own gold standard — "Maybe I hit the silver or the bronze standard." [paraphrase + quote]
- **The "home stretch" is a summary slide** near the end (slide ~9) that recaps all the points, because "there's a lot for us to remember" by the end of the carousel. Note: "home stretch" was a label in his template, not meant to be printed in the final carousel — several students left it in by mistake. [paraphrase]
- **Bookend the carousel**: answer the opening question at the end (mirror the hook on slide 9). Critiques James Saunders' "Why type matters?" for ending on "so why not listen?" instead of answering the opening question. [paraphrase]
- **End with ONE clear call to action** — a question that prompts comments works well. "I also don't recommend having more than one call to action... People will barely do one. So don't confuse them." [paraphrase + quote]
- **Use no more than ~2 point sizes per slide**, especially for beginners — fewer variables simplify the design. Technique: "find the longest thing that you're going to write on a slide and design around that and then work backwards from there." [paraphrase + quote]
- **Reduce oversized headlines / preserve negative space.** Told one student to cut the hook size "by maybe 80%" so the image and text have room to breathe. Filling the entire frame (textures, big type) kills contrast. [paraphrase]
- **Get a professional headshot** for the call-to-action slide. Wide-angle phone lenses distort (bigger forehead, narrower chin); a bad headshot "throws people for a loop" after a good aesthetic. "You can afford to hire a photographer to shoot one or two really good head shots, which you can use on all your social platforms." Praises Gerard Adams' corporate photo (with what looks like Jay Shetty) as "the gold standard for head shots" — but warns against posing next to a celebrity because "you'll pale or... be in this person's shadow." [paraphrase + quote]
- **Prioritize writing/presentation over "design sauce."** Praises Gerard Adams' minimal, single-point-size, flourish-free layout: "less is more... it's more important to focus on the writing and the presentation of the information than pouring design sauce all over it." Add design flourishes "when you're on your 20th carousel," once you start to own the look. [paraphrase + quote]
- **Give value first before selling.** Critiques Gerardo Herrera's carousel for turning into a PDF ad: "we're very sensitive to being sold something... focus on creating value for others first and do this consistently without any expectations for some period of time... give value in abundance before you ask for something." [paraphrase + quote]
- **Err toward simplicity / less copy.** "If you're going to air [err], say less on a carousel versus saying too much." Too much copy breaks the slippery slide (reader gets stuck/fatigued). Chris made this mistake early and saw "a vast improvement in... engagement when I just reduced it down to like one idea per slide." [paraphrase + quote]
- **Craft/typesetting details he flags:** use proper apostrophes/quote marks, not prime marks ("a little pet peeve of mine"); hanging punctuation; match color exactly between slides so gradients tile seamlessly; keep alignment consistent (flush left, since Western reading is top-to-bottom, left-to-right); avoid unnecessary background textures that introduce muddy colors and reduce pop; ensure image and words are thematically married (criticized a random sailboat / mismatched imagery). [paraphrase]
- **Copywriting technique Chris admires: "reframing"** — using similar words and rephrasing slightly to show an idea in a new light (praised Anton Rays' identity-vs-brand carousel: "Identity is a look that identifies you. Brand is an idea you identify with."). Also praised wordplay like "naked on the page" / "undress your thoughts." [paraphrase]
- Endorses a **Brené Brown quote** a student used: "Clear is kind. Unclear is unkind." [quote, attributed by Chris to Brené Brown]
