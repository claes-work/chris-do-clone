# Find Your Passion Find Your Purpose

## Summary
Chris Do delivers a short solo monologue on how to find your passion and purpose. He argues that clarity of purpose is "rocket fuel" that carries you through hard days, and that living someone else's dream fails the moment things get difficult. His central diagnostic: look into your past at what you did in your free time, for free, when nobody was watching. He closes with autobiographical detail about how the clues to his own creative calling were present since childhood even as he denied himself the pursuit. Entirely Chris-Do-attributed — trainable.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] To find your passion, look into your past — at everything you did in your free time when nobody was watching: what you stayed up all night for, what you waited in line to see, do, or buy. It's a strong clue to what you'd do if you didn't have to worry about money. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase] Chris references a Jason Silva TED talk claiming the new "billionaires" will be measured not by the zeros in their bank account but by the number of lives they've positively impacted on earth. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase] Chris (attributing the idea to Zig Ziglar) says you can get everything you want in life if you help enough other people get what they want. His job description is earning a living by helping other people achieve their goals — a feeling that "galvanizes" the work. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase] There are tough, dark, challenging days when you don't feel like getting up — but clarity of purpose and mission acts like rocket fuel to carry you through. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase] Not everyone on earth can pursue their passion, for many reasons and circumstances. Chris acknowledges he (and the listener) are in a place of privilege — they get to choose rather than being forced to do something. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase] Living someone else's dream only takes you so far. Chris observes cousins living their parents' dream who took the easier route when things got hard — e.g. one who was meant to be a doctor/dentist but stopped after not getting into grad school, another who became a nurse instead of a doctor. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase] The passion differentiator: with two people of equal talent, the one who is genuinely driven and passionate will not quit when it gets tough, while the other will. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase] It's hard to "read the label from inside the jar" — without help or enough exposure, you can still be searching for your passion into your 40s, 50s and beyond, and that's okay. Some people start their careers in their 70s or 80s and go on to live wonderful lives. Ideally you find it earlier so you can reap the full benefits. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase] Chris denied himself the pursuit of being a creative professional because he believed being a creative meant a lifetime of suffering, pain, poverty — he wanted a piece of the "American dream" instead. But the signal was there all along. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase / biography] At around age seven, Chris's uncle — the family didn't have much money — gave him a stack of IBM dot-matrix printer paper and a set of Crayola markers as a birthday gift, and he was "over the moon"; his first act was to draw a thank-you card. He didn't care about the Star Wars toys and cars others gave him. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase / biography] Childhood clues to his creative calling: he read comic books and wanted to attend a school for comic-book illustration; he was fascinated by the graphics on skateboards more than the decks or the pro riders; he was enamored with street art and hand-lettering done via airbrush on t-shirts at the fairgrounds. His mother, noticing him looking at them, bought him an air compressor and airbrush with no instructions. (2020-12-21)
- [paraphrase / biography] Chris kept denying his path even when others pointed it out: his commercial art teacher asked if he was considering a career in design after high school (he said no), and a family friend noted his talent and asked if this was what he wanted to do (he said no). (2020-12-21)
