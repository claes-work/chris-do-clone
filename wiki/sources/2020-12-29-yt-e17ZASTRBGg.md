# How To Get Great Clients — Build Better Relationships On Trust

## Summary
Chris Do (solo, direct-to-camera monologue) argues that a "great client" is not one who lets you do whatever you want, but a relationship of trust earned incrementally over many jobs. He diagnoses the creative's core failure: not actually listening to the client, making assumptions, and building work that only satisfies the designer's own vision. He reframes the fix through the golden rule (imagine being the paying customer who is ignored) and stresses that designers were never trained in school to talk to and extract needs from clients. Ends with a promo for his "How to Negotiate" course and the "Socratic six" questions.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-29) A great client relationship is not someone who lets you do everything you want unearned — it is developed over time, with little bits of trust given, earned, then amplified.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-29) Most designers are not really listening to the client during briefings; they nod along to vague, undefined, abstract words ("organic," "simple," "modern") while making assumptions.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-29) When you filter the brief through your own genius/inexperience and present the result, clients say "that's different" / "that's interesting" / "not what we expected," creating cognitive dissonance because you only ever saw your own version.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-29) Creatives get addicted to the high of "nailing it" and the low of feeling their creative soul is being drained — an emotional swing rooted in not communicating.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-29) Chris's business coach told him to "just ask them"; at first he felt asking the client was cheating or robbing him of his craft, but it was the fix.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-29) He applies the golden rule (learned from his father): do unto others as you'd have them do to you — imagine being the paying customer who is not listened to (e.g., a barber who cuts without asking about your hair, style, or texture).
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-29) In real client work, unlike a haircut, there are many stakeholders you're usually not talking to; those people have jobs, rent, and families to protect — so ignoring their needs is inconsiderate and risky.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-29) A great client eventually calls you first, before deciding anything or consulting anyone else, for projects bigger than your proven capability and beyond your portfolio — and that is earned one job at a time.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-29) Design school never trained designers to interrogate the client: at Art Center (in his experience) the brief and parameters were given, and you were allowed to break the brief as long as you justified your interpretation — so extracting needs from a client is a foreign process.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-29) To work with big clients on cool, high-paying projects, you must learn to talk to the client; the path to great clients is focusing on customer service and taking care of clients.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "A great client relationship is not someone who lets you do everything that you want to do unearned. A great client relationship is something that's developed over time — little bits of trust is given, earned, and then amplified over time." — Chris Do

> "Most of the times you're not even listening to the client. You're making a lot of assumptions. There's language that are undefined and unclear and abstract, and you're like 'yeah yeah, organic, got it, simple, got that, modern, just totally nailed it.'" — Chris Do

> "When my business coach told me this — he said 'just ask them' — I thought it was just cheating. I thought it was robbing me of this thing that I didn't even know how to put words into." — Chris Do

> "Do unto others as you wish them to do to you." — Chris Do (attributing the golden rule to his father)

> "At some point a great client calls you up first before they make any decision, without talking to anybody else, for projects that are much bigger than what you are capable of doing... that is a great client, and that's earned one job at a time." — Chris Do

> "We aren't trained to ever talk to the client to extract this from them, so it's a totally foreign process. But if you want to work with really big clients and make a lot of money doing really cool projects, you got to learn how to talk to the client." — Chris Do
