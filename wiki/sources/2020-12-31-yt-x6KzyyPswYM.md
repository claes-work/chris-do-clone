# Letting Go Of Perfectionism

## Summary
Short solo monologue in which Chris Do distinguishes two kinds of perfectionism: a healthy kind (seeing a problem and working iteratively toward how things *should* be) and a harmful kind (the belief, common in creative circles, that "you're only as good as the last thing you create"). He argues the second kind traps creatives into repeating safe work, avoiding risk, and stagnating for years, while those who embrace the probability of failure "zoom right past" them. He models the healthy stance with a personal anecdote about not expecting to dance like Fred Astaire on day one.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-31) There are two forms of perfectionism; conflating them causes harm.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-31) The *healthy* form: seeing a problem and wanting to move things from the way they are to the way they *should be*. It transcends design — it applies to cultural, communication, relationship, even traffic/commuting problems.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-31) Reaching the ideal state is not one giant leap forward but many small iterative steps — climbing a staircase, learning what works and what doesn't, and adjusting each time.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-31) The *harmful* form, popular in creative communities: the belief that "you are only as good as the last thing you create." That is too much weight to carry and is a tremendously harmful thing to think and to repeat to others.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-31) That belief discourages deviation and experimentation, because trying something new carries a high probability of failure — so people just repeat their last success with minor changes.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-31) It is unrealistic to enter something new and expect to be the best at it immediately.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-31) People who embrace the possibility of failure and high risk progress much faster and "zoom right past" those who play it safe.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-31) Playing it safe leads to stagnation — a decade later you realize you still like the same things and design the same way you did as a beginner, because you're stuck.
- [paraphrase] (2020-12-31) Chris expects struggle, awkwardness, and the urge to quit when learning something new; the key is not quitting and putting his heart and soul into it, which is what produces improvement.

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "for me there's two forms of perfectionism the first one is a healthy and a good form of perfectionism which is i see a problem and instead of the way things are i'd like to change it to the way things should be" — Chris Do

> "in order to get to where we want it to be the ideal state it's not one giant leap forward it's many small iterative steps ... it's actually just climbing a stair" — Chris Do

> "you are only as good as the last thing that you create ... that's a lot of weight to carry on your shoulders" — Chris Do

> "i don't know how to dance i do not expect to go to a dance class and just be fred astaire on day one or even on day 55. i expect to struggle i expect to feel awkward i expect to want to quit but i don't" — Chris Do

> "if i don't quit and i put my heart and soul into it i get better and that's what matters" — Chris Do

> "you'll see the people who embrace this idea of failure ... you'll see them zoom right past you" — Chris Do

> "that's the perfection monster that most creative people suffer from — not the first one but the second one" — Chris Do
