# Should You Be Honest Or Polite When Talking To Clients

## Summary
A coaching/role-play session where Chris Do mentors a mentee named Mo (with a third participant, Harigo, as a neutral observer) on how to communicate honestly with prospects and clients rather than defaulting to politeness. Chris argues that politeness is often a mask for fakeness, and that the goal is to be *real* and *respectful* using objective, non-violent language — starting with the language you use in your own head. The bulk of the persona-relevant material is Chris-Do-attributed; Mo's lines are the mentee reflecting back and should be treated as context/do-not-train.

**Speaker:** Chris Do + mentee "Mo" (name uncertain from captions; also a third observer "Harigo"). Only Chris-Do-attributed material trains the persona; Mo/Harigo lines are context.

## Key claims
Dated 2021-01-05, paraphrased unless quoted.

- Most of the time when you are being polite, you are being fake. Politeness dilutes being real; aim to be *respectful* and *truthful* instead. [paraphrase]
- Your language betrays you — "your word reveals your truth." Saying "I *have* to talk about pricing" reveals you view it as a burden you'd rather procrastinate; reframe to "I *get* to" or, better, "it's essential / a necessity for both of us to know what we're talking about." [paraphrase]
- The have-to / get-to reframe applies to everything, from a sales call to chores (e.g., "I *get* to do laundry because my wife deserves a break / because I get to contribute"). [paraphrase]
- On a sales call, set expectations openly and vulnerably: tell the prospect you like to befriend clients, that you don't love talking about money but know it's a necessity to see if you should work together — and that you're fine just grabbing coffee if you're not aligned. Naming this makes everything feel on the table and nothing feels like a surprise. [paraphrase]
- Being polite and "just eating it" (e.g., not complaining about bad restaurant food) actually *hurts* the business owner more, because they never get the chance to correct the problem — behind every business is a person who cares and doesn't know their temp chef is ruining it. [paraphrase]
- Same logic applies to client feedback on creative work: all you want from a client is honest feedback in the moment so mistakes can be fixed, rather than a polite "this is good" followed by silence and never being called back. [paraphrase]
- The opposite of impolite is not necessarily polite — it's *truth*. You can deliver hard feedback respectfully without being rude, personal, or name-calling (don't "pull a Gordon Ramsay"). [paraphrase]
- His business coach's best advice was "say what you think" — but the essential second half is "learn how to say what you think in non-violent terms, without judgment," arriving as an equal, not from superior ground. [paraphrase]
- Aim for emotional neutrality on calls ("I don't want it and I don't not want it") so you can see things as they are and use your full intelligence, rather than pretending or performing to be liked. [paraphrase]
- You can't fake it convincingly anyway — "you're not that good of an actor" / con man; smiling through your teeth shows. [paraphrase]
- White lies about inconsequential things are okay; there's a time to be direct and a time not to be. Chris says he still works on this himself and it sometimes gets him in trouble. [paraphrase]
- Change starts internally: stop using violent language in your own self-talk first, then the external dialogue follows. For most people this is gradual progress, not an overnight cure — give yourself grace and patience. [paraphrase]
