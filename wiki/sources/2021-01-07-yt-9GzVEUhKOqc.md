# 3 Things Designers Get Wrong

## Summary
Chris Do argues that three mindsets sabotage designers: (1) confusing being a designer (a problem-solver hired to serve the client) with being an artist (self-expression), (2) presenting themselves as risky rather than the least-risky option, and (3) showing undifferentiated end-product work with no story. He frames design as service to the client's problem, not the designer's ego, and reframes winning clients as reducing perceived risk. The clip ends with an unrelated podcast promo by Greg Gunn (not Chris — do not train).

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (main content). Podcast outro (lines 122-145) is Greg Gunn, producer of The Futur podcast — context/do-not-train.

## Key claims
Dated 2021-01-07, paraphrased unless quoted.

- A designer accepts money to solve someone's problem, so the work is service, not self-expression — the problem belongs to the client, not the designer. [paraphrase]
- Chris contrasts this with an architect he spoke to who collects art at home but refuses to impose his own taste when designing spaces for clients; artists, by contrast, are trying to share and express a unique emotion. [paraphrase]
- It is a disservice for young creatives to walk around thinking of themselves as artists with that attitude. [paraphrase]
- The client-blaming reflex ("if the client hadn't messed this up it would have been great") uses a skewed lens: what is good for you isn't necessarily good for them. [paraphrase]
- When a client pays a few thousand dollars for a logo for a multi-million-dollar business, they are the ones carrying the real risk if it fails — so caring only about awards or a calling card is egocentric and dangerous. [paraphrase]
- "Rather than try to be original, just try to be good." Being good is the hard, worthy goal. [paraphrase of quote below]
- Clients have options; you're not the only person they're talking to. Buyers avoid the riskiest option. [paraphrase]
- Risk signals include: not being in business long, poor communication, no track record, slow callbacks, and presenting wild/egocentric ideas. [paraphrase]
- Reframe: "We don't try to win clients because we're the best option; we win clients because we present ourselves as the least risky option" — and what reads as least-risky differs per client. [paraphrase of quote below]
- Undifferentiated work absent a story forces you to compete with everybody; to an untrained eye, nuanced differences in color, typeface, and layout are hard to spot, so buyers default to picking the lowest bid. [paraphrase]
- The fix is not being original, risky, or avant-garde — it's telling the story of your process ("here's how I got from here to here, and if you work with me this is how it will play out"), which both reduces risk and creates uniqueness. [paraphrase]

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "When you're a designer you agree to accept money from somebody to solve a problem... it's not our problem." — Chris Do

> "You're using a very skewed and distorted lens as to what is good. What is good for you isn't necessarily good for them." — Chris Do

> "All you really care about at this point with that mindset and that attitude is: is this going to win me an award, is this going to be a calling card that reflects me. It's all egocentric and that's a really dangerous place to be." — Chris Do

> "So rather than try to be original, just try to be good. That's a difficult thing — just try to be good." — Chris Do

> "We don't try to win clients because we're the best option; we win clients because we present ourselves as the least risky option." — Chris Do

> "Undifferentiated work absent a story is undifferentiated — you're going to compete with everybody." — Chris Do

> "You have to give them a reason to care about you. This is not about being original... it's about saying here's how I got from here to here, and if you work with me this is pretty much how it's going to play out. So you're diminishing risk and you're creating uniqueness in what you offer." — Chris Do
