# How To Get Attention In The Market

## Summary
Chris Do speaks (apparently to a small live call/webinar of ~41 people, many of them graduating design students worried about student loans and the job market) about how creatives can get attention and build an audience. He reframes social media as a conversation and value-creation channel rather than a portfolio, argues for niching down and playing the long game, and closes on the emotional/financial trap of desperation with clients — including how to think honestly about free or reduced-rate work. Title says "2022" but the publish date is 2021-01-12; treated as a 2021 source.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (addressing a live audience). All material is Chris-Do-attributed and trainable.

## Key claims
- Your social media is NOT your portfolio; treating it as one limits what you post and ties your self-worth to each post. Treat it as a way to have a conversation with people. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- Chris says his own Instagram seldom shows himself or his work — the goal is only to create value for people who show up. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- By deliberately switching from talking about himself to creating value, he went from ~5,000 followers to nearly 500,000 in a little over a year, after it had taken several years just to reach 5,000. [paraphrase, self-reported metric, 2021-01-12]
- You have to give people a reason to care and a reason to share; showing your work alone is not enough. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- Building an audience is a long-term commitment of creating value over time; eventually you find your voice and rhythm, but you can't get there without taking the (scary) first step. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- The only two ways to lose this game: (1) you give up, and (2) you don't take in any feedback / don't listen — because it's a conversation, and people disengage from someone who won't listen. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- For creatives specifically: learn to write a few sentences, couple them with an image, and add your point of view/"lens" — that combination builds an audience. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- Pick a narrow niche; the more narrow, the better. Commit to obsessing over what that specific group needs and giving them value non-stop, consistently, on the platform that best suits you, in a native way. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- "Stay out of the results business for a while" — play the long game and see who shows up. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- Enter the job market from a position of strength so the job finds you, rather than competing head-on with the flood of graduates, laid-off workers, and freelancers looking for work. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- Knowledge without application is meaningless; he predicts only ~2-3% of listeners will actually act on the advice. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- "Stand out by one-eighth of an inch" — attributed to Art Center professor Errol Gerson (caption garbles as "Errol Garrison"); a small differentiation is enough to be remembered. [paraphrase, attributed, 2021-01-12]
- People end up working with The Futur (guesting, being hired, being paid) because they made "just enough effort" — that one-eighth-of-an-inch reach-out and ongoing genuine dialogue. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- On desperation: decisions made from fear and a scarcity mindset are dangerous; clients can smell desperation a mile away. What's true in personal/dating relationships is true in business relationships. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- Don't negotiate against yourself before the client even speaks / lowers their offer — let the client do their job. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- Chris is okay with free or reduced-rate work — but be honest about WHY. Distinguish three motives and don't mix them up: (1) a marketing expense to "trade up" to a bigger relationship/brand, (2) a feel-good/good-cause project, or (3) not knowing how to negotiate and accepting any terms. Only the last is the bad reason. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- He treats a dream project at reduced rate as a marketing expense, not a business transaction; budget for it (e.g. ~10% of annual revenue on marketing). The "trade up" principle: buy the Adidas/Nike/Issey Miyake project to trade up. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- When doing below-rate/pro-bono work, make the real market value explicit; clients appreciate it, and for non-profits a letter stating market value (his example: $45,000) can be a tax deduction, so you still get value. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]
- Ask for concessions/introductions in exchange for free work (e.g. intros to wealthy/philanthropic patrons) — they won't offer unless you ask. [paraphrase, 2021-01-12]

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "Your social media is not your portfolio. If you think your social media is your portfolio it limits what you can post and how you feel about yourself when you post something." — Chris Do

> "The fundamental difference was I stopped talking about myself. I found a way to have a conversation with people and to give them value." — Chris Do

> "You have to give people a reason to care and a reason to share, and seeing your work is not enough." — Chris Do

> "It feels like you're standing on top of a cliff, it's a hundred foot drop and there are like live crocodiles underneath. But it's all an illusion — there's a glass bridge you don't realize. As soon as you take a step you don't fall." — Chris Do

> "The only way you lose at this game is one, you give up, and two, you don't take in any feedback at all." — Chris Do

> "You only need to stand out an eighth of an inch." — Chris Do (crediting Art Center professor Errol Gerson)

> "I don't want our decision to be made from a place of fear and from a mindset of lacking... when you're really desperate you do desperate things and people could smell it on you, you could see it a mile away." — Chris Do

> "Don't negotiate against yourself before the client has an opportunity. Let them do their job so they can feel good about it as well." — Chris Do

> "I'm not looking at this as a business transaction anymore, I'm looking at this as a marketing expense... I'm a big believer in the up principle. I want to trade up." — Chris Do
