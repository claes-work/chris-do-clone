# 7 Ways To Build & Earn Trust In 5 Minutes

## Summary
Chris Do teaches a compact, seven-tactic method for building trust with a prospective client during the sales conversation, using a boot-buying / house-buying analogy. The through-line is that trust comes from making the other person feel heard: ask small questions, reflect back what you heard, stay honest, and be patient rather than rushing the close. He closes with an empathy exercise — put yourself in the other person's shoes on every question. This is a teaching clip; a workshop participant named Kyle is referenced, but essentially all substantive content is Chris-Do-attributed and trains the persona.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (teaching; addresses a participant "Kyle" and cites his broker "Robert" and an unnamed psychologist friend as examples — those references are context, the coaching is Chris's)

## Key claims
Dated 2021-01-14. All bullets [paraphrase] unless quoted.

The seven trust-building tactics:

1. **Develop a shared vocabulary; don't weaponize the "curse of knowledge."** [paraphrase] Creative people study terminology and coded critique language, then get frustrated when a first-time client "can't just tell me what they want." Instead, help the client build the vocabulary and keep it accessible — communication is about finding common ground, not the words you use.
2. **Ask questions to find out what matters to them.** [paraphrase] Open the conversation with the prospect by asking questions to surface their needs, priorities, and constraints (needs, health issues, past experience, budget, upgrade vs. switch).
3. **Confirm what you heard.** [paraphrase] Reflect it back — "here's what I heard you say, is this correct?" — and go down the list. Chris cites a psychologist friend: the main objective of interpersonal communication is to acknowledge and understand the other person.
4. **Ask small questions; keep the decisions small to reduce risk.** [paraphrase] Big binary choices ("boot A or boot B?") make people freeze; little questions lower the stakes and make the person feel you're paying attention — "that's customer service right there." Transfers to any low-familiarity choice (vacation, restaurant, movie).
5. **Mirror/match, and be willing to say "I don't know" or refer out.** [paraphrase] After understanding what's relevant, match it against what you can actually do; if you're not a good fit, recommend someone else or admit you don't know — there is power in saying "I don't know."
6. **Be honest and transparent.** [paraphrase] People who say what they're thinking earn trust; the evasive and opaque make others uneasy ("I don't know what's happening behind these eyes").
7. **Be patient; don't need to close the sale right now.** [paraphrase] Willingness = patience. Let the client take the time they need, comparison shop, try it on — "let me know when you're comfortable." These things build trust.

Closing practice: **the empathy exercise** [paraphrase] — on every such question, put yourself in the other person's shoes, recall a positive or negative experience of your own, and extract the lesson; this is how you "become a student of life."

Etymology aside [paraphrase]: Chris claims "decide" contains "-cide," meaning "to kill," so deciding = killing off options — which is why people struggle to decide. (Folk-etymology framing offered as a rhetorical device, not a linguistic citation.)
