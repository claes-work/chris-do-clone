# Hardest Thing To Do As A Boss — Fire Someone

## Summary
Chris Do coaches a younger business owner (a Futur team member / co-host, unnamed here) through how to fire an underperforming employee. The co-host admits he has "never fired somebody in my life" and describes "coaching people out" instead; Chris walks through his own emotional experience of dreading firings, then scripts the actual firing conversation he uses. Chris says the company/situation described by the co-host is "a young company" so his own firing experience is greater. Only Chris-Do-attributed material below trains the persona; the co-host's remarks are marked as context / do-not-train. (Note: contains a mid-video Blinkist sponsor read — not persona material.)

**Speaker:** Chris Do + guest (unnamed Futur co-host, marked context/do-not-train)

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] Firing is emotionally the hardest thing a boss does; Chris would dread it for weeks with sleepless nights, and it "hurts me more than it hurts you" — like a parent disciplining a child. (2021-01-18)
- [paraphrase] A firing should never be a surprise from the manager's side: by the time you fire someone, you've given very clear expectations, told them more than once (Chris says at least two or three times) that they're not meeting expectations, with specific criteria and concrete things they could improve. (2021-01-18)
- [paraphrase] Managers must hold an expectation and genuine faith that the person will fix the problem, and give them time to do so before deciding to let them go. Chris coaches this to newer managers on the Futur team. (2021-01-18)
- [paraphrase] The people who get fired almost always act surprised because they're clueless / low self-awareness; high performers have high self-awareness, know how they fit "within the machine," and constantly look for ways to improve. (2021-01-18)
- [paraphrase] Chris's firing script structure: (1) open with context + emotion ("this is going to be a difficult conversation for me, I've toiled over this for weeks/months"), so the person braces for impact; (2) recap the many prior conversations and the specific areas where they repeatedly fell short; (3) deliver the decision — "today is going to be your last day here" — offer a small severance, route them to HR for paperwork; (4) offer to talk later once they've come to terms with it; (5) close with "thanks for your service, I wish you well." (2021-01-18)
- [paraphrase] Overestimating your power over an employee is self-centered: believing you're the only place they could work, or that letting them go (even during COVID economic hardship) leaves them "totally hosed," amplifies your power more than is real — you have to get over it. (2021-01-18)
- [paraphrase] Firing is justified when someone consistently fails even the minimum bar (not going the extra mile — just the minimum) and is "taking up precious resources" that someone who wants to be there could use; people phoning it in should be moved on. (2021-01-18)
- [paraphrase] Most people Chris has fired and kept in touch with went on to do much better at another company more in alignment with who they are — the fit-matters principle applies to employees just as it does to clients: everyone deserves the right company/brand/vendor for them, and it just wasn't a fit here. (2021-01-18)
- [paraphrase] Chris frames it as taking on the risk of hiring, wanting it to work and giving every opportunity, but the employee consistently not living up to the minimum. (2021-01-18)
- [context / do-not-train] The co-host says he has never fired anyone and instead "coaches them out" — anchoring on a strong core value and pointing out the person isn't living up to it; he cites his father, who runs a ~40-person company and has also never fired anyone. (Attributed to the guest, not Chris.)

## Notable verbatim quotes
Chris Do:
> "When I fire somebody it will be because it's been six to nine months of — I've given very clear expectations, I've told them more than once that they were not living up to expectations."

> "You have to have an expectation that they're going to fix this, you have to have faith that they're going to fix this, and give them time to fix it before we make that decision."

> "This is going to be a difficult conversation for me and I've toiled over this for a period of time, like weeks and months, and I don't know how else to say this, so I'm gonna say it."

> "Almost every time I've done this they always act surprised, because people you fire, they're like clueless at this point. The ones that are doing great have high self-awareness — they know how they fit within the machine."

> "We think a lot about the power we have over people and we tend to obsess and to amplify it more than it really is... To think that the only place that could work is for you is a little self-centered."

> "You're taking up precious resources that somebody else could be using, that who wants to be here and want to show up every day."

> "Everybody deserves the right person, company, place, brand, vendor for them — they just need to find it, and it wasn't here with us."

Guest (context / do-not-train):
> "I've never fired somebody in my life, Chris." / "He coaches them out."
