# My Former Student Became A Millionaire Doing This

## Summary
Chris Do narrates (solo) the story of Karen Wang, his former student, TA, intern, and
protégé, who went from burnt-out and nearly broke to a multi-millionaire by launching
Dispel Dice — handcrafted designer dice for tabletop RPG players — on Kickstarter. He
uses her story to argue that creative professionals should apply their creative and
problem-solving skills to their own ideas, not just clients' work, and closes by
plugging his own ikigai ("reason for being") workshop. Only Chris's narration and
lessons are persona data; the biographical details about Karen are context.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (narrating a case study about Karen Wang; Karen does not
speak on camera — all statements are Chris's account)

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-21) Karen Wang was Chris's student at Art Center, became his TA
  multiple times, then interned for him; she had a sizable scholarship but chose to drop
  out because she was already earning as a professional and felt she could do more. *(context — biography of Karen, not Chris)*
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-21) Karen worked as an entry-level storyboard artist at Cartoon
  Network, burnt out and went through a depression; Dungeons & Dragons pulled her out of
  it and led her toward making resin dice. *(context)*
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-21) Chris coached her craft: he told her to light the dice like
  jewelry — a "Tiffany's ad campaign" high-end level; she then bought a camera and
  taught herself photography. This reflects Chris's belief that presentation/quality of
  imagery elevates a commodity product. *(Chris-attributed)*
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-21) Chris was initially skeptical: he told Karen dice are a
  commodity item and questioned whether people really want to buy dice — but urged her,
  "You have a following. Go make something." *(Chris-attributed)*
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-21) Chris's core lesson: when a group is neglected or overlooked
  (here, women in a tabletop-gaming market "designed by boys for boys"), that is
  opportunity. *(Chris-attributed)*
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-21) Chris reads the passion in Karen's Kickstarter copy as the
  signal of authenticity — she "speaks the language" of the tribe (sharp edges,
  inclusions, aesthetic-conscious RPG players), which is why it resonates. *(Chris-attributed)*
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-21) The numbers Chris cites: Instagram/Twitter following grew to
  ~51,000–52,000 followers (started March 2019); Kickstarter launched Nov 6, 2019; hit
  $1.2M in under 24 hours; ended at $2.3M; then raised an additional $875,000 via
  BackerKit. Two weeks before launch she was overdrawn, down to $12. *(context — Karen's
  figures, per Chris; caption numbers unverified)*
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-21) Closing thesis: creative service providers are trained to
  apply creativity to clients' goals — but should apply that same "creative magic" to
  their own ideas. Cites Tony Gaskins: if you don't build your dream, someone else hires
  you to build theirs. *(Chris-attributed)*
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-21) Chris ties the whole story to "eight traits" (passion, hard
  work, focus) and to ikigai — a Japanese concept meaning "a reason for being" — which
  he built into a workshop to help creatives generate their next idea/business around
  purpose, passion, and skills. *(Chris-attributed)*
