# What To Say In A Meeting

## Summary
Chris Do coaches a designer (guest, on a Zoom-style call) who feels unfulfilled at a small-team full-time job where the creative director owns all client contact, while side freelance gives them the client relationship they crave. Chris counsels on how to grow into client-facing work by asking to "shadow" the boss, then reframes the designer's fear of being "the weird person on Zoom who doesn't say anything" — arguing that in high-stakes, choreographed meetings a junior's job is to listen, observe, and take great notes, NOT to contribute ideas. The video pairs this with a broader career meditation on the corporate-vs-flashy-studio spectrum and treating a creative career as a lifelong marathon. Only Chris Do's statements train the persona; the designer's questions are context.

**Speaker:** Chris Do + unnamed guest designer (Chris = coach/mentor; designer = mentee). Outro by Matthew Encina ("Matthew and CNN here"), Chief Content Officer at The Futur — promo, do-not-train.

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) The projects you get aren't always tied to the environment you work in; some studios do amazing, flashy work with normal hours, but the highly-polished studios everyone covets have owners/creative directors with impossibly high standards — so you get less autonomy and fewer "high fives," because whatever you do, their expectation is 3x yours in both quality and volume.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) He cites working under Kyle Cooper in motion design as one of the best-but-brutal shops: demanding and really hard, but many people with great careers passed through his company — you have to decide for yourself whether that's what you want from your life.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) The opposite failure mode: a comfortable corporate job with a nice paycheck but meaningless work — within about 2–3 years your work becomes irrelevant to the job market. He tells of a former student/intern/freelancer who took a corporate job (married, then a kid); his "shine" dimmed, The Futur stopped reaching out, and his creative growth tapered off — hitting his apex in his mid-20s.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) The other extreme is equally sad: people who quit good jobs with people who care about them to chase fame / cool projects / a famous art director, disappear into "holes," produce fantastic work three years later, but come out like "zombies" — a flicker of the flame they used to be. Don't go to either end of the spectrum.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) A creative career is not a sprint, it's a marathon that lasts your entire life — pace yourself and approach it with that mindset.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) If your corporate job leaves you yearning to "sew your wild oats," nothing stops you from doing crazy cool projects after hours — your "5-to-9" and weekends — and posting that work on portfolio sites to grow an audience and develop your own unique style on hypothetical/dream projects.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) That side work pays off two ways: your bosses may notice and put you on newer/cooler business, OR — more likely — clients who want that work reach out to you directly, and you begin your career as an independent business owner.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) No job and no boss should be the answer to ALL your problems — they're the answer to one or two. It's unrealistic to expect that from them, just as it's unrealistic for them to expect you to solve all of theirs. It goes both ways.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) To get into client meetings, raise your hand and ask the boss: "Next time we do a client meeting, would it be okay if I just shadow you?" A shadow's job is to follow — that's all shadows do.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) Ask BEFORE you're ready, so they know your intention — but asking doesn't mean it happens tomorrow; it could be days, weeks, months, or years before the opportunity comes.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) It's unwise to think you're in those meetings to contribute or say something. The quiet person you see as "weird" is actually listening — when you don't know what you're doing, default to listening.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) The best thing a junior can do in these meetings is take amazing notes, then afterward send the boss an organized summary: the to-dos, what was agreed, and who said what, plus a thank-you for being included. Your objective is to witness and observe, not to say "hey boss, I've got an idea, stop talking" — doing that unprompted could end your job.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) High-stakes meetings are choreographed — the more money on the line, the more choreographed. Everyone knows the rules and how to respond, and the last thing they need is a newcomer changing the flow or giving a client a bad response. You're not brought in to win the business, save the company, respond to objections, or offer unsolicited ideas.
- [paraphrase] (2021-01-26) Reframe your self-image: you're not the weirdo on the call who isn't talking — you're the smart person who's listening, watching, taking notes, and being of service to the people who brought you in.
