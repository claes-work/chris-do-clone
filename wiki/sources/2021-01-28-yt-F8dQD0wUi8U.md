# 7 Mistakes To Avoid When You Start A Business

## Summary
Chris Do frames and lightly narrates this video, but the "7 mistakes" are **Tim Brown's** — Chris distills a prior long-form conversation he had with Tim Brown (a designer who transitioned from freelancer to agency owner) into a concise list. The bulk of the first-person mistake narration is **Tim Brown recounting his own experience**, with Chris Do interjecting (notably on value-based pricing vs. deliverables). Because most substantive content is Tim-attributed, this source is **mostly context / do-not-train**; only Chris Do's framing and his pricing interjections train the persona.

## Key claims
Dated 2021-01-28. The 7 mistakes (Tim Brown's list — **context, do-not-train** unless marked Chris):

1. **Depending totally on freelance income before systems/habits for client acquisition are in place.** [paraphrase, Tim] Tim tried running the business during college, quit a restaurant job dramatically with one client, got ~3–4 clients that summer (websites, logos). Doing the freelance→full-time transition at the tail end of a ~2.5–3 year agency stint was far easier because income pressure was lower. Cites Sean McCabe's "overlap method" — overlap freelance with a job until freelance income exceeds the salary. [Sean McCabe named as prior guest.]
2. **Charging hours on bigger projects.** [paraphrase, Tim] Tim's old agency billed hourly; he felt it dishonest — he hates tracking time and often finished faster (or slower) than quoted, creating internal pressure. Prefers charging for value and clear deliverables.
   - **Chris Do interjection [paraphrase]:** Chris says he "likes deliverables too." Tim adds that pure value-based pricing is hard because in marketing the result is obscured — hard to pin a revenue/profit increase — so he prefers clear deliverables with clear pricing, while keeping the framing "this is an investment, not an expense."
   > ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (nuance, not conflict): Chris Do is the canonical value-based-pricing evangelist across the wiki; here he concedes he "likes deliverables too" and the guest argues deliverable-based pricing is more practical for marketing outcomes. Note the softening but it is a guest-led framing, not a Chris reversal.
3. **Not filtering clients to ensure you can exceed expectations.** [paraphrase, Tim] Run an "education and expectations meeting" before a proposal to set what results to expect over a 6–12 month period; if that misaligns with budget, it's not a fit. Also filter by how much value the work is worth to the client's business — a 25% increase on a $500,000 business isn't much money, so a small client shouldn't spend on you even if the work is great. Cold-open echo: "if you can't exceed expectations you probably shouldn't take that client."
4. **Stopping pushing on your own marketing once you have a good amount of business.** [paraphrase, Tim] With 2–3 client projects Tim felt "swimming in business" and dropped all business development; another agency owner warned him to keep client-acquisition systems running regardless of workload. Prescription: have ~3 proven systems for new clients (theirs: referral, Google, LinkedIn) — not 10, but more than one.
5. **Not prioritizing a highly motivating work environment for employees** (significance and mission). [paraphrase, Tim] Owners see clients' lives change up close; back-office employees "toiling away" don't, so work isn't meaningful to them. Hire people who resonate with core values; have a real mission (not corporate "synergy" fluff). Their mission: change clients' lives, save them time, get them more leads, positive community impact — wall slogan "champion the underdog," plus swag, volunteering, donating.
6. **Being the linchpin.** [paraphrase, Tim] References Seth Godin's book *Linchpin*. As an employee Tim wanted to be the linchpin; as an owner he wants NOT to be — centering the company on his own talents is exhausting and you need other talented people (even with egos). Private-equity term "the key man problem": company too dependent on one individual gets stuck. Around the ~10-person mark, start handing off crucial elements; once systems ensure quality, pitch the systems instead of a "unique genius."
7. **Depending on only a couple avenues for new business.** [paraphrase, Tim] Their biggest were organic Google traffic + referrals; diversify to 3–4 solid long-term channels. Now emphasizes: (a) strategic partnerships with people who own the ideal client's attention (e.g., doing content with a roofing CRM; sometimes a signed agreement, sometimes a "$500, just give us a client" referral card); (b) "current client delight campaigns" — a web-design/SEO firm going out to shoot inexpensive video with clients (kitchen remodels, roofs) for social, building tribe/peer-group affinity ("people like us do things like this").

Company/biographical context (Tim Brown's firm, not Chris's): works with construction and home-services companies; web design + SEO shop; lead channels referral/Google/LinkedIn. [Do not attribute to Chris Do.]
