# Brand Strategy Step 2: What You Need To Know Before You Start

## Summary
Chris Do, teaching a live Futur Pro Group session (archive clip), debriefs a mock client/strategy exercise involving a "Stanley" scenario and argues what a brand strategist actually needs to know before running strategy: broad business/marketing literacy plus the discipline to stay unbiased. His central rant: designers waste their potential obsessing over craft minutiae (logo kerning, the Zara/Slack logo) instead of learning business, marketing, and psychology — the knowledge that lets them "engage in a business conversation." He models the facilitator's method: ask good questions, verify claims against data (e.g. look up real email/funnel conversion rates), and treat all findings equally like a researcher avoiding cognitive bias.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (Futur Pro Group teaching session — persona-trainable). A brief unnamed Pro Group member testimonial plays in the outro (lines 240–255) and is **not** Chris — do not train on it.

## Key claims
All dated 2021-02-09, paraphrased unless quoted.

- Winning the exercise depended on the strategist already knowing the domain — knowing that "a list of tens rather than thousands is not going to work"; great questions alone aren't enough, you still need to know what you're doing. [paraphrase]
- In a client engagement, at least one party must actually know the domain. If the client doesn't know what they want AND the strategist doesn't know how to ask, the engagement fails; the strategist has to supply that knowledge. [paraphrase]
- Chris's core rant: designers should stop talking about graphic-design minutiae (e.g. whether the Zara logo has too much/too little kerning) because the industry has trained designers "like monkeys to fight for the scraps" — arguing over things that don't move business. [paraphrase]
- You will never engage in a business conversation if all you do is obsess over logos; it's a "complete effing waste of time." [paraphrase]
- Chris says he's never read a great *design* book; the books that grew him were business/thinking books like Simon Sinek's *Start With Why*, which let him hold deeper conversations. [paraphrase] — book title is a caption-verifiable reference ("start with why").
- Prescription: spend one weekend (a Saturday and Sunday) searching business/marketing topics — social media, conversion rates, sales funnels, ClickFunnels — and you'll be far smarter and ready to talk to clients about what they actually need. [paraphrase]
- "Everything is marketing." If people don't know who you are, don't know your story, and don't attach to it, you'll struggle to get real work, sell a service, or launch a product. The game always has been and always will be about marketing / getting known. [paraphrase]
- Marketing has a "dirty tinge" because people equate it with advertising (someone yelling repeatedly). The new era of marketing is different: use the guideline **inform or inspire** — teach people something, give value, because you're exchanging their time and attention. [paraphrase]
- When your prospective client has a problem doing that very thing (marketing/getting known), that's your opening: "let's talk, because I figured out how to do these things and here's where you're doing it wrong." [paraphrase]
- Even with zero domain expertise, a strategist can add value purely by asking great questions and then verifying — e.g. ask whether 14 signups and 2 engagements is high or low, then look up real email/funnel conversion rates and discover it's ~3–4% (or lower). Keep asking "how do you know this is true? can we verify this?" [paraphrase] — numbers (14 signups, 2 people, 3–4%) are from the caption and reflect an illustrative example.
- A "true facilitator" needs only broad, surface-level knowledge across many things, but must **remain unbiased**. [paraphrase]
- On avoiding cognitive bias (which he researched one weekend to build a talk): from a researcher's standpoint, treat all data/findings equally — don't pre-believe anything true or untrue; play devil's advocate and look at both sides; be skeptical especially when everyone agrees with you. So when the client says a number is "low," ask "are you sure? what if I said it's high?" [paraphrase]
- Chris frames craft-obsession as "shiny object syndrome" — designers willingly run toward it. Instead, spend your time expanding your mind on business, marketing, and social-psychology concepts; every such concept helps you grow. [paraphrase]
- On his own learning habit: he replays the same podcasts (Radiolab, TED Radio Hour) repeatedly because each pass surfaces something slightly new or a different way to phrase an idea. [paraphrase]
