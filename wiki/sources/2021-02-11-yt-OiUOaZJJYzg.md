# How To Price Design & Bid Projects (Part 1)

## Summary

Archive segment from **The Futur Pro Group** in which **Chris Do**, speaking solo to a
member audience, narrates the **evolution of his own bidding methods** across his career
— from a naive solo hourly/day rate, to marking up freelancers, to a formal **cost-plus
fixed bid** learned from a producer named **Karen Rainey**, to the **statement-of-work /
fixed-plus-variable** approach he uses today. He walks through actual (redacted) bid
forms on screen and gives concrete present-day price ranges for strategy, branding, logo,
and messaging work. Part 1 of a 3-part bidding series (pt2, pt3). This is a **mechanics /
"how the sausage is made" pricing talk** and is the useful counterweight to his canonical
value-based pricing doctrine — see contradiction flag below.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (Pro Group archive). One brief unnamed interviewer/interjector
speaks near the end (lines ~452–498, asking clarifying questions about messaging) — that
person is **context, do-not-train**. All numbered/substantive content is Chris Do.

## Key claims

All dated **2021-02-11**. All [paraphrase] unless quoted below. Chris Do speaking.

**The evolution of his bidding (autobiographical):**
- Early career he treated bidding as "the dark art of bidding" — he'd pull a number "out
  of my butt," submit it, sometimes win, often lose, and never knew why. [paraphrase]
- **Stage 1 — solo hourly/day rate.** Coming out of school as a freelancer/solopreneur he
  thought only about his hourly and day rate: ~$40–50/hr ≈ ~$400/day, and he "foolishly
  thought that that was profit." [paraphrase]
- **Stage 2 — multiply yourself with freelancers.** He hired designers, did 3–4× the work,
  and thought the spread between what he paid them and what he charged the client was
  profit. [paraphrase]
- **Stage 3 — Karen Rainey's cost-plus spreadsheet.** He brought in **Karen Rainey**, who
  produced a complicated Excel bid and showed him that merely marking up over what
  freelancers charge is not real profit. This became how the company (Blind) bid "for a
  really long time." [paraphrase]

**Cost-plus / markup mechanics (the Blind-agency method):**
- **Cost-plus = calculate your cost + overhead (salaries, rent, utilities, miscellaneous)
  + a profit margin percentage on top** to ensure profit. Early on he counted only himself
  as the cost, ignored overhead, and added zero profit margin — "that's where I sold myself
  short." [paraphrase]
- He states an agency markup norm of roughly "between 50 to 50" [caption garble — figure
  unreliable; the intended range is unclear, likely e.g. 25–50%]. [paraphrase]
- **The top sheet** is a one-page summary of the multi-page bid; the client-side producer
  "just cares what the grand total is" — are you in the ballpark — then reads the detail
  pages. [paraphrase]
- On the shown bid: line items A–G (creative, production, design & animation, editorial,
  digital compositing, misc.) subtotal to **$18,120**, then a **25% production fee / markup**
  ≈ **$4,530** shown as profit. [paraphrase] (Note: the earlier spoken "120" subtotal is a
  caption/mis-speak; $18,120 × 25% = $4,530 confirms the real figure.)
- **"Design fee"** was a separate arbitrary creative fee added above the production fee —
  "what you pay us just to work on the job above and beyond everything else." He calls it
  "a remnant of the old times" / "feasting times." [paraphrase]
- **Historical production fees ran as high as 40%** at some companies; Blind did 30–35%,
  and fees "have been going down ever since." [paraphrase]
- **Pad the day rates.** They never actually paid producers the "$750/day" shown on bids;
  rates were padded to protect against underestimation and client scope changes, because
  these are **fixed bids** ("we promise to do the job for this price no matter what unless
  the client changes something"), not open cost-plus. Freelancers saw the padded rates. [paraphrase]
- They modified the Excel so a **zero-quantity line hides its rate** — you don't want to
  reveal all your rates. [paraphrase]
- **Charge for workstations** (Karen's lesson): a 2D Mac workstation and a beefier 3D
  render PC each get a day rate (renting, software, plugins, setup) — otherwise "you're
  actually not making the profit you think you are." [paraphrase]
- **Charge for everything you touch:** shipping/messenger, working meals ("in advertising
  clients will pay for your working meals"), web posting, FTP transfers — any service that
  costs you money gets billed back. [paraphrase]

**Fixed-bid pros/cons and the migration away:**
- A pure fixed bid (no breakdown) makes it **hard for the client to negotiate or audit** —
  a pro — but **requires a very clearly defined scope**, or "you're going to get eaten up
  alive." Explicit warning to software people about scope. [paraphrase]
- They migrated away from the **AICP bid form** [caption: "aice bitform" — this is the
  AICP commercial-production bid form] toward a one-sheet. [paraphrase]
- Rationale for dropping the man-hour breakdown: on a fixed bid, showing hours only invites
  **cost consultants** to mark up your bid, cut rates, and drive the price down; and the
  client won't pay you *more* if you undershoot the hours. So instead they **invest heavily
  in writing the Statement/Scope of Work (SOW)** in great detail, list **assumptions**
  ("client to provide…", "bid does not include…") and terms, and promise the deliverable
  for one price. Clients push back less on this. [paraphrase]

**How Chris prices today — fixed-plus-variable one-sheet (present-day, 2021):**
- He now uses a **fixed + variable** bid: fix the price on parts he knows well, leave the
  rest variable. Sends the client a broad visual schedule too. [paraphrase]
- **Strategy: $20k–$40k**, priced to the client — smaller client $20k, "really small" $10k,
  but he dislikes doing strategy for $10k ("too much work for me"). [paraphrase]
- **Brand definition & positioning: $20k–$30k** — deliverables: 3 customer profiles,
  positioning statement, competitive audit, 3 stylescapes. [paraphrase]
- **Logo design: $16k** — logo, color palette, primary & secondary typefaces, brand
  guideline, font specs; goes higher with complexity. A recent **logo with a mascot** was
  quoted ~**$40k–$50k**. [paraphrase]
- **Application design: ~same** — typically 3 items (business card, letterhead, envelope). [paraphrase]
- **Messaging: $12k** ("12 grand") — deliberately **separated out** from discovery because
  bundling it stalls sign-off (clients withhold "job complete" over copy) and invites
  fuzzy-scope revisions/secondary copy. Deliverable: tone, voice, value proposition
  synthesized into **3 concepts** ("it takes three for them to see the pattern") — a few
  powerful sentences / words against an image on ~3 slides. Includes 3 ad concepts worked
  until approved. [paraphrase]
- Closing principle: **"all of these things are variable depending on the size of the
  client and who you are and the value you're going to deliver."** [paraphrase]

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (method vs. canonical doctrine): Most of Part 1 teaches **cost-plus /
> markup / padded-day-rate fixed bidding** — the "price the job" mechanics of his Blind
> agency era — which sits in explicit tension with his signature **value-based pricing
> ("price the client, not the job")** doctrine ([[wiki/topics/pricing/pricing]]). Chris
> frames this as an **evolution/history**: cost-plus was how they used to bid, and the
> present-day section (strategy $20–40k, logo $16k, messaging $12k priced to client value)
> is the value-based end state. Read the cost-plus material as **dated historical method /
> what agencies are used to seeing**, not current best-practice advice. This is consistent
> with the pricing hub's noted position evolution (loose cost build-up → formalized method).

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do:

> "Before I used to look at is the dark art of bidding… I just went with my gut, I pulled
> the number out of my butt, broke it out and submitted it to the client."

> "If I'm just making back the money on top of what my freelancers are charging me, it's
> not really profit."

> "The truth is we never paid our producers 750 a day, and they saw the bids all the time.
> It's because we had to pad out all the day rates because we had to protect ourselves."

> "You have to charge for all your workstations, because otherwise you're actually not
> making the profit that you think you are."

> "If all you do is show a fixed bid and you don't break it down… you have to have a very
> clearly defined scope, and you guys that are working in software are going to need to
> really take care… you're going to get eaten up alive if you don't have a clearly defined
> scope."

> "They have cost consultants going through and saying, well, we don't pay for that, and
> that's too high of a rate. So all they do is go through your bid, mark it up, and send it
> back to drive it down."

> "All of these things are variable depending on the size of the client and who you are and
> the value you're going to deliver."
