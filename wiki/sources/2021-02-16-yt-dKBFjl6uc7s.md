# How To Bid (Part 2) — Undefined Scope

Part 2 of a 3-part bidding series pulled from The Futur Pro Group archives.
Cross-links: Part 1 (`OiUOaZJJYzg`, 2021-02-11) and Part 3 (`kt2oLrl1EHY`, 2021-02-18).

## Summary

Chris Do answers Pro Group questions about how to bid when a project's scope is still
undefined. His core move: don't quote a fixed deliverable price against an undefined
scope — front-load **discovery and strategy** as paid phases 1 and 2 so the scope gets
defined before design/build is priced. He explains that he qualifies budget through the
sales conversation (deck + follow-up document) rather than showing deliverable examples
inside the bid, and that he screens out clients who can't afford or aren't interested
before spending time on a call, referring unfit clients to his network. He credits Blair
Enns's "retreat and follow" technique for the constant qualifying.

**Speaker:** Chris Do (primary, persona-eligible), in Q&A with an **unnamed Pro Group
facilitator/interviewer** and a member named **"Gabrielle"** who ask the questions
(their questions = CONTEXT, not persona training data). The closing segment is a **Futur
Pro Group promo** by an announcer plus a **member testimonial** — both CONTEXT /
do-not-train.

## Key claims

Chris Do, `[2021-02-16]`, paraphrased unless quoted:

- Discovery and strategy (phases "1 and 2") are effectively non-negotiable. He has run
  jobs both ways; when clients skip paying for discovery/strategy he ends up having to do
  the work anyway, and the process becomes more complicated. [paraphrase]
- Analogy: "We can build you any kind of house — I just need to know what kind of house
  and who's going to live in it." Discovery/strategy is how you learn that before you
  build. [paraphrase of verbatim below]
- He onboards clients by showing documentation/results of prior work; that is why keeping
  documentation of past projects matters — prospects see it, are impressed, and hire.
  [paraphrase]
- He does **not** show deliverable examples inside the bid/budget document. Instead he
  sends a mostly-generic **sales deck** (lightly customized, not hours of work) as the
  first sales tool. [paraphrase]
- Screening line: if you think this is a good fit after reviewing the deck, get on the
  phone and I'll walk you through it — otherwise no call. He does not want to waste an
  hour of his and the client's time on prospects with no interest. [paraphrase]
- Money is discussed throughout the dialogue, so by the time he quotes he already knows
  roughly what budget the client has. [paraphrase]
- If he senses the client can't afford it, he states the cost directly and asks whether
  they're comfortable / have that money to spend; if no, he tells them they're not the
  right client and refers them to people in his network. [paraphrase]
- He is "constantly doing the retreat-and-follow technique from **Blair Enns**" —
  retreating repeatedly to test whether the client is genuinely interested. [paraphrase]
  (Caption reads "Blair ends" — corrected to **Blair Enns**, author of *The Win Without
  Pitching Manifesto* / *Pricing Creativity*.)
- He changed his team's default: producers used to reflexively ("Pavlovian") schedule a
  call after sending the deck; he now instructs them **not** to auto-schedule a follow-up
  call unless there is real interest and perceived value. [paraphrase]
- Sales engagement is not pestering the client; making a prospect feel obligated to get
  on a call wastes time when they can't afford it or have no interest. [paraphrase]

> generation/round of work). Treat the exact wording as unreliable.

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Chris Do (persona-eligible):**

> "We can build you any kind of house — I just need to know what kind of house and who's
> going to live in it."

> "I don't show it in the bid. I send over a deck ... and the deck is our sales tool, and
> it's fairly generic ... I'm not spending hours working on it."

> "If you think, after looking at this, this is a good fit, let's get on the phone and
> I'll walk you through this deck. So I know if they don't want to call me back, it's
> fine — I don't want to waste my time."

> "This is what it's going to cost — is this what you're comfortable with? Do you have
> this kind of money to spend? If they say no ... well, we're not the right company for
> you; there are people in my network I can refer you to."

> "I'm constantly doing the retreat-and-follow technique from Blair [Enns]. I'm
> retreating all the time to see if they're interested."

> "A lot of times I think in sales you think engagement is just pestering the client, and
> I don't think it is that way at all. I don't want to ... make them feel obligated to get
> on the phone with me."

**Interviewer / "Gabrielle" (CONTEXT — not persona training data):**

> "Are they non-negotiable for you? ... is it required to do the number one and number
> two for you at this point?"

> "For smaller clients who may not understand what [phase] means, do you show them
> examples in your bidding, in the budget, so that they understand what they're going to
> get?"

**Pro Group promo / member testimonial (CONTEXT — do-not-train):** closing segment
advertises the Futur Pro Group; a member recounts landing a first job at "$25,000 ...
five times more than what we usually would charge."
