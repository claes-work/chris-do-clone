# Easy & Painless Way To Bid (Part 3)

## Summary
Part 3 of a 3-part Futur Pro Group segment (from the archives) in which Chris Do answers member questions about how to bid/estimate work after a discovery ("Core") session. Chris explains that pricing is not a separate later step: during discovery he runs a prioritization exercise, ranks the client's needs on an important/urgent grid, and assigns budget numbers to each item live in the room — so by the time he recommends next steps he already knows the client's willingness to pay. The bulk is Chris Do solo/facilitating with members (Daniel and others) asking questions. A closing ~45-second Pro Group promo and a member testimonial are non-Chris material.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (answering Pro Group member questions; member questions themselves are context). The closing Pro Group promo voiceover and the member testimonial (lines ~181–224) are **non-Chris / context — do-not-train.**

## Key claims
Dated 2021-02-18, paraphrased unless quoted:

- Pricing should be baked into the discovery/Core session, not sent afterward as a menu. Chris runs a prioritization exercise during discovery, ranks needs together with the client, and assigns prices to them there — so next steps are easy because the client has already determined what matters most and budgets are already blocked out. [paraphrase]
- Clients initially say they "need everything." Chris's move: write everything down, then score each item. [paraphrase]
- He replaced the "desirability vs. doability" matrix (which he says confuses him and the client) with a simpler **important + urgent grid** — "is it important (will it have an impact) and do we need it right now." High-scoring items get marked. [paraphrase]
  > ⚠️ Note: the caption transcribes "desirability and doability"; this appears to reference a desirability/feasibility-style prioritization matrix Chris used previously and abandoned as too confusing.
- After scoring, in the room he asks the client to put a budget next to each item — "what do you want to spend on this and what will it cost" — doing two jobs at once: identifying next steps and their cost. [paraphrase]
- He guides clients to numbers by asking what they spent before and how it turned out (e.g., a logo they paid $3,000 for that went "not so good"), then giving an experience-based range and landing on a figure the client confirms feels right (example figure ~$20,000 for identity/logo). [paraphrase]
- As facilitator, Chris positions himself as **objective and an advocate for the client** — he plans not to do the work himself, so he'll argue to spend less on some things and more on others based on his point of view, not self-interest. [paraphrase]
- Answering member Daniel's question about needing experience to gauge numbers: yes, experience matters, and he's careful not to quote a number too low that he'd have to walk back later. He warns that recommending a price nobody can actually deliver at (e.g., a logo for $20,000 that no one can do for that) creates a problem. [paraphrase]
- The budget exercise is diagnostic — like a Rorschach test / "monopoly money" — it reveals the client's real priorities and their "pain threshold" (what they're used to spending). Example: a client saying a trade-show booth is worth $60,000 tells him something he'd never have learned if he hadn't let them talk. [paraphrase]
- When a client lowballs something important (e.g., "we can do the video for six thousand bucks"), he pushes back: you can, but it won't be the video you want — so let's move money around and be smart about allocation. [paraphrase]
- He frames this whole exercise as "business design," sketching/playing with numbers to allocate spend intelligently — and says doing it live with the client demonstrates even more value. [paraphrase]
- One worked example: a client's "marketing needs" list where they wanted to spend $85,000 on their website. [paraphrase] (Watch caption garbles: "ex's marketing needs" / "sbj one" are likely anonymized client labels, not real names.)

## Notable verbatim quotes
Chris Do:
> "During the core session I do a prioritization exercise and that allows me to define what their needs are and we do it together and we rank them and now I assign prices to them. And so I already know what they're willing to spend at that point."

> "Instead of using a desirability and doability which always confuses me and the client, I just now do the important and urgent grid — is it important to us, meaning it'll have an impact, and do we need it right now."

> "So I'm doing two jobs at once. I'm helping to identify what the next steps are and what it's going to cost to do."

> "When I come in as a facilitator I try to be as objective as possible, as if these numbers don't mean anything to me, because I don't plan on doing any of this work. I'm an advocate for my client."

> "This is just the beginning of understanding how important something is to them and what they're used to spending — their pain threshold, if you will."

> "The interesting part is this is like some kind of Rorschach test where they say the trade show booth is really important to us and we want to spend sixty thousand dollars — I wouldn't have known that if I didn't let them talk."

> "[On a client wanting video for $6,000:] Well, you can, but it won't be the video that you want. So let's be smart, let's move some money around."

> "This is to me like sketching — like business design and marketing. We're just playing around with numbers to see how we can be smart about where we spend the money. And I think when I do this with our clients I'm demonstrating even more value."
