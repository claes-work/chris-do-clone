# Boost Your Productivity With This Simple Time Management Exercise

## Summary
Chris Do runs a live whiteboard/exercise session teaching a hands-on time-management method: treat time literally like money (a finite, non-renewable resource), physically map how you spend your day on Post-it notes (one yellow note = 1 hour, one blue = 30 min), then record your actual time for a day to change your behavior through self-observation. He walks two Futur team members (Ricky/"Peter" and another) through auditing their day, adding the tasks needed for a new two-week goal, and cutting existing activities (notably video games) to make room. Chris supports it with a personal story about quitting video games entirely and several quotable maxims about not "majoring in the minors."

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (teaching/facilitating). Two team members are participants — a member states his goal ("I want to create content, a YouTube video," lines 101–106) and another discusses cutting video games (lines 183–252). ## Key claims
All dated 2021-02-20, paraphrased unless quoted.

- Chris's usual advice is "wake up an hour earlier, go to sleep an hour later," but this video is for people who can't do that and still want to get more done in the same waking hours. [paraphrase]
- Think of time literally as money: imagine spending your discretionary time (collected on weekends/after work) the way you spend money, and you'll make different decisions. Throwing away time is like a rich person throwing $100 bills into the street. [paraphrase]
- Time is a non-renewable, finite resource regardless of wealth; we don't know when we'll "expire," so act as if you must take advantage of every moment. [paraphrase]
- The exercise, step 1: write down every task you do in 60-minute chunks (yellow Post-it = 1 hour; trade one yellow for two blue 30-min notes to split a task) to snapshot how you *think* you spend your day — this records assumptions, teaches nothing yet. [paraphrase]
- Step 2: tomorrow, actually record how you spend your time in 30-minute chunks as the day happens. The act of recording yourself changes what you do — like athletes and bodybuilders who measure and record everything; awareness ("somebody's watching you") drives better decisions. [paraphrase]
- Step 3: pick something you want to accomplish — a two-week sprint (or four-week, or half-year goal) — and write the granular sub-tasks it requires on Post-its (research, write, produce, shoot, edit, post). [paraphrase]
- Estimate the total time the new goal needs, then divide across the days/week to find the daily commitment (worked example: a member estimated ~10 hours total for one weekly YouTube video → ~2 hours/day). [paraphrase] — numbers (16-hour work week, 5-hour work week, 10 hours, 2 hours/day) are from the caption and are illustrative of the participant's example, not Chris's own schedule.
- To add the new commitment you must *eliminate* an equal amount of existing time — physically transfer/remove Post-its so you can see the tradeoff. Making the tradeoff visible makes the hard prioritization decision easier. [paraphrase]
- Chris's personal story: he used to play video games "avidly" (Halo, Call of Duty, Skyrim), at least ~2 hours/day, but cut them out entirely once he got serious about The Futur; he keeps a brand-new PlayStation 4 unopened in the box and refuses to set it up because playing isn't worth the time right now. [paraphrase]
- Core tension: people say they want to accomplish a goal but won't give up things they *like* for things they *need*; adding what you want requires giving something up. [paraphrase]
- Maxim: "being busy is the biggest distraction from living" (a quote Chris found) — busyness keeps us from asking whether what we're doing is important or contributes to our life. [paraphrase]
- "Don't die living someone else's dream" (Chris attributes this to "Bronnie Ware" — caption "brownie ware," almost certainly Bronnie Ware, author of *The Top Five Regrets of the Dying*): e.g. pursuing a degree you don't love is living someone else's dream. Find what makes you happy sooner so you have more time to invest in it. [paraphrase]
- Chris references his own earlier exercise "how to do what you love — the ikigai exercise" (caption "icky guy" = ikigai) as prerequisite work to make sure the goal is truly yours. [paraphrase]
- Consolidate the largest block of free time possible — Chris credits this to Peter Drucker's *The Effective Executive*. [paraphrase]
- "Don't major in the minors" (Chris credits Jim Rohn): we make a major effort to achieve minor things; something with a looming deadline or someone barking in your ear may feel important but won't add up to much. [paraphrase]
- How you avoid majoring in the minors: say **no** to almost everything; be the thriftiest person with your time. [paraphrase]
- Closing frame (recurring Chris sign-off): "you're not defined by the past; the future is what you make it." [paraphrase → see quote]
