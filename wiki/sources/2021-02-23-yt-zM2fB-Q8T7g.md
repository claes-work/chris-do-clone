# Running A Profitable Business (In 11 Minutes)

## Summary
Chris Do teaches (whiteboard-style, with a small live audience and one participant "Ricky") the distinction between three words creative business owners conflate — **cost**, **price**, and **value** — and how profit and risk sit between them. Core throughline: cost is what it takes to make something (no profit); price is cost plus profit; value is subjective and determined by the buyer. When perceived value exceeds price, the buyer pays. He argues that charging only your costs (e.g. hourly billing) leaves you at break-even or losing money, and that profit is compensation for the risk you assume on fixed-fee work.

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (teaching to a small audience; a participant "Ricky" and unnamed audience members give short answers — those are context, do-not-train; only Chris-attributed material trains the persona).

## Key claims
All dated 2021-02-23, paraphrased unless quoted.

- When clients ask "how much does this cost," what they actually mean is "what is the **price**" — the two words get conflated. [paraphrase]
- **Cost** = what it takes to make something, measured in time, effort, and materials — inputs like raw materials, salaries, rent, interest, taxes, duties. It's the money the company (i.e. you) spends to produce a product or create a service. [paraphrase]
- If you charge clients only what it costs you to make, the best you can hope for is to **break even**. [paraphrase]
- Hourly billing effectively bills only the cost of your time and omits overhead you're absorbing — your own laptop, Creative Cloud subscription, desk, and the rent you pay to do the work. [paraphrase]
- Personal anecdote: at 16 or 17 Chris started a design firm making t-shirts while working at a silk-screening company; he charged people what it cost him to make, assumed he had profit, and quickly realized he was running near break-even or actually losing money. [paraphrase]
- Charging only your costs will bankrupt you because cost doesn't account for mistakes, clients changing their minds, delays, mishaps, or things you didn't plan for. [paraphrase]
- **Price = cost + profit.** [paraphrase]
- A common misconception: people think an item's value is the labor it takes to make it (100 hours at $1/hr = $100) — but that ignores profit. [paraphrase]
- Creatives feel "sheepish" talking about profit in front of clients, but profit is **implied** in any price. [paraphrase]
- There's no easy rule for how much profit to include. Illustrative "on the street" figures floated: ~15–20%, and he sketches **25%** profit as one pie example. [paraphrase] — percentages are illustrative teaching examples, not prescriptions.
- Profit margin range is dramatic: in some cases he has **marked a project up 300%** and can charge in multiples / orders of magnitude more than cost; margins can be as little as **5–10%** (some businesses survive on that) or as much as **80%** or even more, depending on the business. [paraphrase] — watch these figures (300%, 5–10%, 80%) as caption-transcribed numbers.
- A big factor determining profit is **risk**. Chris cites **Peter Drucker**: "all profit comes from risk." [paraphrase] — attribution to Drucker is Chris's framing.
- On hourly billing, the **client** bears the risk, not you: work 40 hours, get paid 40; work 5, get paid 5. Eventually a client says "I'm taking all the risk — give me an estimate / one fixed fee." [paraphrase]
- On fixed-fee work you assume the risk of unknowns (client changes mind, dislikes the work, a new stakeholder alters things, "unknown unknowns"), so you **add more profit / raise the price to accommodate the risk** you're taking. [paraphrase]
- **Value is subjective** and it's the **buyer** who determines it, not the seller — most people wrongly assume the seller sets value. [paraphrase]
- Division of authority: the **seller determines price**; the **buyer determines value**. The buyer determines value because they don't have to buy — they can decline a price and ask "is this fair, based on my needs and wants?" [paraphrase]
- Pricing trade-off: price too low and you may sell a lot but margins are thin; price high and you may sell less but with big margins. He references a prior pricing episode: sometimes to sell **more** product you actually **raise** the price. [paraphrase]
- Setting a high price is **not** taking advantage of the customer: a buyer won't buy unless they see value, and you can't force (through "all powers of manipulation") someone to buy something they don't value. Example: a $10,000 flawless diamond is genuinely valuable to some buyers and "just a shiny rock" to others (Chris says he doesn't value diamonds) — no amount of De Beers advertising changes that. [paraphrase]
- Summary equation of the talk: cost = total cost to make (no profit); add profit → price; how much price to charge depends on how much profit you want, how much risk you're taking, and how well you understand what's **valuable to the customer**. [paraphrase]
- His own client intake move: when a customer says they need "an X," he asks whether it's a big, important problem worth solving — if not, he steers toward one that is, because he wants work with **material impact** that "moves the needle" for their business. [paraphrase]
