## Summary

A ~5-minute Futur Pro Group archive segment in which **Chris Do solo** breaks down Dan Mall's book *Pricing Design* (caption garbles his name as "dan maul") and applies it to how a designer should talk about money on a sales/discovery call. Chris walks through the value-pricing model (cost → profit/price → the client's profit as untapped value), reads a client-vs-provider script verbatim from the book, argues for a risk-based sliding scale, and shares a two-page list of discovery questions to ask clients — most notably reframing "budget" as "money." He closes noting the book pairs well with (and is more plain-spoken than) Blair Enns (caption: "blair").

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (teaching/coaching). One brief closing testimonial by an unnamed Pro Group member is context / do-not-train.

## Key claims

_All dated 2021-03-03 (source publication). Paraphrased unless quoted below. [paraphrase] unless marked as a direct quote._

- Standard pricing looks only at cost → anything charged above cost is profit → that sets the price; it ignores the **customer's profit**, which is the value you create and "a whole new chunk of money" to think about. [paraphrase]
- The service provider's job is to create as much value as possible for the customer — full stop; stop obsessing over whether the graphics are cool, on-trend, or the right color, because "none of that matters" to pricing value. [paraphrase]
- Chris genuinely wants clients to spend as much money on him as possible, because he believes every dollar they spend returns ~10x in results; his job isn't to sell them anything but to help them, and being paid is what lets him allocate time to their project. [paraphrase] [self-reported]
- He often helps clients win new business, so the benefit to the client far exceeds his fee. [paraphrase] [self-reported]
- **Sliding scale by risk:** price should scale with company size because bigger companies carry greater risk — a small company survives a mistake, a big one eats huge implementation/printing costs, and a publicly traded company can see its stock drop if you get it wrong. The exact same website can be $40,000 or $300,000 depending on the client's risk and your role in it. [paraphrase]
- Two additional surcharges from the book: a **rush charge** (same work, less time) and the **"pain in the butt" charge** for difficult, nitpicky clients. [paraphrase]
- On nitpicky clients: it's usually not that they're bad people — Chris feels frustrated mostly because *he didn't charge enough*; he goes back and asks for more money, it works out, and he's happy again. [paraphrase] [self-reported]
- **Reframe "budget" as "money":** ask "how much money have you set aside for this project" — Dan Mall's point (which Chris says he's heard elsewhere too) is that the word "budget" makes it sound cheap; call it what it is, money, because that's tied to value. [paraphrase]
- Discovery is the mechanism that reveals value: spend as much time as possible understanding what the client needs and wants, then propose a few options that are simultaneously "a steal" for the client and lucrative for you. [paraphrase]
- Don't walk in trying to sell everything — ask what they actually want you to do (design-build-launch vs. design only) and focus on the parts they need; the answer also reveals who else they're considering. [paraphrase]
- Timelines are often artificial; ask whether a real event depends on the launch, otherwise you'll rush and burn out on a fake deadline. [paraphrase]
- When a client has an in-house team, position to work *with* them, not replace them — day-to-day work is low-paying and eats your time; showing you collaborate with internal teams reassures the client. [paraphrase]
- The "golden" closing question from the book — "anything else we should know about?" — Chris phrases as "am I not asking you a question that I should be?", which surfaces things the client hadn't volunteered. [paraphrase]

### Discovery questions Chris endorses copying verbatim from the book [paraphrase]
- What's the time frame — does a certain event depend on this project launching?
- What are you looking for from us: design-build-launch the whole site, or only design (you have developers/partners lined up)?
- Have you already started any part of the project (a new logo, rough ideas)?
- How large is your team, and what roles do you envision on your end?
- How did you hear about our work, and what interests you about it / which projects are you keen on?
- How much money have you set aside for this project?
- Are you talking to other companies about this project — might we ask how many, and what do you like about their work?
- Anything else we should know about?

## Notable verbatim quotes

_All spoken by Chris Do unless noted. (Some lines are Chris reading Dan Mall's book script aloud — noted inline.)_

> "I want the clients to spend as much money on me as possible, because I believe every dollar they spend they will get back ten times the results."

> "My job is not to sell them anything. My job is to help them. In order for me to help them, I need them to pay me so I can allocate time towards working on their project."

> "You can do the exact same website for forty thousand dollars or three hundred thousand dollars. It's all based on the risk that the client takes and what your role in that is going to be."

> "Every time I have one of those clients that are nitpicky... I feel frustrated mostly because I didn't charge enough. And so I go back and I charge more, I ask them for more money, and it works out — then I'm really happy again."

> "He makes a big point to refer to it as money and not budget... when you put the word budget it makes it sound cheap. Call it what it is — it's money. How much money have you set aside? Because that's tied to value."

> "The way I've asked this question in the past is: am I not asking you a question that I should be? And they're like, oh yeah, you didn't ask me this — and then they tell you."

> "Your job as a service provider is to create as much value as possible for your customers. Period."

Chris reading Dan Mall's book script aloud (client ↔ provider):
> "The client: 'What's your rate?' Me: 'I don't have one.' 'So how do you know what to charge me?' 'I'll spend as much time with you as I can to understand what you need and want... based on what I hear I'll come up with a few options that simultaneously seem like a steal for you and are lucrative for me.'"

_Closing testimonial — unnamed Pro Group member (context, do-not-train):_
> "Our very first job we got at twenty-five thousand dollars, which is actually five times more than what we usually would charge for work."
