## Summary

A short excerpt from a **Clubhouse** room in which **Chris Do** coaches an unnamed participant (a designer/copywriter, context / do-not-train) who is upset that a client's internal team has, months after delivery, cut-and-pasted and rearranged the messaging/copy Chris's questioner delivered so that it no longer resembles the recommended quality. Chris reframes the problem through **ownership** (the client paid for it, so they own it and can do what they want with it) and **detachment** (a paid deliverable is like a gift — once given, you release attachment and expectation), then offers a practical, non-defensive path forward: a friendly three-month check-in. Greg Gunn's closing podcast promo is an ad read (context / do-not-train).

**Speaker:** Chris Do (coaching) + one unnamed Clubhouse participant/questioner (context, do-not-train) + Greg Gunn podcast ad read (context, do-not-train).

## Key claims

_All dated 2021-03-12 (source publication). Paraphrased unless quoted below. [paraphrase]_

- When a client alters or degrades work you delivered, the underlying truth is that **they've stopped paying you to care** — you no longer have standing or a mandate to police the quality after handoff. [paraphrase]
- Chris's framing of what a client actually buys: "you don't pay me for the work, you pay for my attention and what I care about in the moment" — attention and care are time-bound to the engagement, not perpetual. [paraphrase]
- **Ownership follows payment:** if someone pays for something, they own the thing — the client owns the deliverable and can theoretically do whatever they want with it, including breaking it. [paraphrase]
- A paid deliverable is like a **gift** (Socratic walk-through: the questioner agrees it was created as a tool for the client, paid for, therefore owned by them): once you give something freely, you should hold **no more attachment or expectation** about what's done with it. Getting upset afterward "means she didn't freely give it." [paraphrase]
- Analogy: giving money to your mother — what she does with it (spoil the grandchildren, buy a dress) is her prerogative; the joy was in the giving, and staying upset over the use retroactively spoils the gift. Your joy is separate: you did good work, everyone was happy, and you got paid. [paraphrase]
- Deliverables are **like your children** — once out in the world they do their own thing (some do good, some "commit crimes") and you no longer control them. "Ownership is a crazy idea to begin with." [paraphrase]
- On professional standards: Chris acknowledges the questioner has professional standards, but "we just can't live in that world anymore" — clinging to control over delivered work "is not healthy for you." Emotional detachment is framed as self-care, not indifference. [paraphrase]
- **Feel it, don't express it:** if you feel insulted or enraged by what the client did, that's okay — just don't express it externally (no "rage text," no "what is this?"). Regulate the reaction rather than acting on it. [paraphrase]
- Practical move — the **generous check-in:** because the client may sense things are "falling apart" without knowing why, do a friendly check-in a few months out ("it's been three months since I delivered this; how are you doing with it? Do you need any additional help or sharpening of the messaging?"). Keep it open and generous, never accusatory. [paraphrase]

## Notable verbatim quotes

_All spoken by Chris Do unless noted. The questioner's lines are context / do-not-train._

> "They've stopped paying you to care. And I always say: you don't pay me for the work, you pay for my attention and what I care about in the moment."

> "If somebody pays for something, who owns the thing? ... They own it. And theoretically, if you give somebody something, you should then have no more attachment or expectation."

> "It's really not yours anymore. The joy was for you to give it. Your joy is different — you did something good, they were happy, you're happy, everyone's proud, and on top of that you got money. So at this point we don't own this anymore, and ownership is a crazy idea to begin with."

> "It's like your children — they run up, they do their own thing. Some of them commit crimes, some of them do good things. We just don't have control over [it] anymore."

> "I know that you have your professional standards, but we just can't live in that world anymore ... it's not healthy for you."

> "If you feel that way, that's okay — just don't express it externally."

> "I would just do a friendly check-in: it's been three months since I delivered this thing, I just want to check in to see how you're doing with what I gave you. Do you need any additional help or sharpening up of messaging? I would leave it very open and generous."

_Questioner (context, do-not-train):_
> "I see it as a tool for the client to drive success in the business."
