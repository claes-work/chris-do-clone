## Summary

A ~25-minute keynote-style solo talk by Chris Do titled "Reinvent Yourself — Obstacles Are Opportunities In Disguise." Structured deliberately on the classic hero's-journey story arc (refusal of the call → mentor appears → threshold → trials → outcome → conclusion), Chris argues that the difference between people who get "stuck" and those who reinvent themselves is how they respond to obstacles. He recounts his own reinvention arc — graphic designer → motion designer → studio owner → brand strategist → YouTube content creator/educator — framing each transition as an inflection point where he chose the "different path."

The talk weaves personal autobiography (his wife's blunt feedback pushing him beyond classroom teaching; being persuaded onto camera by an old classmate; hating his own voice; the failed pitches to AIGA and Art Center that ultimately birthed The Futur) with two profiles of people he mentored (game developer Alex Preston / Hyper Light Drifter, and dice-maker Karen Wang) and cautionary corporate case studies (Tower Records, Blockbuster, Kodak). It closes with an applied framework: deal with attachment, redefine your identity, and spot trends — illustrated with COVID-19-era shifts (2020-2021).

The talk is delivered entirely first-person by Chris Do; no MC/host intro appears in the transcript. Attribution is clear throughout.

## Key claims

_All dated 2021-03-16 (source publication). Paraphrased unless quoted below._

- The thing that allowed Chris to reinvent himself is that he has always viewed obstacles as a gift — a call to action and an opportunity to transform. [self-reported]
- His career arc ran graphic designer → motion graphic designer → founding his own company → brand strategy → content creation on YouTube; each step had an inflection point of staying on the known path vs. trying something different. [self-reported] [biography-relevant]
- "Fortune favors the bold" — choosing the different, unfamiliar path is where growth happens.
- Innovation and reinvention are byproducts of how you respond to obstacles; the brain can be retrained to see obstacles as opportunities (explicitly credits Ryan Holiday's *The Obstacle Is the Way* and the Stoic virtues: patience, courage, humility, resourcefulness, reason, justice, creativity).
- Method for turning obstacles into opportunities: figure out what people want, take small steps toward it, adjust as you go — "improvise, adapt, and overcome" (Marines).
- The OREO formula: "how you Respond to an Obstacle Equals your Outcome" (O-R-E-O).
- Reinvention need not be all-or-nothing: apply the 80/20 rule — keep 80% of what works, invest 20% of time/energy/resources into new initiatives as personal R&D and "insurance policy" for future-proofing.
- Applying reinvention has three parts: (1) deal with attachment, (2) redefine your identity, (3) spot trends.
- Attachment: we cling to old identities, ideas, tools, and ways of working driven by loss aversion — we feel the pain of losing about twice as powerfully as the pleasure of gaining, which is why change is hard.
- Identity labels matter: define yourself by the value you create for others, not by what you make. Companies that defined themselves by their product died — Tower Records should have become Spotify, Blockbuster should have become Netflix, Kodak should have become Canon/Sony/Panasonic.
- Kodak commanded 90% of US film sales and 85% of camera sales by 1976, and patented the first handheld digital camera in 1975, but dropped it fearing cannibalization of its film business.
- Chris reframed his own identity from "graphic designer" to "an educator / hyper-learner who lives at the intersection of design and entrepreneurship." [self-reported] [biography-relevant]
- Rebrand-yourself exercise: start with what you do → ask why it matters / how it benefits others → then ask "how else might we achieve this?" to open new directions.
- Trend-spotting: "skate to where the puck is going" (Gretzky); recognize interconnectedness (butterfly effect / Lorenz) and Newton's third law — when something goes away, something fills its place.
- COVID-era (2020-2021) application: as big offices, retail, travel, live events recede, growth shifts to work-from-home, home improvement, meal delivery, distance learning, streaming, van life, home gyms/fitness equipment, telemedicine, content creation, etc. — design your life and business to "surf these trends."
- Peloton stock was up nearly 500% over the prior year on connected-fitness demand during the pandemic (cited via Motley Fool).
- Closes on Joseph Campbell's *The Hero with a Thousand Faces*: to be the hero of your story you must answer the call to adventure, which almost always appears as an obstacle — "the obstacle is the way."

### Autobiographical details surfaced [self-reported] [biography-relevant]

- In 2013 Chris taught sequential design at Art Center; his wife (also a designer) accompanied him and gave critical feedback on the drive home to Santa Monica. Her challenge — that traditional classroom teaching was repetitive, limited in reach, and not living up to his full potential — is framed as the catalyst for everything that followed. (No family names stated in transcript beyond "my wife.")
- An old Art Center classmate, **Jose Caballer**, reappeared, collaborated on a web project, and pushed Chris to start making YouTube videos and appear on camera — the mentor figure who "removed every obstacle/excuse."
- Chris initially hated being on camera and the sound of his own voice; he made a policy for editors to mute videos if they saw him walking by. He has two sons (unnamed) who confirmed the video "sounds like you, dad."
- Early videos averaged ~50 views/day at launch. The breakthrough video, "Identity Design and Branding" — prepared as a scripted talk after staying up until 4am, with the rule "no pitching, no selling, just give value" — reached ~750,000 views and ranked top-10 for "branding" search on YouTube (as of the talk).
- Chris pitched putting courses/speaker videos online to both AIGA and Art Center; both refused/dismissed the idea (AIGA left produced videos dormant for 9+ months; Art Center faculty laughed it off). He notes Lynda.com (founded by former Art Center teacher Lynda Weinman) sold to LinkedIn for $1.5 billion, making Weinman #55 on Forbes' richest self-made women. Their refusals are framed as the reason he built The Futur instead of their vision — "a million subscribers later."
- Art Center graphic design department chair **Nick Hafermaas** lamented the institution's "rate of forgetting" as instructors aged; lettering master Doyle Young had passed away. This motivated Chris's idea to preserve teaching knowledge online.

### Mentee case studies (context, not subject-attributed)

- **Alex Preston**: artist/gamer facing autoimmune illness and $30k+ in hospital bills, with ~1 hour of usable energy/day; used illness as a motivator, Skyped with friend Beau to build an 8-bit-inspired game, ran a Kickstarter aiming for $27k and raised $600k+. *Hyper Light Drifter* became a viral hit; caught Kanye West's attention.
- **Karen Wang**: former illustrator, Chris's former student/TA/intern; dropped out of school for financial reasons, prior venture failed, burnt out. Made handcrafted designer dice for aesthetic-conscious (esp. female) tabletop gamers. Despite being overdrawn by $12 two weeks before launch, her Kickstarter (Nov 6, 2019) hit $1.2M in 24 hours and finished at $2.3M plus $875k in backer-kit — making her a multi-millionaire.

> "It never got easy, but it got easier."

> "All my life I thought I sounded cooler than I really did, but came to the realization that all my friends and everybody that cared about me already accepted my non-cool voice as me. Now I needed to do the same."

> "If they had said yes, I would have forgotten my own dream and built theirs instead. It was my opportunity wrapped inside the obstacle."

> "We feel the psychological pain of losing twice as powerfully as the pleasure of gaining. In other words, we don't want to give up what we already have, even though something else might be a lot better. It's the reason why change is so difficult."

> "You are more than what you make. Let that sink in. What you make is a byproduct of who you serve, how you improve the lives of people, and the value you generate for others."

> "I used to define myself as a graphic designer, one who solves graphic and visual problems. Now I describe myself as an educator, hyper-learner, who happens to live in the intersection between design and entrepreneurship."

> "The words you choose to define yourself matter. It opens up or limits how you see your future self, so choose carefully."

> "When you reframe what you do to why it matters, and you look through that lens, the world opens up."

> "Design your life and business to surf these trends."

> "Reinvention doesn't have to be an all or nothing approach. Try applying the 80/20 rule: keep 80% of what works, invest 20% of your time, energy and resources toward new initiatives. Think of this as your personal R&D — it's a great insurance policy towards future-proofing your life."

> "In real life it can be hard to recognize a call to adventure. It's almost always something that you're afraid to do, but know inside that you need to do. It appears as an obstacle. To be the hero of your story, you must answer the call — and the obstacle is the way."
