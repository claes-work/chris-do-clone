# How To Deal With Difficult Clients Instead of Firing Them

## Summary

A short clip pulled from the archives of The Futur **Pro Group** coaching calls. Chris
Do responds to a member's question — *"When do you let an anchor client go?"* — and
reframes the whole framing away from the binary "fire or endure." Nearly the entire
clip is Chris speaking (advising, then role-playing/freestyling an actual conversation
he'd have with a difficult long-term client he names "Thomas"). The member's question
is paraphrased into the setup and is context only. The closing ~40 seconds is a Pro
Group promo plus an unnamed Pro member testimonial (context, do-not-train).

**Speaker:** Chris Do solo (coaching + role-play). Member question = context/do-not-train;
closing promo + Pro member testimonial = context/do-not-train.

## Key claims

All dated 2021-03-30, paraphrased, attributed to Chris Do unless noted.

- Practice **non-judgment**: don't assign value to your observations. Being a neutral,
  objective observer helps you make better decisions. [paraphrase]
- When you frame the decision as "I'm going to fire them — when? tomorrow," you box
  yourself into a false binary ("do I kill it or do I kill it"). Widen the option set
  first — write down roughly five options before deciding. [paraphrase]
- Reframe the "difficult" anchor client: the friction may be complacency and taking
  each other for granted after a long relationship, not the client being inherently
  bad. A loyal, high-revenue client who has been instrumental to your growth is hard
  and costly to replace. [paraphrase]
- The five options he lays out (roughly): (1) fire the client; (2) have an adult
  conversation to remedy what isn't working for both sides and establish boundaries;
  (3) swallow it because the money is good and replacement is hard; (4) push the work
  offshore / onto a new team; (5) out-of-the-box thinking — e.g. install an account
  manager/director. [paraphrase]
- Option 2 (honest conversation + boundaries) is the one he clearly favors and
  demonstrates. [paraphrase]
- A creative out-of-the-box solution for a pain-in-the-butt-but-well-paying client:
  embed an account executive / project manager **at the client's office** (e.g. three
  days on-site, two days interfacing with your team) to gather requirements, organize
  internally, and get things on a schedule. There's a financial hit up front, but long
  term it runs smoother and reduces stress on both sides. [paraphrase]
- Root-cause the friction before firing: often the real problem is **timelines and
  expectations** — work reaches your desk too late, leaving too little time to react,
  forcing bad decisions "behind the eight ball." Fix the upstream process, not just the
  symptom. [paraphrase]
- How to open the repair conversation: lead with genuine appreciation and shared
  ownership ("we've come to take you for granted," "I've felt compelled to say yes to
  everything"), name that the pattern isn't sustainable for you or your team or your
  mental health, and note what you've already tried (new team, outsourcing). [paraphrase]
- Keep the "nuclear option" (parting ways amicably) explicitly on the table as the
  named alternative — mention it so both sides understand the stakes, while making
  clear you're trying to avoid it. [paraphrase]

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do:
> "one thing i would like for you all to practice is to use non-judging — do not assign
> value to the your observation, to be a better observer of things, to be objective and
> neutral when you look at things, because it'll help you make better decisions."

> "if you box yourself into that kind of thing there's no other options. it's like, do
> i kill it or do i kill it."

> "maybe we got a little complacent, maybe we take each other for granted a little bit.
> let's look at the alternatives."

> "we could have an adult conversation with a great client and try to remedy the things
> that aren't good for both of us. we need to establish some boundaries."

> "we've come to take you for granted and you've been a big financial influence on how
> we make decisions, and at times i felt compelled to say yes to everything you've
> asked me to do — and we've just developed a pattern of behavior that is not
> sustainable for me or my team."

> "it's just not something that's conducive to doing good work, or to my mental health."

> "the nuclear option is always available... obviously, but i'm trying to avoid that."

> "by the time it reaches our desk we have so little time to really react and respond to
> it... it puts me behind the eight ball and we make decisions not truly understanding
> what the real problems are."

Unnamed Pro member (context / do-not-train):

> usually would charge for work."
