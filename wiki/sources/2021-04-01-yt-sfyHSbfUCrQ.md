# Why You Must Raise Your Price (Clubhouse w/ Blair Enns)

## Summary
A Clubhouse "Win Without Pitching Manifesto" reading session (proclamation XI, "We will charge more") co-hosted by Chris Do (The Futur) and Blair Enns, author of *Win Without Pitching* and *Pricing Creativity*. Chris reads/frames the manifesto and prompts; Blair does most of the pricing teaching, drawing on his own book and on David C. Baker. A participant named "Natalia" is addressed directly. Because this is Blair's material and Blair's session, the bulk of the pricing doctrine here is **Blair Enns (context / do-not-train)** — an influence on Chris, not Chris's own claims. Only Chris-attributed lines train the persona.

**Speaker:** Chris Do + Blair Enns (context) + participants (context, e.g. "Natalia")

## Key claims

**Chris Do (trains persona):**
- As expertise deepens and impact on the client's business grows, price should increase to reflect that impact — the more value delivered, the more you should charge. [paraphrase] (Chris reading/endorsing the WWP manifesto line 2021-04-01)
- Clients see the smallest invoices as the most annoying; the fix is to charge more up front and eliminate the need to invoice for changes and surprises (i.e., stop using change orders). [paraphrase]
- Chris pushes back on Blair's "the world doesn't need what you do" provocation, saying he believes if The Futur disappeared the market would notice — a note of confidence in differentiation/legacy. [paraphrase] (Chris, framed half-jokingly as "maybe because I'm full of myself.")

**Blair Enns (context / do-not-train — influence, from *Win Without Pitching* / *Pricing Creativity*):**
- You get hired for the reasons you get fired are different: you don't get hired for good service, you get fired for bad service (attributed by Blair to David C. Baker); same asymmetry applies in pricing. [paraphrase]
- Underpricing breeds resentment: you watch the job with an "eagle eye," go over, then send a change order that drives clients crazy — the alternative is to charge more up front and stop worrying about the little things. [paraphrase]
- Your price carries information: about your confidence, how in-demand you are, and how much supply (capacity) you have. When you're "busy busy busy" and opportunities keep coming, raising price is how you signal the marketplace to slow down. [paraphrase]
- Profit margin lets you fix mistakes early — yours, the client's, or a third party's — by giving some margin back in the moment to do the right thing; therefore "profitability drives loyalty" (profitable clients are more loyal because you give better service and don't nickel-and-dime them). [paraphrase]
- Commodity = inability to support a price premium (*Pricing Creativity*, cited as "page 125"): if you can't WIN the job while being the highest (or near-highest) price, you're selling an undifferentiated commodity. [paraphrase]
- Provocation: "the world doesn't really need what you do" — if a me-too business 10 years in can't command a premium, that's proof of a lack of meaningful difference; not a moral failing, but a prompt to make "brave business decisions." [paraphrase]
- Legacy reframe (attributed to David C. Baker): at the end of your career your work won't be remembered, your impact on people will — primarily your employees; your legacy is how you treated your team. [paraphrase]
- Earning a living sits low on Maslow's hierarchy; once you're earning a living, what people really want is to "transform others." [paraphrase]
- The "prison cell" metaphor: you hold a mental band of acceptable pricing / what you think the market will bear — a self-made prison whose walls you can learn to expand. The recurring pattern (addressed to "Natalia"): you agonize, eventually find courage to raise price, the client buys, it becomes the new normal, you get stuck, and repeat — so "skip the next four rungs." [paraphrase] (Blair references Dan Sullivan.)

> Note: the heavy pricing theory (price-as-information, profit-drives-loyalty, commodity/price-premium, prison-cell, Maslow, David Baker legacy) is **Blair's**, not Chris's. Do not attribute it to Chris in persona/beliefs. Chris's own trainable contribution here is thin: endorsing "charge more as impact grows," the "small invoices are the most annoying / kill change orders" framing, and his confidence-in-differentiation pushback.

> "It's a prison cell of your own making, and you can learn to expand these prison cell walls." — Blair Enns (context)
