# How To Run A Creative Business (Whiteboard Session)

## Summary
A live whiteboard teaching session in which Chris Do coaches a room of participants on
scaling a creative studio "from solopreneur to entrepreneur." He works two calculations
on the board with a videographer participant ("Ricky") to show why doing the work leaves
no time to grow the business, then teaches the "handoff tax" supply-chain concept and the
positioning logic of staying closest to the client. Chris does essentially all the
teaching; participants only answer prompts. A short end-card outro by two Futur staff is a
channel plug (context).

**Speaker:** Chris Do teaching + participants (Ricky, "Devon", plus a closing outro by
Futur staff — all **context, do-not-train**). Only Chris-Do-attributed teaching trains the
persona.

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] **Working *in* vs *on* the business are different jobs.** Time available to
  work *on* the business = hours available − hours used doing the work. Board example:
  Ricky has a ~40-hour week and spends ~37 hours producing one promo video, leaving only
  ~3 hours to work *on* the business — and "it takes an hour and a half just to get warmed
  up." (2021-04-06)
- [paraphrase] **The owner's critical work is what only the founder can do** — vision,
  and talking to clients (sales). When you're shooting, editing and doing voiceover, you're
  not talking to clients; a business with no sales makes no money. (2021-04-06)
- [paraphrase] **Lead generation** is the umbrella over marketing + in-person marketing
  (networking); forms include content marketing, social media, short articles, reels, and
  spec projects. Skipping the networking event or the marketing because you're too busy
  making the work starves your pipeline. (2021-04-06)
- [paraphrase] **The vicious cycle → virtuous cycle.** Too busy making the work → can't
  market → fewer leads → more desperate → next client pays you less → you agree → it spirals
  down. At some point you must make a big decision to flip it the other way: "nothing
  changes if nothing changes." (2021-04-06) (Caption garble: "bill vicious… vicious
  correctly" is the transcriber mangling "vicious cycle.")
- [paraphrase] **Stealing time** to cover the gap means stealing from your weekend / personal
  time, i.e. from personal relationships — a bad trade-off, not sustainable. Chris frames
  managing time better (not burning the candle at both ends) as something he values more as
  he gets older. (2021-04-06)
- [paraphrase] **The "handoff tax."** Every time a product passes to another hand, that hand
  marks it up — usually ~2× — even though they added little. Bottled-water chain on the
  board: source/God (free) → Fiji bottles it (~$0.50) → water company (~$1) → distributor
  buys in bulk (~$2) → gas station buys in small lots (~$4) → you pay ~$8. The gas station
  "cleared four dollars for doing nothing" — didn't make or bottle the water. (2021-04-06)
  (Caption garble: "shark god" at line 211 is "how much does God charge.")
- [paraphrase] **Be higher up the supply chain — "we can be God."** Creatives create value
  at each step of the chain: come up with the idea (God), lay out the vector (plant the
  seed), assemble images (farmer), do the coding/build the website — then sell it as one
  price. This is the difference between the entrepreneurial mindset and the
  solopreneur/freelancer mindset. (2021-04-06)
- [paraphrase] **Hand off the "hot potato" fast.** The longer you hold onto a job yourself,
  the more it "burns your hands." Chris keeps his own plate clear and gives tasks to people
  he pays good money for. (2021-04-06)
- [paraphrase] **Effective hourly rate must exceed the cost to hire out the work, or you go
  broke.** Board example: Ricky's effective rate ~$54/hr, but a cinematographer he'd need to
  hire charges ~$140/hr — so you lose money and go out of business. To hire someone good you
  must charge much more than you're charging now. (2021-04-06)
- [paraphrase] **Position yourself closest to the buyer to capture the lion's share.** Chris
  says there are people far better than him at every individual craft (keynotes, speaking,
  logos, typography), yet he makes the most money by being **closest to the client who makes
  the buying decision** — because he takes the most risk and does the most groundwork, he
  gets the lion's share of the profit. (2021-04-06)
- [paraphrase] **Walk-away power = pricing power.** Your ability to walk away from a project
  dramatically increases your ability to charge more. (2021-04-06) (Stated as the video's
  thesis — it bookends the piece.)

**Overlap with existing business hub:** This video is a compact 2021 restatement of ideas
already documented — working *on* vs *in* the business (§1/§8), owning the client
relationship over the craft (§2), and especially the **"sell what the world can do" / "the
gap"** margin logic in §8 (the handoff tax is the same concept as the specialist-cost-to-
price margin). It adds a memorable **bottled-water / handoff-tax metaphor** and the crisp
**effective-hourly-rate vs cost-to-hire** test, but no genuinely new framework. Stays L2.
