# Easy Sales Technique Even If You Hate Selling

## Summary
A Clubhouse room framed as an "application companion" to Blair Enns' *Win Without Pitching
Manifesto* — Chris Do argues the book is great theory but light on how a creative actually
*does* it, so he demonstrates. He runs a live role-play with an audience member ("Natalya," a
UX-design agency owner) in which he closes a deal **using only questions, providing no
solutions and pitching nothing**: he diagnoses the problem (unqualified leads), quantifies the
prospect's own numbers on the call, derives a value-based fee from her projected upside, and
lowers her perceived risk with a money-back guarantee — then closes verbally before any
proposal exists.

**Speaker:** Chris Do (seller/coach in the role-play) — trains the persona. Natalya (audience
member playing the prospect) and "Daniel" (her business partner, brought on at the end) are
**context, do-not-train** — their lines are demo prospect responses, not anyone's belief. Only
Chris-Do-attributed lines train the persona.

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] **Theory vs. application are two different animals.** The *Win Without Pitching
  Manifesto* is a great manifesto but has little on how a creative actually applies it; this
  session is the missing companion showing you can do it "in a conversational tone,
  non-confrontational, without providing any solutions, just asking questions." (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **Win by asking questions, not by pitching.** Chris closes the entire role-play
  without offering a single solution or idea — "you have no idea how I'm gonna do this, and that
  was the whole point of this call." He could get off the call and *then* farm out the actual
  work (hire lead-funnel people, a sales coach, even Blair Enns) and still keep the lion's share
  of the profit as the entrepreneur. (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **Diagnose before you prescribe.** Good questions "point you right to the
  problem"; when the client names the problem, they also tell you what it's worth, and *then*
  you can get to work. "It's hard to score when you don't know where the goal is" — finding the
  problem first eliminates possibilities and makes the solution far easier to find. (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **Qualify the prospect by their own numbers.** Chris walks Natalya through
  budget floor/ceiling ($5k → $50k AUD), her ~30% outsourced-development cost, close rate, and
  leads needed (six/month) — then coaches her to *raise her minimum* to $10k because the current
  floor "is not enough money" to do a website and outsource dev. (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **Derive the fee from the prospect's projected upside, not from your costs.**
  Having established ~$210k AUD/yr gross profit if she gets six $10k leads a month, Chris asks
  "how much of that would you be willing to spend to develop this program?" — she says 20–30%,
  and the fee is anchored to that (~$40k+). Let the client size the value; the fee is a share of
  the outcome you create. (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **Use a 1–10 scale to force acknowledgment.** He repeatedly asks her to rate her
  confidence in closing (60–70%) and her happiness with the projected outcome (a "10" =
  "fireworks"). The scale surfaces her own commitment and makes her say the value out loud.
  (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **"Knowledge acquired is not knowledge applied."** Her high confidence after
  eight days of Win Without Pitching is a good sign, but until she actually does a live call
  "we don't know" — she may panic on a real $10k lead. (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **Welcome the "what if it doesn't work?" objection.** When Natalya (in role) asks
  what happens if it doesn't bring results, Chris says he's *glad* she asked — a prospect who
  doesn't ask that is "a reckless business owner." The right question is a buying signal, not a
  threat. (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **Lower the prospect's sense of risk to close.** Design the deal so the risk
  makes sense for the buyer: small money up front, a trial period with the option to cancel
  after a couple of months, and/or a money-back guarantee ("if we don't hit these numbers you
  don't pay us a thing"). A stronger guarantee lets you charge a higher percentage — "that means
  I don't stop working until you get your results." (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **"Do with words what we used to do with paper."** (Win Without Pitching
  proclamation #5.) Get the client to agree verbally and commit *on the call* before sending
  anything; only then send a one-page deal memo / proposal that recaps the already-agreed terms.
  The proposal confirms a yes, it doesn't create one. (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **Close the loop: bring in the decision-maker and remove every remaining
  reason.** Chris asks if a business partner needs to be on the call, gets Daniel on, and
  repeatedly "double-triple-quadruple checks" — "is there any other reason why you wouldn't want
  to move forward?" — clearing objections before promising the memo by end of business day.
  (2021-04-08)
- [paraphrase] **The work is diagnosis first, execution after payment.** "First we diagnose
  before we prescribe." You still have to be creative and execute — but once the problem is
  clear, "solution's not that far away," and you've eliminated half the wandering. (2021-04-08)
