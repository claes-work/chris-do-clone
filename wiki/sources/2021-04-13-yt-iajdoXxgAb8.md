# How To Determine A Client's Budget Using Price Bracketing

## Summary

**Speaker:** **Chris Do** delivers this Pro Group archive clip; he role-plays the vendor
against **Jennifer** (Pro member, playing the "typical client"/producer) and narrates the
tactic to **John** (another Pro member) as he goes. A promotional intro/outro and an
unnamed testimonial member close the video (context — not persona training data).

Chris demonstrates **price bracketing** live: he opens with wide, high goal-post ranges
("100 to 200k … two to five hundred … or over a million"), reads Jennifer's verbal
comfort/discomfort, and each time she pushes toward the low end he pushes back toward the
high end of that band, landing on a **$180,000 not-to-exceed** number against her secretly
written **$200,000**. This mostly **reinforces** the bracketing framework already in
[[wiki/topics/pricing/pricing]] (§12 bracket-up, §22 anchor-high), but adds one distinct
wrinkle: he explicitly extracts the client's **verbal agreement not to surprise/lowball
him later**, weaponizing the commitment for a moral-leverage "FU call" if they renege.

## Key claims

_All dated 2021-04-13; paraphrased unless quoted below._

- **Two concepts run at once.** Chris names them explicitly: **price bracketing** plus
  **listening to the client's comfort/discomfort** in the conversation. He brackets "so
  high and so wide," then narrows by reading her reactions.
- **Bracket high and wide, then narrow toward the client.** Open with widely-spaced
  goal-post ranges (he uses "100 to 200k," "two to five hundred [thousand]," "over a
  million") and ask "what ballpark are we in?" — then zero in.
- **Push back toward the high end of the client's stated band.** When Jennifer keeps
  steering to ~$100k, Chris re-brackets inside her range ("closer to 200 or to 100?") and
  proposes a number near the top ($180k), rather than accepting her floor.
- **Frame budget discovery as saving mutual time, not prying.** He refuses to guess-build
  an estimate: coming back with a wildly out-of-range idea "is a waste of my time … a
  waste of your time." This is the same "don't build estimates blind" logic as
  [[wiki/sources/2018-10-18-yt-87CPZdh6FIc]].
- **Get an explicit verbal commitment against the lowball — the genuinely-new wrinkle.**
  Chris makes the client confirm, on the record, that if $180k works she won't later come
  back with a surprise low number ("you know what, we have forty-five thousand"). He
  invokes **"I don't like surprises"** and secures her agreement so the number is jointly
  owned. This is a commitment-and-consistency move used as pre-emptive leverage — not
  documented in the existing bracketing sections.
- **Moral leverage / the "FU call."** If a client agrees and then reneges (goes with a
  cheaper vendor at, e.g., "65" or "30k"), Chris says he calls them back — not smooth,
  but an "FU conversation" — to say they wasted his time and disrespected the agreement.
  He concedes the truly "unscrupulous" client will lie anyway, but says directly asking
  makes most people's conscience honor the deal.
- **More budget = better work (soft upsell inside the ceiling).** He tells the client his
  creative directors "know how to stretch a dollar, so the more money you give us to work
  with, the better we can make it" — reframing the ceiling as an investment lever.
- **Result of the role-play.** Jennifer's hidden number was $200k; Chris landed at $180k
  "not to exceed," i.e. he "didn't leave money on the table" and didn't waste time
  building a bid at the wrong scale.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (vendor role / narration):

> "We can do commercials on a number of budgets, it's all over the place … we pride
> ourselves on our ability to work with a range of budgets."

> "I don't need an exact number, but how big is the sandbox we need to play in? Are we
> talking about 100 to 200k, are we talking two to five hundred, or over a million
> dollars? Because we've done projects in all ranges."

> "I don't want there to be any surprises. I don't like surprises. It's not like a
> birthday gift or a surprise anniversary party — those are good. This is the kind of
> surprise where … you're like, 'you know what, we have forty-five thousand dollars.'"

> "The more money you give us to work with, the better we can make it."

> "I'm using two concepts right now, John — price bracketing … I bracket so high and so
> wide, and I'm listening to her comfort or discomfort in the conversation."

> "This is not a smooth talking conversation, this is an FU conversation … 'I feel
> horribly disrespected right now, because we had that conversation for this reason … and
> you just wasted my time.'"

Jennifer (client role — context, not persona data):

> "I'd feel more comfortable seeing what we can get in that range before deciding where
> the budget is."
