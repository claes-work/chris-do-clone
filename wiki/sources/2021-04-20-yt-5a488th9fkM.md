# Focus To Build Expertise Rapidly (Win Without Pitching Clubhouse)

## Summary
Night 7 of a 12-part Clubhouse mini-series walking through Blair Enns's *The Win Without
Pitching Manifesto*, one proclamation per night. This session covers the 7th proclamation,
"We will build expertise rapidly." **Blair Enns (the book's author) does the bulk of the
substantive teaching** — how positioning works (choose a focus → claim expertise → build
proof), why an aspirational claim accelerates you, the buying-cycle logic of inspire-early /
reassure-late, codifying your methodology, and the tolerant-vs-intolerant firm culture from
David Maister. Chris Do hosts: he reads passages, frames questions, reflects the ideas back,
and adds a handful of his own crisp restatements. Blair's material is context/do-not-train;
only Chris-Do-attributed lines train the persona.

**Speaker:** Chris Do (host — trains persona) + **Blair Enns** (guest author — context,
do-not-train) + brief participant/audience references (context). Named third parties cited
inside Blair's teaching (David Baker, David Maister, John Gleason) are context.

## Key claims
Attribution is called out on every bullet. Only **[CHRIS]** bullets are persona-training.

- **[CHRIS]** [paraphrase] **Clients don't hire the best option — they hire the least risky
  option.** So your job is risk management / risk mitigation, and you achieve it by
  formalizing (codifying) your process; a documented formula makes the client more confident
  because what they fear is the bad surprise. (2021-04-20) Chris flags he may share this with
  Blair: "I don't think I've stolen this from you Blair." (This is a long-standing Chris theme;
  see business/pricing hubs.)
- **[CHRIS]** [paraphrase] **Positioning recap (Chris restating the book):** first select a
  focus, then articulate it via a claim of expertise, then quickly add proof to the claim.
  Making the claim is "planting a flag in the ground" — the competition, who ignored you as an
  unthreatening generalist, suddenly takes notice. (2021-04-20) (Chris is summarizing Blair's
  framework, not originating it.)
- **[CHRIS]** [paraphrase] **The generalist's life is easier but "not as lucrative and not as
  fulfilling," because nobody attacks an unthreatening generalist.** Chris reads this from the
  book and endorses it. (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **Three steps to positioning** (from the 1st proclamation,
  "we will specialize"): (1) choose a focus = discipline × market (what you do, and for whom);
  (2) articulate that focus in a claim of expertise (plant your flag / claim territory);
  (3) build the skills, capabilities and processes to support the claim. (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **Make the claim before you're comfortable making it.**
  Positioning can be a bit aspirational; the act of making the claim sharpens your focus and
  hurries you to build the missing skills "like nothing else." Then you run like hell to earn
  it. (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **Most people change their desires — even their values —
  before they'll change their behavior.** "Are you most people?" ~90–95% of people won't do the
  easy fixable work (sleep, diet, exercise); they "live with the undesirable circumstances
  rather than do the undesirable work." Same dynamic blocks creatives from building deep
  expertise. (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **Competitive pressure to pay off your claim is powerful.**
  Once you sense a leadership position you work twice as hard to keep it; marketplace stature is
  directly tied to how good you actually are. (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **You don't have to be smarter or more creative than the
  competition — only more focused.** Two equally brilliant people, one broad and one narrow: bet
  on the narrow one to build expertise exponentially faster. (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **"Have you ever tasted competence?"** (quoting David Baker
  to a room of designers). Repeated application on the same problems reveals patterns; you hone
  the craft and finally "taste competence." Creatives resist this because it's in the creative
  nature to want to do all things for all people. (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **The ditch-digging metaphor for codified methodology.**
  Dig one ditch and you're slow; dig 50 and you get faster, better, and develop a repeatable
  method (diagnose the ground → select tools → prescribe the ditch shape → timeline). Draw it
  out so a nervous late-stage prospect is reassured: **little variability in process implies
  little variability in outcome**, so the client infers low likelihood of screwing up. (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **Buying-cycle asymmetry.** Early, buyers overweight the
  possibilities/upside of change; late, they overweight the costs/consequences of failure. So
  great work *inspires* the interested early but doesn't close; late in the cycle you must shift
  from inspiring to *reassuring / calming down*. "Your work says look how good things could be;
  closing is about how bad things are not going to be." (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **Great creative is table stakes, not a differentiator.**
  "It's pretty good — you shouldn't win or lose business because of it." Good work (if findable)
  gets you on the consideration list, but doesn't get you hired. (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **An ideology/point of view drives thought leadership.**
  Once you have a codified way of doing your discipline-for-market, content becomes easier to
  produce because it's all framed through that ideology; a codified methodology is itself a
  secondary differentiator (attributed to John Gleason, ex-P&G procurement, from an earlier
  night). Most firms fake this with a generic four-step "D" process (discover, develop,
  design…). (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **Generalist is a legitimate but costly choice.** Being a
  broad craftsman who doesn't specialize is "a rich life" — but the trade-off is that sales and
  marketing become very hard (and you'll likely have to get famous). "If you bought a book called
  the Win Without Pitching Manifesto, it's because you have a sales and marketing problem."
  (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **Relevance isn't enough — positioning is an act of
  relevant differentiation.** (Pushing back, on semantics, against John Gleason's "differentiation
  is overvalued, be relevant.") Be *meaningfully different*; being selective means pursuing only
  the clients to whom you're most relevant. (2021-04-20)
- **[BLAIR — context]** [paraphrase] **Two firm cultures: tolerant vs intolerant** (from David
  Maister's 2001 essay "The Problem of Standards"). Tolerant = "we're all a family," nobody is
  held accountable, nobody gets fired. Intolerant = "we'll invest in you and support you to be
  the best, but if you can't keep up, you're out." Both are valid; you can't mix them — a firm
  trying to carve out a leadership position has to be intolerant, and the leader must set the
  learning pace ("learning never ends, the pace never lessens"). (2021-04-20)

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (nuance, not a hard clash): Chris's own catalog repeatedly urges creatives
> to **specialize externally / pick a lane** (branding Framework 34). Here Blair grants that a
> broad generalist path is "a rich creative life" and not a mistake — only a trade-off (hard
> sales/marketing). This is the same specialize-vs-generalize tension Chris flags elsewhere;
> both frame it as a deliberate trade-off, not a moral rule.
