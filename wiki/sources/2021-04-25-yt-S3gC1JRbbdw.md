# Business 101 – How To Leverage Money For Time

## Summary
A live whiteboard workshop in which Chris Do walks a Pro-group member through the true
cost of doing your own production work instead of hiring it out. Using a real member's
case — $3,000 talking-head video jobs — he builds the cost-of-goods-sold / gross-margin
math, then reframes the "kept" $2,200 as 20 hours of time that could be reinvested in
marketing to generate far more revenue. The core lesson: keeping your hands in the work to
save money is a poor trade against the opportunity cost of what that freed-up time could
compound into.

**Speaker:** Chris Do, teaching a live room. Named members answer prompts and supply the
numbers for the worked example — "Rodrigo"/"Driego" (the member with the $3k video jobs),
"Mo" (index-fund investor), and "Franklin" (does arithmetic); "Melinda" is referenced.
Greg Gunn delivers a podcast-promo outro at the end. All non-Chris voices are **context,
do-not-train**; only Chris-Do-attributed teaching trains the persona.

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] **The rich-vs-poor-mindset framing is the thesis.** The instinct that money
  matters more than time — "if I do all the work, I keep all the money" — is the poor-designer
  mindset; buying back your time is the entrepreneur/"rich designer" move. Chris frames the
  whole lesson as "entrepreneurship 101." (2021-04-25)
- [paraphrase] **Cost of goods sold (COGS) defines real profit.** On a $3,000 project it
  costs ~$2,200 to have 2–3 freelancers produce the work; gross profit = price − COGS =
  $3,000 − $2,200 = $800. You should compute profit as what's left *after* paying others to
  do all the work, not by counting your own labor as profit. (2021-04-25)
- [paraphrase] **Your labor is really a time cost, not profit.** Doing the job yourself
  "saves" the $2,200 but consumes ~20 hours; you are effectively exchanging $2,200 for 20
  hours of your time. The honest question is "what's my time worth / what's the opportunity
  cost / what else could I be doing?" (2021-04-25)
- [paraphrase] **Time equals currency — reinvest the freed hours into marketing.** With 20
  hours freed, invest them to grow the business. Worked at a "reasonable" 8-hour day, 20 hrs
  ÷ 8 ≈ 2.5 days → ~10 Instagram carousels. (2021-04-25)
- [paraphrase] **A marketing-ROI funnel dwarfs the saved COGS.** Worked example: 10 carousels
  × ~75 new followers each = 750 followers; ~2% become leads ≈ 15 leads; at a 25% close rate
  ≈ 3 clients (rounded down conservatively) × $3,000 = ~$9,000. At a more optimistic 50%
  close ≈ 7 clients × $3,000 = ~$21,000. (2021-04-25)
  > ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (internal, minor): Chris runs the close-rate both ways in the same
  > session — first 50% (~$21k) then walks it back to 25% (~$9k) as the "less optimistic"
  > figure. The $9k number is the conservative one he ultimately compares against; the $21k is
  > the optimistic ceiling, not a second claim.
- [paraphrase] **Opportunity cost = the gap between reinvesting time and hoarding the cash.**
  If instead you kept the $2,200 and invested it in an index fund at ~10% ROI, after one year
  you'd have ~$2,420. Compared to the marketing funnel's ~$9,000 (conservative) or ~$21,000
  (optimistic), the opportunity cost of doing the work yourself is roughly $6,580 in the first
  year alone (9,000 − 2,420). (2021-04-25) (Caption note: "24 20" = $2,420; "driego"/"three
  go" are caption garbles.)
- [paraphrase] **Reinvesting time compounds beyond money.** Delegating and doing 3 more jobs
  also builds craft/practice, grows your freelancer pool, and buys capacity for higher-value
  work — money is only part of the return. (2021-04-25)
- [paraphrase] **Hire from day one so you can work *on* yourself.** Chris says that since
  founding his company in 1995 he hired people to do the work so he could do vision-casting,
  planning, strategizing, learning to bid, and building the freelance pool: "when the team was
  working on those award-winning commercials, I was working on me" — knowledge he still trades
  on decades later. (2021-04-25)
- [paraphrase] **Entrepreneurship is a choice, not a superiority.** He repeats that
  entrepreneurship "is not for everybody" and neither path is superior — craftspeople who love
  the work make a good living and are needed ("I need somebody to help me do the work"). His
  audience is specifically people who *want* to move from doing-the-work to owning-the-business.
  (2021-04-25)

## Notable verbatim quotes
> "This is the mindset of rich people versus poor people, a rich designer poor designer.
> This is entrepreneurship 101." — Chris Do (2021-04-25)

> "Can you afford to hire others instead of doing the work yourself? Why should you buy back
> your time? Because right now it would seem and feel in your heart that the money is more
> important than your time." — Chris Do (2021-04-25)

> "We think because it's me, we calculate this as profit. But the only real way… is to say:
> if I could not touch this at all, I had to hire other people to do all the work — what is
> left, that is profit." — Chris Do (2021-04-25)

> "Time equals currency… your time — because I'll contradict myself in about two seconds — but
> time is currency." — Chris Do (2021-04-25)

> "Not only do you have more money in the bank account, but you were able to do three more
> jobs, so you got better practice at your craft… and you were able to potentially even hire
> more freelancers to build your freelance pool." — Chris Do (2021-04-25)

> "From the very beginning, in 1995 when I started the company, I started to hire people to do
> the work for me so I could do all the things… building up the freelance pool, vision
> casting, planning, strategizing, learning how to bid. When the team was working on those
> super cool award-winning commercials, I was working on me — that's the benefit, and now I'm
> cashing in on that." — Chris Do (2021-04-25)

> "Work is just honorable, period… I'm just trying to teach people who want to move from that
> world to this world." — Chris Do (2021-04-25)
