# How To Go From Design To Selling Strategy To Clients

## Summary

Archive clip from The Futur Pro Group in which Chris Do coaches members on how to move up-market from being hired for execution (design, web development) to selling strategy. He walks through an "embrace and pivot" script for introducing strategy to clients who came for design, anchoring a high price ($10k) on a discovery session, demonstrating strategic value by *showing* rather than *talking*, and reframing the maker's identity away from the artifact they produce and toward "clarity of thinking." The segment closes with a Pro Group promotional outro.

**Speaker:** Chris Do (primary, persona-trainable). Two member interjections: **Emily** (recipient of the coaching; context/do-not-train) and **Anthony**, a web developer who shares his "offer strategy as a value add" tactic (context/do-not-train). A closing testimonial from an unnamed Pro Group member (context/do-not-train). Note: line 123 addresses "emily" and line 131 has a member ("i'd like to add one thing to that chris") — caption does not cleanly label who is Emily vs Anthony; Chris later names Anthony (line 153, 190).

## Key claims

Dated 2021-04-27, paraphrased from Chris Do unless marked otherwise:

- **Move away from defining yourself by the thing you make.** Chris urges creatives to stop letting the artifact (websites, branding) define them and instead "sell clarity of thinking and communication" — the deliverable becomes merely the vehicle through which that thinking reaches the client.
- **The "embrace and pivot" technique.** When a client comes praising your design, first thank them and affirm you'd love to do brilliant design for them (embrace), then pivot: mention you've "incorporated a new thing" into your business that has helped clients grow, and ask if they're curious. Curiosity earns permission to proceed.
- **Use scaffolding questions to find a problem worth solving.** After the pivot, ask for ~20–30 minutes about who their core customers are, the customers they want more of, those customers' pain points, and why buyers choose competitors — so you have "a shot of solving that problem."
- **Anchor a high price on the discovery/strategy session.** Chris scripts offering the first meeting free ("this first meeting is on me, the next meeting will be on you") while stating "I typically charge ten thousand dollars to do this" — anchoring a high price and assigning value before doing the work.
- **Show strategic thinking, don't talk about it.** "Demonstrate strategic thinking and not tell them about it" — give the client a taste by actually doing a quick profile (revenue streams, where to focus energy) rather than describing your capability.
- **"Tell me which wall it's on, not where the bullseye is."** A metaphor for discovery: the client doesn't need to hand you the exact answer; directional information about where to focus is enough to avoid "solving a problem that's not worth solving."
- **Reframe of Seth Godin's *This Is Marketing* (paraphrase of Chris paraphrasing the book — attribution: secondhand).** Chris cites the book's argument that advertising ≠ marketing: advertising is noise/mass appeal ("I have a key and I look for a lock"); marketing is "the generous act of helping others solve *their* problems, not yours" ("looking for locks and inventing... a key"). Attribution note: these are Seth Godin's ideas relayed by Chris — do not train as Chris's original claim, but Chris's endorsement and framing are his.
- **The guide/mentor chain.** Your role is to be the guide who helps clients close the gap between where their customers are and where they want to be; the client then becomes the mentor to their own customers. "People aren't running away from a problem, they're running towards a solution."
- **Value add first, then charge (advice to Anthony).** For someone not yet confident selling strategy, offer it free / as a value add the first time to build confidence and practice — but within two or three iterations, "time to charge for it all." Chris frames this as "scaffolding" / "bridging yourself to the next evolution of who you're going to be."
- **Positioning around what you *make* leads to price competition.** "Too many of us position ourselves around the things that we do, so then we're stuck competing against other makers... the only thing that differentiates you in the client's eye is price." Because "nobody thinks like you," putting *you* into your brand removes competition and lets you raise rates.
- **Aligning stakeholders on the problem = 80% of the work.** Once you've achieved clarity on the problem worth solving and aligned the key decision-makers, "80% of the work has been done," and execution can then run fast ("run like the Flash").
- **Changing your positioning means losing old clients — and that's a sign of progress.** "Be prepared to get a lot of no's... the no is an affirmation that you've changed." The buyer who hired the old you is stuck in that world and rarely follows you; move on to people with "a more expansive definition of who you are," and serve them exceptionally.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (persona-trainable):

> "I want you to move away from the thing that you make as what defines you. I want you all to sell clarity of thinking and communication — and just by the way, we make websites."

> "The way that you do this is you practice the art of embracing and then pivoting. ... Appreciate them for loving you and then pivot hard."

> "Since I'm asking you to do this, this first meeting is on me, the next meeting will be on you, because typically I charge ten thousand dollars to do this."

> "This is where I say show, not talk about — like just do it. ... Demonstrating strategic thinking and not telling them about it."

> "I don't need for you to tell me where the bullseye is, just tell me which wall it's on."

> "Too many of us position ourselves around the things that we do, so then we're stuck competing against other makers, and that's where you're going to get slaughtered — because the only thing that differentiates you from somebody else in the client's eye now is price. But here's the cool part: nobody thinks like you. So when you put you in your brand, there is no competition and then you can just raise your rates."

> "The no is an affirmation that you've changed. ... Very rarely do the people you work with today follow you along this journey of becoming the new you."

Anthony (Pro Group member — context, do-not-train):

> "I'm a web developer, people come to me they want web development. It's really hard for me to sell the strategy... but I learned from you that I can offer strategy as a value add, get them used to doing the strategy, and then the next time I'll be able to sell it because I'll be confident about offering it."
