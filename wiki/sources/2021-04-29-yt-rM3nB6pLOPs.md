# Should You Charge More? (WWPM Clubhouse)

## Summary

Day 11 of the **Win Without Pitching Manifesto (WWPM)** Clubhouse rooms that Chris Do
co-hosted with **Blair Enns** — author of *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto* and
*Pricing Creativity*. The format is a guided book-club: Chris reads passages aloud (he
cites pages 122 and 125), poses framing questions, and reorders concepts for the
audience; **Blair supplies almost all of the substance**, drawn from his own books.
**Speakers:** Chris Do (SUBJECT — host/framer), **Blair Enns (context — do-not-train;
the pricing doctrine here is his)**, plus an unnamed participant ("Natalia") addressed
near the end. Because the material is overwhelmingly Blair's, only Chris's own framing
and one personal anecdote (his past use of "charm" prices) train the persona; the rest

## Key claims (2021-04-29; paraphrased unless quoted)

**Blair Enns (context — do-not-train):**

- The reasons you get **hired** differ from the reasons you get **fired**: you are not
  hired for good service, but you are fired for bad service (Blair credits **David
  Baker** for this).
- **Change orders breed resentment.** When a client beats you down on price, you watch
  the job "with an eagle eye," something unforeseen arises in the "gray area" nobody
  scoped, and billing extra for it drives clients crazy — the alternative is to **charge
  more up front** and let the little things go.
- Origin of the "$185" hook: a 2003 consulting client of Blair's — an independent bank,
  the agency's most profitable account — told the agency owner, *"I'm never going to
  question your invoices, but if you ever send me an invoice for $185, you're fired."*
  (This is **Blair's story**, quoted by Chris in the intro montage, not Chris's own line.)
- Some clients are "**price buyers**" (incl. professional procurement) for whom a low
  price matters most; charging more up front makes the annoying out-of-scope items —
  "for which you commonly lose the account" — go away.
- **Charging more than competitors is itself an advertisement** that you deliver
  higher-quality outcomes; companies that charge more "generally do better work"
  (book p.122).
- **"Pricing is positioning"** (again crediting David Baker). Price is the market's
  primary **signal** (economists' "signaling"): a whole market-based system runs on
  prices as its signal carrier. You can tax, regulate, or incentivize and the market
  still works, but **fixing prices destroys the signaling and supply cannot meet
  demand.** A price carries information about your confidence, your demand, and your
  supply.
- When you're "busy busy busy," the way to signal the market to slow down is to **raise
  your price.** Illustrated with his pre-2000 agency story: a rival proposed a website
  for **$20k** (rejected as too cheap to be credible); his big-network agency then
  proposed **$200k** for the same Fortune 100 client and won — the higher price signaled
  competence and "attention of senior people."
- **"If we're not losing business on price occasionally, we are not charging enough."**
  The ultimate test of positioning is commanding two things simultaneously — a **sales
  advantage** (you usually win) *and* a **price premium** (you win while charging more).
  Winning at a premium is a validation of your positioning work.
- **Money is not zero-sum.** Wealth is created, not taken; view currency (or bitcoin) as
  **"certificates of appreciation for creating value in the world."** Most people have an
  unhealthy relationship with money — more people feel *guilty* about earning than are
  greedy. Reframe selling from "taking dollars for your time" to "reaping a share of the
  value you helped create." (From *Pricing Creativity*, "the final obstacle is you.")
- A **commodity** is defined by an "inability to support any price premium." Framed
  hard: *"the world doesn't really need what you do... if you disappeared nobody would
  notice."* If you're a "me too" business ten years in that can't command a premium, the
  provocation is meant to spur "brave business decisions."
- **Legacy** (David Baker again): at career's end your *work* won't be remembered, your
  *impact on people* — primarily your employees — will be. Ties into a Simon-Sinek
  "start with why" / Maslow argument and a **Dan Sullivan / Strategic Coach** exercise
  where 39 of 40 entrepreneurs shared the same "why": to lift yourself by transforming
  others. Push clients to fund the transformational (strategic) job, not the cheap
  tactical one ("I just need a video").
- **Use big round numbers ending in zeros; avoid "charm" prices.** Any price that
  **implies a formula** invites the client to reverse-engineer it cheaper (attacking your
  rate or your hours). A round $50k is a **"hermetically sealed box"**; a specific
  $48,985 is a **"rubik's cube"** the client will try to solve. Defend the round number
  with value ("multiples of that in value") or "that's what we charge companies like
  yours" — using client size as a **proxy for value.**
- On **Chris Voss** (*Never Split the Difference*) advocating *precise* numbers: Blair
  says the context differs — "you're negotiating with a hostage taker." For a committed
  price buyer, get specific; but to lead the engagement and command a premium, "put on
  your big-boy or -girl pants" and give the big round number.
- Your acceptable-price band is a **"prison cell of your own making"**; you can expand
  its walls. The lifelong cycle (a Dan Sullivan point): summon courage, raise the price,
  the client buys, it becomes the new normal, repeat — so "do yourself a favor and skip
  the next four rungs."

**Chris Do (SUBJECT — trains persona):**

- **Hosts and structures** the session around Blair's books, reading passages verbatim
  and repeatedly "reorienting or reordering concepts in people's minds" for the audience.
- Frames the book's thesis as: *when you charge more you are saying "I believe I can do
  this better than everyone else"* — Chris articulating and co-signing Blair's
  premium-pricing logic.
- **Personal anecdote (voice/biography):** Chris admits he **used to use "charm" prices**
  himself — deliberately odd numbers like "$489.95" or "$8,995" — because a strange,
  specific number *felt* precise, "like I actually really thought about what it's going
  to take to do this job," even though he hadn't. He now favors Blair's round numbers.
- **Cross-references books to surface a contradiction** (a signature Chris move): he
  points out that other authors argue the opposite of Blair — naming **Chris Voss**, who
  likes precise numbers because they imply you've thought it through — and asks Blair to
  reconcile the two opposing views.
