# This Ad Made Millions, Here's Why

## Summary
Live lead-generation / sales workshop in which Chris Do dissects the Dr. Squatch soap
direct-response video ad that actually got him to buy, then generalizes it into a
copywriting/lead-gen framework: understand the customer's current world before selling,
"paint the pain," create a villain and a hero, frame benefits (explicit and implied),
overcome unspoken objections, and drive one clear action. He closes by mapping the
awareness → consideration → purchase journey and setting the class exercise: build your
own lead-generation tool that moves a cold lead to warm/hot.

**Speaker:** Chris Do throughout (first-person "this is the one that made me buy," his own
"brand messaging kit," his reading of Fitzpatrick/Hunt's *Workshop Survival Guide*). The
video's outro is delivered by **Ricky, a Futur content director** — that segment is
**context, do-not-train**. Only Chris-Do-attributed teaching trains the persona.

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] **There is no universal "best" lead-gen tactic** — the right one depends on
  each person's goals, bandwidth, and circumstances; it is individual, not one-size-fits-all.
  (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **Diagnose what sold you.** His opening prompt to the room: "in the last
  30–90 days, what sales technique has worked on *you*? What triggered you to take action?"
  — where action means clicking, visiting, or stopping the scroll, not only buying.
  (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **Marketing builds a bridge from the customer's ordinary world to the world
  as it should be.** He credits this "ordinary world vs. the way things should be" framing
  to Nancy Duarte's TED talk on communication/persuasion/storytelling. (2021-05-04)
  (Caption garble: "nancy dart" = Nancy Duarte.)
- [paraphrase] **Make the customer's current world undesirable first.** Dr. Squatch's ad
  opens "you're not a dish, you're a man" and equates body wash/"detergent" with washing a
  dish — reprogramming the viewer in the first 30 seconds so they're primed to want the
  solution. (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **Activate senses video normally can't.** Casting a diverse blind-smell-test
  panel ("I'd smell you all day long") makes the viewer *believe* the soap smells good — a
  sense beyond sight/sound, delivered by proxy through the cast. He notes men often buy soap
  on fragrance, not skincare. (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **Sell the felt benefit, not just features.** Implied feelings the ad
  provokes: women will find you attractive, you'll feel more confident, you're smarter for
  caring for your skin, and (deeper) you're setting a good example for your kids — none of it
  stated outright; it's "reading between the lines." Tone: over-the-top, funny, "man humor."
  (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **Every story needs a villain and a hero.** The villain is "Big Soap" — named
  deliberately to echo "Big Tobacco," an NLP-style association exploiting 20+ years of
  anti-smoking conditioning; Big Soap "cuts corners to make more money at your expense." The
  hero is Dr. Squatch: natural ingredients, made in the USA, time-honored small-batch
  hand-cut process. He says he'll be adding the villain/hero device to his brand messaging
  kit. (2021-05-04) (Caption garble: "us fa" = USA.)
- [paraphrase] **Close with social proof, risk reversal, and one call to action.**
  Thousands of reviews / hundreds of thousands of customers, a satisfaction-guarantee refund,
  then a CTA that just says "go to the website" — no pricing, no product matrix, "give it a
  try, what do I have to lose." (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **Reduce choice and harvest micro-commitments.** The site's onboarding quiz
  fights the paradox of choice (tell me the one thing to do), is on-brand (fun/weird),
  captures email to send results, and the results email contains a purchase code — a chain of
  small micro-commitments/"micro-transactions." (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **Four diagnostic questions for any ad that worked on you:** how did the brand
  (1) present the problem, (2) frame the benefit, (3) overcome your unspoken objections, and
  (4) get you to take action. (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **"Painting the pain": understand the problem before offering the solution.**
  Most people jump straight to the solution; instead render the pain in "full beautiful
  detail." Infomercials spend ~80% of the time on the problem — this is especially necessary
  for cold leads who didn't know they had a problem until told. (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **If you've bought, joined, or subscribed, you've been sold** — you don't need
  any sales jargon to recognize that something worked; not understanding *why* is why a group
  struggles to generate sales. (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **Know exactly who you're talking to.** Dr. Squatch spoke to "bearded hairy
  dudes in the Midwest" and sold millions in soap; clarity of audience is what makes the copy
  land. Clients who feel deeply understood ("you need to know me") will pay a premium, travel,
  and stay loyal — the "Mr. Right vs. Mr. Right Now" contrast. (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **The buyer journey is awareness → consideration → purchase, and each stage
  drives the next.** Awareness alone is worthless ("run naked down the street with your hair
  on fire" gets attention, not consideration). A single marketing piece rarely closes a
  creative-service sale end-to-end; getting a prospect from unaware to aware-and-considering
  is already a win. (2021-05-04)
- [paraphrase] **Higher price = longer journey / more touchpoints.** Impulse buys sit at the
  supermarket register (gum, tabloids, candy) because they're cheap; multi-dollar-sign
  creative services need many touchpoints to build trust — so don't expect one ad to make
  someone spend $10k tomorrow. The class exercise: build your own lead-gen tool that converts
  a cold lead into a warm/hot one. (2021-05-04)

**Overlap with existing hubs:** The underlying principles (sell the transformation, paint
the pain, know your audience, sell outcomes not features) are core recurring Chris Do
teaching. The genuinely fresh material here is the **structured direct-response ad-breakdown
method** — the four diagnostic questions, the villain/hero device, the awareness/
consideration/purchase-vs-price-tier mapping, and micro-commitment funneling — a concrete
copywriting/lead-gen playbook rather than a restatement. Stays L2.
