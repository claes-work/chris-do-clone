# Justify An $18k Logo?!

## Summary
A live role-play in which a workshop participant plays devil's advocate: another designer
("Amy") quoted the client $1,500 for a logo, and Chris Do quotes $18,000 — so how does he
justify the 12× gap? Chris turns the objection around by questioning the client about risk
and decision criteria (proof, case studies, tenure, referrals, credit history, office,
headcount), argues that clients don't buy the best option but the *least risky* one, and
that a high fee is a bet on value and risk-reduction rather than on the deliverable. He
then breaks the fourth wall to tell the room that anyone can charge these rates from day
one — you just have to believe the price and be able to say it out loud.

**Speaker:** Chris Do, teaching a live room. A participant voices the skeptical "client"
(and briefly a self-doubting designer). All non-Chris voices are **context, do-not-train**;
only Chris-Do-attributed teaching trains the persona.

## Key claims
- [paraphrase] **Answer a price objection with questions, not a defense.** Instead of
  justifying the $18k, Chris interrogates the client's own risk: is the logo important to
  the business? If it's wrong you have to redo the whole process — so there's real risk in
  picking the wrong vendor. (2021-05-06)
- [paraphrase] **Make the client name their decision criteria — then out-perform on them.**
  Push the client to articulate what makes a "good vendor": proof, case studies, years in
  business, number of referrals, credit history, office space, headcount. On all of those a
  credible vendor can win, which is what justifies the premium. "Think like a client, don't
  think like a designer." (2021-05-06)
- [paraphrase] **Clients don't choose the best option — they choose the least risky one.**
  The job is to *appear as the least risky thing*. A high fee is defensible when it buys
  down risk (proven track record, social proof, expertise). (2021-05-06)
- [paraphrase] **Price is relative to the risk to the client's company.** The same work is
  worth different amounts depending on what's at stake for that specific client — the price
  has to be "relative to the risks of the company." (2021-05-06)
- [paraphrase] **You can charge these rates from day one — credibility/awards aren't the
  gate.** Chris insists the participant's lack of Blind's track record doesn't bar them from
  the same ballpark: "the first day I started I wanted to charge these rates anyway… you just
  have to be able to say it out of your mouth." (2021-05-06)
  > ⚠️ CONTRADICTION: This "just charge it from day one, credibility doesn't matter" framing
  > sits in tension with the same video's core argument that the premium is justified *by*
  > proof, case studies, referrals and being the least-risky (most-credible) option. Chris is
  > toggling between the pricing *mindset* (believe the number, say it) and the pricing
  > *justification* (evidence lowers client risk) — worth reconciling if promoted.
- [paraphrase] **Ratchet the price up by testing the next number.** He describes charging
  $10k for strategy the first time (not building up from $100→$200), finding it landed, then
  moving to $20k — and being humbled that others charge a million. Prices climb by daring the
  next tier, not by incrementing. (2021-05-06)
- [paraphrase] **Value is set by what it's worth to the buyer, not by who you are.** His
  "mom-and-pop… does it for a sandwich" aside: the same person prices the same work by what
  it's worth to the client in front of them. (2021-05-06) (Caption garble: the transcript's
  "you can be Paul Ryan… he's not alive anymore" is muddled/misheard — treat the specific
  name as unreliable; the point is "anyone, regardless of stature.")
- [paraphrase] **Expertise and authorship are social proof.** Closing beat: "I wrote the
  book literally on the brand… I teach and consult people how to do this" — being the
  acknowledged expert is itself risk-reduction (delivered with a wink: "I'm much friendlier
  in person"). (2021-05-06)

**Overlap with existing hubs:** Restates familiar Chris Do pricing doctrine — value/risk
pricing, "sell the outcome not the deliverable," clients-buy-least-risk, believe-your-price.
Logo/high-fee pricing is already well covered, so this stays L2. The mildly fresh angle is
the compact **"make the client list their own vendor criteria, then beat them on each"**
move as a way to justify a specific premium.
