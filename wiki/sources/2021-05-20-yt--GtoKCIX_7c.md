# Selling Through Curiosity & How Not To Be Salesy

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do (as the designer/service-provider) opposite an unnamed female client character (a role-play partner — context / do-not-train). A short (~2.5 min) live sales role-play in which a prospect calls about "webcamps"/web comps and Chris, instead of pitching, opens with curiosity ("I'm curious *why* you're calling me"), runs a discovery/diagnosis conversation, reframes the stated request as a marketing problem, quantifies the value at ~$480,000/year, and closes a $40,000 engagement.

## Key claims

Dated **AIGA 2019** (republished 2021-05-20). All Chris-Do-attributed, all paraphrase unless quoted below. This clip **reinforces** existing hub material (§2 discovery, §3 anchoring, and the curiosity-as-discovery-opener theme); it **adds nothing new**.

- **Open with curiosity, not a pitch.** When the client praises his work and asks him to help their brand, Chris's first move is to ask *why* they specifically believe they need webcamps — turning a flattering inbound into a diagnosis. (Reinforces the "just out of curiosity" discovery opener, 2018-04-04, and "selling is about being curious," 2023-12-23.)
- **Diagnose before you prescribe.** Chris keeps asking questions ("anything else?", "who built your current web comps and why don't you call them?") to surface the real situation — declining sales, poor retention, a competitive market, and a previous vendor who went non-responsive — before proposing anything.
- **Whoever asks the questions controls the conversation / makes the client do the talking.** The client supplies every diagnostic fact; Chris mostly summarizes and asks the next question.
- **Summarize / play back the client's own words** to confirm understanding and build agreement ("here's what I heard you said…") — reinforces the SALES-framework "Summarize" step.
- **Reframe the presenting request as the real problem.** "Maybe there's nothing wrong with the webcamps — maybe it's a marketing issue." The client concedes it's a marketing problem, so the client sells themselves on the reframe.
- **Convert the goal into a hard number, then to annual value.** Engagement at 400,000 impressions/month, goal 600,000 (a +200,000 increase) → +$40,000/month in profit → ×12 = **$480,000/year**.
- **Anchor the fee against the value.** 10% of $480k = $48k; the client's own instinct ($10k, ~2.5% of the value) is framed as "anemic." Chris then discounts $48k to **$40,000** ("I won't even charge you 48, I'll charge you 40").
- **Proportionality — Jim Rohn effort/result principle.** "Do not spend a small effort for a big result, and don't spend a big effort for a small result." Getting the client to agree that a big result deserves a proportionate spend collapses the price objection.
- **Cost of under-paying / self-diagnosis.** Chris suggests the previous vendor was "undervalued" and lacked clarity "because you self-diagnosed and paid too little to solve a big problem."

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (already logged, not new): the fee-anchoring here ("you spend $2.5k to make $480k… we're in the wrong business") is Chris stating a value number and price first, which sits against his "let the client name the value/price — when they say it you're closing" rule. This is the standing anchor-vs-let-them-say-it tension reconciled in the sales hub (§3) — *let the client name the **value** in discovery, then **you** anchor the **fee** against it.* No new instance to flag.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (designer role — voice/persona data):

> "I'm curious — why you're calling me about web comps… but why do you specifically believe you need web comps?"

> "Here's what I heard you said: that you're looking for a solution, there's an engagement issue, you see a decrease in sales, and that there's greater competition — and the last partner you had wasn't a partner at all… is that what I'm hearing?"

> "It's like you came in here for web comps — maybe there's nothing wrong with the web comps, maybe it's a marketing issue. What do you think?"

> "You would spend ten thousand to make 480,000?… If you can make an investment today of 2.5[%] and get this kind of return, we're in the wrong business — tell me your secret."

> "I'm really into business philosophy… [Jim Rohn] believes in this: do not spend a small effort for a big result, and don't spend a big effort for a small result. Is this a big result for your company?"

> "Here's the good news: I won't even charge you 48, I'll charge you 40. How's that?"

Client character (role-play partner — **context, do-not-train**):

> "We've had a very successful fiscal year in the past but we've seen our sales going down and we're not exactly sure why."

> "I've never seen it like that before — I didn't realize that we could be making this much."
