# Small Goals Get Big Results

## Summary

A short Chris Do monologue arguing that big results come not from fixating on a
distant finish line but from setting the *next* small goal and moving to it, over and
over — the way a runner aims for the next mile marker rather than the horizon. He
diagnoses burnout as reaching an early goal ("get paid for the thing") and stopping,
having lost the original spark in the grind, and insists that motivation ultimately
has to come from the person themselves — you can't outsource the work. He closes on
memento-mori urgency (time works against you as obligations grow) and his own
parenting as a live example.

**Speaker:** Chris Do throughout (solo monologue). The final ~20 lines (from
"hi my name is greg gunn") are a **podcast promo read by Greg Gunn, producer of The
Futur podcast** — context/**do-not-train**, not Chris Do.

## Key claims (2021-05-27, paraphrased)

- Owning the tool doesn't make you the maker: "having a camera does not make you a
  cinematographer / a storyteller"; the storyteller finds the tools and makes the
  story. Craft identity comes from the work, not the equipment.
- People lose motivation after they gain *proficiency* (not even mastery) and start
  thinking about getting paid — the original spark that was the origin of the thing
  disappears "in the grind itself," and they lose their way.
- Chris's own career is a chain of deliberately escalating next-goals: top of class in
  graphic design → knew he'd get bored → moved into the then-emerging field of motion
  graphics (chosen precisely for its many complex sub-disciplines) → local TV spots →
  national spots → best-in-class brands → dream agencies/clients. Each ceiling met, he
  "adds something new" and "moves the mile marker away." This can keep you driven for
  20 years.
- The core metaphor — the Nike double-page magazine ad: a long undulating street
  receding to the horizon, copy reading "just to the mailbox / just to the car / just
  to the [next point]…" Passion means not aiming to run the whole distance to the
  horizon, but only to the next mile marker, then the next — "I don't know when I'm
  going to stop, I'm just going to keep going."
- Burnout / "why you don't show up anymore" has one explanation: you hit an early
  marker (e.g. the third goal in — "to the car"), told yourself "I'm done," and are now
  upset you "only made it to the mailbox." The people who keep going simply never
  declared themselves done.
- **Time is not on your side** (memento-mori): the more obligations you accumulate, the
  less your body complies with your mind, and the harder it becomes to do what you want.
  "Beauty and youth is wasted on the young" because you don't yet know what to do with
  it.
- Motivation cannot be given to someone or wanted for them: "I can't want it more for
  you than you want it for you." You can open doors, but the person has to walk through.
  "You can't hire someone to do the push-ups for you" — it starts with being clear about
  what you want from your life.
- Parenting example: his two sons differ — one is self-motivated and already knows his
  direction; the other shows talent, so Chris steers him toward what he's good at and
  enjoys. He pulled them out of regular school into art school, and says if he could
  redo his life he'd want to be in his son's shoes: a supporting father giving him what
  he needs to pursue his goals out of joy, not pressure. [self-reported]

## Notable verbatim quotes (Chris Do, 2021-05-27)

> "Having a camera does not make you a cinematographer, having a camera does not make
> you a storyteller. The storyteller finds the tools and makes the story."

> "That spark that was the origin of this thing has somehow disappeared in the grind
> itself, and you've lost your way."

> "When you feel like you've hit some kind of ceiling you add something new to that and
> you continue to explore, you keep challenging yourself, you move that mile marker
> away."

> "I'm not gonna run 10 kilometers, I'm just going to get to the next mile marker, I'm
> just going to keep going and keep going. I don't know when I'm going to stop, I'm just
> going to keep going."

> "You got to that marker, to the car, the third goal in, and you're like 'I'm done'…
> and that's what's separating your head and then being upset that you only made it to
> the mailbox."

> "Beauty and youth is wasted on the young… because you don't know what to do with it,
> you do dumb stupid things thinking you have all of your life."

> "I want that for you too, but I can't want it more for you than you want it for you.
> You can't hire someone to do the push-ups for you — you got to do the work, and it
> starts by being clear with what you want from your life."
