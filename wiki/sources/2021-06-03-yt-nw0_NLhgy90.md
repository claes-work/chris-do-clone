# Get More LinkedIn Post Engagement — 3 Examples

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do (verified — first-person teaching throughout: he critiques three Pro-group members' LinkedIn posts as "chat with Chris... just telling a friend at a bar," references "a one-on-one with me," and issues "challenge number two" to the group). Chris live-critiques three LinkedIn story posts from members inside The Futur's Pro group (Mo, Anna Lee Hansen, and Matthew Encina/"Cena"), using a story rubric — conflict/struggle (highlighted yellow) versus lesson-learned/ordinary-world-to-new-world (highlighted green) — to show how each writer can make their post more vulnerable, specific, and engaging. He closes with tactical LinkedIn advice (line breaks, posting cadence, the 400-character limit, thoughtful commentary over "straight fire" comments) and a Futur Pro-group promo. A short unnamed female Pro member gives a testimonial at the very end (context; do-not-train).

## Key claims

_All dated 2021-06-03, paraphrased, attributed to Chris Do unless noted. Auto-captions were garbled in places — names/quotes reconstructed conservatively._

- **A story needs two core parts: conflict/struggle and the lesson (ordinary world → new world).** Chris says these two elements are "necessary for almost all stories" and color-codes them (conflict = yellow, lesson = green) when analyzing each post.
- **Example 1 — Mo (multicultural identity):** The post was well-crafted but "too professorial" — intellectual language ("cultural chimera, a multicross hybrid") creates a barrier between the writer and the reader. Chris wants Mo's actual pain and the concrete benefits of learning to embrace his uniqueness, neither of which the post shows.
- **Communication is not about a big vocabulary — it's about using the language that connects with your audience.** Strip academic language: "stop being a professor and just tell me as a human."
- **Show the struggle and its consequences in real, specific terms** so the reader can "walk in your shoes... look at the world through your eyes" — that is what creates connection.
- **The accompanying image should be vulnerable and candid, not a selfie looking at camera.** Chris suggests a real, candid photo (e.g. from Mo's time in Senegal or Saudi Arabia) so "one image almost tells the entire story."
- **Example 2 — Anna Lee Hansen (fear of being laughed at):** Praised for tapping into her emotions and vulnerabilities. Her struggle (afraid people would laugh; couldn't say her dream out loud; feared her English wasn't good enough) and takeaway (a dream stronger than fear) are clear, but the benefits/outcome are missing because the events are very recent. Chris advises digging back into older history/timeline for stories whose consequences and benefits are already known.
- **"Crossing the threshold"** — Chris frames each story via the hero's-journey beat of the moment the person crosses into the new world (for Anna Lee: jumping on an Instagram Live and exposing her English).
- **Example 3 — Matthew Encina / "Cena" (fear that no one will care):** Held up as the strongest — "just crushing it." His three chosen images are awkward, candid, doubtful and convey vulnerability (framed lower than Chris despite similar height), and the outcome image tells the story without reading the words.
- **Put the struggle/conflict close to the start of the post** ("right after car crash") — proximity of the struggle to the beginning increases the chance someone reads the whole story.
- **Pile up specific, relatable details of the struggle** (wanted to be perfect, hated the sound of his voice, made 26 versions, feared looking bad). Each detail makes a reader silently "raise their hand" and think "me too, I've been there."
- **Tight editing matters** — Matthew's post is "compact and potent" because it was clearly rewritten and crafted many times.
- **Name the takeaway explicitly.** Matthew's implied lesson ("I switched my thinking to be valuable to others") plus his results (dozens of videos, millions of views, more comfortable, now invited to speak) show the "hero returned" sharing the boons — but the lesson would be even stronger if crafted a bit more clearly.
- **LinkedIn formatting tactic — don't over-space with line breaks.** LinkedIn only previews a couple of lines; if you space a post out like a screenplay/rap, readers see ~1.5 lines, so those first 1.5 lines "better be really hooky."
- **Post consistently / daily.** Mo hadn't posted in ~8–9 months; inactivity hurts you. Make "intentional action every single day to develop a new habit... the algorithm is going to love you for it." (Playful deadline: write your next post "between now and the next full moon.")
- **Drive engagement with thoughtful commentary, not empty hype.** Comments like "yo bro you nailed it" or "straight fire" don't drive engagement; thoughtful, critical, constructive feedback (using the rubric as a lens) grows followers and connections and builds thought-leader status.
- **The teaching goal: transfer his critique lens.** Chris wants members to be able to critique their own work "as good as anybody in this group including myself," so their self-feedback matches what a one-on-one with him would produce.
- **Challenge #2 (the assignment):** Write and tell a personal story that elicits an emotional response with a clear takeaway. Story elements — an **attractive character (you)** made attractive by details, struggles, imperfection, and a world-view that might polarize; what the character **wants**; the **obstacles**. (Mo's multiculturalism / wanting to love his dark skin used as the worked example.)
- **LinkedIn post limit is 400 characters** — "just a little bit more than Twitter." Use the limitation to force reduction to the absolute essential: start with the struggle, focus on the key takeaway, then add only the elements needed to pay it off.
- **Go extremely deep and vulnerable on the struggle — "struggle porn" — then back off.** Overshoot the vulnerability, then pull back; the results "will probably most likely shock and surprise you" positively.
- **Pair image + words in synchronicity** — pull an attention-grabbing image from your archives to illustrate the story; "image and words combined together make a very powerful thing."
- **Evoke the five senses** (see, hear, touch, taste, smell) — the more vividly you paint a picture in the reader's head, the easier it is for them to visualize and feel your struggle and story.
- **Community/accountability claim (Pro-group promo):** Taking action with a partner or a group of highly motivated people makes you far more likely to follow through. The Pro group is pitched as a diverse global creative community; goal of the group is to help members get business leads / speaking / writing / client opportunities as people of influence.
