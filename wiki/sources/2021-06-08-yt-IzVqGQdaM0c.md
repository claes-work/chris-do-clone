# How To Reframe and Silence Your Critical Inner Voice

## Summary

In an intimate small-group coaching session (recorded pre-Thanksgiving during the 2020 COVID lockdown, published June 2021), Chris Do walks a young man named Rigo/Drigo through the difference between a critical inner voice and a growth-oriented one. Chris diagnoses Rigo's "I could be doing more" as coming from judgement rather than encouragement, then argues the feeling was already inside Rigo — Chris merely triggered it — and coaches him to "judo flip" the negative self-talk into positive motivation. The session closes with Chris praising Rigo's coachability, warning against being an "askhole," and reframing three attendees' repeated show-ups as intention rather than coincidence.

**Speaker:** Chris Do (primary coach, TRAINS the persona). Participant "Rigo"/"Drigo" and others in the room (context / do-not-train). Greg Gunn appears only in the closing podcast promo (context / do-not-train).

## Key claims (2021-06-08, paraphrased unless quoted)

- A critical inner voice that makes you feel bad ("I could be doing more" that beats you up) is NOT a good voice — it comes from a place of judgement and criticism, not growth and encouragement.
- You must ask whose voice the inner critic actually is; it is often learned early from a parent, teacher, or someone whose approval you needed, then buried and dormant, unrecognized as external in origin.
- Chris claims he has no power to make anyone feel or think something they don't already carry: if you feel what he says, it's because the idea was already in you and you "invited it in."
- Analogy: a coach's words are like Tylenol — they only work because your body already produces the matching chemical internally; the insight only lands if the thing that matches is already inside you.
- Negative emotion attached to a task you know you need to do is like swimming with a 5-lb anchor on your leg — you can fight it a while, but eventually it drags you down; so the mind must be reprogrammed.
- The fix is to "judo flip" the emotion: change "it's not enough" (self-attack) into "it's not enough" (fuel) — because you won't consistently show up for anything you have a strong negative emotion attached to.
- Chris frames wanting more for Rigo in terms of what he'll EARN, explicitly not what he "deserves" — "not that you deserve, but that you earn."
- Coachability markers Chris values: neuroplasticity/openness, quick application of what's learned, and willingness to cast away old ideas when a superior idea appears — "the bedrock of people I think are highly coachable."
- Chris won't invest energy in someone "dense as a doorknob" who has too much invested in staying static; such a person can't move forward.
- "Don't be an askhole" — someone who asks for advice and never acts on it, only seeking advice that aligns with what they already believe.
- Chris rejects coincidence: the three people present all showed up to an earlier event he didn't even headline, which he reads as each setting an intention and saying yes — "nobody held a gun to your head."
- You have to learn to love the process, not so much the results — because when it gets hard you'll quit anything you don't enjoy (gym analogy: a couple of curls with 10-lb weights and "I'm done").
