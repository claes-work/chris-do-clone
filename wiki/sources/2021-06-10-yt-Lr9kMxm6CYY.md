# 8 Client Objection Responses in 8 Minutes

## Summary

A rapid-fire objection-handling role-play from The Futur (2021). **Speaker:** **Chris Do**
voices himself / the designer-service-provider and answers each objection; his friend
**"Mo"** fires the objections as the client (context, do-not-train). The framing is a
"bell" game — Mo throws an objection, Chris responds, and Mo "hits the bell" when the
answer satisfies him. It is a demonstration reel of Chris's live objection-response
technique — mostly **answering an objection with a question** to flip control back to the
client and re-open the value/diagnosis conversation, rather than defending or discounting.

> **eight** — the outro confirms "eight objections handled by myself... and my friend Mo
> playing the client." Follow the outro: 8 objections.

> belief — context, do-not-train.

## Key claims

All dated 2021-06-10. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Attributed by speaker/role.
Each item = one objection (client, Mo) + Chris's response move.

1. **"At that rate, I can just hire someone in-house to do it."** → Chris flips it back:
   **"Why haven't you done that before?"** (Answer-with-a-question; exposes that the client
   hasn't/can't, so the in-house alternative isn't real.)
2. **"I'm confused — I'm paying you just to learn about my business in a workshop; why isn't
   that just part of what you do? It feels weird I'm not getting anything from the
   workshop."** → **"No — you're paying me to help you figure out what you *don't* see."**
   Chris runs the **doctor analogy**: when a doctor runs a battery of tests, you wouldn't
   ask them to do it for free. When the client protests "you're not a doctor, you're a
   service provider," Chris presses the logic: *you don't understand your problem, you don't
   understand how to solve it — yet you want to tell me how to solve it for you?* Close:
   **"Only if you want the answers"** — the paid workshop/diagnosis is what buys the answers.
3. **"Why are you so high? I've called other people — you're pretty expensive. Why is it so
   expensive?"** → Chris **refuses to justify the price**: **"It's just my rate."** Then
   pivots to value via questions: "Is there a way we can work around that, or is that not
   what you need? Are you trying to solve a big problem?" Client: "I just know I need X."
   Chris: **"And what does X do for you?"** (Consistent with the §4d *never justify your
   price* stance — present the number, redirect to the problem/value.)
4. **"I don't want to be locked into a contract."** → Chris separates *contract* from
   *length of engagement*: **"I don't need to lock you in — a contract protects both of
   us."** He surfaces that the real issue is the **90-day/retainer minimum** he floated:
   "I don't want to promise you something where I know it's not going to work — it takes
   time to make this stuff work." Holds **three months as the minimum commitment.**
5. **"Just send me everything you talked about in a packet and I'll get back to you."** →
   Chris confirms agreement *before* investing in a proposal: **"Before I send you all this
   in a document you probably won't read — are we in agreement on the price, the scope, and
   the deliverables?"** (Same *confirm-before-you-propose / never build free proposals* move
   as the first-call framework, §15.)
6. **"This seems like a lot — all I really need is a logo; the workshop/3-day/8-hours-with-
   my-team stuff seems unnecessary."** → Take-away test: "Would you be better served
   designing it yourself or hiring a cousin?" (Client: "No, I like *your* work.") Then the
   close: **"When I show you just a logo, will you know if it's going to work for you or
   not — just by looking at it?"** (The process/workshop is what makes the logo *work*; a
   logo can't be judged in isolation.)
7. **"I'm happy to pay that price — but is there a way for you to do *more* than that one
   thing for the same price?"** → Chris **mirrors the ask** back until its absurdity is
   visible: "You're happy to pay me for what I'm going to do... but you want me to do more
   than what you're happy to pay for?" Uses the **car analogy** ("I'd love to buy your car
   at that price, but I want two cars for that price") and then turns it on the client's own
   business — the client sells e-learning courses, so Chris asks: *if a customer said "I
   love this course, I'd like to buy it, but I want two courses for one price," what would
   you say to them?* (Make the client feel the request from the seller's side.)
8. **"This all sounds great — is there proof of you doing this before and the results you
   got?"** (framed: the client has already heard the vendor's proposed solution) → Chris
   declines to sell on proof/guarantees: **"No, there's no proof. Did the plan I laid out
   for you make sense to you?"** (Client: "It did.") Close/take-away: **"You could take that
   plan to someone else if you'd like."** (The value is the *thinking/plan*, not a
   guarantee; leaving the door open is the take-away move.)

> the §4d *never justify your price* line and sits in the same soft tension with the
> value/ROI-framing plays already flagged on the [[wiki/topics/sales-clients/sales-clients]]
> hub — resolved there as *proactive value-framing vs. defensive justifying*.
