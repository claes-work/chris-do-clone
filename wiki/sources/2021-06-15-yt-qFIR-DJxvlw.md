# Planning a Personal Website: Does My Portfolio Matter?

## Summary

Episode 1 of "Built by Hand," a Futur series in which **Ben Burns** — designer, creative
director, and (at the time) a partner at The Futur — documents rebuilding his long-unfinished
personal website. **Chris Do does NOT appear in this video.** Ben interviews two guests —
author/ex-agency-owner **Michael Janda** and content creator/creative director **Matthew
Encina** — plus anonymous audience "user research" voices, to figure out the site's purpose,
whether his past portfolio work matters, and how to structure a site that blurs designer /
educator / content-creator. The through-line: at a certain career stage, reputation and story
sell better than a portfolio, so the work should be *present but not prioritized*.

**Speaker:** Host is **Ben Burns** (not Chris Do). Guests **Michael Janda** and **Matthew
Encina**, plus unnamed audience feedback voices. **No Chris-Do-attributed content in this
video — the entire source is CONTEXT / do-not-train for the persona.** Only logged here for
corpus completeness and because it reinforces themes Chris covers elsewhere.

## Key claims (2021-06-15, paraphrased — all NON-Chris speakers; context only)

- **(Michael Janda)** Once you build a reputation, people buy *because of the reputation*, not
  because they saw your portfolio — "your portfolio doesn't matter nearly as much anymore."
  This is the thesis of his book *Burn Your Portfolio*.
- **(Michael Janda)** He personally dropped the agency work from the front of his site when he
  rebranded from agency to author/coach/speaker; the work still exists but is "buried a couple
  clicks deep" (behind About → past-agency pages). Lead with who you are and your content, not
  the project reel.
- **(Michael Janda)** Reframe the personal site around identity, not job title: "Ben Burns is
  not a designer — he's a family man, a thought leader, a partner at The Futur." Showing that
  human layer is how people connect on a deeper emotional level and become "fans for life."
- **(Matthew Encina)** Present career work as a **scrolling timeline of milestones** (first
  logo, started a business, joined Blind, awards) rather than a wall of clickable case studies;
  highlight only one or two interesting things and let people push deeper if they want. It
  "validates" you simply, without forcing the visitor to consume everything.
- **(Matthew Encina)** A **resources page** — a catch-all hub of the books, tools, and things
  your audience keeps asking about — is high-value; give people a single place to be pushed to.
- **(Ben Burns)** A standard portfolio site (home / about / work / contact) is optimized to
  land clients or a job; that no longer fits him because he isn't seeking either. He wants a
  site focused on *relationship*, not conversion — "get to know me / hear my story" and "see
  what's new" became the two homepage goals.
- **(Ben Burns)** Process claim: start website planning with (1) a visual **sitemap** to
  organize pages and (2) basic **wireframes** to organize a single page's content; identify
  users and their goals first, then translate that into the map. Homepages without a conversion
  goal are "the toughest nut to crack."
- **(Ben Burns, echoing Michael Janda)** Decision: keep the work on the site but de-prioritize
  it — a hidden, unlinked "projects list" page for anyone who really wants to see everything.

## Notable verbatim quotes (attributed per speaker — all context / do-not-train)

> "My first book is called *Burn Your Portfolio*, and the concept … is that at some point you
> build reputation, and people buy because you have built a reputation — that's what they're
> purchasing from, not because they saw your portfolio." — **Michael Janda** (not Chris Do)

> "It doesn't matter nearly as much anymore what my portfolio looks like. What matters is 'oh,
> I heard of Mike Janda, he's that guy on Instagram, I love his content, I read his books.'
> That's the reputation that happens." — **Michael Janda** (not Chris Do)

> "You have an opportunity here to say Ben Burns is not a designer — he's a family man, he's a
> thought leader, he's a partner at The Futur … and people connect with you on this deeper
> emotional level and become those fans for life." — **Michael Janda** (not Chris Do)

> "If it can be presented like a timeline … as you're scrolling down it's like 'oh, 2007 I
> first started playing with Photoshop, 2012 I started my business' … only highlight the things
> that are interesting, without consuming everything." — **Matthew Encina** (not Chris Do)

> "People always have these questions, and it's nice to just have a place to push them to." —
> **Matthew Encina** (not Chris Do, on the resources page)

> "A standard design portfolio site's purpose is to bring in new clients or land that sweet new
> job, and at this point in my life I'm not really interested in either … I have more to offer
> my audience than just a repository of old projects." — **Ben Burns** (not Chris Do)

> "It's not entirely focused on content, it's not entirely focused on a portfolio, and it's not
> really focused on conversions … it's focused on creating a deeper relationship with people
> online." — **Ben Burns** (not Chris Do)
