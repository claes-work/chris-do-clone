# Web Design Process – Finding Inspiration for Personal Website

## Summary

Episode 2 of "Built by Hand," a Futur series in which **Ben Burns** — designer, creative
director — documents rebuilding his long-unfinished personal website. This episode covers the
creative-research / inspiration-gathering phase: how Ben moves from sitemap and wireframes (Ep. 1)
to setting a visual direction via mood boards. He interviews Croatian design director **Hrvoje
Grubišić** (caption-garbled throughout as "her voya / urvoid / orvoie") about how top designers
find inspiration, then walks through his own "micro-research" method of sourcing seven design
ingredients and settling on a grotesque typeface (Stelvio Grotesque). **Chris Do does NOT appear
in this video.**

**Speaker:** Host is **Ben Burns** (not Chris Do). Guest is **Hrvoje Grubišić**, design director
from Croatia. **No Chris-Do-attributed content in this video — the entire source is CONTEXT /
do-not-train for the persona.** Logged for corpus completeness; nothing here trains the clone.

## Key claims (2021-06-24, paraphrased — all NON-Chris speakers; context only)

- **(Ben Burns)** The Futur's client-discovery research process runs: (1) exercises that help the
  client describe the look they want, (2) group the words they use and identify patterns, (3) use
  those patterns to find visual references fitting the brief, (4) compile everything into
  stylescapes that communicate the direction. There is a deliberate "give and take" — a game of
  telephone where the designer's interpretation of the client's words is "where the magic happens."
- **(Ben Burns)** Designing for himself is *harder* than for clients: he creates better "from an
  empathetic position" with an outside perspective, and coming up empty for himself is the very
  reason this personal site was never finished. (Biographical — Ben, not Chris.)
- **(Hrvoje Grubišić)** Top designers make research a *daily habit*, not a project-triggered
  phase — they research and self-educate every day, constantly collecting and curating, then
  "make a withdrawal" from their archives when a project needs references.
- **(Hrvoje Grubišić)** Move away from the "basic platforms" for inspiration; he favors **Tumblr**
  for print-design and graphic-design collections. He was building a categorized online typography
  base for the community (e.g. "good alternatives to Helvetica") to make finding fonts easier.
- **(Ben Burns)** **Mood board vs. stylescape:** a mood board is a collection of images on a board
  that sets visual direction; a stylescape is the same content *curated and edited into a single
  composition* so it "looks like one thing instead of a collection of things," making it far easier
  to communicate a vision to clients. He uses stylescapes whenever a client is involved, but sticks
  to mood boards for himself since he can read the patterns unaided.
- **(Ben Burns)** Best inspiration often comes from *outside* the usual digital platforms — photos
  of typography, iconography, and signage while traveling or walking downtown; museums, libraries,
  books, flea markets. He regretted not "mixing up his sources" (COVID-era constraints limited him
  to online research).
- **(Ben Burns) "Micro-research" method:** if the finished design is the main dish, research each
  *ingredient* separately rather than copying whole websites — this prevents the temptation to
  clone someone else's design. He sourced seven categories: (1) grid & layout, (2) typography,
  (3) navigation, (4) color, (5) link/button/form-field styling, (6) imagery, (7) interactions
  & animations.
- **(Ben Burns)** Direction choices for his own brand: full-screen menu, subdued palette leaning
  toward "hunter green" as primary for interactive elements; deliberately breaking the typical
  **12-column grid** in favor of off-center, asymmetrical, "fresh" compositions; and avoiding
  full-width "banded" layouts.
- **(Ben Burns)** He defined his brand via a set of productive contradictions: "humble but
  confident," "sophisticated without being standoffish," "a little grit / rough around the edges
  but still capable of beauty" — patterns that then guided reference-gathering.
- **(Ben Burns)** Typography journey: drawn to **grotesque** typefaces (low contrast in height and
  weight, quirky characters like double-story a's and g's, deliberately un-polished). Loved
  **Founders Grotesque** but was bothered by its tight apertures on C/G and its high cost (all 35
  styles), so he chose **Stelvio Grotesque** (by Danilo De Marco, Italy) as a near-match with
  balanced apertures that works both as display and body text.
- **(Ben Burns)** Picking the typeface is only half the battle; using it well is the other — he
  collected reference usages (huge size contrast between headings and body; small all-caps
  headings paired with larger body paragraphs).
- **(Ben Burns)** Framing note repeated at the end: "I am not here to tell anybody what they should
  do — I'm here just to share my process… find the way that works for you." (Method, not doctrine.)
