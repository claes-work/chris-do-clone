## Summary

**Built By Hand, Ep. 3.** Ben Burns designs his own personal portfolio website, moving
from the mood-board/research phase (Ep. 2, the companion) into pushing pixels on the
actual layout. The episode is half design tutorial (his desktop-first process, grid →
type → color → detail, type scale vs. type system) and half candid diary about the
emotional low of designing for yourself — imposter syndrome, self-doubt, and the
"creative process" curve. It weaves in short guest reflections from other designers on
how they cope with doubt and where their work comes from.

**Speaker:** Ben Burns (Futur team designer/creative director) — CONTEXT, do-not-train.

## Key claims (2021-07-16 — Ben Burns, do-not-train context)

_All bullets are paraphrased Ben Burns statements unless quoted below. Retained as
design-craft reference only; NOT persona material._

- Designing for yourself is harder than client work — you lose the outside perspective
  and get "too close" to see the work; there's no client to validate it (2021-07-16).
- His personal habit: always start on **desktop / full desktop layout first**, explicitly
  rejecting mobile-first ("I know mobile-first is a thing… I just don't like it") — framed
  as personal preference, not the "correct" way (2021-07-16).
- He always begins a site with two specific pages: the **home page** (most creative
  freedom, but the hardest page — get it out of the way early) and a **blog-post page**
  (packed with all the typography — paragraphs, headings, block quotes, ordered/unordered
  lists — so you can iron out type on one page first) (2021-07-16).
- His design order: **grid first** (the "backbone" — plot a good grid even if it changes)
  → typography → color → imagery → interactive elements → **detail design last** (buttons,
  forms, links) (2021-07-16).
- Client vs. personal: for clients he wants **all content finalized before designing**
  ("I need to know what I'm designing for"); for his own site he uses **placeholders**
  because he has "ultimate control" over content and can shoot new images or cut sections
  as needed (2021-07-16).
- **Type scale vs. type system** (definitions he offers): a *type scale* is a group of
  sizes that work together because they grow/shrink in the same proportion (e.g. start at
  16px, then 32, then 64); a *type system* is how you actually deploy those sizes
  (primary headings vs. captions vs. paragraphs vs. sub-headers). Establish the system
  early. He landed on ~22px body text ("pretty large for body text… I'm a fan") (2021-07-16).
- Detail choices this episode: a high-contrast display typeface ("Vultura"-ish; caption
  garble likely) with brush-stroke terminals, a hover/mouse-takeover reveal on a lower
  quadrant, a 5-column grid with a left menu column that reads "centered" despite an
  offset layout, and a desired full-screen menu (2021-07-16).
- On the creative process: self-doubt is "the curse of the creative," and great work sits
  "on the other side of that." He describes a well-known **creative-process curve** —
  baseline → inspired high → make something and crash ("it sucks" → "I suck," where most
  people quit) → keep pushing until it's "okay," then "pretty good"; if you abandon ship at
  the low you never close the gap between taste and ability (2021-07-16).
- Biographical (Ben Burns, context — not subject): never went to design school,
  self-taught / "homegrown," learned the craft on the job; recently moved across the
  country and is building a house, working from a small office above the garage; carries
  ongoing imposter syndrome partly from being "part of the Futur" (2021-07-16).
- Process nudge: he forgets to use his **sketchbook/pencil** mid-project (starts with it,
  rarely returns until the next project) — re-inspired by Sebastian to sketch the
  full-screen menu by hand (2021-07-16).
- Framing disclaimer he repeats: the series isn't a flex or a "how-to-do-it-right" — it's
  about showing the hidden struggle/creative journey; "you'll never create anything great
  if you never create anything at all" (2021-07-16).

## Notable verbatim quotes

_All quotes are context, do-not-train. Attributed per speaker._

**Ben Burns (host — CONTEXT, not the subject):**

> "My name is Ben Burns, I'm a designer, creative director and father of the year, five
> years running. I'm on a journey to rethink, revamp and redesign my personal website —
> it's the only project that I've never finished."

> "Every time I start designing a website I start with the desktop, full desktop layout,
> and I always start with two very specific pages: the home page and a blog post page."

> "I usually begin with the grid… even if it changes later, because the grid is really the
> backbone of the entire design… then I start figuring out my typography and I start
> bringing in color and imagery and interactive elements, and usually the last step is
> detail design."

> "The personal doubt is just the curse of the creative, and I think that great work is
> just on the other side of that."

> "You'll never create anything great if you never create anything at all."

> "I'm not here to tell you how to do things, I'm simply sharing my journey. There are
> better designers and engineers out there who you can learn from — just pick up what you
> need, leave what you don't, find the process that works for you."

**Sebastian (guest UI designer — CONTEXT):**

> "I create all of this from scratch every time, I don't copy-paste stuff… it's more like a
> flow and go with it, and then think about: okay, what does data visualization have — it
> has bars, it has some kind of scales — then think about elements, components in UI
> design… mix it up and try to make it look as functional as possible."

**Mr Cup (guest — CONTEXT):**

> "The creative process for me is still, I will say quietly, hard… it's not really as smooth
> as it seems, there are always doubts."
