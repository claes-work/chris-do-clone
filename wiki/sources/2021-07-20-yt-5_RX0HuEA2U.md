# How To Tell Stories That Win Hearts & Minds

## Summary

Part of a Futur series on helping viewers create content that comes naturally to them, find their voice, and build community. Chris Do restates his storytelling formula — a **personal story that elicits an emotional response with one clear takeaway** — but the genuinely-new contribution here is a push to make story quality **objectively measurable**: convert the fuzzy word "good" into yes/no, true/false, binary criteria so ideas can be critiqued fairly and without ego. He also offers shareability tells (would you share it, save it, take notes on it?) as an objective signal that content carries a real takeaway. **Speaker:** the teaching is **Chris Do** (first person — "I want to tell you what the goal of stories are," "I want to enlist your help," references his own content-for-leads philosophy). A **Futur team narrator** frames and recaps in third person ("tris has always been an advocate…," lines 15–31, 217–259) — marked context / do-not-train below.

## Key claims

- [2021-07-20] The formula to win hearts and minds: tell a **personal story** → that **elicits an emotional response** → with **one clear takeaway**. (paraphrase — restatement of the [[2021-05-13-yt-AzrL1TOPS54]] formula; you must feel the emotion for the audience to feel it)
- [2021-07-20] Content must earn its keep — **ROI for the effort**: the goal is highly engaging content that connects with your audience of choice; "return for the effort" is the objective bar. (paraphrase)
- [2021-07-20] A **personal** story means revealing something of yourself that **no one else can claim** — it must carry your voice and creative DNA; someone else writing it disqualifies it. Test: "Can someone else write this story other than you?" If yes, start over. (paraphrase)
- [2021-07-20] The emotional response is what **separates you from a machine / a copy-and-paste creative** — anger, tears, relief, inspiration; filter the story through your lens to connect human-to-human. (paraphrase)
- [2021-07-20] Aim for **one** clear takeaway, not many — build around a single lesson and you may end up with more; try to do too much and you get zero. (paraphrase — matches 2021-05-13 "one clear point")
- [2021-07-20] **Make story quality objective, ideally binary.** Turn "write a good story" (unmeasurable — "what is good?") into criteria with yes/no answers (e.g. "write a story that has 400 characters" is checkable). Binary removes the guesswork that feelings and subjective observation introduce. (paraphrase — the new, central idea of this video)
- [2021-07-20] Use a **checklist** after writing: does it make the reader feel something? can they remain indifferent? is there a struggle in it? If it can't score positive on each, re-engineer the story. (paraphrase)
- [2021-07-20] Reframe **critique** to be objective and neutral, without judgment: instead of "I like it / I don't like it," say "I don't recognize where the struggle is" or "I'm not feeling anything — it'd be more relatable/empathetic if you did X." That way the receiver hears rather than defends. (paraphrase)
- [2021-07-20] **Shareability as an objective takeaway signal:** the tells that content is valuable are behavioral — are you likely to **share** it, **save** it for later, or **take notes / highlight** it? No takeaway, no sharing. Chris says he often drags memes/posts into a folder because he senses "there's something there." (paraphrase)
- [2021-07-20] Summarize the lesson explicitly for the audience — people don't always understand what you mean; make it easy and give them one thing they can act on in their life, or nobody will do it. (paraphrase)
- [2021-07-20] Content creation is Chris's advocated route to **leads and trust**; this series aims to lower the friction so people who struggle to create can find a format that fits them — and Chris notes he's stepping out of his own comfort zone to share more. (paraphrase; the framing narration echoes this — context)

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (teaching):

> "You want to tell a personal story, so you have to be in your story, it has to have your voice… that means you have to reveal something of yourself that no one else can claim."

> "This separates you from a machine and a robot — you're not a copy-and-paste creative."

> "If it's going to have a lesson in it, it's going to be memorable… we want a clear takeaway — not takeaways, just one takeaway."

> "If I tell you 'write a good story' — well, what is good? But if I tell you to write a story that has 400 characters in it, we know we can hit that. It either has 400 or it does not."

> "We almost want to be binary in it, because it removes the guesswork. When we introduce feelings and subjective observations, it makes it much harder to figure out if we're on the right path."

> "My question would be: can someone else write this story other than you? If the answer is yes, go back to the starting point."

> "We don't want to just say 'I like it, I don't like it.' We want to say, 'I don't recognize where the struggle is,' or 'I'm not feeling anything after reading this'… you see how it changes it from being subjective to being much more objective and neutral, without judgment."

> "Are you likely to share this? …will you save this, and are you likely to take notes? …that's a really good indicator that this is becoming a piece of valuable content."

Futur team narrator (framing/recap — **context only, do-not-train**):

> "If you are a long-time viewer of the Futur, then you probably know that [Chris] has always been an advocate for content creation to get leads — that is the best way to get trust from your customer."

> "The formula to tell a story that can win heart and mind is that you need to tell a personal story that elicits an emotional response from your audience… after you [tell] your story you make people feel how you feel."
