# How To Write Better Instagram Content To Get Engagement

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do (first-person throughout the substantive clip, lines 1–101). The
closing 20 seconds (lines 102–122) are **Greg Gunn**, producer of The Futur Podcast,
delivering a podcast-promo outro — CONTEXT, do-not-train.

Chris walks through his personal, "into-the-weeds" workflow for turning a raw idea into an
Instagram carousel + caption and then into a cross-platform content loop. The genuinely-new
wrinkle beyond the existing IG playbook is the **caption-writing method**: he writes the
caption *the next morning, after he's forgotten what the carousel was about*, so the copy
reads fresh and clear — and he frames the caption as a *longer story you've given him
permission to tell* by clicking in. He also stresses that engagement comes from being
present for dialogue (answering every comment for the first half hour), and that audience
reactions feed the *reshaping* of the same idea into a differently-framed LinkedIn post.

## Key claims (all `[2021-07-25]`, paraphrased unless quoted)

- **He is an "emotional writer" — writes from anger or joy, not a calm/neutral state.**
  Writing done "cool and calm and collected" comes out "robotic," "dry," and uninteresting;
  emotion makes the writing much better. `[2021-07-25]`
- **His idea pipeline starts from being asked a question.** A question forces a succinct
  answer; when an answer "feels pretty good," he copy-pastes it from a DM into Twitter to
  test how the audience reacts. `[2021-07-25]`
- **He escalates by audience energy.** If a tweet gets "a lot of energy behind it, good and
  bad," he refines the message and builds an Instagram carousel from it, then attaches it to
  a Story. Reaction, not a content calendar, is the promotion signal. `[2021-07-25]`
- **Carousels must be light on text** — "the carousels cannot have too much type in it
  because it's boring for people to read a lot of text." (Reinforces the existing
  carousel-rubric guidance.) `[2021-07-25]`
- **★ The caption is written fresh the next morning, after forgetting the carousel.** He
  posts around 7:00 a.m. right when he wakes up; by then he's forgotten the carousel's
  content, so he quickly scans it and writes "a story about the carousel" with a clear mind.
  Result: overlapping-but-added detail, written cleanly because it's first thing in the
  morning. `[2021-07-25]`
- **★ Caption = a longer story you've been given permission to tell.** His rationale: if the
  carousel made you curious about the story behind it, you'll read the caption — so by
  reading it "I already have your permission to tell you a longer story," which keeps him
  "respectful of your time." (A permission-marketing framing applied at the post level.)
  `[2021-07-25]`
- **Engagement is driven by presence, not the post.** He reads and responds to *all*
  comments for roughly the first half hour after posting; over time the audience recognizes
  he's "there for the dialogue," not for thumbs-up. Moderator "Mo" tells him the comments on
  his posts are "just fire." `[2021-07-25]`
- **★ The reaction feeds a cross-platform reframing loop.** Audience feedback reshapes how he
  thinks as "an artist," and he uses it to create a *different* post on LinkedIn — "same
  content, but framed differently." He resists pure copy-paste (though he admits doing it
  sometimes "cuz I'm lazy or there's just not enough time"); he prefers to *react to the
  conversation*. `[2021-07-25]`

The full documented loop: **question/DM answer → Twitter test → refine → Instagram carousel
+ Story → next-morning caption → respond to all comments → reframed LinkedIn post.**

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "I'm an emotional writer. I can't write when I'm not angry, or if I'm not feeling joyful,
> and I need to write from an emotional place because the writing is much better." — Chris Do
> `[2021-07-25]`

> "Often times, if I'm writing in the just cool and calm collected space, the writing is
> really robotic and really dry and not interesting." — Chris Do `[2021-07-25]`

> "By the next morning, when I'm ready to post it... I've forgotten about what the carousel's
> about. And I have to write a caption. So I quickly scan the carousel, and then I write a
> story about the carousel." — Chris Do `[2021-07-25]`

> "If you looked at the carousel and you're really curious about the story behind the
> carousel, then you'll read the caption. And then therefore I already have your permission
> to tell you a longer story... I'm respectful of your time." — Chris Do `[2021-07-25]`

> "Over time people have come to recognize that I'm there for the dialogue with you. I'm not
> just there to like get your thumbs up... I'm here for the conversation. So I read their
> comments. I respond to them, all of them, for the first half hour or so." — Chris Do
> `[2021-07-25]`

> "As an artist... I put something out into the world. I have a feeling about this thing. And
> when the way you react is a different feeling than what I thought, it makes me rethink how I
> want to shape what it is I'm saying." — Chris Do `[2021-07-25]`

> "I use all of that feedback... to then create a different post on LinkedIn. Same content,
> but it's going to be framed differently. Cuz I've learned, right?" — Chris Do `[2021-07-25]`
