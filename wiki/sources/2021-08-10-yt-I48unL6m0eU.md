# Tips On How To Price Your Design Work And Make A Profit

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do (solo teaching, live whiteboard/spreadsheet demo). Identity confirmed by his signature Futur sign-off ("you can send 10% to me... I got kids, they go to private school, somebody's gotta help me out with the bills") and the coaching-a-student format (he prompts an off-camera person "Frank"). Pricing is Chris's core domain — **train.**

Chris walks through how to build a project **bid** the way a business owner does rather than the way a beginner freelancer does. The naïve method — estimate hours × an hourly rate and submit that number — "is not a business, there's no profit and there's no room for error." He rebuilds the same job as a line-item **cost stack** (artist, producer, art director, workstation, hardware/software) where every input is **marked up above what you'd pay to fill that role**, then adds a final **profit margin** on top of the subtotal. The illustrative job goes from a $5,000 naïve bid to a ~$20,400 business bid.

## Key claims

_All dated 2021-08-10; paraphrased unless quoted below._

- **How NOT to bid:** calculate your time, pick an hourly rate, and guesstimate. Example: $50/hr → ~$500/day → 10-day job → $5,000 submitted. That's not a business — no profit and no room for error.
- **The business reframe:** the right question is "how much would you have to pay somebody else to do that job?" — and you must also pay yourself. You bid based on what it costs to *staff* the job, not what it costs *you* to do it.
- **Never charge what you pay.** You cannot charge the client the same amount you pay the person doing the work — if the job runs long or you underestimate, you go into the red. There must be a spread between cost and price.
- **Mark up the maker's rate.** An artist who knows what they're doing is ~$500/day; mark that billed rate up to ~$750/day because "artists always underestimate what it's going to take." At 10 days that turns a $5,000 line into $7,500 — a potential $2,500 of profit that absorbs delay/estimation risk.
- **Add a producer/coordinator** (~$400/day × 10 days = $4,000) — someone has to manage the project. Whether or not you actually hire one is irrelevant; the line has to be in the bid because the function is real. If you produce it yourself, that money becomes your profit.
- **Add an art director** (~$900/day) but **prorate** the days — you don't art-direct every day, so bill e.g. 5 days across the length of the job (~$4,500). Prorating spreads your directing time across the project rather than billing it daily.
- **Bill overhead as line items.** You can't itemize rent/utilities directly, so roll them into billable resources — e.g. a **3D/PC workstation rental** at ~$500/week × 2 weeks = ~$1,000, plus rolled-in **hardware and software cost** (rather than delineating every piece). Note: you can't bill the laptop used to *make the bid*, but you can bill the production workstation.
- **Then add profit margin on the subtotal.** After the cost stack subtotals (~$17,000), add a **markup of 10–30%** (varies by country and industry) as explicit profit. "You just tell them it's markup, it's our profit — everybody who runs a business understands that." At 20% the example lands at ~$20,400.
  - > ⚠️ Caption garble on the numbers: at line 197–208 the transcript says "let's say it's 20 [%]" but then computes "10 percent of that is seventeen hundred so thirty four... thirty three thousand four hundred." The internally consistent reading is a **20% markup** on a ~$17,000 subtotal (2 × $1,700 = $3,400) → **~$20,400** final bid, which matches his closing recap ("your new bid at [$20],400... the difference being $15,400"). The stray "$33,400" and "10 percent" are transcription noise, not a second figure.
- **Where the profit comes from:** the markup and padding exist to cover overhead and to survive things "out of your control" — delays, underestimation, rendering time. Profit is the reward for absorbing that risk.
- **Extend the stack as needed:** modelers, and even **rendering time**, can be billed as separate line items because they're outside your control.
- **The headline delta:** same job, naïve bid $5,000 vs. business bid ~$20,400 — a ~$15,400 difference, all of it from staffing the bid like a business and adding margin.

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (method, not doctrine): This video builds the bid **bottom-up from a marked-up cost/day-rate stack** — closer to *cost-plus* mechanics than to pure **value-based / "price the client, not the job"** pricing, Chris's signature current doctrine (see [[wiki/topics/pricing/pricing]] §1). It is not a true contradiction: this is aimed at *production/execution bidding* (motion/3D-style staffed jobs with hard costs and deadlines), where a defensible line-item bid protects margin — a different situation than value-pricing a strategic/brand outcome. Frame it as the **cost-stack / bid-buildup** technique that coexists with value pricing, not as a reversal of it.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "So how do you bid? Here's how you don't bid: you calculate your time, and you do an hourly rate, and you guesstimate." — Chris Do

> "That's not a business. There's no profit, and there's no room for error. The way you look at it as a business person, or the entrepreneur, is: how much would you have to pay somebody else to do that job? And that's how you bid it — and you have to pay yourself." — Chris Do

> "You cannot pay the person and charge the same amount as you're going to pay the person. It doesn't make any sense." — Chris Do

> "Now whether or not you hire a producer and pay that amount isn't relevant. It needs to be in there, because somebody has to manage the project." — Chris Do

> "Now as a business we need to make profit. This is where we cover our overhead... profit is going to be anywhere between 10 to 30 percent depending on what country and where you're from and what industry you're in. So you just mark that up — and you just tell them it's markup, it's our profit. Everybody that runs a business understands that." — Chris Do

> "The difference between your initial bid at 5,000 and your new bid at [20],400 is fifteen thousand four hundred. You can send me a check — ten percent of whatever that profit difference is — because I got kids, they go to private school. Somebody's gotta help me out with the bills." — Chris Do
