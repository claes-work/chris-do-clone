## Summary

**Built By Hand, Ep. 4.** Having designed his personal website in the previous episodes,
Ben Burns now builds it — a "no-code" development episode centered on **Webflow** (a
disclosed sponsor). It's part tutorial, part diary: he explains the CSS **box model**
(margin/padding, divs, containers), lays out his page-structure framework
(sections → containers/rows → up to five columns), solves **responsive typography** with
a referenced third-party "wizardry" technique, and builds a single CMS collection that
drives a multi-content-type template (blog / video / podcast). He closes with a
motivational "you don't need permission to start" note.

## Key claims (2021-08-10 — Ben Burns, do-not-train context)

_All bullets are paraphrased Ben Burns statements unless quoted below. Retained as
design-craft / no-code reference only; NOT persona material._

- The premise "Built By Hand" means doing everything himself; normally he'd **hire
  developers** to build designed sites (custom theme or fully custom) and hand off to
  experts, which he still recommends as a "beautiful collaboration" — designers and
  developers are "two sides of the same coin" (2021-08-10).
- Web development felt like "a different language" coming from an identity/print/packaging
  background; he learned by "hacking things together" and trial and error, never fully
  learning HTML/CSS/JS, "picking up bits and pieces" instead (2021-08-10).
- Platform choice is the biggest make-or-break decision for a no-/light-code site; after
  years of stock themes, page builders, WYSIWYG editors, Flash and Dreamweaver, the only
  no-code platform he builds with now is **Webflow** — a visual development tool
  (2021-08-10). *(Note: sponsor-disclosed; he states he'd use and pay for it regardless.)*
- Why he favors Webflow: complete visual control, **clean front-end code** (confirmed with
  engineer friends), a process similar to how a developer would actually build, intuitive
  basic animation controls, and client benefits (maintenance engagements, hosting on your
  own account, locking areas clients shouldn't edit) (2021-08-10).
- Webflow **teaches web fundamentals as you build** because its right-hand controls use the
  same naming/principles as HTML and CSS — one of his main reasons for recommending it
  (2021-08-10).
- **Box model** explainer: on the web everything is a rectangular box (images, text,
  video); boxes nest inside container boxes; the biggest is the page **body**, and smaller
  invisible container boxes are **divs** ("the Legos of a webpage"). Position is determined
  by an element's relationship to everything else — you can't just drop something anywhere.
  **Margin** = clear space *around* an element; **padding** = clear space *inside* the
  parent box (2021-08-10).
- His **page-structure framework**: page body → **sections** (header/content/footer) →
  **containers** (which he treats like table "rows") → **columns** (up to five per row in
  his grid). Flexible: an element can span all five columns or split e.g. 3+2. On narrow
  screens columns stack; on wide screens they expand to fill (2021-08-10).
- **Responsive typography** was his sticking point: he wanted type to scale with the
  browser (viewport units) but it "breaks at specific screen sizes." He knows he could do
  it in custom CSS with a `calc()` function but didn't want to hand-code the whole site.
  He solved it with a referenced Webflow tutorial by **Timothy Rix** ("wizardry") — type
  scales responsively but **locks at a certain point**, avoiding odd wrapping/orphans/widows
  (2021-08-10).
- Workflow tip — the **class effect**: once you style a piece of text (a class) you never
  restyle it; reusing classes, symbols and components sped him from ~3–4 hours for the
  first page to ~2 hours, then ~15 minutes per page (2021-08-10).
- **Class-naming** (beginner vs. pro): don't use Webflow's auto-generated names
  (e.g. "header 712" tells you nothing); use a systematic naming scheme; **never use capital
  letters or spaces** in class names (they're hard to reference in custom code)
  (2021-08-10).
- **CMS plan**: one single collection/database holds all content, driving one template that
  conditionally shows a header image (blog), video embed (video), or podcast player
  (podcast) via show/hide blocks tied to a content-type field. For podcasts he uses an
  **HTML embed** div so different shows' embed codes all fit (2021-08-10).
- Because of his column framework, making the site **mobile-friendly** was mostly ensuring
  columns stacked correctly and text stayed legible — the development was "pretty painless,"
  which he admits didn't make for a dramatic episode (2021-08-10).
- Motivational close (his recurring theme): you don't need permission, a degree, or "enough
  experience" to start — "here it is… go try something." Roughly **20 hours** of development
  were compressed into the episode (2021-08-10).

## Notable verbatim quotes

_All quotes are context, do-not-train. Attributed per speaker._

**Ben Burns (host — CONTEXT, not the subject):**

> "My name is Ben Burns, I'm a designer, creative director and father of the year, five
> years running. I'm on a journey to rethink, revamp and redesign my personal website —
> it's the only project that I've never finished."

> "How do I develop a custom website — a quality custom website — from scratch without
> knowing how to code?"

> "On the web everything you put on screen is considered a box… these elements all live
> inside other containers, which are also boxes."

> "Once you've styled a piece of text you never need to do that again… that's the class
> effect."

> "Here's a key separation between a beginner and a pro: don't use the names that Webflow
> comes up with… having some kind of a system is absolutely essential to staying organized."

> "In the tech world I'm an old dog, and if I can learn new tricks, trying new things, so
> can you… if you're waiting for permission or a sign, well, here it is."

> "I'm not here to tell you how to do things, I'm simply sharing my journey — so pick up
> what you need, leave what you don't, find the process that works for you."

**Ron Segal (guest designer/educator — CONTEXT; transcribed "Ron Seagal"):**

> "Back in my day when we were chatting with people it wasn't Slack, it was just forums…
> there was a way to embed HTML, so if you wanted your posts to look really cool you'd put
> a border on it or make the font bigger — so we'd have to learn a little bit of HTML and
> inline CSS."
