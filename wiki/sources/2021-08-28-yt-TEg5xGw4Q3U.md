# Bidding Fundamentals — How To Price Projects

## Summary

A short Futur Pro Group archive clip in which Chris Do explains how to give a
client a number when the project scope is still unknown. He introduces a
"scope variation" funnel (uncertainty is huge at the start, tightens through
discovery), the vocabulary of an **ROM** (rough order-of-magnitude bid) and a
**SWAG** (scientific wild-ass guess), and a **contingency-fee** mechanic for
protecting a fixed bid against scope creep. He frames it all against Blair
Enns's *Win Without Pitching* proclamation "replace proposals with
conversation."

**Speaker:** Chris Do — trainable. First-person throughout ("I'm in the brand
strategy space… the design consultancy space"), narrating his own studio's
practice; the outro identifies the material as being "from the archives of the
Futur Pro Group" (Chris's coaching calls). Only the closing member testimonial
(an unnamed pro member, ~lines 149–162) and the pro-group ad read are
context/do-not-train.

## Key claims

_All dated 2021-08-28 (clip is an archived pro-group call; original call date
not stated). Paraphrased unless quoted._

- **Building detailed estimates for clients who won't pay near your number wastes
  your time** — the framing problem the clip solves for.
- **Chris positions himself out of the "estimate/bid-building business"** and into
  brand strategy / design consultancy, invoking *Win Without Pitching*'s
  proclamation "thou shalt replace proposals with conversation." Reinforces the
  value-based / anti-proposal doctrine already in the wiki (§1–§2, §37–§38).
- **Scope variation funnel.** At the project's start the scope has so many
  variables it's "almost impossible to predict" — "it could be at sea level or it
  could be as high as Everest." Discovery in the middle tightens the scope; only
  once there's a definitive plan can you give an accurate **fixed bid** (you know
  "how many screws, sheets of drywall, linear foot of flooring").
- **ROM (rough order-of-magnitude bid)** — used when you know nothing and the
  client is just exploring feasibility: "Is it a $10,000 job, a hundred[-thousand],
  a million-dollar job, or ten million?" A first gate to see if the project is even
  feasible.
- **SWAG = "scientific wild-ass guess"** (vs. a plain **WAG**, "wild-ass guess").
  Chris uses "SWAG" deliberately because it signals the estimate is "not based on
  nothing — it's based on some theories," i.e. an informed guess you can defend.
- **When a client demands a fixed bid before scope exists, estimate HIGH.** The
  alternative — bidding low and hitting the client with overages later — creates
  friction, and "the scope almost never decreases… so the price eventually goes
  up." Options: overshoot at the start, or start at a baseline and build up.
- **Contingency-fee mechanic.** Present a fixed bid plus a stated contingency
  (Chris's example: **15%**). "My bid is 100; if all goes well it'll be 100, but it
  could be as high as 115,000 — I want you to be prepared for that." The overage is
  only charged if scope expands or variations are needed, so the client isn't
  surprised when you come back for more money.
  > ⚠️ CONTRADICTION / EVOLUTION: The "estimate high on a fixed bid + tack on a
  > contingency fee" mechanic is **fixed-bid / cost-plus-era pricing**, not the
  > value-based end state. Chris explicitly de-recommends it in the moment: "I
  > don't recommend bidding this way — I'm just telling you what you may be
  > comfortable with today to protect yourself." **Value-based pricing (price the
  > outcome, not the job; replace proposals with conversation) is the current
  > doctrine** (§1, §37–§38); treat the contingency-fee tactic as a defensive
  > fallback for those still selling fixed bids. Consistent with the standing
  > value-based-is-current / cost-plus-is-history callout on
  > [[wiki/topics/pricing/pricing]] §37.
- **Smart clients already carry their own contingency.** A client receiving a $100
  bid may submit ~$115 (or a 30% contingency → ~$130) to their accounting
  department so they're pre-budgeted and don't have to go back "hat in hand" for
  more. If you build one in and they build one in, "everybody's protected — and if
  you don't spend that money, everybody's a hero."

> ⚠️ Caption-garble note on numbers: the clip's "$115,000" (line 96) and the
> $100 / $115 / $130 client-side figures are transcript-rendered and should be
> read as illustrative round numbers, not exact quoted prices.

## Notable verbatim quotes

All Chris Do unless marked.

> "Thou shalt replace proposals with conversation… I'm not in the estimate-building
> business. I'm in the brand strategy space, I'm in the design consultancy space."

> "Have you guys heard of the term WAG before? … a wild-ass [guess]. But I'm gonna
> tell you about the real term — it's called SWAG: a scientific wild-ass guess…
> it's not based on nothing, it's based on some theories."

> "At the beginning, when we don't know anything about the project, the scope has
> so many variables to it it's almost impossible to predict. It could be at sea
> level or it could be as high as Everest — we just don't know."

> "There's a term, it's called an orders-of-magnitude bid — the real term is ROM
> — that's when we don't know anything and the client's just exploring… is it a
> ten-thousand-dollar job, a hundred, or a million-dollar job, or ten million?"

> "When some clients ask us to do a fixed bid when there's no scope, what do we do?
> We estimate high… the scope almost never decreases, because that's an ideal world
> that we do not live in — so the price eventually goes up."

> "So here's my fixed bid, and depending on where this goes I need you to know that
> there's a fifteen[-percent] contingency fee. My bid is 100 — if all goes well it'll
> be 100, but it could be as high as 115,000."

> "I don't recommend bidding this way. I'm just telling you what you may be
> comfortable with today to protect yourself."

> "If you build it in and they build it in, everybody's protected. And if you don't
> spend that money, everybody's a hero."
