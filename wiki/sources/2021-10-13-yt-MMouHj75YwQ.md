# Change The Way You Solve Problems Using Design Thinking

## Summary
A Futur explainer/tutorial teaching design thinking as a repeatable, human-centered problem-solving methodology. It defines design thinking (via Herbert Simon), argues for keeping people at the center and asking questions to reach root causes, and demonstrates with the Washington Monument case study plus a personal Silicon Valley consulting anecdote. The presentation ends with a call to download a "problem tree analysis" template from the description.

## Key claims
Dated 2021-10-13, paraphrased unless quoted. All attributed to Eric (Futur), not Chris — context only.

- Design thinking, per economist Herbert Simon (late 1960s), is "nothing more than moving from an undesirable situation to a desirable one" — e.g., hungry → fed; you design every day at different levels. [paraphrase]
- Design thinking asks you to look at problems as interconnected systems to reach a more elegant solution. [paraphrase]
- It is essential for two reasons: (1) it puts people first — the closer you stay to the person and their stories, the better the solution; (2) it provides a repeatable process that reliably leads to successful solutions, backed by studies from Stanford to Fortune 500 companies like PepsiCo. [paraphrase]
- Design thinking can solve any problem, from business to personal — the process is the same, only the content differs (Eric uses it for client communication strategy and to help his daughter communicate with peers). [paraphrase]
- The "problem tree analysis" tool maps a problem as a tree: the trunk is the problem, visible effects branch upward, and causes sit below the ground; the real challenge is getting to the invisible root causes. [paraphrase]
- Design thinking helps you question whether you have everything you need to solve the problem, and reframe to reach the true root. [paraphrase]
- Washington Monument case study: the monument was crumbling; the maintenance team blamed cleaning chemicals, but repeated "why?" questioning traced it to pigeons → spiders → moths → lights turned on an hour before dusk. Turning the lights off later produced an ~85% reduction in moths (fewer spiders, fewer birds/poop) and lower cleaning costs. [paraphrase]
- Personal anecdote: a Silicon Valley company in 10x growth had top sellers dragged down by admin work (contracts, SOWs) and slow onboarding; stakeholders arrived "pre-diagnosed," insisting the fix was new software. [paraphrase]
- Before solving, ask three questions: (1) "Do we know this is true?" (surfaces biases / mental models / past research), (2) "Who else believes it's true?" (get researchers and context into the room), (3) "Who makes the final decision?" and are you all approaching the problem the same way — mismatched approaches create misalignment and red flags. [paraphrase]
- Three takeaways: keep the human at the center to reach elegant solutions and raving fans; use a repeatable process (like problem tree analysis) to build a universal language others can join; and ask questions to get to root causes. [paraphrase]
