# Dealing With Negative Feedback – Whiteboard Session

## Summary
A ~90-minute live whiteboard session (Futur Pro group, delivered on Zoom) in which Chris Do lays out his full personal guide to both **giving and receiving** feedback and criticism. He builds from a foundation — you need an internal compass so external opinions can't wreck you — through frameworks for decoding hostile feedback (Criticism = Observation + Judgment; Marshall Rosenberg's Nonviolent Communication "jackal vs giraffe"; Tony Robbins' "expression of love or cry for help"), a belief→action→result loop for breaking creative fear, and a four-step method for giving feedback well (I-statements, observe-without-judgment). It closes with three takeaways: give grace (be a giraffe), separate opinion from data, adopt a learner's mindset.

**Speaker:** **Chris Do** — confirmed. Self-describes as "this weird bald Asian guy" in a cap (l.7-9), 20+ years in commercial production, taught 15+ years at Art Center / Otis (l.192-196); references his wife and son (names withheld per fidelity policy — family not-public) and his education company built on client work. First-person material trains the persona. Audience participants (Futur Pro members) are **context only, do-not-train**.

## Key claims
Dated 2021-12-21, paraphrased unless quoted. All attributed to Chris Do unless marked context.

- Most people lack an **internal compass / north star** for knowing whether their own work is good, so they outsource their emotional well-being to strangers' opinions; feedback "can wreck you if you have no internal sense of what is good and what is bad." [paraphrase]
- Opening anecdote: an executive producer read a lost pitch as "the team isn't giving its best" and wanted Chris pulled back in; Chris instead made the EP take responsibility for judging the work's quality *before* the client did — pushing ownership of the internal standard onto the person. [paraphrase]
- **Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation**: intrinsic = doing it for your own fulfillment/challenge (reward comes from you); extrinsic = to please others, gain reward, or avoid punishment. Both can coexist and that's fine — but relying only on external validation is what makes feedback dangerous. [paraphrase]
- The **inner critical voice**: we tend to parent ourselves the way we were parented; a belittling upbringing installs a harsh inner dialogue that we then apply to our own creative work. [paraphrase]
- Many creatives are "crippled by fear" of others' opinions and so never make the scarier, growth-expanding work; "if you step outside your zone of comfort, that circle, the zone expands to meet you." [paraphrase]
- We often *perceive* negative feedback where the message is actually neutral-to-positive — a childhood "flashback moment" to someone who had power over us (age ~3–9) amplifies neutral words into attacks. [paraphrase]
- **Belief → Action → Result triangle** (credited to Matthew Essom / "Matt" from a Clubhouse call): the loop is *bidirectional* — you can't just tell someone to believe differently; change the action or the desired result and you can rewire the belief. Method: paint the desired future-state result, then reverse-engineer the actions and beliefs (Chris ties this to his branding "paint the future state" teaching). [paraphrase]
- **Why good feedback is rare** (crowd-sourced list): people spare feelings / stay polite; few are actual experts; few have the vocabulary to articulate precisely; people report their pre-processed bias not what's actually there; missing shared context/objective; avoidance of conflict; not actionable; too prescriptive (robs autonomy); emotional projection; and some have bad intentions — "that's a troll," an online bully hurting others to preserve their own reality. [paraphrase; several points from participants — context]
- **Criticism = Observation + Judgment** ("COJ"). Observation done well is neutral and objective; judgment carries opinion, bias, and assigned value. To process feedback you separate the two — keep the observation/data, strip out the judgment ("pull the kernel away from the husk"). ★ genuinely-new framework. [paraphrase]
- **Three types of feedback**: (1) destructive/critical — "old mother-in-law feedback," pure opinion and projection, meant to belittle; (2) arbitrary analogies — knee-jerk "that reminds me of…" non-sequiturs from untrained people; (3) constructive/helpful — actionable, context-aware, genuine desire to give you data. Constructive does NOT mean positive; negative feedback can be constructive. Good feedback "threads the needle" between too abstract and too prescriptive. [paraphrase] ★
- **Marshall Rosenberg's Nonviolent Communication** (Chris cites the late Dr. Rosenberg's book/workshop, links it in the description): the *jackal* (aggressive) vs the *giraffe* (gentle). You can't control how others speak, but you can *listen with giraffe ears*. Binary reframe: people are either saying "thank you" or "please [help me understand]." Everything that doesn't sound like a genuine thank-you should be decoded as a "please." ★ genuinely-new framework. [paraphrase]
- **Tony Robbins reframe** (attributed): every human interaction is either an expression of love or a cry for help. If it's clearly not love, interpret it as a cry for help — even brutal comments ("your video is stupid") get bent into a request ("please make it shorter / watch your tone"). [paraphrase]
- **Processing feedback step-by-step**: strip judgment → keep data; if it confirms what you know, say thanks but it doesn't help you grow; if it challenges you, ask "Is this useful? What else could this mean? What can I learn? What's actionable?" Don't throw the baby out with the bathwater — nuggets hide in negative feedback. Some feedback (slurs, "hey baldy") has nothing actionable — just move on / mute it. [paraphrase] ★
- **Giving feedback well** — use **I-statements / me-statements**, not you-statements, to sound nonviolent and build rapport. Walk the person through: what I *see* → how I'm *processing* it → what I'm *feeling* → what's *working* for me → what's *not working* → all *relative to the stated objective/goal*. Never fake praise ("don't say it unless you genuinely mean it"). [paraphrase] ★
- **Mandates vs. parameters** (prompted by Paul): mandates are non-negotiable (logo must be in corporate colors, in this corner, for X seconds); parameters are the freedom zone ("within this clean, modern area, go to town"). Concrete parameters + clear objectives let creatives thrive. [paraphrase]
- **Four-quadrant method to get better at feedback**: (1) observe without judgment (be a "human recorder"); (2) extract key insights/takeaways from the notes; (3) abstract those into universal rules you can apply broadly (his example: specific type-pairing lesson → "scale and contrast is the key to good design"); (4) turn it into actionable checklist steps. [paraphrase] ★
- Evolution of his own teaching: early on he gave prescriptive feedback ("here's what you should do"), then adopted the **Socratic method** (ask calculated questions to lead people to discover the answer themselves) — more powerful but slow and skill-intensive; took him 10+ years. He teases a "Socratic version" of the session if it hits 200k views. [paraphrase]
- Status/influence amplifies feedback (Paul): "what they whisper, they hear as a shout" — a creative director to a junior, a parent to a child, a boss to a subordinate; be mindful because your words carry magnified weight. [paraphrase; participant framing, Chris affirms]
- **Three closing takeaways**: (1) give grace / be less judgmental — act like giraffes not jackals, because how we treat others rewires how we treat ourselves (the grammar/kerning police who savage typos "don't make anything either"); (2) separate opinion from data and keep the good data to grow; (3) adopt a learner's mindset — "in life, you either win or you learn." [paraphrase] ★
- On the "internal compass" payoff: fewer than ~5–10 people you know on social media are actually capable of nuanced, actionable, well-intentioned feedback — so most negative feedback deserves to be muted, especially if it stops you from creating. [paraphrase]

> ⚠️ Note on overlap: the maxim **"no opinion can hurt you without your permission"** (already in `persona/voice.md` and `beliefs`, dated 2021-02-05) is *thematically* present here but is **not stated verbatim**. This session does not merely restate it — it supplies the underlying *machinery* (COJ formula, NVC jackal/giraffe, love-or-cry-for-help, the process for stripping judgment from data). That mechanism is genuinely new and worth promoting; the slogan itself adds nothing new.
