# How To Get Work & Make More Money

## Summary

Short (~2.5 min) rapid-fire Q&A/roleplay on @thefutur in which an off-camera interviewer poses common career questions ("How do I get a job in design?", "How do I raise my rate?", "How do I get more clients in my city?") and Chris Do answers each with a concrete tactical script. Six scenarios are covered: landing a design job, moving up inside a company, raising rates with an existing client, setting your rate as a beginner, and getting local clients. The through-line is targeted, personalized outreach and problem-solving over broad self-promotion, plus having multiple clients so no single one has leverage over your pricing.

**Speaker:** Chris Do (answering all questions; confirmed at line 44–45 where the interviewer asks "chris do you have an example of this" and Chris replies "i make youtube videos"). The interviewer's lines are the setup/role-play prompts — context only, not persona training data. All advice is Chris-Do-attributed and trainable.

## Key claims

All dated **2021-12-26**, paraphrased, attributed to **Chris Do** unless noted.

- To land a job at a design company: pick **three target companies**, engage with them on social media (follow, write thoughtful comments, share their good posts), and make your avatar look like you across all platforms so they can connect a face to the name when they meet you. (Chris Do)
- Write each target a **"love letter"** — a credit to **Dan Mall** ("dan maul" is a caption garble for Dan Mall) — a customized landing page addressed to the specific person you've been speaking to, so it feels like a personal connection. Pair it with portfolio work chosen to be **specific and complementary** to the work that company does. (Chris Do, attributing the tactic to Dan Mall)
- To move up in a company you want to stay at long-term: schedule a meeting with your supervisor on the subject "how can I contribute more to this company in a way that's valuable?" If they don't hand you tangible tasks, reference **SMART goals** — get something specific and measurable you can check off. (Chris Do)
- For a content/video role, the **four things that matter most are views, engagement, subscribers, and revenue.** Set a benchmark of where you are now, have supervisors set the target, and — if the goal is realistic — ask upfront whether hitting it would warrant a pay raise or title change, tying the promotion to agreed measurable benchmarks. (Chris Do)
- To raise your rate with an **existing client**, do it by phone (not email). Script: "Thank you very much for thinking about me. I just want to let you know I have a new rate. My new rate is X. Does that work for you?" (Chris Do)
- The client is **allowed to say no**, just as you're allowed to say no. The way to hold the line is to **have more options** rather than depending on one client. (Chris Do)
- If you have more than one client, go to whoever pays you what you're worth; if you're really good, clients will realize they're losing out on your talent and pay more. (Chris Do)
- For a **beginner** setting a first rate: don't worry too much about the rate. Seek an opportunity where you can grow or that makes you feel happy and fulfilled — get **3 to 6 projects** under your belt first, because nobody wants to hire someone with no experience. (Chris Do)
- Once you have that experience, **ask people around you** (crew, peers) what someone with your skill set should charge, and calibrate from there. (Chris Do)
- To get more clients in your city: do **"hand-to-hand"** local outreach — meet local business owners (ideally ones you already have a rapport with) and look for a problem your service can solve. (Chris Do)
- Cold-open opener: ask "**How is business going?**" If it's not going well, that's your opening to solve their problem. If it's going well, ask "**But could it be better?**" — entrepreneurs always want to do better, so "yes" leads you right back to a problem you can solve. (Chris Do)
