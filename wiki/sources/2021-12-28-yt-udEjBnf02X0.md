# Can You Do It All & Be Successful? (Multi-hyphenate)

## Summary
A live coaching call plus a follow-up interview about whether a creative "multi-hyphenate" can pursue several careers at once. A coachee named **Connor** — juggling a DJ career, a teaching/community business, and a virtual-spaces (metaverse) company — is coached by a Futur creative-business coach named **Matt** (his self-told backstory of being a DJ, event photographer, and digital-marketing-agency owner ~5–6 years before coaching identifies him; consistent with Futur coach Matt Essam). A second Futur team member, **Jonah**, later interviews Connor ~4–5 months on to hear what changed. **Speaker:** NOT Chris Do — the material is voiced by **Matt (coach)**, **Connor (coachee)**, and **Jonah (interviewer)**. Treat the entire page as **context / do-not-train** for the Chris Do persona; none of it is Chris-attributed.

## Key claims
Dated 2021-12-28, paraphrased unless quoted. All attributed to Matt, Connor, or Jonah — context only, NOT Chris.

- (Matt) Most things do not end well when pursued in parallel — "if you try to catch two rabbits you don't catch any." Being pulled in multiple directions makes the first hard task simply *deciding*. [paraphrase]
- (Matt) "Decision" traces to a Latin root meaning to cut off; deciding means cutting off other options. [paraphrase — folk etymology, uncorroborated here]
- (Matt) Good decisions start from a *specific* desired outcome. "More money / more time / more opportunities" are too vague to act on; you must name the specific outcome for the specific area of life. [paraphrase]
- (Matt) Distinguish **vehicles** (the activities we think will make us feel a certain way) from **values** (the feelings themselves). The mistake is chasing the vehicle; you cannot chase multiple vehicles. Find *one* vehicle that meets all your emotional needs, and the other pursuits stop being missed. [paraphrase — core frame of the call]
- (Matt) His own most successful, highest-earning, highest-impact period came only when he had a *singular focus* (coaching), after quitting DJing/photography/agency work. [paraphrase]
- (Matt) If your rule is "I only feel significant when I'm the world's top DJ," you will feel insignificant most of the time; instead build a "menu" of everyday actions that already deliver the feeling — we author our own rules for when a value is met. [paraphrase]
- (Matt) **Law of familiarity:** the craved feeling is usually the one you've gone longest without; repeated exposure (e.g., DJing to 300+ people weekly) dampens the buzz over time. [paraphrase]
- (Matt) You must pick which "flavor sandwich" you'll eat (crediting Mark Manson) — every path has hard, sucky parts; the goal is to still get your values met incrementally rather than staying emotionally deficient until some far-off milestone. [paraphrase]
- (Matt) Recommends the book *Refuse to Choose* (attributes it to "Barbara Scheer" — actually **Barbara Sher**; caption/name garble), which argues creatives needn't choose — but in Matt's coaching experience the multi-focus path rarely proves financially sustainable, especially with high-ticket ($10–20k) clients who demand real focus and delivery. [paraphrase; ⚠ author name misheard]
- (Matt) People with six companies "didn't start out like that"; even Will Smith is "only good at like two or three things" and holds a belief that you should be very good at a *few* things. Once you get one thing successful, keeping it there and building on it is far easier. [paraphrase]
- (Matt) "Most people overestimate what they can achieve in a year and underestimate what they can achieve in a decade" (attributed by Connor to Tony Robbins). At ~20 explore broadly; by ~30, focus and build a path. [paraphrase]
- (Matt) The inability to say no to opportunity is an insecurity / scarcity mindset. "Nothing will give you more opportunity than saying no." His DJ anecdote: saying no to an $80 booking freed the date and pushed his rate from $80 → $200 → $600 as people "fight for a yes." [paraphrase]
- (Connor, follow-up ~4–5 months later) "Matt was right." Trying to do everything at once, "life will force you into one of them" — you get cornered by time limits and must choose. He set DJing aside (a booking's cancellation was "one of the biggest reliefs"), and **combined** teaching + virtual spaces into one offer (teaching people to build metaverse worlds), following Ben Burns's course model. [paraphrase]
- (Connor) His coping system while ideas incubate: a "right foot / left foot" alternation — plant/water one venture, switch to the other during the natural lag, until the two "combine into a single direction" like riding a bicycle. Businesses take time regardless of hours thrown at them. [paraphrase]
- (Connor) Chose to learn by experience despite knowing Matt was right ("my forehead is nine inches thick... a stubborn bastard"); as a teacher himself he respects students who follow their gut against advice — they may be the next innovation. [paraphrase]
- (Connor) Advice to others: when a coach/teacher says something your gut and heart disagree with, disregard it and follow your gut; when it rings true, go with it. Caveat: someone under time/financial pressure should take the expert's fast "escape hatch." [paraphrase]
- (Connor) "Mistakes are way more valuable than successes" — you always know *why* you failed, but success can come by luck and trick you into thinking you're the reason ("good luck is bad luck"); early success is dangerous. [paraphrase]
- (Connor) Reframes his 15+ years as a "creative generalist" as an asset: transferable creativity "pierces" all mediums, but he concedes he "won't be able to achieve mastery in any of these domains unless I exclude the others." [paraphrase]

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION: This call's thesis — collapse to **one vehicle / singular focus / say no** — runs against Chris Do's own repeated positioning advice to **specialize externally, generalize internally** (be known for one thing publicly while remaining a broad generalist privately). Matt's framing treats generalism as a trap to be escaped; Chris frames it as an internal capability paired with an external niche. Because this is NOT Chris-attributed, do not reconcile it into `persona/`; only flag if it is ever mis-cited as Chris's view. See [[wiki/topics/branding/branding]].

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (internal to source): Connor simultaneously concludes "Matt was right, you must choose" AND defends the generalist path ("I would do it again"). The tension resolves as: choose ONE focus at a time, but let skills compound and recombine over years — not a clean endorsement of either pole.

## Notable verbatim quotes
Attributed per speaker. All are context / do-not-train (no Chris Do speech in this video).

> "If you try to catch two rabbits you don't catch any." — Matt (coach)

> "There's a difference between vehicles — the things that we think are going to give us those feelings — and values, which are those feelings themselves. And the mistake that I was making was I was chasing the vehicle... What you need to do is find one vehicle to give you all of your values." — Matt (coach)

> "The most successful I've ever been in my life is in this business, because it's the only time I've ever had a singular focus." — Matt (coach)

> "If you have to be the world's top DJ to feel significant, dude, you're going to spend a lot of time feeling insignificant." — Matt (coach)

> "Nothing will give you more opportunity than saying no. That's just facts. Nothing will give you more money than saying no." — Matt (coach)

> "If you try to do all things at once, life will force you into one of them. You will end up cornered against what is possible for you to achieve in a certain amount of time." — Connor (coachee)

> "Mistakes are way more valuable than successes, because... when you fail you always know why you failed... good luck is bad luck, because if you get good luck you don't know why you're succeeding." — Connor (coachee)

> "I won't be able to achieve mastery in any of these domains unless I exclude the others — so I would do it again, because my transferable knowledge... has helped me spearhead in a way that is very unique." — Connor (coachee)
