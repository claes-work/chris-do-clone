# Create A Worthy Goal

## Summary

A Futur workshop built around **Michael Bungay Stanier's** book *How to Begin*, teaching his framework for drafting a "worthy goal" — a goal that is simultaneously **thrilling, important, and daunting**. Chris Do hosts and co-facilitates, working through the exercises live as a participant and closing the session with his own coaching on distilling a goal into five words.

**Speaker:** Two speakers. **Michael Bungay Stanier (guest, teacher)** delivers nearly all the goal-setting framework (worthy goal, the three elements, the four tests, the drafting method) — this is HIS material and is marked **context / do-not-train** for the persona. **Chris Do** appears as host/participant; his own contributions (the closing lesson on reducing a goal to five words, the Pareto-on-itself idea, clarity-then-musicality, the McCartney story) ARE Chris-Do-attributed and eligible to train the persona. Attribution is called out per bullet and per quote below.

## Key claims (2022-01-11)

**Michael Bungay Stanier (guest — context / do-not-train):**
- A "worthy goal" must combine three elements: **thrilling** (lights you up, delightfully selfish, internal motivation), **important** (gives more to the world than it takes, external motivation), and **daunting** (you know how to start but not how you'd finish, the engine of personal growth). (paraphrase)
- SMART goals are a weak *starting* point: "smart" is an acronym for tightening/lowering/shrinking a goal; a well-shaped SMART goal can still be "polishing a turd" if it's the wrong goal in the first place. (paraphrase)
  > ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (framing, not Chris): This anti-SMART, "go big / daunting" stance sits in tension with productivity advice elsewhere in the corpus favoring small, incremental goals and habits (e.g. "stop-hourly", small-goals framings). Note this is the GUEST's position, not Chris Do's — do not attribute to the persona.
- "Worthy" means worthy *of you and your life*, not on a moral pedestal; worthy goals can exist at an **intimate scale** (family, a street, a small audience) — you don't have to lead a revolution to have one. (paraphrase)
- Missing one element has a diagnosis: thrilling+important but not daunting = you've plateaued/stopped growing; thrilling+daunting but not important = too self-centered; important+daunting but not thrilling = you lose internal drive. (paraphrase)
- Four tests to pressure-test a draft: the **Spouseish test** (share it with someone who tells you the truth — their answer isn't truth, just a sounding), the **FOSO test** ("for the sake of what?" — a gentler substitute for "why," which triggers defensiveness), the **Goldilocks-zone test** (right heft — not too big/vague, not too small/a mere habit), and the **Voting test** (score thrilling/important/daunting 1–7 each; if the total is below 18/21 it isn't right yet). (paraphrase)
- Method: write a deliberately **"crappy first draft,"** then an **"active second draft"** that starts with a **verb** — because focusing on the finish-line outcome can trick the brain into feeling done (the way vision boards can *lessen* ambition); greatness comes from *working on* hard things, not from achieving/winning them. (paraphrase)
- Refine by adding a single word along five axes: commitment, audience/geography, time (by when), standard (e.g. his podcast's "top 3% / 10k downloads per episode"), or outcome (profitable/sustainable). (paraphrase)

**Chris Do (subject — trainable):**
- To sharpen any goal or message, **reduce it to five words or fewer** — the fewer the words, the more memorable and impactful; complexity fights memorability. (paraphrase) ★
- Apply the **Pareto principle to itself**: take the vital 20%, then the 20% of that, and keep cutting until you reach the single essential idea. (paraphrase)
- Sequence for crafting a line: **clarity first, then rhythm/musicality** — say it out loud, rewrite it many ways, use a thesaurus and native-speaker friends until it "sings." (paraphrase) ★
- A worthy goal is stronger when it connects to your existing sense of self and a promise you already make to the world (he praised the "loud introvert" tagline tying to Chris's own goal). (paraphrase)
- When he drafted his own worthy goal live, he narrowed "teach people" to "teach introverts" using the Goldilocks test to make it harder and truer to himself, then escalated it to "at TEDx" to add the daunting element. (paraphrase — Chris as participant)

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Michael Bungay Stanier (guest — context / do-not-train):**
> "We unlock our greatness by working on the hard stuff." — Michael Bungay Stanier

> "A worthy goal needs to be thrilling and important and daunting." — Michael Bungay Stanier

> "Daunting is when you know how to start but you don't really know how you'd finish." — Michael Bungay Stanier (crediting Liz Wiseman)

**Chris Do (subject — trainable):**
> "Whenever you can, try to reduce your ideas, your words, your headline to the fewest number of words, because that makes it more memorable and more impactful." — Chris Do

> "We want clarity first and then we want a rhythm or musicality to it second." — Chris Do

> "It's a reduction, and then you make this super short, potent thing. And the challenge is: can you say it in five words or less?" — Chris Do

> "[Paul McCartney] is like, 'Yeah, I know that makes more sense, but it don't sing good, and it has to sing good.'" — Chris Do (retelling, on word choice and musicality)
