# Design Thinking vs. Problem Solving

## Summary
A Futur explainer/tutorial contrasting "traditional problem solving" with "design thinking" across six shared components — decision making, collaboration, brainstorming, designing, outcomes, and accountability. The host argues each component is done worse in a traditional/analytical mode (in a vacuum, siloed, assumption-driven, perfection-seeking) and better under design thinking (inclusive, co-created, cross-functional, MVP-oriented). Appears to be a direct follow-up to the 2021-10-13 design-thinking video.

## Key claims
Dated 2022-01-28, paraphrased unless quoted. Attributed to the Futur host (not Chris) — context only.

- Problem solving and design thinking share six components: decision making, collaboration, brainstorming, designing, outcomes, and (underlying all) accountability — you must get the work done and not let brainstorming run forever. [paraphrase]
- The difference is not the components but how they are performed: traditional performs them "in a vacuum," design thinking performs them openly and inclusively. [paraphrase]
- Traditional decision making is often based on unchallenged assumptions (e.g., "my website isn't getting traffic because it's not pretty"); those assumptions must be challenged. [paraphrase]
- Traditional collaboration silos people (designers with designers, developers with developers, managers with managers), which destroys trust between teams. Design thinking replaces silos with co-creation and trust. [paraphrase]
- Traditional brainstorming dismisses wild/outlandish ideas as "too expensive" or "that'll never work"; design thinking lets wild ideas flourish. "There are no bad ideas, just priorities." [paraphrase + quote below]
- Traditional design treats the designer as a job title brought in at the end; design thinking brings the designer in along the way as a cross-functional collaborator who talks to clients and coders (e.g., "will this call-to-action button break your code?"). [paraphrase]
- Traditional outcomes use rigid metrics and withhold the solution until it's "perfect"; "perfection is the enemy of good." Design thinking replaces perfect with MVP (minimal viable product / minimal viable audience) to learn fast. [paraphrase + quote below]
- Prefers "learn fast" over the term "fail fast"; ship something good and learn from it (e.g., a logo designer doing 20–50 iterations should keep showing and kicking ideas around rather than perfecting one). [paraphrase]
- On "inclusive": means including the people who can help solve the problem and ensuring everyone is heard so the team is informed and solves the right problem — it does NOT mean everyone gets their way. [paraphrase]
