# Legal Tax Write-Offs for Designers (w/ CPA)

## Summary

A long interview episode in which **Chris Do (host)** interviews **Hector Garcia, a CPA licensed in Florida** (a QuickBooks-focused content creator who self-describes as "a marketer in nature") about tax deductions for designers, freelancers, and content creators. The bulk of the substance is Hector's tax explanation — pro-bono work, charitable-contribution floors/ceilings, the "ordinary, necessary and reasonable" IRS test, wardrobe/prop deductibility, vehicle wraps, tax deferral, and loopholes. **All tax advice is attributed to Hector Garcia and is context / do-not-train** (and Hector himself gives an explicit CPA disclaimer that none of it is tax advice). Only Chris's own framing, analogies, and self-reported attitudes are persona-eligible.

**Speaker:** Chris Do (host — persona-eligible lines only) + Hector Garcia, CPA (guest expert — context/do-not-train).

## Key claims

_Dated 2022-03-10. Paraphrased unless quoted. Tax claims are **Hector Garcia's (CPA) — context/do-not-train**; Chris's framing/attitude claims are marked as such._

**Hector Garcia (CPA) — context, do-not-train:**
- Donated pro-bono work generally yields **no net deduction**: if you never booked the work as income, there is no income to deduct against, and a deduction generally requires a cash (or cash-like) expenditure (2022-03-10).
- The costs of doing pro-bono work (employee wages, rent, equipment) are **already** deducted as ordinary business expenses, so there is no separate "extra" pro-bono deduction (2022-03-10).
- Charitable contributions have a federal **ceiling of ~60% of AGI** (was 50%, may revert to 50% after 2025) and, more importantly, a **floor**: they only help if itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction (~$12,500 single / ~$25,000 married filing jointly at the time) (2022-03-10).
- Calling any charitable contribution a "tax write-off" is misleading marketing because of these floors and ceilings; **donate because you believe in the cause, not for the tax benefit** (2022-03-10).
- Instead of donating, you can **co-sponsor a charity's event/fundraiser** (logo placement, sponsored table) and treat it as a 100%-deductible advertising expense — but you cannot disguise a plain donation (t-shirt/mug) as advertising (2022-03-10).
- The IRS test for a business deduction is **"ordinary, necessary and reasonable"**; audits typically run 2–3 years in arrears, so documentation connecting an expense to real business is essential; an "envy effect" can bias auditors against luxury deductions (2022-03-10).
- **Wardrobe/makeup:** a regular suit or high-end clothing is not deductible; adding a **company logo** makes clothing a "uniform" (unsuitable for normal wear) and deductible; on-camera makeup/hair for a YouTuber/performer is generally acceptable; items tied to your brand/channel are "props" (2022-03-10).
- Cites a tax-court precedent (an 1980s exotic dancer whose surgical implants were ruled deductible **"stage props"**) as the logic extended to hats, glasses, and set pieces used as props (2022-03-10). _(Self-reported case anecdote; not verified here.)_
- **Vehicles:** a fully **wrapped** vehicle is 100% deductible; a mere decal is not permanent, so you must instead deduct the business-mileage percentage (2022-03-10).
- "Avoid tax = legal; evade tax = illegal." Much of the tax code is **social engineering** (Section 179 equipment deduction, child tax credit, R&D credit, temporary 100% meals deduction in 2021–2022 to help restaurants post-pandemic) (2022-03-10).
- Distinguishes **arguable vs. a good argument**: many things can be argued deductible, but only strong arguments should be taken; don't "take the stupid deduction" and open a can of worms (invokes Blair Enns: "if you're going to have a body in the trunk, don't speed") (2022-03-10).
- Three rapid-fire tips: (1) keep a **year-round accounting system** (e.g., QuickBooks), separate business/personal accounts; (2) use **tax-deferral** vehicles (401k, IRA, pension/defined-benefit plans); (3) high earners should **shift ordinary income to passive income** (royalties, capital gains) (2022-03-10).
- Wealthy people use expensive, real (not "elusive") loopholes: layered **corporations + trusts, domestic and offshore**, with documented inter-entity contracts; out of reach for the average earner whose low-hanging option is simply deferral (2022-03-10).

**Chris Do (host) — framing/attitude, persona-eligible:**
- Frames Hector's tax-code social engineering with his own analogy: **tax incentives work like social-media algorithms** — the platform (or government) tells you what behavior it will reward with free reach (or lower tax), and it's "a game" with rewards and penalties (2022-03-10).
- Connects Hector's "story behind the numbers" to Chris's own principle: **document the legitimate business narrative** so that, if audited, there is a consistent story — an inconsistent "party animal" social-media presence undermines your tax return (2022-03-10).
- Self-reported: Chris is a **high-income earner** with income from his business, YouTube, and courses; he "pays a lot of tax and hate[s] it," has become "jaded" about it, but calls it "a great problem to have," and defers what he doesn't need now (2022-03-10).
- Self-reported origin note: in high school Chris was on a path toward accounting but his grades/discipline steered him toward creative/marketing work instead (2022-03-10).
- Recounts prior CPA guidance he's received: you have a "legal and moral obligation to legally limit what you owe," but only a **finite** set of tax strategies exist to reduce liability (2022-03-10).
- Explains why he wears a **hat**: not for sun but because oily/bald-head glare creates a lighting hot spot on camera; it became part of his brand, to the point audiences "don't recognize" him without it (2022-03-10).
