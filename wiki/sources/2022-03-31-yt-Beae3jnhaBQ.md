# Build Your Audience On This One Principle

## Summary

Chris Do makes the case that the single principle behind building an audience or
community is **reciprocity** — the social contract, drawn from Robert Cialdini's
*Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*, that when you give people something they feel
compelled to give back. He reframes the fear that giving away knowledge diminishes you
("as if there's 100 of me and every piece I give chips away at who I am"), arguing instead
that giving costs you nothing and actually *builds* something new — a bridge, a structure
you can later trade on. The payoff: a community shows up for you (buys the book, the
tickets, the workshop) because you already "paid them in advance."

**Speaker:** Chris Do — single-speaker monologue, first person, teaching. Persona-eligible.

## Key claims (all `[2022-03-31]`, paraphrased unless quoted)

- The one principle that builds an audience/community is **reciprocity**: do something
  for people and most (not all) feel innately compelled to give back — "the more you get,
  the more you have to give," because people want to right the balance of the scale.
- Reciprocity is one of the **six triggers** Robert Cialdini describes in *Influence: The
  Psychology of Persuasion* — an unspoken social contract that let strangers form
  cooperative communities. (Caption garble: "robert caldini" = Robert Cialdini.)
- Reciprocity is rooted in humans shifting from nomadic hunter-gatherers to cooperative
  societies (hunter feeds the village; others mind the farm and kids) — we are
  "cooperative and social by nature."
- **Marketers exploit reciprocity** by giving something of low cost to them but high
  perceived value so you feel compelled to give back — examples: tourist-spot free
  book/flower followed by a donation ask; a restaurant's surprise pre-check bite driving
  a measurable tip increase; free candy/chocolate samples that spike sales (he cites
  "~40%" and See's Candies). Once we're wise to it we refuse the free item because
  accepting it makes us feel obligated.
- **The scarcity-mindset fear he refutes:** in a modern context we think giving away
  information/knowledge makes us *less* — "as if there's 100 of me and every piece I give
  starts to erode who I am."
- **The reframe:** giving costs you nothing and takes nothing from you; instead of eroding
  you, giving *builds* something new — "a bridge... a house, a structure" you can then use
  and trade for something else. That is what building an audience/community actually is.
- The community shows up for you because you show up for them: when you have a book, a
  concert, a talk, or workshop tickets, people show up because "you've already given —
  you've paid them in advance — and they will return that to you."

> balance of that scale." — Chris Do
