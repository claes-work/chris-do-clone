# How To Improve Your Content

## Summary
Chris Do lays out his method for improving content: treat each piece as a scientific experiment — form a hypothesis, ship a rough version fast, read the engagement data, and iterate without emotional attachment to the outcome. He argues most people wrongly obsess over a single "perfect" result instead of running many cheap iterations, and that the person who posts 6-8 times learns far more than the person who agonizes over one. He frames staying with hard, unrewarding early attempts as "living in the dip." **Speaker:** Chris Do (solo monologue), first person throughout — persona-trainable. NOTE: the closing segment (lines 158-177) is a podcast promo delivered by **Greg Gunn**, producer of The Futur podcast — context/do-not-train.

## Key claims
- (2022-04-07) Success leaves clues: with enough attention, focus, and problem-decomposition ability, most things can be reverse-engineered — it's a process, not a secret talent or "magic."
- (2022-04-07) Step one is looking at things objectively — removing bias, emotional attachment, and preconceived ideas, and observing what actually exists before breaking it down.
- (2022-04-07) He decomposes complex problems using his "five ingredients" framework: can the thing be understood in five actionable, meaningful, clear, concrete steps? If so, the complex problem is now bite-sized. (References a prior dedicated video on this.)
- (2022-04-07) Speed / velocity is "everything" because it builds momentum: get whatever rough version of your hypothesis out as soon as possible to test whether it's correct.
- (2022-04-07) Too many people falsely weigh the result over the process — they pour all effort into one launch trying to hit every metric, and spend too long; he'd rather ship the same idea 4-8 times, learning through iteration so the 7th/8th piece is far more refined and informed by data.
- (2022-04-07) You must stay "in the dip" — the hard, difficult stretch where you want to quit and most people do. He says he embraces the dip, lives in it, and starts inside it.
- (2022-04-07) Attachment and expectations about how something "should" go blind you to objective data; approach it like a scientist — marry gut/instinct with data.
- (2022-04-07) On Instagram, the returning data is engagement, comments, follows, shares, and saves — imperfect but real signals; as long as data supports it, detachment lets you accept "the data suggests otherwise" and adjust even when you love the piece.
- (2022-04-07) Concrete example: during The Futur's two-week content challenge he "over-taught" and made carousels too meaty/complicated; the data said people aren't on Instagram for that, so he thinned the content out and iterated to a "sweet spot."
- (2022-04-07) Analogy: iterating is like Marco Polo in a pool — each post bounces a signal out and reflects back your position; making only one attempt is like being allowed to yell "Marco" only once, and insisting others are somewhere they aren't (bad data / self-deception) moves you the wrong direction.
- (2022-04-07) On decision-making: "we're emotional creatures" — he's as emotional as anyone, but for analytical decisions he temporarily sets the emotional brain aside to look at the data, then turns it back on. (This framing bookends the video.)
