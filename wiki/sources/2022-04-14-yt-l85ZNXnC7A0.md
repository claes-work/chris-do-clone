# How To Work With Clients From Other Countries

## Summary

Short solo teaching clip in which Chris Do lays out the four obstacles to working with
clients across borders — language, cultural differences, logistics (connectivity/time
zones), and trust — and prescribes a fix for each. His core stance: none of these are
real blockers if you take full ownership; the client's convenience always wins, and
trust is earned by process and proof rather than assumed. Practical, checklist-style
advice aimed at freelancers/agencies in non-US or non-native-English markets who want
to land international clients.

**Speaker:** Chris Do (solo). First-person framing ("if you want to work with me"),
his signature sales process (serve, ask, listen, empathize, summarize), and his
recurring MMA/mixed-martial-artist example all confirm attribution. No second speaker;
`[Music]` is a cue, not a guest. Attribution is clear (Chris) — train.

## Key claims

All dated 2022-04-14, paraphrased, attributed to Chris Do.

- Four things must be overcome to work with clients from other countries: **language,
  cultural/connectivity differences, time zones, and trust** — and none is that hard
  to solve.
- **Learn English from native speakers**, not from others in your own country (Chris
  claims non-native-taught English becomes "a weird version"); tools like Rosetta
  Stone or Duolingo and online classes help. The real goal is the ability to
  articulate your ideas clearly.
- **Cultural difference is often an advantage**, not a liability: an outside
  perspective is "a breath of fresh air" and lets you tap indigenous colors, patterns,
  shapes and forms competitors don't have.
- **Time zones should never be an issue because you work on the client's clock.** If a
  client or brand partner matters to you, you look up their time zone yourself and sync
  your schedule to theirs — getting this wrong reflects badly on you.
- When a client is many time zones away, ask **"What time works best for you for calls
  and updates?"** and adjust your calendar to be fully compatible with theirs. Never
  make any of these logistics the client's problem.
- **Trust is the biggest obstacle** — the client doesn't know you, you're in another
  country, and their legal recourse is weak (they won't hire an attorney to chase you
  over released IP or disappeared assets/money).
- Earn trust by **following the sales process — serve, ask, listen, empathize,
  summarize** — so the client feels understood, listened to, and important.
- Additional trust-builders: **a bank of testimonials from international clients**,
  **referrals** (e.g., connecting them to another client in their exact city/niche),
  **escrow** (via an escrow company so the client can recover their money if there's a
  problem), and **certifications** (Adobe Certified, Apple, BBB, Verisign, etc.), which
  people value "for whatever reason."
- The **single strongest move**: acknowledge the difficulty openly ("I know it's not
  easy to work with someone in another country/time zone — how might I earn your
  trust?") and **propose starting with a small project** to prove yourself before the
  client takes any big, risky decision.

## Notable verbatim quotes

All Chris Do.

> "You want to learn english from a native english speaker and start to work on your
> ability to articulate your ideas and that will help you overcome a lot of the
> cultural differences."

> "Sometimes cultural differences are an advantage because you have a very different
> perspective and you can bring a breath of fresh air to something."

> "Time should not be an issue whatsoever because you work on the client's time. So if
> you want to work with me you need to know where I'm at and sync up your whole life."

> "I will adjust my calendar, my time, my work schedule to be completely compatible

> "You can follow my sales process which is to serve, to ask, to listen, to empathize,
> and to summarize."

> "Probably the number one thing you can do is to say: I know it's not easy for you to
> work with somebody in another country, in another time zone — how might I earn your
