# Why Your Brand Needs a POV

## Summary

Chris Do argues that a brand must have a distinct point of view and personality — otherwise it is just "a vehicle for you to spew out marketing things," which only goes so far. He uses fast-food brands (Wendy's sassy Twitter persona, Arby's "We have the meat," McDonald's) to show how brands become relevant by acting like unapologetic human beings with attitude. He also recounts his own experience: writing content "under the banner of a corporation" felt stifling and miserable, and he only felt liberated once he wrote The Futur's content in his own first-person voice.

**Speaker:** Chris Do throughout the substance of the video (first person; references The Futur, and team members Elle/Matthew/Ben). The closing segment (a podcast promo) is Greg Gunn, producer of The Futur podcast — context/do-not-train, non-substantive promo.

## Key claims

Dated 2022-05-12, paraphrased unless quoted:

- A brand should be thought of as a separate entity with an imbued personality — it acts, talks, and behaves like a human being (he cites Nike, Adidas, Apple).
- A company and a personality can be two versions of the same person, showing different parts; humans are complex, so a brand can carry a broad spectrum of who you want to be.
- When speaking *as a company* you tend to be more formal, professional, business-like, and you filter to topics that attract potential clients. As a *human being* you are freer — you can talk about politics, religion, sex, whatever you want.
- Writing content as a "business" was stifling and miserable for Chris because it had to be inclusive and representative of far more people than just himself.
- The Futur felt liberating early on because it was essentially just Chris (and an intern): writing in the first person ("here's what I feel, here's what I'm thinking") solidified the brand's personality, voice, behavior, and core values.
- As the company grew, they split the writing: someone else writes *for The Futur*, and Chris writes *for himself*. Elle runs The Futur's Instagram social, initially by scraping already-produced content from Chris/Matthew/Ben so it "sounded and acted like us," then adding her own observations.
- A brand voice should not be only one person's voice — insisting "this is The Futur and it's only my voice" would exclude collaborators like Matthew (with whom Chris agrees on much but disagrees on some design decisions); a shared perspective is intentional.
- ⚠️ Tension (not a hard contradiction): as a brand gets bigger and more people weigh in, the voice can get "watered down," "very correct," "very safe." Chris says The Futur doesn't have that problem "for right now" — so the benefit of shared voice is stated alongside its risk (blandness at scale).
- Differentiation via personality/positioning: Wendy's took the #2/#3 burger position by assuming a fictitious sassy "Wendy" persona that claps back (e.g., the "we don't cut corners" reply to McDonald's mocking square burgers); Arby's took an unapologetic "We have the meat" attitude. Both became relevant by "searching to be human again."
- Bottom line: whatever you do — a corporation or a group of people — make sure it has a point of view; without one it's just a marketing vehicle with limited reach.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do:

> "there's a personality that's imbued and they act and talk and behave like human beings and so we start to think of them as separate entities"

> "I found it to be very stifling as a person who would speak in my own true voice yet [un]done the banner of a corporation or a business. And for a long time I tried to write content as a business and it was just miserable, it's horrible because it was inclusive and representative of a lot more people than myself"

> "I was very liberated when we started the future because at that point it was just really me and like an intern... I would just write in the I, here's what I feel, here's what I'm thinking, and that helped to really solidify the personality of the brand"

> "so the way we manage it is somebody else writes for the future and I write for myself"

> "sometimes as you get bigger and bigger it starts to get... watered down, it becomes very correct, it becomes very safe"

> "as each brand tries to find their voice they're searching to be human again"

> "whatever you do, whatever you decide, make sure whether it's a corporation, a bunch of people, that it has a point of view, otherwise it just becomes a vehicle for you to spew out marketing things which it only goes so far"
