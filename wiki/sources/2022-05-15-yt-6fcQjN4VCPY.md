# Limiting Beliefs, Behaviors — Do This Instead

## Summary

A Futur Pro Group archive Q&A in which Chris Do coaches members who are just starting out and stuck in a scarcity mindset. He argues that mounting bills and high expenses put you in a "horrible negotiation position" by design, so the fix is to live like a startup, keep expenses low, and build the internal conviction that you are already a "million-dollar designer." The through-line: your value comes from the results you can deliver and your own self-worth, not from external props (office, car, website, past work). **Speaker:** Chris Do (host, subject) answering Pro Group members — including a member named "Carrie/Kerry" (caption-uncertain) — plus a promotional outro narrator and a testimonial member at the end. Only Chris's statements are voice/belief training data; the members and narrator are context / do-not-train.

## Key claims

All dated 2022-05-15, paraphrased unless quoted.

- **Scarcity mindset weakens your negotiation position — by design.** If you have rent due and you're "starving," you are not in a good bargaining position; when the client offers 85 and you wanted 105, you'll take it. (Chris)
- **Keep expenses low so you can walk away.** Chris built his early career working from home in a loft with low expenses, which meant he could say no and wouldn't collapse "into the fetal position" if he lost a bid. (Chris)
- **Don't buy status props when starting out.** No shiny office, no expensive car, no impressive space to look good. He did multi-million-dollar jobs out of his house for years; agency clients (e.g. Chiat\Day — caption "shy day") came into his living room and he didn't care. (Chris)
- **"Live like a startup, do everything bootstrap"** so you can walk away from bad deals — because needing to close a job forces you into desperation, and clients "smell the desperation" and you start selling too hard. (Chris) — ties to a prior session's "back off on the selling" point.
- **Mental health over money.** He'd rather sit around and do nothing than do a bad job for a bad client who doesn't appreciate him; the mental-health cost outweighs the incoming money. (Chris)
- **"Think like a million-dollar designer."** A member (Carrie/Kerry) reports Chris told her to start thinking of herself as a million-dollar designer even while bootstrapping at her kitchen table; the reframe helped her internalize "I am good at this." (member testimony + Chris affirming)
- **Self-worth should be internal, not externally validated.** Your value isn't the car, home, website, or past work — it's what you're able to do. When you're not confident or "rock solid," you reach for external validation, but those things "come and go," so self-worth built on them fluctuates constantly. (Chris)
- **Identity-based selling: "you are what you say you are."** Don't sell your services or even your process — lead with who you are: honest, straightforward, transparent, a straight shooter who'll tell you if he can't help you. "If you like that, hire somebody else." (Chris)

> The scarcity-vs-abundance and belief-precedes-results cycle is already covered elsewhere in the wiki; this source is mostly reinforcing, NOT new. The genuinely-fresher angles here are the **operational** links — (1) low/bootstrapped expenses as the mechanism that gives you negotiation leverage and permission to walk away, and (2) the "million-dollar designer" identity reframe as a concrete self-worth exercise. Those are the L3-promotion candidates if the wiki lacks them.
