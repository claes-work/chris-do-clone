# Overcoming Stress & Anxiety (w/ Kyle T Webster)

## Summary

A Futur podcast interview in which Chris Do (host) draws out illustrator and Adobe evangelist Kyle T Webster on stage fright, anxiety, public-speaking craft, spec work, and pricing. Much of the runtime is Kyle's personal story and coping methods; Chris contributes his own confessions of pre-talk panic, his hard-won lesson about the "overdelivering" trap, and his coaching frames on valuing creative work and packaging IP. **Speaker:** Chris Do (host, subject — his statements are the only voice/belief training data) + Kyle T Webster (guest — context / do-not-train). Because the two men repeatedly agree they are "the same person," attribution must be read carefully: shared sentiment does not make Kyle's biography or advice Chris's.

## Key claims

All dated 2022-05-28, paraphrased unless quoted. Attribution marked per speaker; **only Chris's claims train.**

- **Chris is a self-described shy, awkward introvert who "freaks out" backstage.** He has the same pre-talk internal monologue Kyle describes and gets nervous before speaking, despite being a public figure. (Chris) — voice/biography reinforcement.
- **The "overdelivering" trap creates the stress.** Chris used to tell himself that if someone paid $500 to attend a multi-day conference, it was his "sole responsibility" to deliver at least $500 of value no matter what — which made him cram so much into a talk that it overwhelmed the audience. (Chris)
- **Cramming more value actually confuses the audience — a paradox to fight.** The human mind can only process so much; by putting more in, you fry the audience's circuits. The instinct to over-give must be actively resisted. (Chris)
- **Edit the talk down late, after it "marinates."** Chris now over-prepares to quell insecurity, then two days before, deletes tangents and non-essentials ("that's not necessary... we don't really need that"), stripping to what matters. He credits his wife for pushing him to stop jamming and rushing the end. (Chris) — Kyle independently describes the same "let it sit, trim the fat" habit (context), citing John Cleese on trimming sketches.
- **"Middle-shelf" advice: stop reaching for your top-shelf, newest material.** Chris relays a line from his friend Joel Pilger (via Pilger's mentor): give people your middle or even bottom-shelf stuff — even that, they'll "love and gobble up" — which eases the anxiety of always trying to debut the greatest, latest thing. (Chris relaying + endorsing; the origin is Pilger's mentor, so treat the maxim as cited-not-original.)
- **"Curse of knowledge" is why experts under-teach.** Chris names the cognitive bias: once you've figured something out you forget you didn't always know it, so you assume "doesn't everybody know this?" and withhold it — but it turns out no, they don't. (Chris naming/framing; Kyle supplies the surrounding anecdote as context.)
- **The real reward of a talk is the "aftertalk."** For Chris, "the work is the talk; the reward is the aftertalk" — meeting people, breaking the ice so the shy host can get feedback ("did you like this? where were you confused?") and re-energize. In-person talks leave him energized/"high"; Zoom talks (no energy back, muted faces, no after-talk) leave him drained. (Chris) — Kyle reports the identical energized-vs-exhausted contrast (context).
- **Ambivert framing.** Chris explains the extrovert/introvert spectrum and the "ambivert" in between: he always thought himself an introvert but online tests put him within ~5 points of extrovert. (Chris)
- **Spec / mock-portfolio work is a legitimate door-opener.** Chris defends building a spec portfolio of the work you *want* to do as a demonstration of skill; he doesn't know why some frown on it. If you're stuck doing work you don't want, "show them what you love to do, put in your own time, invest in that," and doors open. (Chris; Kyle's laid-off-2001 fake-Nike-portfolio story is the context that prompts it.)
- **Value pricing: double the budget to find the ceiling.** Chris coaches creatives to "just try and double the budget or push the boundaries a little bit so it gets uncomfortable" — that way you learn the theoretical ceiling. He notes the common resistance ("I can't do that, someone else will do it for less"). (Chris) — Kyle's parallel "always ask for more" (from Leo Espinosa) and his 9k-vs-4.5k story are context/do-not-train; only Chris's own coaching frame trains.
- **Package what you make for yourself into IP for a second income stream.** Chris: we're so busy making things for other people that we forget to ask "what am I doing for myself that can save other people time?" — package that, and you scale the business and buy back time. A separate income stream gives you the confidence to say no to bad-fit client work. (Chris; Kyle's Kyle Brush → Adobe acquisition is the illustrative context.)

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (voice/belief training data):

> "As a public speaker, as an introvert, as a super shy and awkward person myself, I have similar... the pre-talk talk with myself. Cuz I'm freaking out backstage."

> "I used to tell myself if somebody paid $500 to attend a multi-day conference, it was my sole responsibility to deliver at least $500 of value no matter what. And of course, that starts to stress you out and you start putting so many things into your presentation that it just becomes overwhelming for people."

> "We want to give value so we try to put more in it, but by putting more in it we confuse the audience... their circuits get fried. It's an instinct that we have to really fight."

> "[Joel Pilger's mentor said] just give them your middle or even your bottom shelf stuff because even that people will just love and gobble up."

> "It's a form of cognitive bias... the curse of knowledge, where you've figured it out and then you forgot that at one point in your life you didn't know this stuff, and then you don't want to teach it because it's like, duh, doesn't everybody know this? And it turns out, no."

> "For me, the work is the talk. The reward is what you call the aftertalk. Like, I want to meet people. I'm a shy, weird guy. So after I do the talk, it breaks the ice and people are brave enough to come up and talk to me... and that's how I re-energize."

> "When I say to people just try and double the budget or push the boundaries a little bit so that it gets uncomfortable, so you kind of know the theoretical ceiling..."

> "We're so busy making things for other people that we don't often take time to look at what am I doing for myself that can save other people time... package that, and that's how you start to package your IP and you're able to scale your business."

Kyle T Webster (guest — context / do-not-train):

> "Nobody sees that, but I am absolutely freaking out all the time. I just wear a really good mask."

> "If I tell them just one thing, one little nugget that they can take away they haven't heard before, I've done my job. That's all people can really remember anyway."

> "Whatever the job is, start by asking for more." (Kyle quoting illustrator Leo Espinosa)

> "If I'm my own customer, I can really go to town and knock it out of the park... I'm an artist who needs good digital brushes, so by creating the brushes I need, it's kind of a no-brainer that the rest of the illustration community is going to need them too."
