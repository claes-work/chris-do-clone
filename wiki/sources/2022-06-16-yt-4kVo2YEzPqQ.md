# The Psychological Trick Behind Getting People To Say Yes

## Summary

Chris Do explains how to get a prospect to sell themselves on the engagement instead of you having to persuade, pitch, or convince them. The core mechanism is Blair Enns's "retreat and follow" (from *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto*): take a half step back to test whether the person actually wants to work with you, because people like to pursue and dislike being pursued. He layers Jonathan Stark's "three why questions" (why this / why now / why me) on top, and cross-references Chris Voss's caution against "why" phrasing (use "what" instead if your tone isn't dialed in). **Speaker:** Chris Do (host/teacher; TRAIN). The closing ~20 seconds is a podcast promo read by Greg Gunn, producer of The Futur podcast — CONTEXT / do-not-train.

## Key claims (2022-06-16, paraphrased unless quoted)

- Stepping back is a **test**: it reveals whether the prospect genuinely wants to work with you. If they're not interested, no amount of "verbal ninjutsu" persuasion skill will save the deal — so don't waste time on them.
- To keep the "higher ground" in a sales conversation, **do not pursue**; you retain it by retreating. (Reframes selling as a status/frame game, not a persuasion game.)
- **Retreat and follow** (attributed to Blair Enns / *Win Without Pitching Manifesto*): chasing a job or a person makes them run — true in dating, in nature. Take a half step back; the genuinely interested party naturally follows. Analogy: at a party, step back and interested people move toward you, uninterested people leave.
- People like to pursue and dislike being pursued — so structure the interaction so the client does the pursuing.
- "Try to kill engagement at least three times" — deliberately say things that make the other party re-affirm they're really engaged (also sourced to the *Win Without Pitching Manifesto*).
- **Jonathan Stark's three "why" questions** as the mechanism: (1) **Why this** — why do you need to do this at all; (2) **Why now** — why can't you wait / do nothing; (3) **Why me** — of all your options, why me, given I'm likely more expensive than everyone else. Answering these, the prospect sells themselves *to themselves in front of you*, removing the need for you to convince, persuade, pitch, or sell.
- **Tonality is load-bearing** — the same why-questions land well or badly depending on delivery.
- **Contrast / nuance (not a hard contradiction):** Chris Voss's *Never Split the Difference* recommends **calibrated questions** and advises *against* "why" questions, because poorly phrased "why" makes people defensive. Chris Do reconciles the two: if you're still developing your client-communication and objection-handling skills, prefer **"what" questions** over "why" (and even over "how"), because "what" is open-ended and lets the other person stay neutral and objective. Examples: "What happens if you don't move forward with this project?" / "What happens if you just wait?"
- NB on prior coverage: this repo already covers Cialdini-style consistency/commitment and the broader win-without-pitching frame. **New/reinforced here:** the concrete "retreat and follow" half-step mechanic, the "kill engagement three times" tactic, Jonathan Stark's exact three-why triad, and the explicit Voss "use *what* not *why* while you're still learning" reconciliation — worth promoting.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do:
> "To retain the higher ground in the conversation, don't pursue."

> "You take a half step back to see if the person's interested and they naturally will follow... we like to pursue people, we don't like to be pursued."

> "Ask three why questions: why this... why now, why can't we wait, and then why me — of all the options you have, why would you want to work with me, because I'm more likely to come in more expensive than everyone else."

> "They are selling themselves to themselves in front of you. So you take away all the convincing, you take away all the persuasion and the selling and the pitching by just asking three why questions."

> "If your tone isn't right it can make the person feel really defensive... I would encourage you at this point in time to use the what questions and not even to ask the how questions. What questions really are open-ended and it's easier for people to remain neutral and objective."

> "If they're not interested in working with you, no matter what kind of skills — verbal ninjutsu skills — that you might have... it's not going to work, so don't waste your time with them."
