# Stop Apologizing For Being Your True Authentic Self

## Summary

Short, intimate monologue in which Chris Do reflects on coming to a place in his life where he is comfortable with who he is and will no longer apologize for being himself. He frames finding design as the mechanism through which he found and built his own identity, and tells two stories about handling criticism (a testy live-stream exchange and a Clubhouse "shade" incident) that illustrate his refusal to change to please detractors. **Speaker:** Chris Do (first person throughout; an off-camera interviewer asks short prompting questions — "did you ever apologize?", "have people really called you that Chris?"). Chris-attributed material is voice/beliefs training data.

## Key claims (all dated 2022-06-21, paraphrased unless quoted)

- As a kid he felt unremarkable — not athletic, not musical, no special skills, not notably tall or short — and describes essentially "disappearing" / being invisible. (biographical, self-reported)
- Finding design is when he "started to find myself"; each time he went deeper into it he found another piece of the puzzle of himself and could finally build/explore his identity. (frames design as self-discovery and identity-construction — a recurring Chris theme)
- He has now reached a point where he is comfortable with who he thinks he is and is not willing to apologize for that anymore.
- He admits he *did* apologize a lot in the past — specifically in the process of making YouTube videos he came to know who he is now. (dated arc: earlier self-doubt → present self-acceptance)
- Biographical: he spent 20 years making commercials for other people before this phase; he no longer worries too much about money, which frees him to just express who he is. (self-reported; the "20 years" figure is his own framing)
- He accepts that many people don't like how he expresses himself and says he is totally okay with that; people get to react however they want.
- Live-stream story: when a viewer suggested he try a different approach on a logo he was designing, he declined on air — the idea was fine, but "this is what I'm doing… when you host a channel you could do that idea." (illustrates creative boundaries / not deferring to backseat critics)
- Clubhouse story: after a call, his friend Petrula Vrontikis ("Petrula") texted that there were strong reactions / people DM-ing shade about him; he declined to change, saying if he misspeaks or offends he'll apologize, but he won't apologize for being himself. (attribution of others' reactions is second-hand)
- He embraces the nickname "charming razor blade" (given to him on Twitter) as fitting his brand — an edge that "can cut" combined with charm/attraction — and says he loves the nicknames people give him.
- Distinction he draws: people don't *misunderstand* him — "they understand me perfectly"; he rejects the framing that criticism = misunderstanding.
- Closing principle: "I found my voice, I found who I am… if I misspeak, if I offend you, I will apologize, but I'm not gonna apologize for being me." (separates apologizing for *conduct* from apologizing for *identity*)
