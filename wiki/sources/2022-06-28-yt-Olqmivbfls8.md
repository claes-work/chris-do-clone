# Urgency Is Overrated

## Summary

An interview clip (Futur podcast segment) in which the guest — the co-owner of Basecamp — lays out his company's operating philosophy: the manager's job is to protect people's time and attention, so Basecamp minimizes meetings, removes presence/availability indicators, defaults to long-form asynchronous communication, and aims to give everyone eight uninterrupted hours a day. His thesis is that the *quality* of an hour (60 unbroken minutes beats four fragmented ones) is what enables real output, and that "urgency is overrated, ASAP is poison" unless something is truly necessary.

**Speaker:** The primary voice is the **guest**, the Basecamp co-founder/owner — from context almost certainly **Jason Fried** (co-owner of Basecamp, Chicago HQ, author of *Rework* / *It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work*; the "urgency is overrated / ASAP is poison" line and no-presence/async doctrine are his). ## Key claims

_All dated 2022-06-28. Guest (Basecamp co-founder, likely Jason Fried) unless marked as Chris. Guest material is do-not-train — attribute to the guest, not Chris Do._

- **Guest:** A manager's core job is to help people protect their time and attention, because attention is the thing everyone else wants to take from you. (paraphrase)
- **Guest:** The quality of an hour matters enormously; 60 straight uninterrupted minutes is worth far more than four 15-minute or two 30-minute blocks. Deep work — sketching, drawing, developing product ideas, writing — can't be done in fragments. (paraphrase)
- **Guest:** Basecamp rarely holds meetings and deliberately has **no "presence" indicators** (no red/green/yellow availability dot); the assumption is everyone is busy on their own work. (paraphrase)
- **Guest:** Default to asynchronous, long-form written communication — post a question to Basecamp and let people answer when they can, rather than grabbing someone for an immediate answer, unless it is truly urgent. (paraphrase)
- **Guest:** "Urgency is overrated, ASAP is poison" unless absolutely necessary; downplaying manufactured urgency is a **cultural** commitment everyone must share. (paraphrase)
- **Guest:** The aim is that every person gets a full ~8-hour uninterrupted day to themselves; ~40 hours/week is plenty — no all-nighters, no 70–80 hour weeks, no weekend work (except scheduled customer-support shifts). (paraphrase)
- **Guest:** You can "achieve a lot in a little" when hours are high-quality and stacked back-to-back; having three straight hours to yourself is "kind of a superpower" most people never get. (paraphrase)
- **Guest:** At a ~600-person conference talk, when he asked who had had four uninterrupted hours to themselves at work in the past month, only ~5% raised their hands — which he finds sad. (paraphrase, self-reported anecdote)
- **Guest:** People who work 10–14 hour days usually don't have that much work — their time is so fragmented across many things they can never get into deep work, so the deep work spills into nights/mornings/weekends. Un-fragment the day and the same work compresses into far less time. (paraphrase)
- **Chris (interviewer):** Peter Drucker in *The Effective Executive* framed consolidating the largest possible block of discretionary time as a priority for an executive. (paraphrase — Chris citing Drucker)
- **Chris (interviewer):** Early in the pandemic he was newly exhausted at the end of each day; after ~6 weeks he realized it was because he was working uninterrupted for 8–10 hours a day — something he'd never had since starting his company. (paraphrase — Chris, first person; potential Chris voice/belief data)
- **Chris (interviewer):** Endorses the guest's point — four uninterrupted quality hours lets you do a lot; "your effectiveness must be through the roof." (paraphrase)

## Notable verbatim quotes

_Attributed per speaker. Guest quotes = do-not-train (Basecamp co-founder, likely Jason Fried). Only Chris's line is potential Chris voice data._

> "I feel like my job is to help people protect their time and attention, because it's the thing everyone else wants from each other." — Guest (Basecamp co-founder)

> "There's a bunch of different ways to slice 60 minutes, but the best slice for me is 60 minutes. It's not four 15-minute blocks, it's not two 30-minute blocks — it's 60 minutes straight uninterrupted." — Guest

> "Urgency is overrated, ASAP is poison — unless it's absolutely truly necessary." — Guest

> "When your day is sliced into a billion pieces it's really hard... the quality of our hours are high, and the more of those hours you can stack back to back, it's kind of a superpower." — Guest

> "For those who don't think eight hours is enough, just jump on a plane in Chicago and fly to London — that's eight hours, and just sit there and you're gonna see how long eight hours is. It's an extraordinary amount of time." — Guest

> "The reason [people work 10 or 12 or 14 hour days] is not because there's 14 hours of work to do in a day — it's because their time is spread across so many different things and it's so fragmented that they can't really get into anything." — Guest

> "It dawned on me, like a month and a half into [the pandemic]... why am I so tired? Because I was working uninterrupted for eight to ten hours a day, which I've never had since starting my company." — **Chris Do** (interviewer; potential Chris voice data)
