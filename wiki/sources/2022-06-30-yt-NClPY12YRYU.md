# How To Be Inspired By Nature

## Summary

Chris Do explains his lifelong practice of looking OUTSIDE his own industry for design inspiration — to architecture, gardening, plants, flowers, animals, rocks, ceramics, and furniture — because staying inside your own field just recycles what everyone else is already doing. He argues nature "always works" aesthetically (you'll never find a flower that doesn't work), and defines creativity as the ability to connect two or more disparate things — the farther apart, the more creative it reads. **Speaker:** Chris Do (first person throughout; an unnamed co-host/interviewer asks one question mid-clip, and Greg Gunn delivers the podcast promo outro). This is Chris-taught material and trains the persona.

## Key claims (2022-06-30, paraphrased unless quoted)

- Don't look at your own industry for inspiration; looking at your own field is like looking at your own product — you end up doing what everyone else does. (paraphrase)
- For design inspiration Chris looks to architecture, gardening, plants, flowers, and animals instead; for color he looks outside at nature "because nature's always got it figured out — nothing in nature looks bad." (paraphrase + quote)
- To understand how something should *feel*, he walks into a beautiful building and interrogates why it feels good — textures, how materials come together, scale, proportion, setting — then transfers that thinking to software, which is "unrelated" yet deeply related because both are about space, function, and feeling. (paraphrase)
- Early in his career he leaned on his own industry (Communication Arts, print, business cards, letterhead) and it made him a good *copier* — useful for learning, but he felt less creative; he only started generating his own ideas once he looked away from his field and figured out how to bring outside influences back into the work. (paraphrase)
- Creativity is the ability to find a connection between two or more disparate things; the farther apart those things are, the more people register it as truly creative — a non-sequitur brought into your thinking is what reads as "crazy cool, unique." (paraphrase; he notes he taught this to his design students in art school)
- Staying inside your own industry produces "regurgitating an idea and putting a slightly new coat of paint on it" — the same idea seen over and over. (quote)
- Chris keeps physical inspiration objects around his workspace — rocks (some just found on the beach, one a ~$10 "desert rose"), ceramics from a few artists he likes, and furniture — and studies their textures, shadows, shapes, proportions, and color while working on a design. He frames aesthetic judgment as purely subjective ("there's no objective thing probably here"). (paraphrase + quote)
- Flowers reward close looking: from afar a flower is beautiful, up close it's *more* beautiful — intricate, almost fractal, colors blending into colors — and it always works aesthetically, which he calls "an incredible notion in itself." (paraphrase + quote)
- Takeaway he endorses: there is far more to draw inspiration from if you're willing to look and pay attention — it's everywhere, you just walk outside and look around; he encourages people to look far outside their own trade. (paraphrase)
