# Is Remote Work The Future? (Jason Fried)

## Summary

A long-form Futur interview in which Chris Do hosts Jason Fried (co-founder/CEO of Basecamp, formerly 37signals; co-author of *Rework* and *Remote*) about remote/distributed work, the service-to-product transition, protecting people's time, and delegation. The bulk of the substantive claims about remote work, asynchronous communication, and company scaling come from **Jason Fried, the guest**, and are recorded here as **context / do-not-train**. Chris Do is the interviewer; his own lines (his firm's service-to-product transition, his delegation experiment after reading Fried's book, his reflections on the "quality of an hour") are the only subject-attributable material and are marked as such below.

**Speaker:** Two speakers. **Chris Do** = interviewer (the SUBJECT; his interjections and personal anecdotes may train). **Jason Fried** = guest, and the dominant voice on remote work (**context / do-not-train** — must NOT flow into `persona/` voice/beliefs). A closing promo is read by producer **Greg Gunn** (also do-not-train).

## Key claims

_All dated 2022-07-05, paraphrased. Attribution is explicit per speaker. Jason Fried's material is do-not-train context; only Chris Do's lines are subject-attributable._

**Jason Fried (guest — context / do-not-train):**

- Remote work is fundamentally about organizational **resilience** — the ability to work from anywhere, anytime, so the company isn't crippled if the office disappears; COVID caught many companies "flat-footed" because they only knew how to work in person. (paraphrase)
- Remote work is "a fundamentally different approach to work," not just co-located work done far apart — it leans on **asynchronous** communication and **trust** rather than real-time presence, which is essential across time zones. (paraphrase)
- The last two years were **"pandemic work," not remote work** — normally remote workers can use coffee shops, libraries, co-working spaces and see local co-workers; lockdown removed all of that, making it far harder and more isolating (especially for single people). (paraphrase)
- Remote work lets you **hire great people anywhere**, not just within ~30 miles; Fried rejects the tech "talent war" over a supposed top-50/top-100 — there are millions of exceptionally good people elsewhere. (paraphrase)
- Remote work must be **all-or-nothing** on eligibility: you can't let executives be remote while denying it to others (retail-vs-corporate customer-facing splits are a separate, legitimate case). (paraphrase)
- Remote work is **"more respectful at a human level"** — people aren't forced to live near an office, so work has less influence over major life decisions (a partner's job, where to raise kids). (paraphrase)
- **Service-to-product transition:** Basecamp (then 37signals) built Basecamp internally to run client work better; ~a year after launch it out-earned client work, so they went "all in" — but only because client revenue was a safety net. He advises **against going "cold turkey"** with no safety net; transition gradually. (paraphrase)
- **Mass/physics of change:** the more mass (people, history, legacy) a company has, the more energy needed to change direction; a 5-person switch was easy, a 50-person one would be "much much harder." (paraphrase)
- Distinguish **"taking a risk" from "putting yourself at risk"** — make calculated, gradual, reversible bets; avoid "bet the company" decisions. (paraphrase)
- On attention/morale when leadership shifts to a new product: everyone at Basecamp works across all products (six-week cycles), so no one is "left behind"; share early work internally once it's solid so people feel part of it (you can share without asking for feedback). (paraphrase)
- **Protecting time and attention** is Fried's core job as CEO: the "quality of an hour" matters — one uninterrupted 60-minute block beats four 15-minute ones; Basecamp targets an ~8-hour uninterrupted day per person, ~40-hour weeks, few meetings, no "presence" dots, "ASAP is poison," urgency is overrated. (paraphrase)
- **Delegation/ego:** owners often stay involved to feel important, but you're most valuable when the business can run — and even walk away — without you. (paraphrase)
- Prefers **"context over consistency"** — willing to break his own past rules ("fight your own religion") because today's context differs; comfort comes from "I'll figure it out," not upfront certainty. (paraphrase)
- Books are **retrospective summaries of what they've actually done**, not predictions — which is why *Rework*/*Remote* line up with how the company operates. (paraphrase)
- Signals a strategic reversal: after years of shrinking to stay small ("do less but better"), Basecamp now intends to **grow, add managers/leadership, and become multi-product again** ("tick-tock development"). (paraphrase)

**Chris Do (subject — may train):**

- Chris ran the same service-to-product transition at his own company and found culture/mindset had to change; roughly **half his people struggled** with the shift because they were externally motivated (needed a brief and client goals) rather than truly autonomous. (paraphrase)
- After reading Fried's work, Chris emailed his team to **push decisions down** — "if you feel like you can answer it, you should just do it" — which made people more accountable and recovered his own time; previously interruptions every ~15 minutes meant he couldn't work until 8 p.m. (paraphrase)
- Chris admits owner **ego** ("only I can answer that question") kept him over-involved; he's consciously stepped back and problems get solved as well or better. (paraphrase)
- Working from home most of the time during the pandemic was **taking a toll** on him socially despite walks and exercise. (paraphrase)
- Cites the framing "all strategy is autobiographical" (attributes it to hearing it from **Blair Enns**) and Peter Drucker's *The Effective Executive* on consolidating discretionary time. (paraphrase)

> Note: no hard contradiction between the two, but do NOT let Fried's remote-work theses stand in as Chris Do's positions. Chris agrees and amplifies, but the doctrine (resilience, async, all-or-nothing eligibility, mass-of-change) is Fried's and is do-not-train.

## Notable verbatim quotes

**Jason Fried (guest — context / do-not-train):**

> "Remote working ultimately is about building resilience in an organization — the ability to work from anywhere, anytime." — Jason Fried

> "The last two years have not been remote work, they've been pandemic work, which is a different kind of thing." — Jason Fried

> "There's a top 100 people, whatever. There's another 10,000 that are exceptionally good... go out and find them." — Jason Fried

> "There's a big difference between taking a risk and putting yourself at risk." — Jason Fried

> "Urgency is overrated. ASAP is poison — unless it's absolutely truly necessary." — Jason Fried

> "I'm much more interested in context than consistency." — Jason Fried

> "The place you want to get to is when you could potentially walk away and everything would be okay." — Jason Fried

**Chris Do (subject — may train):**

> "Where the boss's attention is going — if we're not on that train or that boat, it's probably a bad sign for us." — Chris Do (describing his team's read on his shift to a new venture)

> "If someone else can answer it, please ask them, and if you feel like you can answer it, you should just do it, and don't worry if it doesn't work out." — Chris Do (paraphrasing the email he sent his team to delegate decisions)
