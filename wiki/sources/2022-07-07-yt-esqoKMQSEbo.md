# Don't Do This When You Give A Presentation

## Summary

A short two-person conversation about the biggest mistake speakers make: over-delivering
— cramming so much value into a talk that the audience's "circuits get fried." Both
speakers confess to the same instinct and share how they now fight it: narrow to two or
three takeaways, over-prepare then edit ruthlessly, and give people your "middle-shelf"
material rather than straining for the newest, most advanced insight.

## Key claims

_Dated 2022-07-07. Paraphrased unless quoted. Chris-attributed unless marked (guest)._

- The human mind can only process so much in a talk (5, 10, or 40 minutes); the danger
  is the instinct to **over-deliver**. (both)
- (guest) He used to believe that if someone paid ~$500 to attend a multi-day
  conference, it was his sole responsibility to deliver "at least $500 of value" — which
  itself becomes a stress trap that overloads the presentation.
- **Chris's ICON cautionary tale:** he once covered "way too much ground" at the ICON
  illustration conference, even ending with a guitar song and bringing a friend up to do
  a comedy bit; it became "discombobulated" and felt like "a mess of stuff." He learned
  his lesson from it.
- **The fix — narrow to 2–3 takeaways:** since then Chris focuses a talk down to maybe
  two or three things he wants the audience to walk away with, and reiterates them so
  they're "really really clear."
- As an audience member, Chris zones out when a speaker gives too much, but remembers
  fondly the speaker who stays focused, concise, and gets off stage on time — so he tries
  "to be that guy."
- **The paradox:** we add more because we want to give value, but more input confuses the
  audience and fries their processing — an instinct you have to actively fight.
- **Over-prepare, then edit out:** (guest) prep a lot, then ~two days before, start
  deleting tangents and the non-essential. Chris agrees — let it "marinate," come back,
  and cut the redundant parts down to the essence (he cites John Cleese on "trimming the
  fat" when writing sketches). Editing is the part of the process most often overlooked.
- **Over-preparing quells insecurity:** (guest) preparing more than needed calms the
  "this is not enough" anxiety; the editing pass afterward reduces it to what actually
  matters.
- **"Middle-shelf" doctrine:** (guest, quoting Joel Pilger) we exhaust ourselves reaching
  for our "top-shelf" material; instead give the audience your middle- or even
  bottom-shelf stuff — people still love it and "gobble it up." Chasing the newest,
  greatest, latest thing is where the stress comes from.
- **Teach what you already know:** what you take for granted as obvious is news to most
  people; you don't need a brand-new discovery to be valuable.
- **Curse of knowledge:** Chris names this as a cognitive bias — once you've learned
  something it becomes habit and you forget there was a time you didn't know it, so you
  hesitate to teach it ("doesn't everybody know this?") when in fact most people don't.

**Relation to existing "Pitch This!" presenting doctrine:** the *narrow-to-a-few-points*
idea overlaps with prior pitch/presentation coverage, but the angle here is largely
**new** — this is about *keynote/teaching* delivery, not client pitching, and the
distinctive material (over-delivery as the core failure mode, over-prepare-then-edit,
"middle-shelf," and the curse-of-knowledge justification for teaching the basics) is not
captured by the pitch doctrine. Worth promoting.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do:
> "I did a presentation a few years ago at ICON, the illustration conference. I covered
> way too much ground and I even ended it with a guitar song... it was just all too much

Chris Do:
> "Since then I've really focused on narrowing it down to maybe two or three things...
