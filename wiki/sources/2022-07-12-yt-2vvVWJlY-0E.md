# When Should You Change Careers?

## Summary

Chris Do answers an interviewer's questions about why he changed career paths, describing his multi-stage pivot: from making commercials and music videos, to branding/brand design, and ultimately to education. **Speaker:** Chris Do (first person, interview format; an off-camera interviewer poses the questions "what drove you to change your career path" and "what was the trigger"). He frames himself as a "canary in the coal mine" who senses cultural shifts early, and describes teaching as the "life-affirming" work that eventually became his calling once he found a viable business model. Biographically dense but consistent with his known Blind → Futur arc.

## Key claims (dated 2022-07-12, paraphrased)

- **Career stage 1 — commercials/music videos:** Chris's company made commercials and music videos and was paid "relatively speaking a lot of money" for that work. (Consistent with Blind's early advertising work.)
- **Reading the cultural shift early:** He describes an ability to "fly above" his own work and see the larger cultural landscape — noticing audiences moving off cable/TV to streaming and subscription services, where young people "don't watch commercials at all." He concluded demand for commercials was in structural decline.
- **"Canary in the coal mine":** He claims he senses change faster than most people, attributing it to being "less attached" to what he's doing.
- **Warned his reps, met resistance:** He told his reps "we need to build a new ship"; they thought he was being alarmist. He insisted "the writing's on the wall — you're just not willing to read it."
- **Career stage 2 — branding/brand design:** He shifted away from commercial advertising into branding and brand design. He found it rewarding and doesn't fear it going away, but concluded he had "exchanged one master for another" — still a "slave to client work."
- **Career stage 3 — education:** Dissatisfaction with being tied to client work led him to explore education, which "filled my heart and soul" so much that he decided it "has got to be my calling in life" — even though he "didn't have a business model yet."
- **Biographical: ~15 years teaching:** At the time of the trigger he had been teaching for "almost 15 years" at Art Center and Otis. (Confirms his longtime adjunct teaching at Art Center College of Design and Otis College of Art and Design.)
- **Teaching as labor of love:** Teachers "don't get paid anything," so it was a labor of love; the reward was watching students "on the verge of having a breakthrough," which motivated him to go home and "read three more books" to teach better.
- **The trigger = economics of teaching:** The pay was "upside down" relative to the time invested. His question became: how to get the fulfillment of teaching and helping people transform, "but do it in a way that I don't have to go and work another job which is servicing clients." That search for a business model led to where The Futur is "today."
- **Core mental model on when to change careers:** Change proactively when you can sense the tide shifting (falling demand for your current work), not reactively after it collapses; and pivot toward what is genuinely fulfilling once you can build a sustainable business model around it.
