# Public Speaking Tip For Introverts

## Summary

A short two-person clip in which **Chris Do interviews an unnamed guest storytelling
expert** about how introverts can become better public speakers. Chris frames the
question — he identifies his creative community, and himself, as introverts who aspire
to speak better — and the guest delivers the actual tip: **open your presentation with a
prepared story.** The guest's core teaching is CONTEXT (see attribution note); only
Chris's own framing lines are persona-eligible, and they merely restate his
already-documented introvert self-identification.

**Speaker:** Two speakers. (1) **Chris Do** — interviewer; his lines (self-identifying
as an introvert who wants to be a better speaker) are persona-relevant voice/mindset

personal friend of Susan Cain (author of *Quiet*, garbled in captions as "susan kane"),

## Key claims
_All dated 2022-07-14; paraphrased. Attribution labeled per speaker._

- **[Chris] Self-identifies as an introvert:** Chris states that his creative community
  is "predominantly introverts," that he is "an introvert myself," and that he "aspires
  to be a better public speaker" and to "overcome [his] natural tendencies." (2022-07-14)
  — **Not new**: reaffirms the heavily-documented introvert material in
  [[wiki/topics/mindset/mindset]] §14–15 (2017 introvert-coaching episode). Note a mild
  framing nuance vs. his usual "loud introvert" self-label — here he casts himself as
  someone still *aspiring* to overcome introverted tendencies, positioning the guest
  (extrovert) as the naturally comfortable speaker.
- **[Chris] "We don't want the extroverts to have all the fun":** Chris frames better
  public speaking as something introverts can claim, not concede to extroverts.
  (2022-07-14)
- **[Guest — CONTEXT] Start with a story:** The single tip — open a presentation or
  high-stakes communication event with a prepared story. This matters *more* for
  introverts/low-"big-stage-energy" speakers than for natural extroverts, who can win a
  crowd on showmanship alone. (2022-07-14)
- **[Guest — CONTEXT] Why a story works:** Signaling "a story is coming" makes an
  audience relax and lean in ("the DNA within them"), which drops hostility, opens them
  up, and — via reciprocity — makes the presenter feel more comfortable too; the whole
  presentation improves from there. (2022-07-14)
- **[Guest — CONTEXT] The story needs structure:** It doesn't have to be highly emotional
  or high-conflict, but it needs "the four components" and should follow a
  "normal → explosion → new normal" arc; a little self-deprecation and back-and-forth
  dialogue help. (2022-07-14; "four components" not enumerated in this clip)
- **[Guest — CONTEXT] Rehearse out loud in advance:** Test the story on trusted
  colleagues/friends first; she personally rehearses aloud — in the shower, in front of
  the mirror, to her husband, walking around the city — before putting a story on stage.
  (2022-07-14)
- **[Guest — CONTEXT] Real-world pattern:** She's repeatedly seen CFOs/executives coached
  to "have a story" when following a storyteller on stage — against their nature, but the
  audience loves it and you can feel the energy shift. (2022-07-14)
