# Stories Need This One Thing

## Summary

A short clip from a Futur interview in which the guest teaches that the "one thing"
every story needs is **emotion** — but that the emotion can be small and everyday
(frustration, annoyance, curiosity), not a dramatic trauma or "Hallmark moment."
**Speaker:** the substantive teaching is entirely by the **guest, Kindra Hall**
(author of *Stories That Stick*; she references "in stories that stick I outline the
four key components"). Chris Do appears only as the interviewer, asking a question on
behalf of a friend who struggles to tell stories with feeling.
⚠️ **Context / do-not-train** — Chris contributes no storytelling doctrine here; only
the guest does. Nothing in this clip should flow into `persona/` voice or beliefs.

## Key claims

_All dated 2022-07-19. Attributed per speaker. Guest claims are context only._

- **[Guest — Kindra Hall]** Emotion is one of four key components that make a story
  compelling and "influential," drawing people into what she calls the co-creative
  process. (Paraphrase; she cites her book *Stories That Stick* for the four
  components — the four-part structure is not enumerated in this clip.)
- **[Guest]** A common early-stage mistake is "reporting" — narrating what happened
  step by step ("then this happened, then this happened") without emotion. It isn't
  wrong, but it leaves a lot on the table.
- **[Guest]** Authenticity/emotion does **not** require sharing your deepest, darkest,
  most private material ("you don't have to be a contestant on the Bachelor").
- **[Guest]** Emotion can be one of the most basic feelings — frustration, desire, the
  agitation of an unsolved problem, wonder, curiosity, annoyance at an inefficient
  system. You do not need a "broken childhood" story.
- **[Guest]** You get to **choose which stories you tell**; if hardships aren't
  worked through or you don't want to be known for them, don't tell them — that's
  full permission, not a failure of storytelling.
- **[Guest]** What keeps people from telling stories is the false belief "I don't
  have a story to tell" because they haven't climbed Everest, overcome a terrible
  illness, or experienced great loss — none of which are requirements for using story
  effectively.
- **[Chris Do — interviewer]** Framing/observation only: notes that his friend is
  European and, in his experience, people in some (not all) European cultures are
  socialized to be less open about revealing emotions, so her stories come across as
  a reporter speaking objectively about someone else. (Anecdotal cultural observation,
  not a storytelling framework — and not persona-load-bearing.)

**NB (story-formula coverage):** The generic "emotion is essential to storytelling"
point is already covered elsewhere in the wiki. The specific angle here — that the
emotion can be *small/everyday* and that dramatic trauma is explicitly **not**
required — is a useful nuance, but it is **the guest's** framework (Kindra Hall /
*Stories That Stick*), so it is not new *Chris* material.
