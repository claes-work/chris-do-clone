# How To Tell A Great Story (w/ Kindra Hall)

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do (host, SUBJECT) interviews **Kindra Hall** (guest, storytelling
author/keynote speaker — *Stories That Stick*, *Choose Your Story, Change Your Life*).
Kindra lays out her framework for what makes a story work and how to use stories
strategically in business, sales, and speaking; her material is **context / do-not-train**
(the persona clones Chris only). The genuinely-Chris content is his own storytelling
pedagogy (he used to teach sequential-design storyboarding) — a "normal → explosion →
new normal" arc, an everyday-conflict/"life-and-death feel" reframe, a dialogue +
sensory-detail + struggle checklist — plus a personal thread on his own speaker
self-consciousness (the Simon Sinek "memorized talk" problem, and changing a talk that
got worse) and his self-identification as an introvert speaking to an introverted
creative community.

## Key claims

_Dated 2022-07-21. Paraphrased unless quoted. Attributed per speaker; **only
Chris-attributed lines train the persona.** Kindra's are context/do-not-train._

**Kindra Hall (guest — context / do-not-train):**
- She writes best with a full, uninterrupted "runway" — wrote her second book in ~6 weeks
  with no calls, podcasts, or obligations; without total focus she has zero focus.
- Told her first story at age 11 (a 5th-grade assignment reading to 3rd-graders); learned
  that when there's chaos and you want to be heard, tell a story. Master's thesis was the
  role of storytelling in organizations.
- **The core business problem:** under time pressure people cut the stories and keep the
  bullet points / facts — but knowing is only half; people need to *feel* it. Information
  must be "bioavailable," which means wrapping it in a story. (Her 18-minutes-for-15,000-
  people keynote anecdote: cutting all stories left only bullet slides; the fix was to
  lead with the story.)
- Storytelling has been "having a moment" for years, but more talk hasn't produced better
  stories — like "authenticity" and "vulnerability," overuse waters the word down.
- **Four components of a great story** (checklist, any order): (1) an **identifiable
  character** (not "hero" — that word excludes people who don't see themselves as heroes;
  and people buy from the characters of a brand, not the brand); (2) **authentic emotion**
  (not tragic/big — even frustration, annoyance, curiosity counts; you need not reveal
  your "deepest darkest"); (3) a **specific moment** in time (puts the listener "in the
  room" — the co-creative process); (4) **specific details** (the smell of coffee, the
  Werther's candy on the bank desk — details signal you truly know the audience).
- Using stories *strategically* takes extra prep; the fix for "losing the plot" mid-story
  is preparation — build a set of ~3 stories per product/pitch/audience and repeat them
  until you can tell them without thinking.
- Storytelling is a **co-creative process**, not memorization — "a living, breathing
  contract between storyteller and listener"; the same story is never told identically.
  Repeating a well-honed story is fine (audiences want "the hits," like a concert); a
  pro makes it feel like the first time.
- Emotion doesn't require trauma; self-deprecation can help but "strategic authenticity"
  (fake-humble, or forced sadness that doesn't serve the point) reads as manufactured.
- **Advice for introverts / nervous speakers:** *start with a story* — the audience's
  "DNA" lights up, hostility drops, they open up, and that reciprocity makes the speaker
  more comfortable; requires rehearsing it out loud in advance.
- She herself was once "miserable" and fear-racked backstage (imposter syndrome at
  SearchLove); had to consciously choose better stories to tell *herself* — the thesis of
  her second book (the internal story). The nerves never fully vanish.

**Chris Do (SUBJECT — trains the persona):**
- Chris has read *Stories That Stick* "so many times," quotes it, and recommends it —
  calls it "the book" on storytelling and gifts it to people. (His documented habit of
  championing a small set of canonical books.)
- **Chris's own story pedagogy** (from teaching sequential-design storyboarding): stories
  are about **conflict**, and even everyday moments must *feel* like life and death — e.g.
  your crush calling and a sibling answering the phone, or a car door falling off at a
  fancy restaurant. "Feel the feels and transmit that" so the audience relates to you as a
  human.
- **Chris's story framework: "normal → explosion → new normal."** The "explosion" is the
  moment of change / change of state, and any change of state contains the conflict — so
  for Chris conflict is baked into the arc (he reframes it as "a soft C conflict, not a
  hard C").
- **Chris's story-craft checklist** (how he guides people): a story needs to be **rich in
  dialogue** (inner or outer), have **sensory detail**, and show **struggle** — "we want
  to know that you're trying." "I will jump into your story when I start to feel like I
  relate to you."
- **The "propaganda piece" tell:** people edit their stories to present themselves in a
  good light and skip the struggle; when Chris senses that, "this is a propaganda piece
  and I'm not really buying into it."
- Chris studies **performers, comics, actors, high-level public speakers**; he loves when
  speakers "play the characters" in their story (inner/outer dialogue) — it makes him
  laugh out loud. (Kindra classes dialogue as *technique*, not a core component; Chris
  weights it more heavily. Mild framing difference, not a contradiction.)
- **The "memorized talk" tension (Chris's own conflict):** watching Simon Sinek deliver
  the same talk beat-for-beat — same pauses, jokes, phrasing — made the "magic" feel like
  a revealed trick; "highly scripted, rehearsed, memorized, and then performed." Because
  one audience member told Chris they'd attend his next talk, Chris changed his talk
  (removed the joke) — "and it wasn't as good... I knew it." Kindra reframes this: he
  should speak for the first-timers, trust the hits, and that fear is his *internal*
  story (negativity bias latching onto a single neutral comment).
- **Chris self-identifies as an introvert**, as does most of his creative community; he
  aspires to be a better public speaker and wants practical craft advice "so the
  extroverts don't have all the fun."

> ⚠️ No hard contradiction. One framing difference: Kindra treats **dialogue** as optional
> *technique* while Chris teaches it as a near-requirement — flagged above, not a conflict.

> "Kendra"); "SearchLove" (conference) appears as "Search Love"; "confliction within the
> character" is the caption's phrasing for internal conflict.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do:
> "I used to teach sequential design storyboarding... stories are about conflict. And even
> though the moments don't necessarily literally are life and death, they need to feel
> like life and death."

Chris Do:
> "The other aspect of how I teach stories is normal, explosion, new normal. That's the
> framework... innately in there is the explosion which is the moment of change... anytime
> there is a change of state, there's conflict in that change."

Chris Do:
> "It needs to be rich in dialogue, there needs to be sensory detail, and there has to be
> this part where there's some struggle — we want to know that you're trying... I will jump
> into your story when I start to feel like I relate to you."

Chris Do:
> "People often edit their stories because they want to present themselves in a good
> light... I feel like I see what you're trying to do — this is a propaganda piece and I'm
> not really buying into it."

Chris Do:
> "I watch [Simon Sinek] do the same talk somewhere else... the same awkward pauses, the
> phrasing, the jokes, they're literally the same beat for beat... what felt like a
> genuine 'I'm just going to pull it from the ether' is actually highly scripted,
> rehearsed, memorized, and then performed."

Chris Do:
> "I did change it and it wasn't as good. It just wasn't. I knew it." _(on removing a joke
> because one person said they'd seen his talk before)_

Kindra Hall (guest — context / do-not-train):
> "Knowing is only part of it. We need to feel it... If you want your information to be
> bioavailable, you need to wrap it in a story."

Kindra Hall (guest — context / do-not-train):
> "The four components are... an identifiable character... authentic emotion... a moment...
> and specific details... A story needs to have emotion, yes, but it can be just annoyance
> that a system is inefficient."
