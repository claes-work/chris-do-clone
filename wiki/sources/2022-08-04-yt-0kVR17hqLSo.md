# Quality vs Quantity on YouTube (What Brendan Learned)

## Summary

## Key claims

_All dated 2022-08-04. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Speaker labeled._

**Brendan Kane (guest expert — CONTEXT, not persona):**

- (Kane) The most common worst practice he sees is **batch-producing content** —
  planning a week/month/quarter of posts on the belief that **frequency is the key to
  scale**. That belief did work 7–9 years ago when platforms had less competition, but
  people now keep pumping out content assuming volume alone will make the algorithm grow
  them.
- (Kane) The Hookpoint approach with clients is **quality over quantity**: do the
  research, identify the **performance drivers**, and get the **format dialed in first**
  — only then move to frequency and scale. Constant batch-producing never teaches you
  what it takes to be consistent long-term.
- (Kane) If you only succeeded because you got on Instagram Reels or TikTok early, you
  have **no solid foundation** for when the algorithm shifts or competition rises.
- (Kane) Frequency *can* play a role — e.g. his friend **Alex Stemp** hit 20M TikTok
  followers and preaches frequency — but it works for Stemp only because his content is
  already dialed in after years of work. For everyone else, "just post every day" is
  **playing the lottery**: if something goes viral, they don't understand why, so they
  can't reproduce it.
- (Kane) Micro-optimizations like **time-of-day and hashtags** could yield results when
  platforms were less crowded, but they will never make a crappy piece of content
  amazing. Focus on the content itself; be a **student of the game** and learn to
  contextualize your message for what audiences and algorithms want.
- (Kane) Their client process fills the gap between **research → creative → execution**:
  they supply the research (a reference to emulate plus its performance drivers), the
  client produces a piece based on it, and then they **play the reference and the
  client's version side-by-side on screen** to expose what's missing. Emulation is of
  **nuanced storytelling mechanisms, not content** — they never tell people to copy
  content, and never change who the person is, their message, values, or brand, only the
  **way it's expressed**.
- (Kane) With the side-by-side method, ~**70% of clients self-diagnose** the gap and
  ~30% need it pointed out; the more you do it, the more you catch on. It takes time and
  won't happen overnight.
- (Kane) This is why he's turning the company into a **research-and-insights company** —
  delivering new breakdowns weekly (e.g. a clinical psychologist going viral on TikTok
  one week, a lawyer on Reels the next, an insurance company using the communication
  algorithm) so creators can study nuances from many perspectives. You have to **immerse
  yourself deeply** in studying it.

**Chris Do (host — persona-relevant framing):**

- (Chris) Reframes Kane's point: instead of little tweaks and hacks (hashtags,
  time-of-day) that give short-term gains, there are **foundational, evergreen,
  universal skills** you can build and take with you — once you understand how to create
  content people care about that touches the **facts, the fun, and the feelings**, you
  can write a book, make a movie, a commercial, a 3-second clip or a 30-minute piece,
  and people will care. This moves creators away from **chasing trends** toward
  **building real, translatable skill**. (persona framing — his own synthesis, layering
  Kane's "facts/fun/feelings" onto an evergreen-skills thesis)
- (Chris) Frames the research/creative/execution gap as a question: the researcher can
  supply beautiful insight, but if someone can't do the **creative translation**, they
  can't turn it into something good — "otherwise we'd all be geniuses making viral
  videos." (persona framing)
