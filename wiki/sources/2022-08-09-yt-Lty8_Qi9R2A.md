## Key claims

_All dated 2022-08-09. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Speaker labeled._

**Chris Do (host — questions only, no new teaching):**

- (Chris) Asks whether Kane's understanding of how algorithms work and how humans
  perceive content translates equally across every social platform he touches.
- (Chris) Asks whether it is possible for **any person in any industry / vertical** to
  produce consistently high-performing viral content.

**Brendan Kane (guest expert — CONTEXT, not persona):**

- (Kane) His approach translates across every platform and virtually every industry
  because the first step of their creative process is always **research** — cited as
  backed by "60 billion views, 100 million followers, and a billion in revenue" for the
  projects worked on.
- (Kane) Research is **not** just looking at your competition. Competitors usually show
  you what *not* to do because they aren't succeeding at the highest level. They extend
  research to other creators/brands excelling at social media even in **unrelated**
  niches — because they study the **context of how the message is delivered**, not the
  content itself. There are 50+ nuances they track: pacing, tonality, personality type,
  number of edits, the first three seconds, thumbnail, captions, meme cards.
- (Kane) Their research framework is **gold / silver / bronze**: gold = highest
  performers, silver = middle, bronze = lowest. Looking only at gold never reveals the
  true performance drivers. They hypothesize a driver from the gold examples, then check
  silver/bronze — if the same trait appears there too, it's **not** the driver, so they
  discard it and go back to the gold to find a real one.
- (Kane) Because research is done **every single day**, the method is platform-agnostic
  and future-proof (even "when things get to the metaverse") — research always informs
  the creative decision about how to **contextualize** for success.
- (Kane) Yes, anyone in any vertical can go consistently viral by **contextualizing** a
  narrow message for the widest possible audience so the algorithm pushes it. Examples:
  - **ClearValue Tax** (YouTube, taxes) — 1M+ subscribers, ~300–400k views/video.
  - **Graham Stephan** (finance for millennials) — "I want to teach you finance" gets no
    views, but "How I bought a Tesla for $78" makes anyone care while still teaching
    finance principles.
  - **Ryan Serhant** (luxury real estate, $10–50M properties) — tours a "$7 million
    closet" / "$250 million ranch" to reach a general audience; even if only ~1% are core
    buyers, that beats every other luxury agent, and he reports selling multi-million
    properties off YouTube.
- (Kane) The core challenge *and* the big opportunity: make the **average person care**
  about your message so it earns millions of views, of which even a small % of on-target
  viewers outperforms everyone else. That is how you consistently go viral.
