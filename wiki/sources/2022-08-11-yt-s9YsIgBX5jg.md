# Science of Social Media Marketing — Masterclass w/ Brendan Kane

## Summary

A masterclass interview on The Futur (@thefutur), hosted by **Chris Do**, with guest
expert **Brendan Kane** — a social-media strategist and founder/managing director of
Hookpoint (a research and insights company on social and viral content), and author of
two best-selling books, *One Million Followers* and *Hook Point: How to Stand Out in a
3-Second World*. Kane is the domain expert here; Chris Do is hosting, interviewing, and
occasionally reframing Kane's ideas in his own designer/educator language.

**Attribution note:** Nearly all the frameworks and tactical claims below are **Brendan
Kane's** — they are recorded as CONTEXT and must NOT flow into Chris Do's
beliefs/voice/persona. The only persona-relevant material is Chris Do's own framing,
questions, analogies (e.g. his "conceptual design = disrupting an expectation" teaching
frame, the magician/misdirection analogy, the carousel example) and his stated
preferences (e.g. valuing a loyal long-haul YouTube audience). Those are marked as
Chris's below.

## Key claims

_All dated 2022-08-11. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Speaker labeled._

**Brendan Kane (guest expert — CONTEXT, not persona):**

- (Kane) Got into social media early (~2004–05, Friendster/MySpace); went to film
  school to become a producer, realized it taught no business, so built internet
  companies to learn. Broke into the film industry by pitching studios that he could tap
  online traffic sources for a fraction of TV/print cost. Ran what he calls the first
  YouTube influencer campaign (~2007) for the movie *Crank*, by cold-emailing the top 100
  YouTubers offering a movie-star interview.
- (Kane) When creatives dismiss social media, the real driver is usually not disdain but
  self-doubt or not knowing how to translate their expertise to the medium; once shown how
  to translate their "zone of genius," they become receptive.
- (Kane) *One Million Followers* grew out of a tested "hook" — he validated the book idea
  with a literary agent and marketing experts before running the 30-day experiment.
- (Kane) What drives social success today is one thing: the algorithms, which control
  reach and distribution. The myth that platforms suppress reach to force paid boosting is
  false — if true, nothing would go viral. Algorithms optimize for **user retention**
  (more time on platform → more ads served → more revenue), so they favor content that
  holds attention for the widest audience.
- (Kane) Two jobs of content: (1) **grab attention** in the first few seconds (stop the
  scroll on TikTok/FB/IG; earn the suggested-thumbnail click on YouTube), and (2) **hold
  attention** with an effective story. Clickbait is irrelevant now because if substance
  doesn't meet the first-3-seconds expectation, the retention graph falls off a cliff.
- (Kane) **PCM / "communication algorithm"** (Process Communication Model): six ways
  people perceive the world, backed by ~1.5M assessments, used by Pixar screenwriters, Bill
  Clinton, NASA. Population split he cites: feeling-based 30%, fact-based 25%, fun-based
  20%, values/opinions 10%, imagination-based 10%, action-based 5%. Creators
  subconsciously communicate only from their own strength, alienating the rest; contextualize
  the message to hit as many types as possible. Lead with the "big three" — feelings, facts,
  fun (75%). Non-verbal cues (background, facial expression, body) can carry a type too.
- (Kane) Illustrations he gives: a Keller Williams / Gary Keller real-estate script layering
  all six types in ~30 seconds; the Dollar Shave Club video (facts + chaotic fun background);
  Bill Clinton's "I feel your pain" debate moment winning the feeling-based voter; Harmon
  Brothers ads (Squatty Potty, PooPourri, Chatbooks).
- (Kane) Anyone in any vertical can produce consistent viral content by **contextualizing
  a niche message for the widest audience** — examples: Graham Stephan's "How I bought a
  Tesla for $78," Ryan Serhant touring a "$7M closet"/"$250M ranch" (millions of views; even
  1% core target beats other luxury agents), a tax YouTube channel (Clear Value Tax) with 1M+
  subs. This inverts the old marketing paradigm of niche content for a niche audience.
- (Kane) Hookpoint's creative process starts with **research**, not imitation. They study
  high performers plus adjacent creators for ~50+ "performance drivers" (pacing, tonality,
  personality types, edits, first 3 seconds, thumbnail, captions). A **gold/silver/bronze**
  method isolates true drivers: a driver that also appears in low performers is discarded.
- (Kane) The agency is pivoting to a **research-and-insights community** (weekly
  breakdowns) plus custom research and team training — they deliberately do NOT produce
  creative for clients; the goal is to make clients self-sufficient. Rationale: a creative
  agency isn't infinitely scalable, and he wants to be world-class at the granular research
  nobody else does.
- (Kane) Monetization: going viral multiplies the raw number of core-target viewers even as
  the percentage drops (his math: 500 → 20,000 potential customers). Options — subtly
  integrate CTAs, retarget the earned audience with ads ("use organic to earn the right to
  make the sale"), or (long game) build brand loyalty (Kylie Cosmetics, Taylor Swift,
  MrBeast). Direct-response is the alternative path (his friend Craig Clemens / Golden Hippo,
  ~$2B from single-video ad sales).
- (Kane) Worst practice: **batch-producing content on the belief that frequency alone
  scales.** Frequency works only once your format is dialed in (e.g. Alex Stemp, 20M TikTok
  followers). Quality/research first, then frequency. Hashtags and time-of-day are largely
  obsolete micro-optimizations — focus on the content.
- (Kane) Coaching technique: put the client's video **side-by-side** with a reference and
  play them together; ~70% of clients self-diagnose the gap, 30% need it pointed out.
- (Kane) YouTube is undervalued — a YouTube view/subscriber is worth ~10x a
  TikTok/IG/FB one because of consumption time and deeper loyalty (cites VidCon: YouTubers
  drew big fan turnout, TikTokers less so). His own Facebook/Instagram growth were
  experiments to land the book deal; he doesn't want to be an influencer/creator, he wants to
  teach — so resources currently go to research, not his own channel.
- (Kane) A **hook point** breaks the pattern to rise above the noise and grab attention at
  the highest level — but must be paired with holding attention, or it's just clickbait.
  Micro-attention is only for grabbing; once you have attention you can hold it for a 2-hour
  Rogan podcast or a Squid Game binge. Method to build hook points: collect the best ones in
  a Google doc, adapt them, then write originals, rank them, crowd-rank with friends, and
  study which billboards/ads fail to grab you (pattern recognition).
- (Kane) Thumbnail/headline: they must play off each other, not repeat the same message;
  apply **visual hierarchy** (viewers hit thumbnail first, headline second); don't overwhelm.
  Study MrBeast thumbnails. No blanket rules on faces — the facial expression and background
  matter more. Best exercise: screenshot a results page with competing suggested thumbnails
  and Photoshop yours in to see if it survives the column.
- (Kane) **Retention via tension** ("the Jenga effect" — tension and release with each
  block): set an expectation that something will happen without revealing what. Examples —
  Laney Gardner's Fleetwood Mac cover (~80M views, delays singing 15–16s to build tension),
  Graham Stephan withholding the Tesla numbers until 8–9 minutes in, and Kane's own
  umbrella/money-from-the-sky video. **Subverting expectations** (his IG titles "Why
  successful people know nothing," "Never ask for help") stops the scroll but must deliver on
  the content — use sparingly to avoid audience fatigue.

**Chris Do (host — persona-relevant framing/opinions):**

- (Chris) Reframes the whole topic away from "social media" toward **influence**: if you
  have an idea, framework, or book, the point is to get people to engage with it — "the best,
  most shareable ideas win," including ideas about sustainability and existential issues, not
  just marketing/sales. (persona framing)
- (Chris) Names common anti-social-media objections he hears (social media is vapid,
  about looking good, emotionally damaging to young people) as a camp to be shifted, not
  endorsed — he's setting up Kane, not stating his own belief. (framing)
- (Chris) Offers his own teaching frame from years at a traditional arts school:
  **conceptual design (in advertising/main-title work) is about disrupting/subverting an
  expectation** — first create an expectation, then, like a magician using misdirection,
  shift the viewer's perspective so they "get the joke" and light up. Maps this onto Kane's
  hook-point idea. (persona — his own pedagogy)
- (Chris) Confirms YouTube growth in his own terms: it comes down to **click-through rate**
  (grabbing attention) and **engagement / percentage of completion** (holding it) — Kane
  agrees. (persona — his own model)
- (Chris) Applies the tension idea to **static carousels**: if frame one tells everyone
  everything, there's no reason to reach frame ten. (persona — his own extension)
- (Chris) States he genuinely values The Futur's YouTube audience because they're "there
  for the long haul," invest their time, and he wants to keep investing in them — contrasts
  with fickle young TikTok audiences (attributed to a friend who consults TikTok creators).
  (persona — his stated preference/value)
- (Chris) Self-critique: in hindsight he'd have run the interview differently — handing
  Kane 10 random viral videos to break down live so lessons could be unpacked against real
  references. (persona — his facilitation reflection)

**Brendan Kane (guest expert — CONTEXT, NOT persona):**

> "The algorithms control reach and distribution of content... What do the algorithms really
> care about? They care about user retention. The longer people spend on a platform, the more

> "You need to grab attention... if you can't stop people's attention, you're never going to

> "We're not talking about tricking people. We're not talking about clickbait here." — Brendan Kane

> "It's not that you go over the top in any direction. You just hit on it so that... people

> "Your competition is going to tell you what not to do because they're probably not
