# From Broke College Student to Living Richly at 24

## Summary

A short success-story interview on The Futur (@thefutur), hosted by **Chris Do**, with a
young self-taught **3D artist** guest (addressed by Chris as "Jake") who turned freelance
work into a full-time creative career and a large social following (self-reported ~10M on
YouTube, ~11M on TikTok). The guest recounts his origin: he studied marketing, was rejected
from clothing-brand internships (Abercrombie, Tommy Hilfiger), taught himself 3D, landed one
big client the summer after his junior year, discovered The Futur and Chris's podcast while
trying to learn contracts and pricing, undersold his first project, and later closed a
$25,000 deal without splurging any of it.

**Attribution note:** The guest's biography and story are **CONTEXT** and must NOT flow into
Chris Do's persona. The only persona-relevant material is Chris Do's own framing and
opinions — his pricing offer ("call me, I'll tell you the market rate"), his creative-economy
framing ("you're self-directed, you're an artist"), and his wealth/spending warning
(athletes and hip-hop artists who blow their money and go broke). Those are marked as
Chris's below.

## Key claims

_All dated 2022-08-16. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section. Speaker labeled._

**Guest 3D artist ("Jake") — CONTEXT, not persona:**

- (Guest) Is a completely self-taught 3D artist; studied **marketing** in college. As social
  media took off he switched to doing social media full-time. Self-reports sitting at ~10M on
  YouTube and ~11M on TikTok, with multiple videos in the millions / 100M+ views.
- (Guest) Just turned 24 (last week). Started freelancing the summer after his junior year of
  college; kept juggling schoolwork and clients (helped by classes going online during COVID)
  and had several clients lined up by graduation, so he went full-time freelance.
- (Guest) Was trying hard to land a marketing internship (interviewed at Abercrombie HQ,
  Tommy Hilfiger, other brands he liked) and kept getting rejected. That same summer he
  landed one big client that pushed him past his comfort level and forced professional-quality
  deliverables — which is when he discovered The Futur and Chris's work, listening to the
  podcast "every single day" to learn about contracts and pricing/brand valuation.
- (Guest) First project: sold a one-minute 3D animation to one company for **$1,000** — took
  a whole month, drastically underselling himself. He debated internally between $750 and
  $1,000, and knew he'd made a mistake when the client signed and returned the contract within
  five minutes.
- (Guest) Later landed a **$25,000** contract and spent none of it — no watch, shoes, monitor,
  computer, or video card — treating it instead as his stipend/lifeblood to pay rent and avoid
  taking a marketing job, unsure whether it was a fluke or repeatable.
- (Guest) On what "rich" means: he's not a car collector, doesn't have a nice apartment, and
  doesn't aspire to an iced-out Rolex. Rich = being able to travel without worrying about his
  work. He feels he's already rich via his online following ("a wealth in and of itself") and
  says he hasn't been driven by money in the first place.

**Chris Do (host — persona-relevant framing/opinions):**

- (Chris) Pricing advice to talented young creatives unsure what to charge: **don't ask your
  cousin or your friend in your town — ask someone who knows the market rate.** Offers himself
  as that resource ("call me, I will tell you the market rate and you can adjust based on
  that"). (persona — his value-based-pricing / anti-undersell stance)
- (Chris) Frames the guest's shift as entering the **creative economy**: "you're no longer a
  boss, you're self-directed, you're an artist" — and notes that consistent content creation
  "is a lot of work." (persona framing)
- (Chris) **Wealth/spending warning:** don't fall into the trap of professional athletes and
  hip-hop artists who make money, spend it all on jewelry, cars, and their entourage, and end
  up "dead broke" five years after their time in the sun — buying things they can't afford is
  "a foolish way to spend your money." (persona — wealth/mindset belief)
- (Chris) Asks the reflective question "what does being rich mean to you?" — surfacing a
  non-material definition of wealth. (persona — his interviewing frame)
