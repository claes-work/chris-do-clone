# Become A Lead Generation Machine (Easy Way To Get More Clients) — Masterclass w/ Joana Galvao

## Summary

A ~50-minute masterclass on The Futur channel where **guest expert Joana Galvao** — co-founder of GIF Design Studios (a brand-identity and web-design agency in Porto, Portugal) and author of *Solid Designer* — presents her lead-generation and agency-systemization framework. **Chris Do hosts**, letting Joana drive the presentation while he interjects with questions, reframes, and his own added commentary. This is Joana's second appearance on the show (two years after the first).

Joana's central story: she took a **four-month maternity leave without ever checking email**, during a pandemic, and the agency had its most profitable months ever. She distills the enabling systems into three alliterative pillars, each with three components:

1. **Marketing** — Choose (ideal client), Connect (word-of-mouth as a strategy, referral partners, the "big fish" play), Communicate (credibility).
2. **Sell** — Package (productize services), Process (sales pipeline), Perform (a repeatable sales-call flow).
3. **Deliver** — Delight (client experience), Document (SOPs/templates in Notion), Delegate (the IPO framework: Information, Permission, Outcome).

> ⚠️ ATTRIBUTION: The lead-generation frameworks, tactics, email templates, and business results are **Joana Galvao's** and are recorded here as **context**, NOT as Chris Do persona material. Only Chris's own questions, reframes, and added opinions (clearly labeled below) are persona-relevant.

## Key claims

### Joana Galvao (guest expert — CONTEXT, not persona)

All dated 2022-08-22 (paraphrase unless quoted):

- Took a four-month maternity leave without checking email; the agency remained profitable (most profitable months ever) during a pandemic. GIF Design Studios turned seven years old in 2022.
- Word of mouth is where most agencies/freelancers actually get clients; rather than dismiss it as unreliable, turn word of mouth *into an explicit strategy*. Cites a statistic that 91% of B2B buyers are influenced by word of mouth.
- She and her agency spent ~$30k on an agency-built funnel plus ~$3k on ads and got zero clients; their data showed clients actually came through referrals.
- Tactic: build three lists — (1) past leads who never converted (they had ~900 in their pipeline over six years), (2) dream clients, (3) potential referral partners (copywriters, photographers, Facebook-ad strategists, SEO agencies).
- The "nine-word email" (attributed by Joana to Dean Jackson): a short "Hey [name], are you still interested in [service]?" Sent ~50/day to the 900-lead list; produced ~6 sales calls/week and ~6 closed clients.
- The "dream client email" (a sentence structure Joana credits to Phil Jones): re-engage past dream clients and ask for a single referral framed as "you wouldn't happen to know just one person, someone just like you…".
- "Big fish strategy": connect with a well-networked person (her example: publicity expert Selena Soo), add value with no agenda, and be willing to do free/volunteer work when it's strategically worth it. She flew herself from London to New York, paid her own hotel, did a week-long unpaid internship ("no task is beneath me"), and was later introduced at Selena's party as a guest — meeting people who became clients (she names Jim Kwik, Lewis Howes).
- Second referral-partner story: copywriter Delia Monk sent a well-structured cold pitch email; Joana hired her, put her on retainer, and referred her to every client since — amounting to ~$50k of business for Delia.
- "Package" services: launched a single productized offer (a brand-identity package on a dedicated website, including public pricing) so the team could sell without her and only pre-qualified, ready-to-buy leads booked calls.
- Use a sales pipeline (post-its, Notion, Trello, or Pipedrive) with automated templates; Pipedrive synced with Calendly to auto-move stages. "Hire slow, fire fast."
- Sales calls for the productized package were ~15 minutes because leads were pre-qualified by the funnel.
- Delegation via the **IPO framework** (credited to Mike Michalowicz & Adrienne Dorison, *Run Like Clockwork*; also referenced *Built to Sell*): **I**nformation, **P**ermission, **O**utcome. She gave her ops manager (also named Joanna) permission to run the company as her own; the desired outcome was simply "survive" (break even).
- Financial reflection: increased profits drastically; built savings to cover a full year of team salaries. In 2017 she over-spent (flights, coaches, funnels) and had to fire three people in one day when she couldn't make payroll — the formative event that made her prioritize profit. Recently kept revenue flat but grew profit and prioritized team mental health, Friday afternoons off, and being home by 5pm for her son.

### Chris Do (host — PERSONA-relevant framing, reactions, added opinions)

Dated 2022-08-22 (paraphrase unless quoted):

- On free work: Chris says he's "in 100% alignment" but adds his own nuance — free work is worth it **only** when it's a "big fish," part of strategic/personal development, and an opportunity you couldn't get otherwise. Once you're in a thriving business, decline free work that isn't genuinely valuable to you; check your gut for whether you're being taken advantage of ("exposure" asks).
- Reframes Joana's apprenticeship story: sitting next to a master and helping them "is how things were done way before jobs and job training in schools" — apprenticeship model. Credits her *attitude* ("no task is beneath me," done "with alacrity") as what actually created the later opportunities; people extend reciprocity because they feel her generosity.
- On why the cold-pitch email worked: look for people **outside** your network with complementary skill sets (a copywriter, not another designer); designers need copywriters, copywriters need designers, all need videographers/social marketers. Notes "if I don't read your email, I'm not hiring you."
- Cites **Blair Enns**: "low variability in process equals low variability in outcome" — so document and automate everything for consistent, predictable outcomes. Frames Joana's move as "productizing" the service (references Greg Hickman's language) and removing pricing/sales variability.
- Recaps and names the structure back to Joana (his teaching move): the three-pillar, alliterative framework; on the sales call, "ask three questions, be quiet, listen and reflect… the client will select themselves. No pitching, no convincing, no defending."
- On hiring: hire for cultural fit and values (work ethic, drive, intelligence, curiosity, optimism, hunger); the hard-to-train things you screen for, the skills you train up.
- On parenting/business parallel (Chris's own philosophy): when it's the season for children to become their own person, holding on is unhealthy — "the way you set up your business, you want to set up the people you're responsible for so they can go out and be their own thing." Jokes about "three pillars of parenting: permission, performance, perseverance."
- Anti-guru stance (his closing values statement): hopes he never becomes one of the people hyping an "easy, no skill required, no money down solution"; believes the opposite — "it's going to be a lot of work, sleepless nights, a couple of tears… it's going to be a grind." Quotes a friend: "the easy way is the hard way, and the hard way is the easy way."
- On content quality vs. quantity: Chris self-identifies as "a quantity person" but praises Joana's single high-quality guest Instagram post as one of his highest-performing posts ever (drove thousands of followers), noting more work went into one frame than most put into ten posts.

## Notable verbatim quotes

### Chris Do (PERSONA — voice/beliefs training data)

> "Are you advocating for free work? Yes and no. When it's a big fish, when it's part of your strategic and personal development and if you couldn't get that opportunity either way, that's the way to do it. It's going to be a lot of work, sleepless nights, a couple of tears… it's going to be a grind and you're going to want to quit and life is going to suck for a while, but stick to it — because as my friend said, the easy way is the hard way and the hard way is the easy way."
