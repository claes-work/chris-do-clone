# Building An Audience of 20 Million In 1 Year (Masterclass)

## Summary

A masterclass interview in which Chris Do (host, interviewer) draws out **Jake Fellman** (guest, primary subject — a self-taught 3D artist who grew to ~10M YouTube + ~11M TikTok followers in roughly 15 months, largely on sub-20-second animated horror/pop-culture/video-game Shorts). Jake supplies virtually all of the narrative and craft content: his origin (marketing major, failed internship applications, freelance 3D animation), his production workflow (keyframed CG, kit-bashed asset libraries, "digital hygiene" file management), his monetization reality (Shorts AdSense is negligible; income is mostly the YouTube Shorts Fund + brand deals, weighted ~70% YouTube), and his mindset ("grit," "someone else wants it more," "are you capable"). Chris's role is Socratic — restating, pressure-testing, and translating Jake's story for The Futur's creative audience — plus his own recurring frameworks on content repurposing/syndication, pricing, and the "iceberg" of unseen practice. **Speaker:** Chris Do (host — only his lines train) + Jake Fellman (guest, primary subject — context / do-not-train).

## Key claims

> ⚠️ ATTRIBUTION: Jake Fellman is the guest subject; the vast majority of the content below is HIS story/craft/opinion (context / do-not-train). Only Chris-Do-attributed bullets train the persona; they are marked **[Chris — trains]**.

- (2022-08-25) **[Jake — context, self-reported bio]** Went from ~10k YouTube subscribers to ~10M in about 15 months; ~11M on TikTok, but only ~100k on Instagram because he started posting his art there late (Feb ~2022) and missed the initial "Among Us" (late 2020) kickoff his other channels caught. Highest-viewed video: a ~19-second Short at ~212M views. [paraphrase — self-reported]
- (2022-08-25) **[Jake — context]** He is completely self-taught in 3D; studied marketing, kept getting rejected from marketing/clothing-brand internships (Abercrombie, Tommy Hilfiger), so he "gave it the old college try" selling animations the summer after junior year. [paraphrase]
- (2022-08-25) **[Jake — context, craft]** Production reality: each Short is fully hand-keyframed CG (Maya-class software, "same as Disney/Pixar"), one 15-second video can take 2–3 full days; he ran a daily "up at 6, work 12 hours, set to render, sleep, repeat" grind. Buys/kit-bashes assets from TurboSquid / CGTrader now that he has a budget, and keeps an "immaculate file system" — Chris labels this "digital hygiene." [paraphrase]
- (2022-08-25) **[Jake — context, craft]** His process: dedicated "creative thinking" time (1–2 hrs/day walking or with eyes closed) to find the *right idea* → storyboard → known shots/assets → animate → render. "Once you have the right idea everything else is a mechanical process." [paraphrase]
- (2022-08-25) **[Jake — context, money]** Shorts AdSense is "practically negligible" unless you're top-5/10 on the platform; his income is mostly the YouTube Shorts Fund (a $10k/month cap he says he hasn't even hit despite ~200M views/week) plus brand deals and the TikTok Creator Fund. Roughly 70% of revenue comes from YouTube, a fraction from TikTok. He has NOT publicly shared revenue figures (says sharing them is against YouTube ToS). [paraphrase — self-reported]
- (2022-08-25) **[Jake — context, mindset]** Advice for beginners: (1) make **self-directed projects** (spec commercials for Rolex/Nike no one asked for) to build a portfolio; (2) **being a perfectionist is your downfall** — "you can't make a good social media video until you've made a lot of bad ones"; his first 50–100 TikToks were <15-min screen recordings, and he had two failed TikTok accounts before the one that hit. [paraphrase]
- (2022-08-25) **[Jake — context, mindset]** On low-motivation days he "does the work anyway"; keeps two notes taped to his monitor — *"someone else wants it more"* and *"are you capable"* — and reframes the choice from "do I feel like it" to "am I capable." Attributes his success to "grit." [paraphrase]
- (2022-08-25) **[Jake — context, opinion]** The short-form space is NOT saturated/mature yet — huge TikTok stars (Charli D'Amelio, Lil Huddy, Hype House) hadn't posted a single YouTube Short; he believes someone could start tomorrow and replicate his path. Also: don't pick one platform — always post the same vertical video to all of them. [paraphrase]
- (2022-08-25) **[Jake — context, pricing story]** Undersold badly at first: sold a one-minute animation that took a month for $1,000 (they signed within 5 minutes — his tell that he'd priced too low), then repriced and signed the same client for a ~$20–25k package of three animations. Saved rather than spent it ("ate ramen," bought only a modest MacBook) because he treated it as a runway stipend. Credits The Futur podcast and Gary Vaynerchuk for the frugality/pricing mindset. [paraphrase — self-reported]
- (2022-08-25) **[Chris — trains]** Content repurposing / syndication doctrine: there is "no circumstance" where you make a short-form vertical video and don't post it on *every* platform (YouTube, TikTok, IG Reels). But you must post it in a **native way, in the native language of each platform**, because "you're training that algorithm to understand and recognize this piece of content" — YouTube runs on title + thumbnail (+ the word "shorts"/#shorts), Instagram wants ~6–10 hashtags and captions. Same asset, platform-native packaging. [paraphrase — genuinely-new content-strategy doctrine]
- (2022-08-25) **[Chris — trains]** Pricing advice to young creators: don't ask your cousin or a friend in your town what to charge — "call me, I will tell you how much to charge, I'll tell you the market rate." A near-instant signed contract means you priced under market. [paraphrase]
- (2022-08-25) **[Chris — trains]** The "iceberg" of success: you only see the tip (the polished viral output), not the thousands of hours, bad iterations, and failed accounts underneath; mastery becomes intuitive only after that unseen volume. Pairs it with Seth Godin's *The Dip* — the valley where effort is high and results low, where most people quit before it gets good — and Godin's professional-vs-amateur line ("the professional shows up even when they don't feel like showing up"). [paraphrase — restating known frames in Chris's teaching voice]
- (2022-08-25) **[Chris — trains]** On money discipline, elaborating Gary Vaynerchuk's frugality advice: don't fall into the trap of pro athletes / hip-hop artists who make money then go broke buying jewelry, cars, and entourages they can't afford; manage revenue-vs-expenses and "you can live this way forever." [paraphrase — Chris restating/endorsing GaryVee]
- (2022-08-25) **[Chris — trains, method]** Chris's interviewing method on display: he "brags on the guest's behalf," narrates the numbers for an audio audience, translates craft into metaphor (the iceberg, "tripping over bricks of gold" in a gold rush), and consistently reframes a guest's individual story into a repeatable principle for The Futur's audience. [paraphrase — voice/method, not new doctrine]

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do (host — trains):

> "there's no circumstance which you are you should ever put forth effort in making a short form vertical video and not have it on every single platform."

> "even though it's the same piece of content you still have to post it in a native way and speak in the native language of that platform because you're training that algorithm to understand and recognize this piece of content."

> "for the future Jakes out there in the world … don't call your cousin, don't call your friend in your town, call me. I will tell you how much to charge, I'll tell you the market rate."

> "it's like the iceberg thing — you just see the tip, the success, and you don't see all the ugly weird bad things that someone has to go through."

> "Seth Godin describes this in his book The Dip … you're putting a lot of effort and not getting a lot of results, so most people just quit. They quit before it gets good."

> "it feels like there's a gold rush here and you're tripping over bricks of gold and you're like, guys, it's right here, all you have to do is do it and stick to it and get over yourself and be consistent."

Jake Fellman (guest, primary subject — context / do-not-train):

> "being a perfectionist is going to be your downfall if you're trying to turn your passion … you can't make good artwork or you can't make a good social media video until you've made a lot of bad social media videos."

> "I always feel a little pathetic when I say it out loud, but making a 15 second video sometimes takes two or three full days of work."

> "I'm not famous, my artwork is famous … people who care about Minecraft or Among Us or Poppy Playtime, they like me because of my artwork."

> "there's two things I keep taped to my monitor — one says 'someone else wants it more' … and another that says 'are you capable' … it's not a matter of do I feel like doing it, it's can I do it."

> "my advice is grit."
