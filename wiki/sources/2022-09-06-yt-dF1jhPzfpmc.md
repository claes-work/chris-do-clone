## Key claims (2022-09-06 — all attributed to guest Ryan Blair unless noted; DO-NOT-TRAIN)

- (Ryan Blair, paraphrase) To become a "hero" you must go through the darkest night —
  isolation, being attacked and "crucified"; he frames this as "the formula the universe
  uses to create heroes."
- (Ryan Blair, paraphrase) The only way out of a hole is to grow; whenever he has been
  in a hole he had to increase his rate of growth to climb out.
- (Ryan Blair, paraphrase) People in the wilderness fall into "suffering from suffering,"
  which creates a loop that reprograms them toward negativity and keeps them stuck
  longer; the antidote is to stop suffering from suffering and start growing — the faster
  you grow, the faster you reach the other side.
- (Ryan Blair, biographical, paraphrase) His first entrepreneurial endeavor: he worked as
  an engineer at a company and convinced them to spin out a division he was running; they
  agreed but gave him a VP title instead of CEO, saying he was too young and inexperienced.
  He hated that and left.
- (Ryan Blair, biographical, paraphrase) He then bought "Skype Pipeline" for **$15,000**,
  raised venture capital, expanded it into an entity, and sold it in a **$25 million deal
  at age 24**.
- (Ryan Blair, biographical, paraphrase) The ViSalus opportunity followed; he first
  joined as Chief Strategy Officer, then told his co-founders he would become CEO.
- (Ryan Blair, biographical, paraphrase) He had previously been a **leader in a gang** with
  ~100 people under him; he says the gap between succeeding as a legal vs. an illegal
  entrepreneur was "easy to learn," and that his street smarts and survival skills gave him
  a confidence his business opponents lacked.
- (Ryan Blair, paraphrase) He believes some people are born to do certain things and that
  his "soul came here to create through entrepreneurship" — he was an entrepreneur even on
  the streets.
- (Ryan Blair, paraphrase) Being a CEO/entrepreneur demands high responsibility, stress, and
  continuous growth as the business scales; many entrepreneurs never get the CEO role or do
  it poorly.
- (Ryan Blair, biographical, paraphrase) As CEO of Skype Pipeline he was "green" — repeatedly
  challenged by sophisticated investors and knew little about financial reporting, analysis,
  strategy presentation, or setting/making numbers; he learned by doing.
- (The Futur host, paraphrase) Draws a parallel to a Robert Kiyosaki line: "bullets couldn't
  kill me, Wall Street's not going to kill me either" — surviving hardship builds the belief
  you can survive anything.
