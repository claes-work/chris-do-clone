The conversation covers reinvention and identity: a constant force distracts you from
the work; mastery in any profession requires sustained, focused attention; most
people who claim they "want it" are unwilling to change their identity, ego, routine,
diet, or relationships; deciding to become a new person is easier than people think,

from serving through entrepreneurship alone to leading with spirituality.

## Key claims (2022-09-08 — all attributed to guest Ryan Blair unless noted; DO-NOT-TRAIN)

- (Ryan Blair, paraphrase) A constant force tries to distract you from the work, and you
  fight it alone; success requires controlling and focusing your attention on one thing
  for a long period until you reach mastery — true of any profession, from pianist to
  entrepreneur.
- (Ryan Blair, paraphrase) Across the hundreds of people he has mentored since starting
  Alter Call ~two years earlier, two recurring themes: (1) people don't know how to
  inoculate themselves against distracting forces, and (2) they don't want it badly
  enough — they say they want success but won't leave their comfort zone, change their
  morning routine, diet, exercise, identity, ego, or confront their "shadow."
- (Ryan Blair, biographical, paraphrase) He wanted success so badly he would change
  anything about himself — including his name, identity, the way he walked, talked,
  dressed, and spoke.
- (Ryan Blair, biographical/spiritual, paraphrase) When he started Alter Call he returned
  to those roots and told his "higher power / God" he would change anything about himself
  to serve at his highest capacity; as a self-run entrepreneur he served many people but
  was "never at my best."
- (Ryan Blair, paraphrase) You can simply decide to become a new person: declare it, do
  the work (e.g. "buy the paintbrush and palette" and declare yourself an artist) — making
  the decision is easier than people think; the hard work begins after.
- (Ryan Blair, paraphrase) After the decision you face intense resistance — from friends
  and from your "lower self" — every day for about a year; on the other side the reward
  is far worth the risk, and the ~20 people you leave behind get replaced by 20+ new,
  more inspiring people. About a year in you realize you made the right decision.
- (Ryan Blair, biographical, paraphrase) He is now two years into Alter Call and his
  reinvention "to be of service through entrepreneurship and to lead with my
  spirituality," which he calls a dream come true. In the first year people tried to stop
  him, "poison" what he was doing, laughed at him for becoming "hyper religious," and
  accused him of trading one addiction for another; some of those same critics now cheer
  him on.
- (Ryan Blair, paraphrase) Resistance is inevitable whenever you step into a new calling
  and make "a new Declaration of the self"; the critics' unwitting role is to build your
  strength, and he teaches people to develop the fortitude to work through opposition —
  "the Deep work."
- (Interviewer / likely The Futur host, paraphrase) Frames the ask on the audience's
  behalf: given a life he himself hasn't lived (never in a gang, never shot at), how can
  the learnings on reinventing yourself and aligning around your purpose be distilled and
  extrapolated for an ordinary listener?
