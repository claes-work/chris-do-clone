# How To Sell Anything To Anyone (w/ Ron Baker)

## Summary

**Speaker split:** Chris Do (HOST) in conversation with **Ron Baker** (GUEST) — value-pricing
authority, author of *Implementing Value Pricing* and other pricing books, founder of the
**VeraSage Institute**. In this excerpt Chris functions almost entirely as the *interviewer*:
he poses the framing questions (why pay an ~$800 premium for an Apple logo over a spec-identical
Dell; is an "unfair price" even possible; how self-esteem factors into pricing) and Ron delivers
the substantive answers. **Ron's pricing frameworks are CONTEXT / DO-NOT-TRAIN** — Ron is a
domain expert whose ideas must be fenced and NOT attributed to Chris. Only Chris-attributed lines
(his framing of the questions and his affirmations) train the persona.

Ron's answers (context only): brands are not dead — a brand is a *promise*, and price reflects
how a company perceives its own value (Apple never goes on sale, never justifies price by rising
costs). Value is determined by the buyer, not the seller's internal costs ("I'm not buying your
costs, I'm buying a great cup of coffee"). Every voluntary transaction profits *both* parties —
his "double thank-you moment." A "just price" is one a willing buyer and seller freely agree to
(he traces this to Aquinas and Greek philosophers); "fair" has no fixed definition. Underpricing
is often a *self-esteem* problem — pricing is "the reputation we have with ourselves," and your
belief in your own worth is "your first sale"; if you don't believe you're worth it, customers
never will either.

## Key claims

All dated 2022-09-15. Attributed per speaker. Only **Chris** lines are persona-eligible.

**Chris Do (HOST — persona-eligible, but mostly question-framing):**
- Frames the core pricing puzzle: two spec-identical laptops (same processor, RAM, video card),
  Dell vs Apple, yet a "rational" buyer will pay an ~$800+ premium for the one "that has a logo
  on it" — what is the psychology behind that? (paraphrase, Chris — posed as a question)
- Affirms buyer-determined value in the Starbucks exchange: the buyer decides when the coffee /
  experience is "just not worth it anymore" — "you the buyer you get to determine the value to
  you." (paraphrase, Chris — echoing/affirming)
- Frames limiting beliefs as a pricing blocker: some people's mindset tells them "I can't choose
  a field to specialize in because I'll lose out on customers" or "I can't ask the customer for
  more money... because [value-based pricing] doesn't work," and asks Ron to expand on self-esteem
  as a driver of pricing. (paraphrase, Chris — question framing)

**Ron Baker (GUEST — CONTEXT / DO-NOT-TRAIN):**
- Brands are not dead; a brand represents a *promise*, and price reflects how a company perceives
  its own value. (paraphrase, Ron — context)
- Apple never goes on sale and never justifies price increases by rising internal costs; it keeps
  "pricing integrity aligned with value." Contrasts Starbucks blaming coffee-cost inflation — "I'm
  not buying your costs, I'm buying a great cup of coffee." (paraphrase, Ron — context)
- Value is set by the buyer, not seller cost; a $5 latte is only bought because it's worth more
  than $5 to that buyer. (paraphrase, Ron — context)
- The "double thank-you moment": in any voluntary transaction both buyer and seller profit — both
  say thank you; otherwise one would say "you're welcome." (paraphrase, Ron — context)
- A "just price" is one a willing buyer and seller freely agree to (attributed by Ron to Aquinas
  and Greek philosophers); "fair" has no objective definition (excepting price-fixing/antitrust).
  Anecdote: pricing mentor **Reed Holden** paid $300 for a same-day ice-maker repair, so by
  definition he valued it above $300. (paraphrase, Ron — context)
- Underpricing is a *self-esteem* / self-respect issue — "the reputation we have with ourselves";
  your belief in your own worth is "your first sale," and if you don't believe you're worth 10–100x
  your rate, customers never will. Each dollar a customer pays is a "certificate of performance."
  (paraphrase, Ron — context)
- Aside on money morality: it's "the *love* of money" that is the root of all evil, not money
  itself. (paraphrase, Ron — context)
