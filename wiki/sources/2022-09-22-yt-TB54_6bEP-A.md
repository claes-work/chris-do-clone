# What Makes People Buy? Price & Value Masterclass w/ Ron Baker

## Summary

A masterclass interview on pricing hosted by **Chris Do** (The Futur) with guest expert
**Ron Baker** — a value-pricing thought leader, "recovering CPA," founder of the
VeraSage Institute think tank, co-host of *The Soul of Enterprise* radio show, and
author of seven books including *Implementing Value Pricing*. Ron does virtually all
the teaching; Chris hosts, frames questions on behalf of his creative-services
audience, and repeatedly voices his own agreement.

**Attribution note — this is CONTEXT, not core persona data.** The frameworks (labor
theory of value vs. subjective theory of value, price justifies cost, price
discrimination, the three-option value conversation, subscription/"value pricing 2.0")
belong to **Ron Baker**. Their persona value here is that Chris Do **explicitly names
Ron Baker as an influence** on his own pricing philosophy and endorses these ideas.
Chris opens by crediting **Blair Enns** ("heavily influenced by" him, reading his
pricing books) and says Blair's podcast refers to Baker's *Implementing Value Pricing*
as "canon," so Chris went "to the source." This corroborates that **Chris's
value-based pricing derives partly from Ron Baker and Blair Enns.** Only Chris's own
statements and explicit endorsements below feed the persona; Baker's content is logged
as influence/context.

## Key claims

**Ron Baker (guest — CONTEXT / influence, NOT Chris persona):**
- Value is subjective (subjective theory of value); nothing has intrinsic value except
  human life — value depends on the utility to the consumer. He claims to have been
  first to tie the discredited **labor theory of value** (Marx) to the billable hour.
- Cost does NOT determine price; **price justifies cost.** If cost-plus explained the
  world, no business would go bankrupt. A coat costs ~10x a hat because customers value
  it ~10x, not because it costs 10x to make.
- Cost-plus pricing reflects an "entitlement mindset" — thinking "the world owes me a
  living" for overhead, lease, employees.
- Pricing sends signals; high price is a heuristic for high quality. Healthy markets
  have a wide array of price points (McDonald's ↔ Ruth's Chris; Hilton ↔ Four Seasons).
- A brand can only stand for one thing (credits Tim Williams) — favors niching /
  specialization; specialists attract clients from a wider geography and command higher
  prices.
- Low-price/penetration leadership (Costco, Walmart, Southwest) is viable but a wrong
  fit for knowledge work — "we're selling our minds, not our hands."
- Price discrimination (charging different customers different prices) is economically
  beneficial and not immoral — enables senior discounts, coupons, cheaper drugs in
  poorer countries. Price the customer/context, not the job.
- A "just price" is one a willing buyer and seller freely agree to (absent fraud,
  coercion, price-fixing); every voluntary transaction is a "double thank you" where
  both sides profit. He argues even anti-gouging laws are bad policy.
- The **value conversation** (credits Blair Enns' three-step process) diagnoses the
  customer's desired outcome before pricing — "prescription without diagnosis is
  malpractice." Illustrated by the three-landscaper story: hourly (inputs) < fixed
  scope (outputs) < the third who asked questions and offered **three options** priced
  on outcome/transformation ($150 / $225 / $300 a month); Ron paid 3x more and was
  happier. Every customer has a "best curbside appeal" — a desired future state.
- Always offer **options** (usually three) — it shifts the customer from "should I work
  with you?" to "how should I work with you?" Every business offers options except those
  who bill by the hour.
- Professionals are not just problem-solvers but "possibility pursuers" — solving only
  problems merely reverts a client to the status quo (credits Peter Drucker).
- **Self-esteem is the first sale:** if you don't believe you're worth 10–100x your
  hourly rate, clients never will. ~95% of pricing mistakes are pricing too low.
- Forthcoming book "value pricing 2.0" = the **subscription business model** (with Paul
  Dunn), moving from transactional to relational; examples: concierge medicine / direct
  primary care, Porsche Drive, Summit CPA (Jody Grund, scaled to $7M+), hassle-free home
  services. "Common services don't command uncommon prices."
