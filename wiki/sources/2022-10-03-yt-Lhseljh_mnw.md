## Summary

**Speaker:** an **unidentified British guest presenter** (NOT Chris Do — see attribution
callout). A short marketing lesson arguing that content should "talk to the symptoms, not
the solution," because you win attention by meeting prospects where they already are. The
speaker maps the AIDA funnel (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action) onto three layers of
problem awareness — (1) the **known and spoken** problem the prospect openly complains
about, (2) the **known but unspoken** problem they're aware of but don't voice, and (3)
the **unknown and unspoken** root problem they can't see — illustrated with a dishwasher
repair story (the visible symptom was a broken machine; the hidden root cause was a broken
isolation valve). The claimed payoff: in *marketing*, speak to the known-and-spoken symptom
to earn attention; on a *sales call*, drill down through the layers to reveal the
unknown-unspoken root problem, which positions you as the trusted guide/partner where the
value lies. Closes with a promo for the speaker's own book and live workshops. Chris Do
does not speak in this transcript.

## Key claims

_All claims attributed to the unidentified guest presenter, NOT Chris Do. Retained as
Futur-channel context only._

- [2022-10-03] In marketing and online posts you should speak to the **symptoms** the
  prospect is experiencing, not to your solution — that is how you capture attention and
  interest. (paraphrase)
- [2022-10-03] The first two stages of AIDA (Attention, Interest) are earned by talking
  about what the prospect is *currently experiencing*; you must "meet people where they
  are" and make them feel understood before taking them on the rest of the journey.
  (paraphrase)
- [2022-10-03] There are three layers of problem: (1) **known + spoken** — the problem the
  prospect openly talks about; (2) **known + unspoken** — a problem they're aware of but
  don't voice; (3) **unknown + unspoken** — the real root problem, invisible to them.
  (paraphrase)
- [2022-10-03] The insight-creation sequence: start with a **symptom** the prospect feels →
  surface their **assumptions/beliefs** about it → highlight the **real (usually unknown,
  unspoken) problem** you can see → then present the **solution**. (paraphrase)
- [2022-10-03] On a **sales call** (not marketing) your job is to drill down through the
  layers to the unknown-unspoken problem; revealing a problem the prospect didn't know
  existed gets you seen as the **guide/partner**, which is where the value is. (paraphrase)
- [2022-10-03] If you only address the known-and-spoken problem, there are usually already
  reasons it hasn't been fixed (e.g., "we don't have the money for it"). (paraphrase)
- [2022-10-03] If a freebie or "no-brainer" offer isn't getting traction, it's likely
  speaking too much to the unknown-unspoken problem, or it reads as just a solution the
  prospect doesn't see as relevant to them. (paraphrase)
- [2022-10-03] Practical prompt for the next client call: ask what they're experiencing
  day-to-day that they *don't* want to be experiencing — the felt symptom that makes them
  believe something needs to change. (paraphrase)
