# Three Critical Steps To Learning

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do — solo whiteboard teaching segment (first person throughout,
draws an input/output diagram, references his own "five ingredients" cooking analogy
and names designer George Paku for grid-logo tutorials). Subject-attributed; trains
the persona.

Chris argues that learning is over-valued as *consumption* (input) and that the
higher-leverage half of learning is *implementation* (output). He splits "learning"
into what happens above the head (consuming books/videos/podcasts) and below the head
(producing something from it), and insists output matters more than input. He then
lays out a three-step output process — reflect, implement, share — layered on top of a
prerequisite: high-quality (single-tasked, focused) input. Consumption without output,
he warns, is only the "illusion of knowledge" and is "intellectually self-gratifying."

## Key claims

_All dated 2022-10-12, paraphrased unless quoted._

- **Framing: input vs. output.** Learning has two halves — *consumption/input* (above
  the head) and *implementation/output* (below the head). Output is more important than
  input, because you have only truly learned something once you turn an input into an
  output. Consumption feels like growth but is only "one small part of the puzzle."
- **Prerequisite — quality of input.** Before the three steps, the *quality* of input
  matters because the output is built from it. Quality drops with multitasking (music,
  TV, phone notifications, several books at once). For most people, single-tasking and
  focus produce better learning; he flags multitasking as a minor exception.
- **Step 1 — Reflect.** After consuming something, make it a habit to pause *before*
  moving to the next item and ask: "What was most useful to me?" and "How might I apply
  this in my life and work?" (also "Where else have I seen this idea?"). Writing the
  answer down is critical. Skipping reflection leaves you with the "illusion of
  knowledge."
- **Step 2 — Implement.** Turn thought into action; you must *make* something. Watching
  is not enough ("we can't just learn by watching… As much as I like to say that I can,
  I cannot"). Example: watch a grid-logo tutorial, then go sketch and make. Doing it is
  how you cement/fortify the knowledge and extract the maximum from it.
- **Step 3 — Share.** Being forced to *articulate* what you learned to someone else
  pushes it deep down the "learning pyramid." He reframes "teaching" (loaded with
  pretentiousness anxiety — "who am I to be a teacher?") as simply *sharing* — a tweet,
  a note, a drawn diagram. He notes his own content is exactly this: him articulating
  what he learned from books and videos.

**Contradictions / tension:** None internal. He explicitly hedges the single-tasking
claim ("some people work better multitasking… these are broad-based things").

**Overlap with existing wiki (NB):** The **learn-by-modeling**, **five-ingredients**,
and **teach-while-you-learn / share-to-learn** ideas are already covered elsewhere in
the corpus. This video's contribution is a *consolidated three-step output framework*
(reflect → implement → share) sitting on a **quality-of-input / single-tasking**
prerequisite, plus the crisp "consumption vs. implementation" / "input vs. output"
framing and the "illusion of knowledge" phrase. The share step here is the same
underlying idea as teach-while-you-learn, restated; likely **not genuinely new** enough
on its own to force L3, but the reflect-step questions and the input/output framing are
a cleaner articulation worth noting to synthesis.
