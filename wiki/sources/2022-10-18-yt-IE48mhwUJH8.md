# What's Your Incentive For Copywriting?

## Summary

**Speakers:** A two-person dialogue — an interviewed **guest copywriter** (a
direct-response copywriter who works on SaaS / SEO-software landing pages, self-taught,
cites David Ogilvy; **likely Neville Medhora of Kopywriting Kourse — identity
attribution: UNCERTAIN**) and **Chris Do** (host/interviewer). This is almost certainly
a clip from a copywriting masterclass on @thefutur.

> definition of copywriting as brain-to-brain communication, the "compression of
> information" argument, and the direct-response-vs-creative-advertising incentive
> analysis (including the David Ogilvy reference) — is spoken by the **GUEST**, not by
> Chris Do. **Guest lines are CONTEXT ONLY and must NOT train the persona.** Only

> "advertising shapes culture" audience note) are eligible to train.

The guest reframes copywriting away from "a person at a typewriter picking the best
words" toward *communicating a message from one brain to another (ideally to millions)*
using the best medium and tools available — words in 1700, but now images, video,
audio, and soon VR. He argues humans keep compressing information (two-hour
documentary → five-minute YouTube → 20-second Instagram) so text-only thinking is
obsolete. He then distinguishes his work — unglamorous conversion optimization of a
page (rewrite the headline, swap a dead image for an explanatory GIF) — from
big-agency "creative" advertising, framed through **incentives**: his incentive is
measurable client ROI (10x), whereas an employee deep inside a 5,000-person agency is
incentivized to show a chart going up-and-to-the-right or win a cinematography award,
not to generate sales. Chris situates this against his own graphic-design + ad-agency
background and his advertising-world friends who say "advertising doesn't reflect
culture, it shapes culture."

## Key claims

_All dated 2022-10-18. Attributed per speaker; paraphrase unless quoted. **Guest claims
are context/do-not-train.**_

**Guest (context only — DO NOT TRAIN):**
- **Copywriting = communication, not wordsmithing.** The goal is to move a message from
  one brain to another (preferably to a million or a billion), using whatever medium is
  best at the time; treating text as "the ultimate thing" is "insane." Text is
  foundational but images and video matter too.
- **Information compression over time.** Learning has gone from two-hour documentaries →
  five-minute YouTube videos → 20-second Instagram teachings; humans keep compressing,
  so the real question is "what's the best way to communicate information — words,
  images, videos, all the above."
- **His work is direct-response optimization.** He looks at a software landing page with
  "fresh eyes," fixes a headline that "doesn't make sense," replaces an uninformative
  image with a GIF that shows the product working. "It's not even that hard… a normal
  person could probably do what I do."
- **Incentives explain the direct-response vs. creative-advertising split.** His
  incentive is client ROI (paid $10k, made the client $100k = 10x). A junior employee
  (e.g. "employee number 1200 at a company of 5,000") answers up a chain of managers, so
  their incentive is a Friday chart that goes "up and to the right" or an award — not
  client sales. Big brand ads (a Ford F-150 drone commercial chasing a cinematography
  award) optimize for prestige, not measurable sales. Credits **David Ogilvy** as where
  he first learned this, later confirming it himself at mid-to-large companies.

**Chris Do (trainable):**
- **His lens on copywriting comes from design + ad-agency roots.** "My background is I
  studied graphic design and then my first job out of school was to work at an ad
  agency, so when I think of copywriting I think of the arts and crafts of what they
  do." (Confirms his first job was at a **medium-sized** agency, not a big one.)
- **Distinguishes advertising copywriters from results-copywriters.** There are many
  copywriters but *fewer advertising copywriters* — a different art form of "spending a
  lot of time to craft a few words for a very few number of clients" — versus the
  guest's model of getting "results from my clients, bottom line, through words and
  images and layout."
- **Audience framing note.** His friends are mostly in advertising and say "advertising
  doesn't reflect culture, it shapes culture" — a mindset "not necessarily tied to
  results and metrics and things you can measure," i.e. a whole different frame from
  direct response.
