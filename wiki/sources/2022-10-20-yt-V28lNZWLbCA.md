# Using Social Media Trends To Write Better

## Summary

**Speaker: Neville Medhora (guest) — CONTEXT / DO-NOT-TRAIN.** This is a clip from a
copywriting session/masterclass on The Futur. Nearly all substantive teaching is
delivered by copywriter **Neville Medhora** (of Kopywriting Kourse), who refers to
Chris Do in the third person ("a headline that says Christo is awesome…") and to himself
as distinct ("if we use *my name* in the email subject line versus *yours*"). **Chris Do
is only the interviewer here** — he asks the framing questions (about split testing, and
"how are you making those decisions?") but contributes no teaching content of his own.

Because the persona is Chris Do, **none of the claims below train the persona** — they
are Neville Medhora's copywriting views, filed as context only.

Neville's thesis: split testing is overrated for most businesses (it needs enormous
traffic and often just splits hairs); clarity beats cleverness in headlines; the real
open-rate driver is *sender reputation*, not subject lines; and good copy comes from
actively tracking current social-media/SEO trends rather than a fixed formula.

## Key claims (dated 2022-10-20)

> All claims below are **Neville Medhora (guest), do-not-train** unless marked otherwise.

- **Split testing can lead you astray; you rarely need it.** Instead of A/B testing every
  headline, just make copy *clearer* — replace vague headlines and remove confusion; each
  small incremental improvement compounds. Split testing requires very heavy traffic to be
  valid. *(Neville Medhora, do-not-train)*
- **Split testing was more useful ~10–20 years ago** when a page was your only touchpoint
  with a customer; today people find you across Twitter, Instagram, blog, YouTube, ads, and
  word-of-mouth, so no single sales page makes the whole difference and it's nearly
  impossible to attribute. *(Neville Medhora, do-not-train)*
- **Prefer clarity over jargon.** SaaS pages that say things like "a fusion between AI
  technology and human design" should instead say "an AI copywriter that will write blog
  posts for you." Big-company committee copy tends toward meaningless jargon.
  *(Neville Medhora, do-not-train)*
- **The "AAA test."** Send the *exact same* subject line to two halves of your list and you
  still get different results — proof that natural audience variation ("a double placebo")
  can masquerade as a split-test "win." So when people agonize over tiny subject-line
  wording differences, they're splitting hairs. *(Neville Medhora, do-not-train)*
- **Email is the best place to split test** (if you have a list) — but the biggest lever is
  not the subject line. *(Neville Medhora, do-not-train)*
- **Sender field beats subject line for open rate.** "If my mom sends me an email… I'm
  still gonna open it, it doesn't matter what the subject line is." You build sender
  reputation over time. *(Neville Medhora, do-not-train)*
- **Keep up with trends to write well.** 2012–2013 "you won't believe what happens next"
  upworthy-style headlines were once king but are now actively demoted/banned on social
  media; writing in an outdated style dates your copy. *(Neville Medhora, do-not-train)*
- **Method for tracking trends:** maintain a separate email account subscribed to many
  companies' marketing, plus a tool that collects it all, to watch which growing companies
  are sending what format/content; also observe what's working across the community's
  businesses. *(Neville Medhora, do-not-train)*
- **SEO trend shift: shorter now wins.** The old advice was "the longer the better"
  (5,000–10,000-word articles); Google now surfaces short-to-medium articles because people
  want a summary, not everything under the sun. *(Neville Medhora, do-not-train)*
- **Cold email: short beats long.** A one-to-two-sentence cold email tends to outperform a
  long pitch (transcript cuts off mid-point). *(Neville Medhora, do-not-train)*

## Notable verbatim quotes (attributed)

- Neville Medhora (guest, do-not-train): "If you do what I call an AAA Test instead of an
  A/B test — test the exact same email subject line on your email list — you'll get
  different results. Try it, see what happens."
- Neville Medhora (guest, do-not-train): "You know what the best indicator of open rate is?
  It's the sender field. Imagine my mom sends me an email — she's not really good at subject
  lines, I'm still gonna open it. It doesn't matter what the subject line is, I want to hear
  from her. You send a reputation over time."
- Neville Medhora (guest, do-not-train): "You have to keep up with trends… if you're stuck
  in that era you'd be writing these spam articles that social media kind of bans now."
