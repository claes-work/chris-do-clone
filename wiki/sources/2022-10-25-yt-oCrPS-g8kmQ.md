# Write Better Copy For Conversions (Masterclass w/ Neville Medhora)

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do (host, The Futur) interviews **Neville Medhora** (guest, copywriter,
founder of Kopywriting Kourse / copywritingcourse.com, formerly of AppSumo and The Hustle).
Neville is the primary teacher here; the bulk of the episode is his copywriting, marketing,
and newsletter frameworks — **context / do-not-train** (he is the expert, not the subject).
Only lines explicitly attributed to Chris Do below train the persona.

Neville reframes copywriting as "communicating a message from my brain to your brain" using
the best available medium (text, image, GIF, video), distinguishes direct-response copy
(tied to client revenue) from award-chasing agency creative, argues most sites just need
"fresh eyes" and clearer headlines rather than heavy split-testing, teaches his "STUPID"
newsletter format (Swipe, Thought, Uplifting Picture, Interesting, Drawing), embraces
AI copy/image tools as amplifiers, and closes with stories about dropping out responsibly
and crashing charity parties to network. Chris's role is mostly interviewer, but he
contributes his own self-description and a substantive pitch for live-audio platforms.

## Key claims (2022-10-25)

**Neville Medhora (guest / context — DO NOT TRAIN into persona):**
- Copywriting = communicating a message brain-to-brain with the best medium of the era, not
  just typing clever words; text, images, GIFs and video are all "copy."
- Direct-response copy is judged on revenue lift (his incentive: pay him $10k, he makes the
  client $100k); big-agency creative is often incentivized toward awards, not sales
  (credits David Ogilvy).
- Most improvement comes from looking at a page with "fresh eyes" and making headlines
  clearer/less jargon-y; a failing product usually has a deeper problem than the copy.
- He will bluntly tell paying clients their product/persona is the problem; has turned
  people away from copywriting toward what they're actually good at.
- Split-testing is overrated for most: run an "AAA test" (same subject line twice) and you
  see natural variance; sender reputation matters more than subject lines; open rates stay
  in a narrow band (~28–38%) regardless of subject.
- Information is compressing (2-hour doc → 5-min YouTube → 20-sec Instagram); short/medium
  articles now beat 10,000-word ones; long B2B cold emails underperform short ones.
- The "STUPID email" newsletter = **S**wipe, **T**hought, **U**plifting **P**icture,
  **I**nteresting, **D**rawing — a repeatable format "backed into" an acronym; converts
  because it's uniquely him. His homepage GIF converts 10–12% and beats everything tested.
- AI copy tools (invested in Copy.ai; mentions Jasper/"Jarvis," DALL·E 2) are amplifiers —
  fear of them is "a loserish way of thinking"; embrace the tool to serve a million instead
  of 10,000.
- Career/mindset asides: drop out only once the side thing is already big ("if you can't do
  it on the side you probably can't do it full time"); stay "10–30% discontented"; crashed
  charity parties to network into rooms he couldn't afford.

**Chris Do (host — TRAINS persona, attributed lines only):**
- Self-description of what he does: *"I tell them I'm an educator and content creator and I
  help creative people who have business problems"* — but notes that saying "graphic
  designer" is punchier/more concrete than the vague "educator" framing (echoing Neville's
  specialize-don't-be-vague point).
- Strongly advocates **live audio** (Twitter Spaces, LinkedIn audio events, Clubhouse) as a
  format: "there's not a lot of places for you to hide" — people of substance get exposed
  as great, pseudo-coaches with copy-pasted formulas get exposed as frauds quickly.
- Reports his community's largest growth period came through live audio: the Pro Group went
  from **under 300 to over 670 members**, many of whom joined via live-audio sessions and
  stayed.
- Offered to co-host a Twitter Space / LinkedIn audio room for Neville and walked through
  the mechanics (pin links, submit pages in advance) to make copy critique work in audio.
- Framing/summary lines he supplies as interviewer: marketing makes good products/services
  perform even better but isn't for rescuing poor ones; the shift to shorter content is
  about "utility to user," not just a Google rule.

> Note: Chris's line "advertising doesn't reflect culture, it shapes culture" is him
