# Win Customers' Hearts With Data (Masterclass w/ Neil Hoyne)

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do (host, @thefutur) interviewing **Neil Hoyne** (guest — Google
Chief Measurement Strategist, Wharton senior fellow, author of *Converted: The
Data-Driven Way to Win Customers' Hearts*). This is a **guest masterclass**, so the
substantive frameworks below are **Neil's and are context / do-not-train**. Only
Chris-Do-attributed lines train the persona.

Neil's thesis: digital marketing has become disconnected from the real person on the
other side of the transaction — "a lot of head, not a lot of heart." He reframes
customer relationships as **dating/courtship** (don't propose to the first person you
meet; don't blast retargeting ads the instant someone leaves the site — wait, like you
would before calling after a first date). He champions **customer lifetime value (CLV)**
as an individual-level prediction of how valuable each relationship will be, buildable
from a simple spreadsheet (name + predicted future value, then add columns for
acquisition channel, product, engagement, and group to find what drives value). His
operating maxims: **"be better, not perfect"** (the bear-chasing-campers parable — you
only have to out-run the other campers), start from a **hypothesis** not a data dump,
and set the **burden of proof / decision criteria BEFORE running a test**. He's skeptical
of machine-learning-as-magic (the "would you jump off a cliff too?" joke; ML is biased
toward the average and toward its training data), argues awareness of one's own bias
(not more data) is what actually changes minds, and closes on social media: chase people
who *act*, not vanity metrics, because the rules for volume differ from the rules for
real relationships.

Chris mostly hosts, reflects, and stress-tests Neil's ideas against his own business —
but he contributes several genuinely-his data points and practices (below).

### Neil Hoyne — CONTEXT / do-not-train

- Over-reliance on dashboards/likes disconnects marketers from the real person; marketing's
  core problem for 25–100 years is being unable to prove it works.
- Marketing today = proposing to the first person you meet at a bar and counting yes/no;
  real purchases take 3–4 interactions. Retargeting the instant someone leaves *lowers*
  intent; waiting 2–3 days (like calling after a date) improves responsiveness.
- **CLV** is an individual-level prediction of each relationship's future value; it needs
  little data (past spend + timing) and no expensive ML. Build a spreadsheet, add columns
  (acquisition channel, first product, gift-vs-not, mobile-app-or-not), group, and see what
  drives value. Real examples: first-purchase **gift** buyers have higher 12-month CLV;
  some channels bring customers who spend 20–40% more on repeat; a mobile app with equal
  CLV means it's just an alt channel, not a relationship-builder.
- **"Be better, not perfect."** The bear parable: you don't out-run the bear, you out-run
  the other campers. Online ad auctions are won by being slightly better-informed, not
  perfectly informed. Start from a hypothesis; only bring data you can *act* on.
- More data rarely changes minds — people just grow more confident in their first view.
  What changes minds is **awareness of one's own bias** (survey-order effects; anchoring;
  McDonald's-ad conditioning). 
- ML skepticism: "would you jump off the cliff too?" — ML is biased toward its training
  data and toward the average; demand to test/validate any "proprietary black box." But
  ML is genuinely useful where validated (Google Ads bidding, self-driving, recommendation
  engines) — and Chris's own title-intuition could itself be turned into a model.
- Set the **decision criteria / burden of proof before the test**; most org disagreements
  come from having no agreed threshold. Rigorous academic tests need years and repeatability;
  businesses can't wait — pick a methodology matched to reversibility and stakes.
- **Ask customers more questions** — put them on the *thank-you page* (peak trust, right
  after payment); rotate them; but always know what you'll *do* with the answer.
- Social media rewards **quantity over quality**; conform to each platform's rules to win
  reach, but the rules for real relationships differ. Chase people who **act** (buy, click,
  follow the next step), not vanity metrics — EA's best-download ad drew the *worst-spending*
  players (98% of mobile revenue comes from ~0.2% of players).
