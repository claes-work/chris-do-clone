# 3 Pricing Strategies - How To Price Your Service

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do — solo, direct-to-camera teaching video (monologue). Confirmed by
self-reference ("you're probably asking yourself well Chris how much more should I
charge…") and autobiographical claim ("I used to run a design firm that did somewhere
between four to six million dollars every single year"). This is Chris-attributed
material and IS voice/training data.

Chris lays out a progression of pricing models for creative agencies and freelancers,
framed as a career-stage evolution: as you take on more risk and move closer to the
client, you should move up the pricing ladder. He walks three primary models — (1)
hourly/day-rate (input-based, for freelancers/temp employees), (2) fixed fees /
project-based (output-based, for the independent "agency of one"), (3) outcome-based /
value-based pricing (charging on the value/percentage of value created) — and adds a
fourth, more advanced model: royalty / commission / profit participation. He uses a
wheat-farmer-to-blueberry-pie supply-chain analogy to explain why removing middlemen and
owning more of the production pipeline earns more (but inherits more risk), and gives
worked value-based examples (an apparel-brand logo priced on usage/licensing; a 25%
conversion lift on an e-commerce sales page priced as a percentage of the $250k net
revenue created).

## Key claims (dated 2022-11-17)

The three strategies (plus a fourth), in Chris's stated progression:

1. **Hourly / day-rate pricing (input-based)** — for the freelancer, defined as a
   "temporary employee" booked for a period of time. When new, ask the booker "what do
   people like me charge people like you." Raise your rate as you demonstrate higher
   output (example: 250/day → 350/day after showing you produce twice as much), and
   raise by ~$50/day or $10–20/hour each new studio you work with. **Problem:** you only
   make money when working, and you're incentivized to work longer, not more
   efficiently — do all the work and they just give you more, so people "quiet quit" /
   artificially slow down. There is also a market ceiling (unlikely to hit ~$1,000/day)
   where you get "priced out."
2. **Fixed fees / project-based pricing (output-based)** — for the independent business
   owner / "agency of one" working directly with clients, having removed the agency
   middleman. Itemize deliverables, estimate hours × your rate, then **add padding**
   (projects go sideways). Clients EXPECT it to cost more than hourly because hourly
   forces them to manage/police your time; a fixed fee transfers the risk to you, and
   "whoever takes on the risk makes more money." Client no longer expects YOU personally
   to do the work, so you're incentivized to be efficient, hire good people, and upgrade
   equipment. Chris's rule of thumb: charge **10% to 100% more** than your estimated cost
   (10k of man-hours → charge 20k, "in fact… a lot more"), scaled to the customer's
   appetite, business health, and perceived value.
3. **Outcome-based / value-based pricing** — charging on the value created for the
   client, not the artifact. Learn from illustrators/photographers who ask "what is the
   usage / what rights do you want?" and **license** the work (they don't even transfer
   ownership) by estimating impressions and market reach. Worked example: a 25% lift on
   an e-commerce page (1,000 → 1,250 sales/month, $100 profit each = $25k/month new net
   income → $250k over 10 months); negotiate a percentage of that value (10%, 20%, up to
   80%), e.g. a flat $25k–$50k fee to capture ~10–20% — "a good deal every day of the
   week."
4. **Royalty / commission / profit participation (advanced, 4th model)** — charge
   nothing up front and collect a percentage of every sale, for a period or in
   perpetuity. Only for well-run companies with good products and fair, honest people.
   Chris would **not** build an entire agency on this — it's "a bit of a Gamble" with no
   immediate cash; keep 1–2 such "partners" alongside more reliable models. Uses the word
   "Partners" deliberately (success is genuinely tied together).

Supporting claims:
- **Supply-chain / margin principle:** the more people between the maker and the client,
  the less the maker earns; own more of the pipeline (farmer → mill → wholesaler → baker →
  slice of pie) to reap more profit, but you inherit customer service, inventory, and
  risk. General markup rule cited: "whatever you buy you charge twice as much."
- **Career progression is explicit:** "at some point in your life you need to graduate
  from charging hourly." Once you're an independent studio, continuing to bill hourly is
  called a "mistake" — you've acquired too much risk.
- **Autobiographical:** his firm did $4–6M/year, "for the vast majority of that time we
  did fix fees," and evolved to make pricing **opaque** (deliverables + terms & conditions
  instead of itemized line items) because agency-buyers just flip to the bold grand total.
- **Terms & conditions attached to fixed fees:** e.g. 3 rounds of revisions max
  (revisions = tweaking, not starting over), 9–5 Mon–Fri, project closes if the client
  delays it more than ~2 weeks (rebid).

**Coverage note (vs. existing pricing pages):** cost/hourly and value-based pricing are
already covered in the wiki. What this source adds that may be genuinely new: (a) the
explicit **four-tier progression tied to career stage and risk transfer** (input → output
→ outcome → profit-participation) as one clean ladder; (b) the concrete **10%–100% markup
heuristic** for fixed fees; (c) the **licensing/usage/impressions** framing borrowed from
illustrators as the on-ramp to value-based; (d) the **wheat→pie supply-chain analogy**;
(e) the **"make the estimate opaque"** tactic for agency clients.

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (apparent, likely resolved by staging, not conflict): Chris presents
> hourly/day-rate pricing as a legitimate model here, which sits against his frequently
> stated position that value-based pricing is the current/ideal. In THIS source he
> reconciles them explicitly as a **career-stage ladder** — hourly is the entry stage for
> freelancers/temp employees, and one should "graduate" to fixed fees then value-based as
> risk and independence grow. He also says you can run a very successful agency on fixed
> fees alone, and that a mix of models (not 100% profit-participation) is healthiest.
> Verify against the pricing topic page that "value-based is current" is stated as the
> *destination* of a progression, not a blanket condemnation of ever charging hourly.

## Notable verbatim quotes (Chris Do — voice data)

- "As many people between the person who makes the work and the client, the less money
  you make. Farther you are away from the client… because everybody in the production
  pipeline or chain has to make some money."
- "Whoever takes on the risk makes more money."
- "The client already knows this and assumes you're going to pad the budget a little bit…
  because if you were to charge them hourly they have no control or transparency over
  what's happening, so what they do is they're going to manage your time — and it's not
  what they want to do."
- "At some point in your life you need to graduate from charging hourly so that you can
  move into more fixed fees or project-based pricing or pricing based on outputs."
- "I used to run a design firm that did somewhere between four to six million dollars
  every single year, and for the vast majority of that time we did fix fees."
- "This is where illustrators, artists and photographers have already figured out
  something that designers haven't… they asked us one question, which is what is the
  usage? What kind of rights do you want to own? … to them they don't even own it. It's a
  wild concept that designers can't figure out."
- "The conversation should be around what percentage feels fair based on value created. I
  want to capture some of that… you can get up to 80 or whatever number you can negotiate."
- "They're looking at spending 50 or 25 to gain 250 — it's a good deal every day of the
  week. That's called value-based pricing."
- "We're Partners is used very deliberately here, because you're actually in partnership
  with them: their success and your ability to make money are tied together. That's a true
  partnership."
- "One is we can charge by hour — pricing based on inputs. Number two, we can do fixed
  fees — we're going to price based on output. Model number three — we're going to charge
  based on outcomes."
