## Key claims (2022-11-23, paraphrased)

Guest-attributed (context / do-not-train):
- Start by defining the core problem your business solves, then refine the goal before
  listing solutions; many clients (e.g. startups) lack brand direction and don't know where
  they're heading.
- List solutions in a doc, then map each to a user type / buyer persona and the audience you
  want to reach.
- The solution is not always the mirror-opposite of the problem; a visible symptom (e.g. lack
  of diverse stock illustration) often stems from a larger, systemic cause ("you don't know
  what you don't know"), so consider whether the real fix is bigger than the obvious one.
- As a business owner, start with low-hanging fruit (the obvious near-term fix) and expand the
  problem/solution scope as you move forward.
- To determine target audience, analyze the product/service across three questions: what need
  do you fulfill, what problem does it solve, and who benefits (illustrated with a Lego
  example — the buyer, the child, and the parent all benefit).
- Founders/agencies/freelancers make too many assumptions and don't ask enough questions; ask
  the right questions to make informed decisions.
- Concrete method: collect audience requests via a website form (name, email, business, desired
  illustration details) piped into an Airtable database, then mine keywords — e.g. of ~500
  form fills, 45 mentioned "healthcare" → prioritize a healthcare illustration pack. Repeatedly
  releasing packs built from the most-requested keywords (education/STEM packs, because many
  users turned out to be educators) reliably lands because the majority already asked for it.

Chris Do-attributed (interviewer):
- Reframe the problem before solving it: people are frustrated *waiting* for the elevator, not
  that the elevator is slow — so the elegant solution is mirrors/artwork in the lobby to
  distract them, not a faster motor. Sourced to the book *What's Your Problem?*
