# 5 Ways to Find Success & Passion in Life

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do (solo talking-head, first person throughout). Verified via
autobiographical detail — Chris recounts his own early career: his student work was
featured on Adobe After Effects software (one of five students), which generated his
first freelance leads from motion-design studios in Los Angeles where he lived. No
guest, no team member. This is voice/persona training material.

A career-direction monologue aimed at young people (early 20s, "out of school trying
to escape the nine-to-five rat race") who want to work remotely and participate in
the digital economy but don't know what to do. Despite the title's "passion" framing,
the actual content is a pragmatic 5-step operating procedure for **picking a direction,
reverse-engineering a role model, and acquiring a valuable skill** — not a discourse on
passion or purpose per se. Passion is treated as something that follows competence and
momentum, not something you locate first.

## Key claims (2022-11-25)

Chris's 5-step framework for finding direction and success:

1. **Identify a goal / direction.** Pick something you'd still enjoy in 3–5 years — it
   doesn't have to be forever, but you need to commit for a period long enough to build
   competence. You won't develop skill or do the necessary learning work if you don't
   enjoy it now. Find clues by looking at people online who live the life / make the
   money you want.
2. **Validate the market by counting competitors.** Quickly search how many people
   already do this. Cites **David C. Baker**'s book *The Business of Expertise* — you
   want roughly **15–25 people** competing for the exact thing: enough to prove a viable
   market, few enough that it isn't generic. Warns against chasing a "one-of-one
   unicorn" whose success rests on rare attributes (height, looks, charisma) you can't
   replicate — be honest about whether you share those conditions. "Graphic designer" is
   too broad (hundreds of thousands); narrow it.
3. **Reverse-engineer one role model's success.** Cites **Jim Rohn** — "success leaves
   clues." Crawl all over one person's accounts: LinkedIn, schooling, work history,
   credentials, website, podcasts, video interviews, TED talks. Then **write down 5
   things** contributing to their success — specific but broad enough to be actionable
   (not "be tall"). Honestly assess the gap between where you are and where they are.
4. **Prioritize the 5 things easiest → hardest, and start at the bottom (easiest).**
   Do the low-hanging fruit first to get early validation and momentum so you don't quit.
   Break each of the 5 into sub-steps if needed, and check things off as you go.
5. **Invest money, time, and resources to acquire the skills.** "Ultra successful people
   with high net worth recommend this." Low end = audiobooks/books; go deeper via the
   author's digital course (a couple hundred bucks), small mastermind, or live coaching.
   Books are "pound for pound, dollar for dollar the cheapest and highest ROI."

Supporting sub-claims:

- **Syntopical reading:** read 5 *different* authors on one topic (not 5 books by the
  same person) to compare/contrast viewpoints and form your own opinion. Spot
  **universal concepts** (repeated everywhere — note them as settled) vs. **contrasting
  views** (argue both sides, then adopt one or a hybrid). This is how you become an
  **autodidact** — "learning how to learn."
- **Absorb, don't just finish.** Read to the point you could *teach* the material —
  "that's a level of mastery." Chris tests people by asking one thing they applied from
  a book; many give a "deer in the headlights" blank stare. Rejects the "fast food
  model" of reading in favor of the "slow digestion model" — margins, highlights, ask
  "how could I apply this / who else could benefit?"
- **Be patient.** Valuable skills people pay for take time to master — that's *why*
  they're valuable (scarce supply vs. demand). Acquiring them is literally personal
  development: self-confidence, self-worth, self-esteem rise as you develop yourself.
- **Opportunities materialize while you're still learning.** As your public profile
  rises (e.g., building a website, posting your work, becoming a TEDx speaker), people
  discover you and inbound opportunities appear. Illustrated with Chris's own After
  Effects feature → LA studio freelance leads.

**On passion/purpose vs. prior coverage:** This video does **not** add a genuinely new
passion/purpose theory — passion is barely defined and mostly implied as a byproduct of
picking a tolerable direction and building competence. The framework's *distinctive*
contributions are the **David C. Baker 15–25-competitor rule** and the concrete
**reverse-engineer-a-role-model → 5 things → easiest-first** procedure, which are more
specific/operational than generic "find your passion" advice. Flag for synthesis:
the "start with the easiest step for momentum" heuristic and the 15–25 market-viability
number are worth checking against existing mindset/business pages for reinforcement or
contradiction.

## Notable verbatim quotes

All Chris Do (voice/persona data):

- "It's important to pick a direction at the beginning and to be able to do it for some
  period of time, because it's going to be difficult for you to develop any kind of
  competence or skill … if you don't enjoy it right now."
- "As Jim Rohn says, success leaves clues."
- "You want somewhere between … 15 to 25 people who are competing for this exact thing.
  This lets you know it's a viable market … but there's not so many people doing it that
  it becomes super generic." (attributing the idea to David C. Baker's *The Business of
  Expertise*)
- "I would just start at the bottom, I would just go with the easiest thing to do …
  because in the very beginning it's going to be difficult and you need to get some
  validation and some momentum … so that you don't quit."
- "Don't read five books on marketing from the same person — read five different books
  from different people about marketing, and then you can figure out what works for you."
- "What's really important here is not only that you read the material, but you absorb
  the material as if you could teach the material — that's a level of mastery that you
  want to get to."
- "Books, I think, are pound for pound, dollar for dollar, the cheapest and the highest
  ROI return on investment that you can have."
- "Don't do the fast food model … do the slow digestion model, which is taking a little
  bit at a time."
- "This is why it's called personal development, because you're developing yourself."
- Autobiographical: "When I got out of school I had this foolish notion that all I had
  to do is graduate and opportunities were going to come and find me — that was not the
  case … one of the things that did help me generate some of the first leads … my work
  was featured on Adobe After Effects software … there were five students who were
  featured on that and I was one of five … I lived in Los Angeles and a lot of motion
  design studios were here … they just started to look me up and call me."
