# How I Scaled My Creative Agency To $80M Business

Solo first-person monologue by **Chris Do** (2022-12-01, @thefutur) narrating how he
scaled his design studio [[../entities/blind|Blind]] and freed himself from working
inside it. Core teaching: the **"I do → We do → You do"** delegation/mentorship model,
illustrated by a running case study of a designer named **Tom** and the documentary
project *Dogtown and Z-Boys*. Figures are Chris's own — see self-reported flags below.

## Summary

Chris Do frames entrepreneurship as fundamentally an act of **teaching**: your success is
determined by how well you can transfer your values, principles, decision-making, and
creativity to others. He argues most creative founders are a bottleneck because they work
by instinct ("secret sauce") and never learn to translate that skill to others — so if
they were "hit by a bus," the company would collapse. He uses Steve Jobs / Apple as the
counter-example of a company that kept growing after its founder's departure because the
knowledge was transferred.

The bulk of the video walks through a three-phase model borrowed from teaching but applied
to business management:

1. **I do** — the trainee shadows you, observes, takes notes, says nothing (even sitting
   silently on client calls). Debrief afterward to explain the *how* and *why* behind
   decisions.
2. **We do** — a hybrid stage: introduce the trainee to the client as the lead (e.g. art
   director), let them field questions, gauge temperament and how they handle adversity.
   Reassure the client they are not being handed to a "B team."
3. **You do** — the trainee becomes the main point person with full autonomy; the founder
   deliberately stays out to avoid undermining their authority, offering only optional
   weekly/bi-weekly office hours.

The payoff Chris describes is being able to delegate whole clients and projects, get
himself out of day-to-day delivery, and reach the point where clients would forget he was
ever involved or would praise the trainee directly. He closes on the theme of "cloning
yourself" so hires share your core values, and the classic reframe of working *on* the
business instead of *in* it.

The video is a solo educational monologue; the opening question ("How are you able to
scale something that seems unscalable...") is Chris self-posing the frame rather than an
interviewer. No co-host is present. There is a mid-video subscribe/comment ask.

## Key claims

Dated 2022-12-01. Paraphrased unless quoted. Chris's own numbers marked `[self-reported]`.

- **[self-reported]** Chris scaled his agency "from zero to 80 million dollars" and this
  process freed him from working inside the business. The $80M figure is his own,
  unverified — treat as self-reported (billings/revenue framing unspecified).
- Entrepreneurship is, more than anything, **teaching**: how well you transfer your
  values, principles, decision-making, and creativity determines how successful you are.
- Most creative founders are a **bottleneck** — they have a "secret sauce" they run on
  instinctively and don't know how to translate/transfer to others.
- A well-run company should survive its founder; Chris cites Steve Jobs / Apple as
  succeeding at knowledge transfer (Apple kept growing after his departure), versus a
  founder whose company "would fall apart instantly" if they left.
- Founders should focus on their **"number two,"** teaching decision-making by having them
  shadow, then debriefing and "scaffolding" them toward the founder's way of seeing.
- Chris's stated "modus operandi from the jump" was to get **other people to do what he
  was doing** — he built up his design studio with many creative directors this way.
- Hiring approach: he brought in **raw talent** that didn't know his way, then found the
  single simplest task to offload (iterating on ideas, research), gave feedback, and
  leveled people up designer → art director → creative director, adding responsibility at
  each step.
- Candidate criteria for the "I do" stage: driven, proactive, takes direction well, good
  listener; PMs/producers should be organized and detail-oriented; junior designers should
  already be proficient with tools (Adobe Creative Suite — learnable via videos/courses,
  which Chris says he has no time to teach) and know design fundamentals (typography,
  composition, photo compositing, retouching).
- In the "I do" stage the trainee's only job is to **observe** — instruct them to say
  nothing, which he says actually *lowers* their anxiety; do a debrief afterward covering
  "what was said versus what was meant."
- In "We do," reassure a nervous client with a script: *"I'm putting [person] in charge
  because I trust him, he's my right-hand person and has been running this project behind
  the scenes the entire time..."* Do this 2–3 times across a few projects/clients before
  moving on.
- In "You do," don't get involved — it undermines the trainee's authority and makes
  clients route around them back to you; replace check-ins with optional weekly/bi-weekly
  office hours for coaching on pricing and client challenges.
- The "best case" outcome: clients **forget who Chris was**, or go out of their way to say
  how much they enjoyed working with the trainee.
- Closing thesis: to be a good entrepreneur you must be a great teacher and effectively
  **"clone yourself"**; done right, hires share your core values, otherwise you stay a
  "company of one."

### Biography / Blind facts surfaced

- Chris ran a **design studio** (his studio [[../entities/blind|Blind]]) that he scaled
  with **many creative directors**, using the "I do / We do / You do" model to build up his
  team. `[self-reported]`
- **Tom** — described as a hire from "many years ago," a new graduate from **Art Center**
  (Art Center College of Design), entered the company as a **junior designer**. Chris
  describes him as well-rounded: could illustrate, do 2D animation and even 3D, knew art
  and design history, articulate, hardworking, ambitious. Tom was progressed through the
  model to art director and became the client-facing lead. (Third party — context, not the
  subject.)
- **Project case study:** Chris's studio designed the **main title design** for the
  documentary film ***Dogtown and Z-Boys***, directed by pro skater **Stacy Peralta**. In
  the "We do" stage Tom was introduced to Peralta as the art director; after the initial
  design exploration was approved by Peralta, Chris did the "official handoff" naming Tom
  the point person, and eventually Tom and Peralta dealt directly as good friends.
  (Registry note: *Dogtown and Z-Boys* is a real 2001 Peralta documentary — the film and
  director are verifiable; the studio's title-design credit and Tom's role are as
  Chris tells it here.)
