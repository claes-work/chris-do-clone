# 5 Sales Skills & Tips To Close More Sales

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do** — a single-speaker monologue delivered straight to camera (self-references confirm it: he recounts a workshop attendee saying "Chris, great workshop, when are you gonna hire me," and he narrates "why is Chris talking so fast at me right now"). This is **persona/voice data** (Chris speaking in his own voice; no guest or co-host).

Despite the title's "5 Sales Skills," the video is framed as **"the five biggest mistakes I see people making on a sales call"** and teaches each skill as the inverse of a mistake. The through-line: before a client will hire you, they need to feel *recognized and understood* — so slow down, ask and listen before advising, don't rush the close, filter for fit, and control your tone. Most of the content restates frameworks already in [[wiki/topics/sales-clients/sales-clients]] (§2 discovery/listening, §9 qualifying, §13 closing discipline); the **tone-of-voice / late-night-FM-DJ-voice** material (mistake #5) is **genuinely new** to the wiki.

## Key claims

All dated **2022-12-08**, all Chris Do, all paraphrase unless quoted below. The five mistakes (and the skill each implies):

1. **Not asking enough / asking the wrong questions** — people rush to *advise* and "become an expert," but "no one likes to talk to an expert." Before hiring you a client needs to feel: "do you recognize me, do you understand me, do you understand my problem, my wants and needs, my fears and hopes." A **bad question is leading/binary** ("Would you like to eat spaghetti for dinner?" — presumes hunger, dinner, spaghetti, and pressures a yes); a **good question is open and directed at what you want to learn** ("If you were to have dinner tonight and hungry, what kind of food would you prefer?"). Keep asking until it's "super clear." *(Restates §2 discovery — open-question technique; NB the "there ARE dumb questions" line inverts the usual platitude.)*

2. **Asking questions but not actually listening** — "listening is an art and most of us are really poor listeners." His concrete technique **contradicts the standard eye-contact advice**: instead of holding eye contact, **keep a notebook and write down what they say** — it signals "your words matter so much I must scribe them." Then **play it back / paraphrase using their own language** ("What I heard you say was you need X by Y and you have a problem with C — is that correct?"). Getting it slightly wrong is *useful* — it lets the client correct you so you don't build on wrong assumptions. He rates these first two skills as the essential pair: "if you are able to listen and ask better questions you don't even need to hear the other three tips." *(Extends §2 "full-value listening / play back"; the take-notes-instead-of-eye-contact framing is a sharper, near-new articulation of the [[human-tape-recorder]] idea.)*

3. **Desire to close too early** — having an agenda and being "so eager for the business" tells the client you're not really listening or solving *their* problem. Illustrated with the workshop attendee who joked "when are you gonna hire me?" — "honey, take me out on a date first… I didn't even know I liked you." Selling too early "in life and in relationships and in business… says I'm desperate and I'm self-interested." *(Aligns with §9 "the ask = advance to the next step, not the close" and the §12 anti-desperation posture.)*

4. **Having no filter** — "anybody with a heartbeat and some money to spend you'll take on as a client." You must be able to tell a prospect they're a good or poor fit. Qualifying conditions to accept the money: *can you deliver value; would it give you joy; do you like this person; are the budget and timeline appropriate.* The need to "keep the lights on" often overrides good filtering, which signals "I have no standards… probably someone you can't trust." **A filter is also a positioning tool**: the highest-paid, most sought-after people are highly specialized and use "sometimes artificial and real filters or barriers," creating wait-lists that make clients want them *more* — "this person doesn't need me… yet they still want to work with me, I trust them a little bit more." *(Restates §9 qualification triad — like / can-do / can-afford — and adds the scarcity/positioning angle from §6/§12.)*

5. **Using the wrong tone of voice** — **GENUINELY NEW to the wiki.** Most people are not mindful of tone in everyday conversation. Talking fast raises the listener's heart rate and reads as anxious/desperate ("is the house on fire, is he in a rush"), which signals *don't trust me*. He cites **Chris Voss's *Never Split the Difference*** and the **"late-night FM DJ voice"**: speak in a slow, low register, take lots of breaths — it calms people down (demoed with a smooth-jazz "cruising down the PCH" bit). Corollary: a slow calm voice de-escalates an angry person; speeding up and raising pitch builds excitement. Bottom line for sales: "if there's anxiety, if there's stress, people are less likely to trust you and therefore less likely to buy from you." *(New material — no tonal-control / Chris-Voss / vocal-register content elsewhere in the sales hub as of this ingest.)*

**Contradictions:** none with prior sources. Note the friendly tension with the general "there are no dumb questions" cliché — Chris explicitly rejects it ("despite what your third grade school teacher told you… there are such things as dumb questions").

**Coverage note (per ingest brief):** The **SALES acronym** (Serve/Ask/Listen/Empathize/Summarize) is *not* named here, but mistakes #1–2 map onto its Ask/Listen/Summarize beats (already covered §1–§2, 2023-12-23). **Serve-don't-sell** is present implicitly (the "recognize/understand me" premise) but the phrase isn't used. **Objection handling** is *not* covered in this video. So the only net-new concept is **tonal control (#5)**.

## Notable verbatim quotes

All Chris Do (2022-12-08) — voice/persona data:

> "No one likes to talk to an expert, because before our client will hire you they need to understand: do you recognize me, do you understand me, do you understand my problem, do you understand my wants and needs, my fears and hopes?"

> "Despite what your third grade school teacher told you… there are such things as dumb questions. How do I know? Well, I get dumb questions all the time."

> "Instead of making and keeping eye contact… keep a notebook and write down what they're saying. This signals to the client I'm paying careful attention… your words matter so much I must scribe them."

> "What I heard you say was you need X by Y and you have a problem with C — is that correct?"

> "If you are able to listen and ask better questions, you don't even need to hear the other three tips."

> "Honey, take me out on a date first. When am I gonna hire you? I didn't even know I liked you, I didn't know I was hiring, and I don't even know what you're qualified for." *(on trying to close too early)*

> "When you try to sell too early — in life and in relationships and in business, all things are the same — it says I'm desperate and I'm self-interested."

> "Anybody with a heartbeat and some money to spend, you'll take on as a client. It's really important for you to be able to say you're a good fit for us or you're a poor fit for us."

> "By being more selective and being more discriminatory, you're going to create the design of the client's mind that this person doesn't need me… yet they still want to work with me. I trust them a little bit more."

> "Chris Voss talks about in his book *Never Split the Difference* about the late-night FM DJ voice: speak in a slow, low tone of voice, take lots of breaths, and it'll calm people down."

> "If there's anxiety, if there's stress, people are less likely to trust you and therefore less likely to buy from you."
