## Key claims

_All dated 2022-12-15. Paraphrased unless quoted. Attributed to **Chris Do**; book
authors credited as the source of the underlying idea Chris is drawing from._

- **[Chris, from Blair Enns — Win Without Pitching]** Positioning matters more than
  pitching. Creatives should replace pitching with conversations and position
  themselves as experts / equals to their clients, not chasers. (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, from Blair Enns]** "Retreat and follow": try to kill an engagement up to
  three times to test the client's commitment — because pursuing/chasing clients reads
  as desperate, and desperate people rarely win business or are treated fairly. You
  half-step back by asking simple questions ("why proceed?", "I'm not sure you can
  afford us") so the expectation becomes that YOU are pursued. (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, personal]** He owned the book but delayed reading it for a long time
  because he assumed the title was clickbait (pitching is "required" in the commercial
  business) — and now warns the longer you wait, the longer you suffer unnecessarily.
  (2022-12-15) `(self-reported)`
- **[Chris, from Michael Bungay Stanier — The Coaching Habit]** Leaders should be
  slower to give answers. Giving answers steals people's thinking, autonomy, and sense
  of accomplishment, and makes the leader the "choke point" of all progress — you'll
  suffocate because you can't answer everyone. Stay curious longer; ask a few more
  questions to help people find the answer themselves. (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, from Bungay Stanier]** The book's seven questions are meant to be asked in
  a specific order; the opening question is "What's on your mind?", followed by the
  "AWE" question — "And what else?" — to push past surface answers. Chris says these
  questions apply broadly to sales and client relationships, not just management.
  (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, personal — experiment]** He ran a sales call with his coaching community
  where he "turned off his listening brain" and only asked Bungay Stanier's questions
  in sequence; participants were blown away by how insightful the conversation was —
  evidence of how powerful the questions are. (2022-12-15) `(self-reported)`
- **[Chris, from Kevin Daley — Socratic Selling]** The most effective, useful selling
  book he's read. Socratic selling = asking lots of questions (Socrates' rhetoric) to
  help the client focus on what they actually want, rather than pushing your prepared
  presentation. (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, from Kevin Daley]** There are only a few ways to make more money. Most
  people wrongly focus on cutting costs (what an accountant advises) — cutting cost
  adds profit but limits resources and doesn't grow the business. The two real levers
  are being better at **marketing** and better at **selling**; these are key skills for
  entrepreneurs. (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, from Kevin Daley]** Open with a question, not the presentation: even when
  invited in to present, ask "before I get into it, what are you most interested in
  hearing from us today?" (a longer form of "what's on your mind?") so you address the
  client's real problem and can edit — or skip — the presentation. (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, from Kevin Daley]** "Relive the past before you rush the future": revisit
  and highlight the pain around the client's problem, because humans pursue gain and
  minimize pain — reliving the pain makes them more acutely aware of why they need you.
  (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, from Kevin Daley]** Listen and play back: summarize the key points you
  heard and confirm ("it sounds like... is this right?") before asking for the sale.
  "A recorder remembers everything, forgets nothing, but understands nothing — you are
  not a recorder." (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, from Kevin Daley]** Use a hypothetical/Socratic close that removes you from
  the sale and avoids the tension of a direct yes/no: "if this problem were solved this
  way, you'd be willing to spend north of $200k... is that correct? If someone could
  solve this for you, would you move forward happily?" Then secure small agreements
  ("I'll get your proposal by end of business tomorrow, okay?") to strip friction out of
  the sales process. (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, from Jim Rohn — Seven Strategies for Wealth & Happiness]** Chris cites Jim
  Rohn to trace the "genealogy of ideas" (a concept he attributes to Austin Kleon) back
  to the source: Rohn was Tony Robbins' mentor, and is cited by Darren Hardy (_The
  Compound Effect_) and Brian Tracy. Chris prefers going to the source material.
  (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, from Jim Rohn]** The book is a "framework for life" — understanding what
  season of life you're in — rather than a list of action items. Chris retells Rohn's
  origin story (dropped out of college, broke, lied to a Girl Scout because he couldn't
  afford cookies, vowed never to be in that position again, then learned business
  principles from a mentor, Earl Shoaff — transcript: "Earl Scheib"). (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, from Marty Neumeier — The Brand Flip]** The paradigm has flipped: we think
  brands create customers, but Neumeier argues **customers create the brand** — you can
  no longer control your brand, only influence it. When we buy things we're joining a
  tribe, seeking identity and meaning; understanding how tribes move lets you tap trends
  that power a brand to the next level. (2022-12-15)
- **[Chris, bonus books]** Three additional recommendations: _Delivering Happiness_
  (Tony Hsieh, Zappos founder); _The One Thing_ (Gary Keller — read it "if you need
  focus" and want to be more productive); _The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F*ck_ (Mark
  Manson). (2022-12-15)
