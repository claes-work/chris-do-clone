# Marketing Vs. Advertising — Email Marketing Strategy

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do (verified — first-person throughout: "I signed up for outside
magazine," "my wife totally on the opposite side," and the running cart-abandonment
anecdote where the retailer emails "hey Chris"). This is Chris teaching a live audience/
class, so the material **trains the persona**.

Chris draws the distinction between marketing and advertising: advertising is only one
(over-dominant) segment of marketing, and most people default to a "really bad version of
advertising" when they post online — content that reads like an ad or a self-promotion.
Leaning on Seth Godin's *This Is Marketing* and *Permission Marketing*, he reframes
marketing as a generous act of helping others get what they want, and email/social as a
permission relationship built on value delivered first. He works the room with audience
examples (Outside magazine, Ulta loyalty discounts) to extract the mechanics of good
permission marketing — anticipated, relevant, value-before-ask — then closes with a
"study the pros" method: treat your own inbox as a free marketing master class.

## Key claims (2022-12-19, paraphrased, attributed to Chris Do)

- Marketing is not advertising; advertising is just one segment of marketing, and
  unfortunately the most dominant one — so people wrongly assume everything they do
  online should look like an ad. (paraphrase)
- Because most people aren't good at advertising, their online posts become a bad
  version of it — content that either tries to sell or tries to promote the self, which
  is "the problem." (paraphrase)
- Cites Seth Godin's *This Is Marketing*: marketing is "a generous act of helping others
  get what they want," without expectations. Social media marketing is "not a license to
  spam." (paraphrase + attributed quote to Godin)
- Email has lost much of its effectiveness as a communication tool because inboxes are
  full of spam; the fix is to appeal to the reader's self-interest — "what's in it for
  me / help me achieve my goals." (paraphrase, crediting Godin)
- The internet, unlike radio and TV, was the first mass medium not designed to sell
  advertising; marketers "ruined" it by spamming, and people then emulate that spam
  because we are products of our environment. (paraphrase, crediting Godin)
- Godin's *Permission Marketing*: audiences volunteer into a long-term relationship where
  they get increasing value over time. (paraphrase)
- Permission marketing requires messages that are **anticipated, relevant, and personal**
  (Chris surfaces two of the three criteria — anticipated and relevant — via the Outside
  magazine example). (paraphrase)
- Sequence and proportion matter: lead with value, then make the ask. If the ask comes
  too early (too far up the email) the whole thing feels like a turn-off; if the "value"
  isn't genuine, people see right through it. (paraphrase; drawn out from audience answer)
- People love to buy but hate being sold to — a paradox; good content (e.g. gear
  recommendations) helps people buy without selling to them, and then they just buy.
  (paraphrase)
- Loyalty/discount rewards work only when the discount is on things the customer
  genuinely wants; discounting things they don't want is worthless. (paraphrase)
- Buying "hack" / marketing lesson: sign up with your real email, leave an item in your
  cart, and let the automated sequence come to you (reminder → scarcity nudge → discount
  + free shipping). Buying on the seller's terms means full price; buying on your terms
  means full price minus their margins. He frames this not to buy more but to **study how
  professional marketers market to you**. (paraphrase)
- "Success leaves clues" (Jim Rohn): keep a running document of the marketing that
  works on you and the marketing that annoys you — copy, paste, screenshot — and within a
  short time you get a free master class in marketing. (paraphrase, crediting Jim Rohn)
