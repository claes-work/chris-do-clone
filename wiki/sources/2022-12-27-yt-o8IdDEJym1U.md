# 5 Money Rules To Make More Money In 2023

Chris Do (solo) shares five money lessons he says he learned in his early 20s that
allowed him to build an "$80 million creative agency" (self-reported), framed as
actionable rules for creatives, freelancers, and solopreneurs.

★ **L3 candidate** — solid, dense wealth/money-philosophy feed. Rules 4 (money
conversation) and 5 (invest in yourself) are strong belief material for `beliefs.md`,
and rule 2 (project fees over hourly) reinforces existing pricing beliefs.

## Summary

The five money rules:

1. **Delegation** — give away non-essential work so you can scale beyond a solo operator.
2. **Shift from hourly to project/value-based pricing** — sell outcomes, not units of time.
3. **Focus** — narrow your offering; reduce the "area" so your force can break through.
4. **Learn to have the money conversation** — talk about budget early and unashamedly; use price bracketing / anchoring.
5. **Invest** — in people, tools, and above all in your own marketable, compoundable skills.

## Key claims

All claims paraphrased from Chris Do, dated 2022-12-27, unless quoted.

- **(Rule 1 — Delegation)** Creatives trade time and skill for money, but time is finite; working weekends and long hours eventually leads to burnout (Chris says this happened to him). The only way to scale beyond a solo operator is to delegate. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 1 — hiring)** To find a first hire, list your essential vs. non-essential work across all the roles you play; you'll realize work you thought essential is not. Chris was hired for design, but design turned out to be non-essential — he could hire lower-skilled people, generate ideas, and elevate their work through art direction until it "would pass as my own." [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 1 — what's actually essential)** The essential role is owning the client relationship. Clients hire people they trust to get the work done on time and on budget; they care less than you think about who actually does the work. As long as you deliver to your own standard, you can hire others to help. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 2 — pricing)** Shift from hourly to project fees. Selling units of time says your time is the valuable thing, but clients don't care how long it takes — they care about outcomes/results. Selling time invites the client to manage your time (the overbearing-client image), and creates anxiety whether you're fast or slow. Move toward project or value-based pricing. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 3 — focus)** Creatives like variety and are taught to show breadth ("soup to nuts" service), but breadth makes you hard to market and hard to build expertise/authority. Chris originally did logo, brochure, animation, and editing — saying yes to everything — and never felt confident because he was always doing each thing for the first time. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 3 — origin story)** A "holy F" moment came on his first film-title job — main titles for the movie *Eraser* (Arnold Schwarzenegger), for client Kyle Cooper / R/Greenberg. The supervising animator sent to help showed him basic things he should have known but didn't, because he was spread too thin. That night he told his girlfriend (now wife) that motion design was the field to focus on; his company **Blind** dropped all other work and focused only on motion design in year one. [paraphrase; company/film names may need verification]
- **(Rule 3 — the principle)** Chris attributes "making the difficult business decision" to Blair Enns. He frames focus via physics: pressure = force / area; reduce the area (number of things you focus on) and the same force produces a powerful breakthrough. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 4 — the money conversation)** Creatives find talking about money awkward/taboo (seen as bragging, greedy, selfish); but "those who don't talk about money don't make it." Learning to have the money conversation can be transformative. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 4 — stress principle)** Stress is caused not by the things you say but by the things you don't say (illustrated by the "friend Jimmy who overstays" analogy). Delaying the money talk creates the most stress. In any buyer/seller transaction, both need to know the price/budget — you can have the best portfolio and charm, but none of it matters unless the client can afford you and the solution matches the size of the problem. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 4 — anxiety kills deals)** Dragging out the conversation while "building rapport" actually creates anxiety, and "anxious people don't buy" — at least not at a high price. Being good at talking about money is a sign of good business acumen, not poor manners. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 4 — price bracketing)** When a client deflects ("you're the professional, you tell us"), say: "For clients like you and for projects like this, we charge somewhere between X and Y." Give a wide range with numbers far apart. Example: if the most you've ever been paid is $20,000, quote "between $20,000 and $35,000." Even if they hire you at the low end, price anchoring makes them feel they got a bargain. [paraphrase; figures per transcript]
- **(Rule 4 — reverse anchoring, "black belt")** Level up by stating the higher number first, then pausing, then the lower: "we charge anywhere between $35,000… [pause] …and $20,000," so the client is relieved by the lower figure. We are contextual learners (Goldilocks) and only judge size/fit through contrast. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 4 — three-tier pricing)** This is also why companies (famously Apple) offer good/better/best three-tier pricing: Chris claims ~80% of people pick the middle option, rationalizing that the cheapest is too weak and the most expensive is too much. Bracketing or tiered options let the client decide to work with *you* rather than comparison-shop against someone else. [paraphrase; ~80% figure is Chris's estimate]
- **(Rule 4 — bonus, small projects)** For small projects where unqualified clients keep knocking, raise the money question as early as possible, right after greetings: e.g. "before we go too deep, I need to make sure you have somewhere north of $15,000 to spend on a project like this," so a bad fit can exit early. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 4 — bonus, large/undefined scope)** For massive projects with undefined scope, don't price on the spot — use a "general order of magnitude" bid (Chris notes the military uses the term), giving numbers in 10x steps: "we can give you a website for ten thousand, a hundred thousand, or a million — whereabouts are you?" then stay quiet. Narrow from the client's answer (e.g. closer to a million vs. $100k → "$800k range"), and note that CMOs/branding officers often have a discretionary spend limit (e.g. under $850,000) below which they don't need board approval — so bidding under that limit avoids committee involvement. [paraphrase; figures per transcript, illustrative]
- **(Rule 5 — invest)** To make money you have to spend/risk money. Chris cites Sean Cannell ("scared money don't make money") and Peter Drucker ("in business all profit comes from risk"), adding that profit comes from taking on risk, taking on responsibility, and creating value for others. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 5 — invest in people & tools)** A service company is nothing without people, so invest profit into hiring, training, systems, and processes. Buy the best computers/equipment you can afford for your team — a great ROI. Running a 20–30 person agency on outdated, janky equipment with shared licenses artificially caps human potential ("take the Ferrari out but let the air out of the tires"). Good environments and tools increase the team's joy and quality of work. [paraphrase]
- **(Rule 5 — invest in yourself)** The biggest lever is investing in yourself. Chris cites Alex Hormozi: after living expenses and rent, invest as much as possible into your own self-development until you make so much money it's no longer possible. Acquiring marketable, compounding skills "raises the basement" of your value — a larger cushion to fall back on. This kept Chris in the game through the dark days of entrepreneurship, because he knew his market rate ("$400–600k a year") was a fallback he could always return to. They can take your house, cars, and equipment, but not the skills you keep "up here." [paraphrase; Hormozi's stripper/Uber fallback anecdote is retold second-hand]
