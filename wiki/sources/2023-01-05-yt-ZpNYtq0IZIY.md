# You Are Not Selling A Commodity, You Just Lack Imagination

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do — CONFIRMED (trains persona voice).** A solo keynote excerpt,
delivered live at **Adobe Max** to a room of creatives ("I only came to Adobe Max with
two things, truths and candy"; "my wife shops at Gelson's"). First-person throughout,
no second speaker; the crowd is addressed but only Chris speaks.

The talk's thesis: there is no such thing as a commodity — only a lack of imagination.
Chris opens on the fear he hears constantly ("I can't charge more because there's too
much competition"), names it as *acting like a commodity* which forces you to compete on
price ("a race to the bottom"), and contrasts it with being a **differentiated business /
a business of one** — "a designer versus THE designer" — which commands a price premium.
He builds the argument on **Ron Baker** (*Implementing Value Pricing*: "there's no such
thing as a commodity, there's just lack of imagination"), illustrating it with a head of
lettuce ($1.69 → hand-leafed/pre-washed bagged → Wolfgang Puck Caesar salad kit ~$11.99,
same lettuce, more branding + imagination), and with the idea that you can even "charge
more for money" — an autographed/framed single share of blue-chip stock (worth ~$99) sold
as a $150 framed gift for grandparents. He closes on **Peter Drucker** ("a business has
one valid purpose: to create a customer") and a challenge to the audience: if you're
stuck and undifferentiated, it's because you *volunteer* to be seen as a commodity — a
head of lettuce no different from the next — and "undifferentiated companies die."

This reinforces **existing** doctrine (branding Framework 29 — positioning increases
value; commodity vs. differentiation; the Marty Neumeier "radically different → command a
premium" line) and pricing's race-to-the-bottom / value-pricing material. It is **not a
new framework**, but it adds three fresh, quotable illustrations (lettuce, framed-stock,
"charge more for money") and an explicit Ron Baker / Peter Drucker citation chain that
the corpus can use as voice/teaching material.

## Key claims

Speaker: **Chris Do** (CONFIRMED). Paraphrased unless quoted.

- `[2023-01-05]` When you feel like and act like you're a commodity, you compete on
  price — and that is "a race to the bottom." (paraphrase) *(reinforces pricing doctrine)*
- `[2023-01-05]` The fear "I can't charge more because there's too much competition" is
  especially strong for creatives in developing countries; Chris acknowledges the
  privilege in a blanket "charge whatever you want" but still holds the position.
  (paraphrase) *(nuance worth noting — he concedes context before insisting on the
  principle)*
- `[2023-01-05]` There is a categorical difference between being "a designer" and "THE
  designer" — a one-of-one, highly differentiated ("radically different," crediting
  **Marty Neumeier**) business commands a price premium. (paraphrase) *(reinforces
  branding Framework 29 / positioning)*
- `[2023-01-05]` In every marketplace there are brands that are commodities and brands
  that command a premium because they're highly differentiated; the premium is
  psychological (community/tribe, being seen and understood — ties to Maslow's hierarchy
  and self-actualization / social status). (paraphrase)
- `[2023-01-05]` "There's no such thing as a commodity — there's just lack of
  imagination." Credited to **Ron Baker**, *Implementing Value Pricing*. If you can't
  command the money, respect, and control you want, "it's probably because you're a
  commodity, and I'm gonna say you lack imagination." (near-quote / paraphrase)
- `[2023-01-05]` The lettuce example: a $1.69 head of lettuce is a commodity; hand-leaf
  it, pre-wash it three times, bag it, and it sells for ~$4 (and yields 2–3 bags); add
  plastic, croutons and dressing under the Wolfgang Puck name and the *same* lettuce
  sells for ~$11.99. The added price is "a little branding, a lot of imagination."
  (paraphrase) *(new illustration; structurally identical to the Rolex/Timex point in
  [[wiki/sources/2018-11-09-yt-ODcdMUgsxMI]])*
- `[2023-01-05]` You can even "charge more for money": a single framed share of blue-chip
  stock (Disney, ~$99) is sold for ~$150 as a keepsake gift because grandparents give it
  to grandchildren — "the same commodity of currency" made differentiated by imagination.
  (paraphrase) *(new illustration)*
- `[2023-01-05]` **Peter Drucker**: "a business has one valid purpose, which is to create
  a customer" — the framed-stock company succeeded by *creating* a customer
  (gift-giving grandparents) for a thing nobody obviously wanted. (paraphrase, Drucker
  credited)
- `[2023-01-05]` "Undifferentiated companies die because there's a race to the bottom,
  and there's a lot of you at the bottom." If you're stuck, ask whether you're *really*
  stuck or just lack imagination; being seen as an interchangeable commodity is something
  you "volunteer for." (paraphrase + quote)
