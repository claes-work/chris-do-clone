### Dakota Robertson (guest) — context, DO NOT TRAIN

- **(Viral formula)** A viral tweet needs four elements: make the benefit huge, make the
  perceived effort very low, keep it relevant to the target audience, and get reach/distribution.
  Example: big benefit = "make you more money than a four-year degree"; low effort = "47
  sentences." Best content with no distribution doesn't matter. [context]
- **(Headlines)** Get very good at headlines — if the first line doesn't capture attention,
  nothing else gets read; study the great copywriters. Target a benefit ("making money"), not a
  feature ("47 marketing tips") — people want the end result. [context]
- **(Simple language)** Write so a fifth-grader or below could understand; replace big words
  ("confused" not "non-plussed," "use" not "utilize"). Uses ProWritingAid and the Hemingway app
  to check grade level. Confused readers move on to the next tweet/TikTok. [context]
- **(Storytelling)** Use stories — people are emotionally moved by them; "if people wanted
  information there's Google." Dakota says this is an area he's still developing. [context]
- **(Concision & mechanics)** Cut anything that doesn't move the message forward — you're
  competing for attention. Use "you/your" to talk *to* the reader, not at them. Use numbers
  (especially odd numbers like 3, 5, 7) plus dollar/percent signs as a "pattern disrupt" that
  stands out in a wall of text. [context]
- **(Funnel philosophy)** Twitter/social is top-of-funnel; give people what they *want* to hear
  (more basic) to pull them into your ecosystem, then raise their level of awareness more
  intimately via newsletter or podcast. Overwhelming cold readers with advanced concepts loses
  them. [context]
- **(Fitness → long game)** Dakota started working out to escape being overweight and socially
  anxious; saw no results for ~5–6 months, then noticed abdominal definition and formed the
  belief that a 1% gain from sustained work is worth it — you must have a long-term vision.
  Same pattern on Twitter: only 750 followers in the first three months, then he switched to a
  writing-focused account and hit 1,000 followers within 40 days. Consistency matters — but
  consistency *at the right thing*, using environmental feedback to adjust rather than "hitting
  your head at a wall." [context]
