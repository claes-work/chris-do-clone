# Hire Better: Ask This One Question To Find The Perfect Match

## Summary

**Speaker:** Chris Do (subject) — voice/beliefs training data. Chris is answering an
interviewer's (a team member/host) questions about how he recruits and vets people. He
speaks in the first person about his own agency (Blind), his "business boot camp
communication module," and real client-overage conversations he has had. The
interviewer briefly plays the candidate role (answering Chris's dinner-bill scenario);
that portion is context, not subject material.

Chris explains his interviewing method for **management/leadership hires**: instead of
trusting resumes, portfolios, or referrals, he poses open-ended ethical dilemma
scenarios ("Scruples" questions with no right/wrong answer) and watches how the
candidate reasons and communicates in real time. The goal is not a correct answer but
finding the person whose **values most closely align** with his and who will
communicate/decide the way he would. He demonstrates with a signature scenario (the
expensive celebratory dinner where the bill comes in 10x higher than expected) and
narrates what each type of answer reveals.

## Key claims (all dated 2023-01-10, paraphrased unless quoted)

- Chris distinguishes **"process people"** from **"non-process people"**: process people
  excel where there's a clear manual/decision flowchart but freeze when a novel
  situation has no playbook; non-process people assess and decide on the fly. He wants
  non-process people for **management positions**, and process-oriented "worker bees"
  for refining/execution — a team needs a combination of both.
- For creative/worker-bee roles he hires on portfolio + vibe ("you do great work, I like
  you, we vibe, let's try"). For **management roles** he can't judge from a portfolio, so
  he needs a different vetting method.
- His stated view: **resumes are BS, referrals are bunk** (candidates just list friends,
  or wouldn't list someone who dislikes them), and stated "best trait" answers are what
  the candidate thinks you want to hear — all unreliable signals for management hires.
- **The method (the "one question" framing):** design open-ended ethics/morality
  scenarios with no right answer; ask which character in a story the candidate is; run
  **four or five** scenarios per candidate. People can't prepare for or hide from them.
- **Scoring:** score each of the ~5 scenarios 1–10 (max 50). He doesn't demand a 49–50;
  he hires the candidate who scores highest relative to the pool and is most likely to
  grow/adapt. "You will never find the perfect candidate" — set an impossible bar and you
  hire no one.
- What answers reveal (from his dinner-bill demo): "taking it on the chin" signals someone
  uncomfortable surfacing hard things and who'd let clients walk over the business — not
  who he'd hire to handle money/frontline. Candidates who **rewrite the scenario**
  ("that wouldn't happen because…") are in denial and he doesn't like it — "you can't
  unspill the milk."
- **Emotional-overwhelm disqualifier:** he's had candidates cry during scenarios; showing
  emotion is fine, but being so overwhelmed you can't process the situation is
  disqualifying — "what the heck am I paying you for?"
- Hiring principle restated: **slow to hire, quick to fire** — don't hire off resume,
  portfolio, or looks; run the scenarios first.
- He grounds the technique in a real practice: his "perfect answer" to the dinner scenario
  (take responsibility, name your embarrassment, speak to feelings, state the reason, say
  what you'd like to happen, release them from obligation) is the same **business boot
  camp communication module** he uses in real **client overage / budget-change
  conversations** ("revision 742," renegotiating current and future project rates).
