# The Creator's Blueprint for Building a Brand in the Attention Economy

## Summary

**Speaker:** Two speakers. The **primary presenter is Alex Antolino** (guest) — a
creative director-turned-creator who built the branding for Typeform and its VideoAsk
product, and now helps creators build brands and brands engage like creators. Alex
delivers the bulk of the material (the "attention is the new oil" thesis, the Creator
economy overview, the "seven Creator virtues," and an implementation template). **Alex's
material is CONTEXT — do-not-train.** Chris Do is the co-host: he interviews Alex, reacts,
and interjects several of his own extended teachings. **Only Chris Do's attributed
contributions are voice/beliefs training data.**

The video is a conversation about how brands can behave like creators and creators build
brands in the modern attention economy: low-production, relatable, high-volume content
beats expensive polished campaigns; word-of-mouth "at scale" via TikTok/Shorts/Reels; and
practical steps for a company to start (hire/partner with a mid-size creator, run a
content system, pitch it internally as a small experiment against existing ad spend).

## Key claims (all dated 2023-01-20, paraphrased unless quoted — ATTRIBUTED per speaker)

### Alex Antolino (guest — CONTEXT, do-not-train)
- "Attention is the new oil"; data matters only because it is used to capture attention,
  which is used to change behavior (credits Alex Hormozi for a video on this).
- The core problem stopping brands from doing organic content is **measurement/attribution**
  ("what doesn't get measured doesn't get improved"), so companies keep buying paid ads.
- Traditional advertisement (one big-budget agency spot) is being replaced by "Creator
  advertisement" (the same budget distributed across many creators) = **word-of-mouth at
  scale**, which TikTok normalized during the pandemic.
- Creator brands are built on **characters and stories, not "why"/purpose** — relatability
  over aspiration, connection over excellence, decentralized over centralized, interactive
  over one-directional; "it's not about design anymore, it's about video."
- The **"seven Creator virtues"**: authenticity, relatability, collaboration, interaction,
  spontaneity (reaction videos), video, storytelling — illustrated with Ryan Trahan, Mr
  Beast, The Cheeky Boyos, Alfred Coffee, and the Poppy Shark-Tank TikTok (49M views, sold
  out in 24h).
- His own proof point: an iPhone-shot, zero-production trailer for a music masterclass
  (Aulart) beat the polished ad **7x on engagement and 3x on click-through at $0 cost**.
- Implementation: shift creative teams from campaigns→volume, design→video,
  excellence→optimization, one-directional→interaction. To get internal buy-in, ask a boss
  to reallocate **5–10% of existing paid-ad budget** to a 2–3 month creator experiment
  ("would you be **willing** to experiment…" — the word "willing" makes it hard to say no).

### Chris Do (subject — VOICE/BELIEFS training data)
- **Technology convergence in production quality:** high-end (Ridley Scott / Michael Bay)
  production and what an average person ("your mom") can shoot on a phone are converging;
  Mr Beast's videos reflect what a common person can do with a point-and-shoot today, but
  as viewing standards change, high-end and consumer output will "start to look very close
  to each other." Chris frames watching what audiences actually want to consume (YouTube)
  as the "due diligence" of brands and creative directors.
- **Overproduced content signals manipulation:** "we don't trust things that are
  overproduced — we feel like we're being manipulated… versus we're just peeking into
  something that's happening in real life." Retrained instinct: younger audiences reject
  polish.
- **Hiring/collaborating with creators (Chris's practical rules):** give them money, don't
  make it too serious, and **let them do their thing** — brands that micromanage (demanding
  scripts and storyboards) kill the creative process; the brands that give Chris full
  latitude get his best work. Trust goes two ways. Before partnering, **dig into a
  creator's past** to check values alignment (e.g., heavy bad language) rather than hiring
  their charisma and then trying to "excise" parts — "a horrible way to work." "Find
  somebody you like, someone who you trust, and let them play."
- **First metrics for anyone starting content = execution, then output, then numbers:**
  month 1 measure only time/execution spent making content (not output); once that's a
  habit, move to output (e.g., one piece a week); only later look at performance numbers,
  because "you have zero control over the algorithm… the one thing you can control is
  actually getting started."
- **Learning the craft compounds / speed increases with reps:** cites Matthew Encina (The
  Futur's former content director) whose own YouTube channel hit 100K subscribers "orders
  of magnitude quicker" than The Futur took to reach 100K — evidence that a second/third
  channel is much faster once you know the tactics.
- **Attention economy case studies (Chris teaching):** Mr Beast leveraged attention (12-hr
  PewDiePie countdown → Mr Beast Burger, briefly the #1 downloaded app; reportedly turned
  down a ~$1B buyout); Reese Witherspoon's book club (~2.5M members) + Hello Sunshine
  production company (sold for ~$900M) as attention-as-kingmaker; also Conor McGregor
  (Proper 12), Dwayne Johnson (tequila), Kardashians. Endorses "attention is the new oil…
  100%." He's more skeptical Mr Beast is a great *storyteller* — "what I know he is great
  at is capturing and managing attention."
- **Content as research:** Chris agrees content is a way to validate ideas by reading how
  audiences respond (aligns with Alex's "use content as research").
- **Biographical/dated data point:** The Futur's YouTube was at **2.02M subscribers**, with
  Chris naming **3M** as the next milestone goal (Jan 2023).
