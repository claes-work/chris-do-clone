# How to Script a YouTube Video That Keeps 'Em Hooked

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do** (verified — subject-attributed, persona-trainable). This is a solo
teaching clip in Chris's own first-person voice: he references "our retention graph,"
his own creative decisions ("the call to action that I ask"), and points to The Futur's
own deeper podcast conversation with Brandon Kane. Despite the caution that
YouTube-scripting tutorials are often team/guest-led, this one is unambiguously Chris
teaching. Safe to train.

Chris lays out his method for scripting mid-to-long-form YouTube videos so viewers stay
past the make-or-break first 30 seconds. The spine: front-load a hook (big rhetorical
"why" question or pattern interrupt), use "storytelling is delaying" to hold tension,
optionally add a *soft* call to action, then get straight into the what/how and deliver
real value — closing with a light, non-salesy CTA. He illustrates with Simon Sinek's TED
Talk, Brandon Kane's Instagram videos, and a viral "red umbrella / raining cash" video.

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-01-24, paraphrased, attributed to Chris Do unless quoted._

- The first 30 seconds are the most important 30 seconds of a video; retention graphs
  show a massive drop-off there, and if you lose 60–70% of viewers immediately you only
  keep ~30% — which is why views stay low. (Chris Do)
- Watching is sequential and compounding: you can only reach minute three if the viewer
  survives minute one, so spend disproportionate time, energy, and even props on the
  first 30 seconds. Treat it like a mini teaser trailer for the rest. (Chris Do)
- "Storytelling is delaying" — deliberately withhold the answer to build tension so the
  audience *needs* to keep watching. (Chris Do; he credits reading a book on this.)
- Open with a big, usually rhetorical question starting with "why" to create a pattern
  interrupt and hook people (e.g., "why do some companies outperform others?"). (Chris Do)
- A second hook technique: state something the viewer believes is false, get them to
  watch, then bridge to your real topic — but use it sparingly, or audiences learn the
  formula and dismiss it as clickbait. (Chris Do, citing Brandon Kane)
- Suspense plus "changing the stakes" can carry an entire video with almost no content —
  the red-umbrella/raining-cash video reached ~700,000 views purely by drawing viewers to
  the end. Weird works too, as long as it sustains attention to the finish. (Chris Do)
- After the hook you can insert a *soft* call to action (e.g., "like and comment, it helps
  the algorithm") — not a hard pitch — and these reminders tend to help videos perform.
  Stealth/"soft" signals (wearing a certain hat) are another form. (Chris Do)
- Then move straight into the topic — the what and the how — and "deliver the goods";
  this is where value is actually delivered. (Chris Do)
- Be careful with end CTAs: a hard sell ("buy my product," "enroll in this bootcamp")
  negates the goodwill you just built and erodes trust once viewers realize they're being
  sold to. (Chris Do)
- But with zero call to action you'll probably make no money — so use one occasionally.
  Appropriate CTAs are audience-serving: subscribe, turn on notifications, or listen to
  the related podcast. (Chris Do)
- Reverse-engineer high-performing content: "whenever you see a hot piece of content,
  consume it, inhale it, break it down, reverse engineer what works." (Chris Do)

## Notable verbatim quotes

_All spoken by Chris Do, 2023-01-24._

- "The first 30 seconds are the most important 30 seconds of the video. You know why? If
  you lose them here you have no chance of watching the rest of the video."
- "Storytelling is already delaying, because we want to create the tension about what
  happens."
- "Spend as much time, as energy as possible writing, crafting, creating, building props
  for the first 30 seconds to hook the person in."
- "Whenever you see a hot piece of content, consume it, inhale it, break it down, reverse
  engineer what works."
- "You got to use that pretty sparingly … or every video is the opposite. I don't believe
  you anymore, it's just clickbait."
- "If you have zero call to action you'll probably make no money."
- "[A hard pitch] kind of negates the goodwill that you just created."
