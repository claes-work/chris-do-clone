# How to Get Paid More & Reduce Competition — Niche!

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do** (solo teaching to a live audience). This is a canonical Chris Do
lecture segment on the broad-vs-specific / niching debate — the voice, the "symmetry of
logic" framing, the creative-as-unicorn dig, and the doctor analogy are all classic Chris.
Subject-attributed → **trains the persona.**

Chris makes the case, "hopefully for the very last time," that specialization is a strength:
the more specific / niched you are, the more you can charge and the more you command a price
premium; the broader you are, the more commoditized you become and the less you make. He
sketches a ladder from generic to hyper-specific ("human → I design things → graphic designer
→ logo design → logo design for food & beverage → logos for food-delivery apps") to show that
specificity is a dial you can keep turning. He grounds it with a real-world medical analogy
(general physician vs. leukemia specialist) and ties the pricing outcome to scarcity: fewer
people can claim a narrow position, so it is valuable, because valuable things are scarce.

## Key claims

All claims dated **2023-01-26**, attributed to **Chris Do** (paraphrase unless quoted).

- **Broad = commoditized = less money; specific/niche = premium.** The broader you are, the
  more people can claim the same thing and the less you can charge; the more specific/niched
  you are, the more you can charge and command a price premium. [paraphrase]
- **Specificity is a ladder, not a binary.** You can always get more specific: human → "I do
  design / I design things" → graphic designer → logo designer → logo design for an industry
  (e.g. food & beverage / hospitality) → logos for food-delivery apps. Each rung down is more
  targeted and more valuable. [paraphrase]
- **"Symmetry of logic" — apply the rule to all cases.** Chris urges the practice of testing
  whether a principle that's true elsewhere should also be true for you: if specialization
  raises value in medicine, law, etc., it should be true for creatives too. Creatives resist
  this because "we think we're unicorns" who are exempt from the rules. [paraphrase — this
  "symmetry of logic" label/framing is a notable articulation]
- **Medical analogy: general physician vs. leukemia specialist.** A GP will "mostly refer you
  out" until you reach the specific thing they treat; the specialist owns that one ailment.
  The specialist faces far less competition and is more highly valued. [paraphrase]
- **Scarcity drives value.** "Valuable things are scarce; scarce things are valuable." A narrow
  niche means fewer people can claim it, which makes it valuable. There might be only ~3 people
  in the country known for one specific disease/ailment. [paraphrase]
- **Authority comes from doing one thing for a long time.** People become well known by doing
  one narrow thing for a long time — that's how the top specialists got their reputation.
  [paraphrase]
- **You want to be the specialist, not the generalist, when stakes are high.** The scariest
  thing to hear from a doctor is "oh, that's new" — you want the person who has seen your exact
  problem many times. (Implicit client parallel: buyers pay a premium for proven specialists.)
  [paraphrase]
