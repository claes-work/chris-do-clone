# Master THIS To Get More Customers (Masterclass 1/5)

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do (the SUBJECT) — trainable.** This is part 1 of a 5-part masterclass
series in which Chris Do teaches "the five core essentials you must master to grow your
[creative] freelance business." The host/interviewer (introduced only as **"Mo"**, a
Futur team member Chris has mentored — non-subject, do-not-train) frames the questions;
Chris does all the teaching. The five essentials are named up front: (1) buying
psychology, (2) sales psychology, (3) pricing psychology, (4) irresistible offer,
(5) attraction marketing. This episode covers essential #1 — **buying psychology** — plus
a long tangent on **focus / niching down** as the mechanism that makes everything else
easier. Standard restatement of Chris's core niching and "current reality → desired
reality" material; no genuinely-new framework, so L2.

## Key claims (2023-02-09, all attributed to Chris Do unless noted)

- **Chris Do:** People struggle to sell because they don't understand *why* people buy —
  they think buying is "receiving a service and paying a price," and miss the point.
- **Chris Do:** Brands are a relatively modern concept. For most of human history survival
  meant no choice; industrialization and rising incomes created *choice*, and with choice
  we ask why we prefer one option over an equivalent one.
- **Chris Do:** We pay more than a cheaper alternative because a purchase signals identity —
  "it says something about who we are, our beliefs, our values, our taste" and lets us
  join a tribe (Audi driver, Porsche driver, etc.). Buying is a search for meaning and
  identity.
- **Chris Do:** The fundamental concept UX designers have long understood — each person has
  a **current reality** and a **desired reality/future state**, with tension/a gap between.
  Your job: **reduce the pain, increase the gain**, and provide a bridge across the gap.
- **Chris Do:** Most businesses only think about what *they* want to do; they're good at
  problem-*solving* but bad at problem-*finding*. No solution is inherently good or bad —
  it depends on the problem and the person seeking it.
- **Chris Do:** To get more business you must **narrow your market** — counterintuitive but
  "emphatically yes." The clearer you are on exactly who the person is, the better you can
  predict their wants, needs, pain and joy, and the easier solutions become.
- **Chris Do:** On the "I'll make less money serving fewer people" limiting belief — if what
  you're doing is already working, don't change it; check your balance sheet, progress,
  team and joy as evidence. He's teaching precisely because he wants those who've plateaued.
- **Chris Do:** Beware mistaking a gut feeling for evidence-based/critical thinking. (Illustrated
  with the "highly paid executive assistant sent to the store with no brief" analogy — too
  many options → paralysis; a specific brief lets you eliminate 99% of the store.)
- **Chris Do:** The instinct to answer fast instead of asking questions is a byproduct of an
  education system that rewards fast answers (gold stars) and punishes clarifying questions.
- **Chris Do:** **FOCUS = Follow One Course Until Successful** — credited to **Aaron Peterson**
  (Chris notes Peterson didn't invent it either). Focus = limiting distractions, concentrating
  energy into one thing.
- **Chris Do:** Magnifying-glass metaphor — the same sunlight that doesn't burn you can start
  a fire when concentrated. From **Allan Dib's** book: "a thousand-watt light bulb will
  illuminate a room, but a thousand-watt laser will punch a hole through steel." (Attribution:
  Allan Dib, via Chris.)
- **Chris Do:** A tight creative brief (clear parameters/checklists) is *liberating*, not
  stifling — it lets a designer focus and play. Too many checklists stifle; too-open
  parameters cause wheel-spinning. **Dr. Holtzman** (a consultant Chris hired): a great brief
  is like a **parabolic lens** — all energy in gets redirected out in one direction.
- **Mo (host, do-not-train):** "Simplicity scales, fancy fails" — offered as a remembered
  phrase (Mo doesn't claim to have coined it); jumping between niches forces you to rebuild
  processes and adds operational complexity.
