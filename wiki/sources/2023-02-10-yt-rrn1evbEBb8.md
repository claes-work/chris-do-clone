# Why You Should Always Bet on Yourself

## Summary

**Speaker:** This is a two-person interview. **Chris Do** (the SUBJECT) hosts and
interviews **Vince Labon** — a Mauritian-Australian footwear designer and founder of the
brand **Roley / Rolly Nation** (called "Roley Nation," not "Roley shoes," deliberately, to
signal an experience brand rather than a shoe company). Vince tells his personal story
(first-generation immigrant childhood in Melbourne's western suburbs, dropping science for
art, getting into a post-grad course straight out of high school, building his footwear
brand, winning an Asics/Foot Locker design competition and a reality-TV shoe-design show).

> Vince gets an entity/context mention if ever needed; he is not the subject.

The episode's throughline — "bet on yourself / chase happiness over money" — is largely
Vince's lived story, which Chris draws out and then reframes through his own lens on
mindset, branding, and the cost of not living your dreams.

## Key claims (dated 2023-02-10)

**Chris Do — attributable (persona-eligible):**

- **The real cost is not living your dreams, not the money.** Chris reframes career risk as
  a cost-benefit analysis where the currency isn't money but "the price you pay for not being
  the person you're meant to be." (paraphrase, Chris's reframe of Vince's point)
- **People don't buy products — they buy meaning and identity.** Chris states this as his own
  framing when probing what a brand's customers are "buying into" (self-expression, identity,
  community). (paraphrase)
- **Commercial-vs-artist friction.** Chris: much unhappiness comes from people who "work in a
  commercial space while acting or thinking like an artist," creating friction between the two
  modes. He draws the distinction between a one-of-one art piece, a bespoke limited edition, and
  a mass-produced product that democratizes access. (paraphrase)
- **Loyalty programs are inverted.** Chris argues conventional loyalty programs make *you* loyal
  to *them* (pressure of expiring points), rather than the brand being loyal to the customer —
  the opposite of what the name implies. (paraphrase)
- **Representation shapes what people believe is possible.** Chris: if you don't see yourself
  represented in professional spheres, it's hard to imagine it for yourself; that self-story
  becomes destiny. (paraphrase)
- **Parenthood should change you.** Chris: "becoming a parent changes you and if it doesn't
  you're not doing it right"; children make you accountable to more than yourself and you can
  model a path for them or damage them by behaving poorly. (paraphrase)

**Chris Do — biographical (no family named):**

- Chris taught for a number of years at a **private arts school** where tuition (as of 2023) was
  ~**$22,000 per semester**; he observed very few Black students and few Latino students, mostly
  Asian (Chinese, then Korean, shifting by decade), and frames lack of representation plus cost
  as a real barrier. (self-reported, dated 2023-02-10)
- Chris owns a pair of Vince's shoes and describes them as extremely lightweight/comfortable.

**Vince Labon — CONTEXT ONLY, do not train (guest's story/claims):**

- Build the business *model* first, not the product; the product is "an outcome of the thinking."
  Compete against yourself, not others (flow state). Use constraints creatively (one base shoe,
  swap colors/materials to multiply SKUs while keeping factory minimums low). "Take every hour
  as a learn, not a loss." Weekly personal goal check-ins with a traffic-light system across
  personal/work/family categories. Launched at a market stall, sold 500 pairs in ~5 weeks.
  (All Vince-attributed — recorded for episode context, excluded from persona.)
