# Super Bowl Halftime Show: Artists Work In Exchange For Exposure

## Summary

The *content* is fully aligned with Chris Do's / The Futur's long-documented "working for

The essay uses the 2023 Super Bowl LVII halftime show (Rihanna headlining) as a case study
in creatives working for free. Its argument: the NFL runs one of the most profitable events
in the world (cited ~$16B in nationwide spending, ~$7M per 30-second ad, ~$3B broadcast
rights) yet pays halftime performers **zero** — the league covers production costs only, no
artist fee — and even recruits dancers as unpaid volunteers under NDA. It walks the history
(1960s college marching bands → 1990s viewership crisis after Fox counter-programmed *In
Living Color* → 1993 Michael Jackson as the pivot to big-name acts) and lands on the lesson
for ordinary creators: "working for exposure" is 99% of the time a bad deal, your only
leverage is the power to say no, and if you are truly one-of-one they will eventually pay
you because that is the only way to get access to you.

## Key claims
_All dated 2023-02-12. Paraphrased unless quoted. These are the narrator's / The Futur's
editorial claims, NOT verified Chris-attributed statements._

- **The Super Bowl is the biggest spotlight for artist exploitation.** Even established,
  chart-topping, Grammy-winning acts (Eminem, Beyoncé, Usher, Coldplay, Rihanna, etc.)
  perform for free — this is framed as exploitation dressed up as opportunity.
- **The NFL pays production costs but no artist fee.** Cites NFL spokesman Greg Aiello: the
  league pays for the halftime production, "there is no fee for the artist"; some artists
  (e.g. The Weeknd, reportedly ~$7M) even fund their own show.
- **Working for exposure trickles down and sets a bad precedent.** If the biggest artists
  work for free, it gives the NFL — and by extension the whole industry — an excuse not to
  pay smaller creatives and dancers.
- **Exposure is never guaranteed.** Even with post-show streaming spikes cited (Shakira
  +230%, J.Lo +335% on Spotify), there is no guaranteed return — a creator is "gambling
  their time," and an off night, wrong audience, or weak performance yields nothing.
- **Free work is a privilege filter.** Only artists who can already afford to work for free
  get the slot, which shuts out up-and-coming artists and narrows what audiences ever hear.
- **FOMO / "someone will do it for less."** There is always another artist willing to say
  yes, but that doesn't make saying yes the right move.
- **Your only power is to say no.** Businesses will almost always try to get the best deal
  for themselves; a creator's leverage is withholding their services.
- **Be one-of-one and they pay eventually.** Framed via Michael Jackson (negotiated a
  $100K charity donation + charity ad time in 1993) and Rihanna (2023 — took no fee but
  signed an Apple TV documentary deal around the performance): if you're irreplaceable, or
  creative about the upside, "where there is a will to make money… there is a way."
- **Verdict: ~99% of the time, working for exposure is a bad idea** — and the Super Bowl is
  no exception, though it's ultimately a personal choice.
