# How To Price Your Services To Make More Money & Give More Value

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do** (co-founder of Blind / The Futur), with brief interjections from
co-host **Mo Ismail** (who asks framing questions and does the wrap-up). This is the
third installment ("core essential 3 of 5": *pricing psychology*) in a five-part
freelance/business series, following Persona/buyer-psychology and handling-the-sale, and
teasing #4 (irresistible offer) and #5 (attraction marketing). Chris does virtually all
the teaching here, so the substance is **subject-attributed and trains the persona.** Mo's
lines are context only.

The talk argues that pricing is a **psychology problem, not a cost problem.** Chris attacks
the **labor theory of value** (which he ties to Karl Marx / Marxism) — the idea that a good
should cost only the necessary labor to produce it — using the TikTok "luxury-wallet
teardown" guy as a foil: deconstructing a $3,500 wallet to a "$275 fair value" *misses buyer
psychology*, because people buy identity, not materials. He then makes the case that **people
*want* to pay more — you just have to give them a reason to**, that **price is a signal**
(low price signals one thing, high price the opposite), and that offering **three options
(low / ideal / premium)** wins because buyers are contextual thinkers who gravitate to the
middle (choice architecture / "Goldilocks"). He closes on **"there's no such thing as a
commoditized service — just a lack of imagination"** (crediting Ron Baker's *Implementing
Value Pricing* and quoting Hayao Miyazaki), and the brick-uses exercise from Alex Hormozi's
*$100M Offers*.

**Relation to existing wiki:** this is **value-based pricing restated**, not new doctrine —
fully consistent with the current [[wiki/topics/pricing/pricing]] hub (no contradiction with
"value-based is current"). The three-options framework is already captured (§5, via Ron
Baker). A few *framings* are comparatively fresh as verbal set-pieces: the **labor-theory-of-
value / Marx critique**, the **fast-fashion pollution** value argument, **price-as-signal**,
and the **Goldilocks / choice-architecture** rationale for the middle tier. Candidate light
promotions, not landmark material.

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-02-23, paraphrased unless quoted, Chris Do speaking._

- **Pricing is psychology, not cost.** The debate over "how to price" inherits an old
  **industrial / labor-theory-of-value** model (associated with Karl Marx / Marxism) — that a
  good should be priced only at the "necessary labor" to create it. Chris rejects this as
  fundamentally flawed because it "fails to understand buyer psychology."
- **The luxury-wallet teardown is the wrong lesson.** A leather-crafter on TikTok
  deconstructs a $3,500 wallet and declares its "fair value" ~$275 (materials + labor + a
  little profit). Chris: this reinforces a flawed concept — it ignores *why* people pay for
  Fendi, Prada, Gucci, Louis Vuitton. "When I buy something I start to form my own identity."
- **Spending is identity / a vote.** Chris quotes (attributing to "Annie Leonard"/Lappé) "how
  you spend your money is a vote you cast for the world you want to live in."
- **Fast fashion as a value argument.** The fashion industry is now the world's #1 polluter;
  fast-fashion brands (H&M, Zara) churn cheap goods shipped country-to-country, an $8 shirt
  falls apart in one wash, and the average garment is worn ~8 times. A well-made, longer-
  lasting garment costs more but reduces footprint — an illustration that higher price can
  mean higher real value.
- **People *want* to pay more — give them a reason.** "People want to pay more for something;
  you just need to give them a reason to." Creatives wrongly assume buyers are motivated by the
  lowest price, triggering a **race to the bottom** (e.g. commenters undercutting an $18,000
  logo down to $400 or $25).
- **Be evidence-based: look at how *you* spend.** Reflect on real-world premium choices —
  reputable/clean LASIK over a discount mall clinic, premium vs. discount bottled water — and
  let that inform how you sell.
- **Price is a signal.** Pricing low signals one thing to the client; pricing high (relatively)
  signals something "totally different." (Chris withholds the full answer for the paid
  workshop.)
- **Buyers are contextual thinkers → offer three options.** Faced with high / mid / low bids,
  people rarely buy the lowest and feel the highest is too premium, so they pick the middle —
  hence triple-bidding, and Apple's mid-tier MacBook/iPhone. "It's very hard for us to make a
  decision absent context." Provide **three options — a low, an ideal, and a premium** — to make
  more money; doing so also **removes the client's incentive to go bid you against other
  vendors** (a single price has no context, so they shop it around).
  > ⚠️ Not new: the three-options framework is already documented in
  > [[wiki/topics/pricing/pricing]] §5, credited to **Ron Baker** (2022-09-22 masterclass).
  > This 2023 telling adds the *choice-architecture / Goldilocks* rationale as the "why."
- **Goldilocks / just-right.** Goldilocks can't choose until she's tasted all three bowls;
  pricing's job is to land the "just right" middle.
- **No commoditized service — just a lack of imagination.** "There's no such thing as a
  commoditized service or product; there's just a lack of imagination." Credited to **Ron
  Baker** (*Implementing Value Pricing*), reinforced by a Hayao Miyazaki quote ("reality is for
  people who lack imagination"). The **brick exercise** from **Alex Hormozi's *$100M Offers***
  (a brick as paperweight, doorstop, etc.) shows that *use* determines value — "not all bricks
  are created equal."
- **Workshop pitch / value-delivery promise.** Chris promotes an upcoming European workshop
  tour and promises to deliver value in "orders of magnitude" over the ticket price — one
  concept could earn attendees "thousands more." `[promotional]`

  Marx, Marxism."
