# Create An Offer Customers Can't Resist

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do (CONFIRMED as the primary/trainable voice).** Episode 4 of a 5-part
series on the "five core essentials" for growing a creative freelance business (prior
episodes: buying psychology, sales psychology, pricing psychology). The host/interviewer
is **"Mo"** (Ⓜ️o Ismail of The Futur) — CONTEXT, do NOT train; his lines are prompts and
worked-example answers, not persona voice. Speaker ID is grounded in Chris-specific
biographical fingerprints: naming his late business mentor **"Kira McLaren"** ("doing good
work is the price of entry to be in business"), having run a **production company making
commercials and music videos** with jobs averaging ~$250K (up to >$1M, low end ~$40K),
judging design shows, and speaking on stages — all documented Chris Do biography.

The episode is a live, unscripted teaching dialogue: Chris walks Mo through building an
**irresistible offer** — defined as an offer so good it removes resistance and compels a
"yes" without pitching. He runs three exercises on-camera: (1) the benefits/consequences
of raising vs. lowering price, (2) the **"10x / divide-by-10"** offer-design exercise
(redesign the same service at 10× and 1/10 the price), and (3) risk/pain reduction. He
explicitly cites and holds up **Alex Hormozi's book "$100M Offers,"** attributing the
value-equation vocabulary (perceived value, effort & sacrifice, "stupid for saying no,"
"an offer so good it sells itself") to Hormozi — states he is unaffiliated and makes zero
money from the mention.

**New vs. already-covered:** the raise-your-prices doctrine and "trading time for money is
a fool's errand" restate existing Chris pricing themes, but this episode adds **several
genuinely-new, Chris-taught constructs** not yet in the wiki: the structured
**raise-vs-lower price pros/cons exercise**, the **10x/divide-by-10 offer-redesign
exercise** ("people just do 10× more of the same — push past the sludge"), the
**"time is money — but the CLIENT'S time, not yours"** reframe (charge for saving the
client's time/effort; reduce delivery time, not price — Amazon example), the
**vertical-integration** insight (McDonald's toy/potato/chicken farms; run a
break-even personal-brand arm that feeds the profitable business), and the resulting
**three-tier price ladder** ($500 / $5K / $50K) that removes the client's need to
triple-bid. This is a strong **L3 candidate** for `pricing/` and `sales-clients/`.

## Key claims

### Chris Do (trainable)

- [2023-03-03] An **irresistible offer** is "an offer so good that it compels the person to
  say yes" — it requires little to no pitching/convincing because it **removes resistance**
  (the tension people feel around a buying decision). (paraphrase + quote)
- [2023-03-03] **"Doing good work is the price of entry to be in business"** (credited to his
  late mentor Kira McLaren). As competition and talent increase, the product being good is
  merely the entry ticket — you can no longer rest on "the work sells itself." (paraphrase +
  quote)
- [2023-03-03] Crafting an irresistible offer is **NOT packaging or "fluffing up" a crap
  product** — it means you have "redesigned / re-engineered the product itself so it doesn't
  matter what the wrapper is." Think radically from the client's point of view; identify the
  top things that create tension in the buy cycle. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2023-03-03] **Price tension follows a "Goldilocks" pattern** — clients balk when price
  feels too high OR too low; it must feel "just right." Two ways to relieve price tension:
  (a) drop price to a no-brainer, or (b) **raise perceived value** — and (b) is where he
  wants to spend the time, because "anybody can tell you to reduce your price to sell more;
  few people can teach you how to raise your prices so you sell less to more qualified
  buyers." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2023-03-03] **The raise-vs-lower price exercise:** list, objectively, the benefits AND
  consequences of raising price vs. lowering price. **Raising** → higher-caliber clients,
  higher perceived value, accountability to a higher standard, more focus per client, room
  in the budget to hire A-players/world-class specialists (status for the client to
  broadcast), more profit → reinvest in equipment/systems/coaching, more runway to say no to
  poor-fit clients, generosity to team, quality of life. **Lowering** → a few benefits (more
  demand, bigger accessible market, volume) but many consequences (can't afford
  subcontractors, do all the work yourself, less time/attention per client, more mistakes,
  no help if you get sick, become "the McDonald's" of your service, burnout, alienated
  family/"workaholic"). Conclusion: "the benefits are few, the consequences are many."
  (paraphrase)
- [2023-03-03] **The higher the price, the less competition; the lower the price, the more
  competition you invite** — charge $100 for a logo and you compete with Sally the hobbyist
  and Jake the design freshman, and you've "educated your customer to buy based on price, not
  value." (paraphrase)
- [2023-03-03] **Reduce risk to strengthen the offer:** "the higher the risk, the worse the
  offer." Increase the *perception* of likelihood of success ("it's a perception, it's not
  real — if they believe it will work, that reduces buyer tension"). Use **assurances** — not
  only money-back guarantees (a valid vehicle) but track record, being published, judging
  shows, speaking on stages. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2023-03-03] **The value/offer levers (explicitly credited to Alex Hormozi's "$100M
  Offers"):** simultaneously **reduce the buyer's pain** and **increase perceived gains**,
  and minimize **effort and sacrifice** the buyer must expend. "Taken to its logical
  conclusion it should sound like: easy, instant, you don't have to do anything, results are
  near-instant." Chris explicitly names Hormozi as the source and says he is not affiliated
  and makes zero money from the mention. *(Attribution: value-equation framing = Hormozi;
  the worked design exercises applying it = Chris.)*
- [2023-03-03] **The "10x / divide-by-10" offer-design exercise (Chris-taught):** take your
  current service (e.g. $5K/mo social content) and redesign it at 10× ($50K/mo) — most people
  reflexively "just do 10× more of the same"; you must "push past the sludge and silt" to
  radically different offers (dedicated per-function crews, 24/7 documentary-style filming à
  la HBO's "24/7," a home studio with one-button presets, an AI "ghost channel" A/B-testing
  concepts before the client records). Then redesign at 1/10 ($500) — the constraint forces
  an **infinitely scalable** product (a premium guide, a deck of prompt cards, a personal-brand
  guide + app coupons). The reverse trap: people just *reduce quantity*, which "is not the
  answer either." (paraphrase)
- [2023-03-03] **"Trading time for money is a fool's errand."** Selling units of time
  incentivizes selling *more time*, not more results; "no client buys your time, they buy
  results" — a client is annoyed, not pleased, if a task takes longer. Raising your hourly
  rate doesn't fix a conceptually flawed unit of measurement. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2023-03-03] **The reframe — "time is money, but the CLIENT'S time, not yours."** The
  client's time is worth more than yours (they hire you precisely to save their time/effort),
  so "the person who saves you your time and effort creates money for you." Therefore, when
  pressured to cut price, **don't cut price — decrease the time in which you deliver the
  desired outcome.** Amazon example: it "destroyed everybody" by compressing delivery time
  (logistics/automation) so competitors like B&H can't compete despite being good people.
  Deliver the *same or better* product faster, not an inferior one. (paraphrase + quote)
- [2023-03-03] **Vertical integration (picked up from a speaker "Jeff" at Neil's Ford
  Mastermind):** McDonald's owns the largest potato and chicken farms and manufactures more
  toys than anyone (selling zero) — complementary break-even businesses that give the core an
  unfair advantage (a Happy Meal toy that would otherwise raise the price $1–2). Apply it to
  offers: run a **break-even/loss-leader personal-brand or social-media arm** whose attention
  and PR generate revenue for the profitable business. (paraphrase)
- [2023-03-03] **Package into a three-tier price ladder** — a $500 option, a $5K option, and a
  "Ritz-Carlton / Four Seasons white-glove" $50K option. Offering tiers "removes the necessity
  for the client to triple-bid you." (paraphrase)
- [2023-03-03] To "land the plane": writing an irresistible offer "is not about packaging —
  it's literally redesigning from the ground up what your offer is," so much so that (in
  Hormozi's words) clients feel "stupid for saying no." (paraphrase + quote)
- [2023-03-03] Aside on **neuroplasticity / staying an innovative thinker** — the older you
  get the more rigid you become; keep the divergent-thinking muscle alive with novelty (take a
  different route home, use your non-dominant hand, walk backward up stairs). Problem-solving =
  understanding constraints and resources. (paraphrase)
