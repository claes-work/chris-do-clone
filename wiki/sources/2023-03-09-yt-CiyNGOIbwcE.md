# Attract Customers Like a Magnet: Marketing Strategies To Grow Your Business + Brand (Masterclass 5/5)

## Summary

**Chris Do teaches this masterclass.** It is the fifth and final installment ("The Fifth Element") of his business-psychology / marketing series for creative freelancers, framed around **attraction marketing**. The format is an interview: an unnamed Futur host asks the questions and interjects reflections, and Chris delivers all the substantive teaching. Chris's material is **persona data**; the host is a facilitator (context only, not persona).

Chris positions attraction marketing as a **hybrid** that lives between two poles: **direct outbound sales/outreach** (transactional, fast money, targeted) and **ambient inbound content marketing / personal-brand building** (transformational, slow, builds goodwill and "super fans"). He argues most people camp at one extreme and that the sophisticated middle is rare and hard. He walks through a concrete hybrid playbook (high-value content → extract nuggets → self-identifying offer → escalating micro-commitments → soft sales call), grounding it in **Seth Godin's permission marketing**. He closes with a personal reflection on his identity shift from "designer" to "teacher" and promotes an April European workshop tour.

★ **L3 candidate flag:** Chris contributes substantial persona material — a named framework (attraction marketing as transactional/transformational hybrid), a step-by-step process, quotable voice lines, and biographical detail (15 years teaching at private art schools, 5 years running a motion design business before teaching, "I'm a teacher" identity shift). Worth promoting to topics + persona (voice, beliefs, biography) in a synthesis pass.

## Key claims

All claims dated **2023-03-09**, paraphrased, attributed to **Chris Do** unless noted.

- Attraction marketing sits between two principles: **direct outreach/sales** (outbound) and **content marketing** (ambient/inbound). It is a hybrid for people who lack the 3–5–7 year runway to build a personal brand purely on goodwill. (Chris)
- Outbound sales content is transactional and hyper-focused ("I do this, I help clients with this, talk to me now") — the viewer instantly knows what you do and what you sell. Chris cites **Richard Moore**, who teaches direct sales via LinkedIn and rarely posts personal/emotional content because he wants to be associated only with direct-sales strategy. (Chris, about Richard Moore)
- A LinkedIn sales tactic (attributed to Richard Moore): anyone who views your profile is a prompt to reach out, because people only look after seeing something you did — a good way to turn a lukewarm person of interest warmer. (Chris, relaying Richard Moore)
- Inbound content marketing means creating work (visual, demonstrations) with **no call to action, no hard or soft sale**; over time you gain followers, some of whom become buyers who reach out to you. Chris cites **James Barnard**, who draws logos and makes funny videos. (Chris)
- Chris gives a real-life example: he saw an After Effects typography tutorial on Instagram, followed and DM'd the creator, and hired him (~$3–4K, settled at $3K) — the creator wasn't prospecting Chris but attracted him by demonstrating skill. (Chris)
- Core distinction: in sales outreach **you inquire to the consumer** (go to the client first); in content marketing **the audience inquires to you** because of accumulated goodwill. (host frames; Chris affirms)
- Philosophical disagreement with Richard Moore: The Futur has made **1,800+ YouTube videos** mostly asking for nothing, building relationship and goodwill; Richard optimizes each action to generate clients now. Short-term, bet on Richard (more money faster); long-term (3/5/15/25 years) the broad goodwill approach builds community, super fans, and greater value. (Chris)
- **Transactional vs. transformational:** the transactional model exchanges a service for money in a 1:1 correlation; Chris's transformational model builds value with no direct correlation between effort and payment. (Chris)
- "Cash-in" examples of accumulated goodwill: The Rock (Teremana tequila), Conor McGregor (Proper No. Twelve Irish whiskey), Kylie Jenner — years of content/goodwill let them later sell products worth hundreds of millions. (Chris)
- You don't have to pick one camp — you can run both — but mixing selling and not-selling muddies the waters. (Chris)
- **When to go outbound/transactional:** if you can't meet basic needs (rent, food, taxes, debt), get your house in order first with direct sales; you don't have the runway to build goodwill. (Chris)
- **When to go inbound/authority-building:** once basic needs and a rainy-day fund are covered, think 3–5–10 years out. Authority-building is roughly chronological — you've trained (self-taught or university), practiced for a while, maybe gone independent (typically mid-20s to early 30s). Building authority at 14 is "a little bit ridiculous." (Chris)
- **Teach your younger self:** create content for the younger version of yourself — you know their habits, resistance, and how they learn, which solves the "I don't know who I'm writing for" problem. Somewhere there's a younger version of you (not literally named you) who will show up for your style, tone, and aesthetic. (Chris)
- Chris pushes back on the "just give give give, never ask" advice (nods to Gary Vaynerchuk, who says ask on the third time but it's never clear when): needing money and being transactional is not a bad thing when you must survive, and you're still giving value. (Chris; host raises GaryVee)
- **Common failure mode:** people mistakenly mix the two strategies — they want sales and are starving but pretend to play the long content game without actually giving value; it's a "thinly veiled disguise" people see through. Better to be clear and transparent about who and where you are. (Chris)
- Selling all the time carries a danger: buyers who aren't currently in-market tune you out or unfollow (may check back in 3/6/9 months). (Chris)
- **The hybrid playbook (attraction marketing process):**
  1. Create something genuinely valuable — teach something new, reframe the known, or deliver it in an entertaining way (examples: a hip-hop-style breakdown of Old-English literature; "Drunk History"). (Chris)
  2. Test value by market rate: what would someone literally pay? If zero, it's worth zero; target roughly **$30–$100 of value**. Ask a neutral friend for an honest price check. (Chris)
  3. Don't just dump a PDF — extract high-value nuggets and post them to build rapport (example: "here are 3 subject lines guaranteed to outperform what you're writing," backed by 10,000 campaigns over 4 years). (Chris)
  4. Offer the full resource free ("comment this word below so I know who to give it to") — this makes the audience **self-identify** and gives you permission to reach out. (Chris)
  5. Escalate via small commitments, each giving increasing value: DM thank-you + a light question → offer a free 25-minute diagnostic call → on the call, park a low-pressure offer to work together only "if it feels right." (Chris)
- This is **Seth Godin's permission marketing** applied to modern social media: audience volunteers to enroll in a long-term campaign where each interaction goes deeper and delivers more value — "strangers to friends and friends to customers." That process **is** attraction marketing. (Chris)
- **First thing to get right:** clarity on your audience — do they have an urgent, uncomfortable pain, and the **means** to buy the solution? Solving a problem for people who can't afford the fix doesn't work (example: someone who can't buy a car needs a transit pass or a "last mile" solution — e-scooters, etc. — that urban planners/industrial designers focus on, since you can't change public-transport infrastructure). (Chris)
- **What people get wrong:** very few attempt the hybrid at all — it's hard because you must master both direct sales AND making genuinely attractive content, so practitioners are inherently few (unaware + it's not easy). Chris admits he has "no easy answer" for this. (Chris)
- **Biography / identity:** before YouTube, Chris taught at private art schools for **15 years**; before that he ran a successful **motion design business for 5 years**. He long identified as "a designer" but was suppressing a deeper identity as an educator; today he answers "I'm a teacher" without pausing — a shift over the last ~2.5 years, catalyzed after COVID by teaching in person again (a workshop tour in Australia made him feel "more alive than I've been in a long time," "my brain was on fire," "I live to grow"). (Chris, self-reported)
- On teaching: quoting **Craig Philip** — "you teach for yourself, you practice for your students." Chris frames teaching as simultaneously a selfish act and an act of generosity; in-person live teaching, where multiple people push back from different angles, forces him to explain ideas in new ways and teaches him things about himself. (Chris; quote attributed to Craig Philip)
- **Promotion:** a European workshop tour in April across 7 cities, full 8-hour teaching days on two subjects (rather than cramming into 4 hours or 30 minutes); London and Berlin expected to sell out soon. Framed as "the transaction is buying a ticket, but the transformation far exceeds what you pay." (Chris)
