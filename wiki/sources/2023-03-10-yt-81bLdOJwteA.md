# The Secret to Building a Personal Brand — Reduce Your Market Size

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do** (verified). This is a workshop/room-teaching excerpt: Chris addresses
a live audience of creative entrepreneurs, references specific attendees by name (John,
Ernesto, Jeff), tells a first-person story about being cornered at "the Grove" while filming
with Neil (Neil Patel — a Futur collaborator), and speaks throughout in his own coaching voice.
All substantive material is **Chris — persona/voice data.** No separate host speaks; the named
attendees are context only (do-not-train).

The through-line: **to build a personal brand you must deliberately reduce your market size.**
Chris says the single biggest resistance he saw in the room was people refusing to make the
decision to narrow their audience — they keep answering "both" (men *and* women, older *and*
younger) instead of choosing. Only once you pick a specific person does messaging become clear;
until then everything stays generic and you fall back on borrowed templates and scripts that
make everyone sound the same. He layers on two supporting ideas: (1) **stop chasing originality
— filter existing ideas through your own voice** (Magritte-sculpture example; Jim Rohn vs.
GaryVee saying the same thing in different voices), and (2) **lean into the weird/shameful/
guilt-tinged parts of yourself** you were socialized to suppress as a child — that suppression
is why standing out feels unnatural, and reclaiming it is how you differentiate.

★ **L3 candidate flag:** genuinely useful persona material — the "reduce your market size" framing
of niching, the coaching move ("assume they don't know what they're talking about; ask *how do
you know this is true*"), the "learn the martial art first, then improvise" template philosophy,
the socialized-suppression/inner-voice-is-your-parent idea, and the "wear the hat to solve a
shiny-head problem" origin anecdote. Most of the *niching* substance reinforces existing topic
coverage (see Cross-links) rather than adding new frameworks; the coaching/critical-thinking and
suppression angles are the freshest. Promote selectively in a synthesis pass.

## Key claims

All claims dated **2023-03-10**, paraphrased, attributed to **Chris Do**.

- **The secret to a personal brand is to reduce your market size** — deliberately narrow who you
  serve. People resist this hard ("they resist and they resist and they resist") because they're
  afraid to make the decision. (Chris) — reinforces existing niching coverage, not new.
- The forcing method is binary elimination: men or women? → women. Older or younger? → older.
  Married, single, mom? As you drill down, **the messaging suddenly becomes clear.** (Chris)
- Until you figure out who you're speaking to, **none of your messaging will work — it stays
  super generic**, so people default to templates and scripts, and then everyone in the room
  sounds identical. (Chris)
- **Templates are a learning scaffold, not the destination.** Follow the template/martial-art
  exactly at first — don't deviate, don't modify — then once you've "put in enough reps," start
  to improvise and add your own formula. (Chris)
- **A coaching stance:** whenever coaching someone, automatically assume they don't know what
  they're talking about / haven't thought it through / wouldn't choose those words. Treat clients
  this way (minus his abrasive attitude) and you'll do well. (Chris)
- **The critical-thinking / negotiation move:** when someone asserts something ("this should be
  cheaper"), ask **"how do you know this is true? / what evidence do you have?"** Most people have
  no proof — they pulled it out — and they start to unravel their own thinking. Same tool works
  in sales negotiation. (Chris)
- We're culturally raised to blend in and not offend, which suppresses critical thinking. Chris
  doesn't try to be offensive but insists on thinking critically, which is sometimes offensive —
  and that's fine, because he's "not here for everybody." (Chris)
- **The pursuit of originality is a fool's errand.** Truly original things usually only come from
  riding a *technological* wave; otherwise everything is a **new application of an old idea**
  (example: a Magritte painting turned into a real-life 3D sculpture — old idea, new application
  via technology). (Chris)
- Because ideas aren't original, your job is to **filter them through your voice and who you are.**
  Same idea, different voice: **Jim Rohn's** "you're the average of the five people you spend the
  most time with" and **Gary Vaynerchuk's** "get rid of one loser friend" are the same law of
  averages — Gary just packages it as a provocative sound bite because "Gary knows who he is."
  (Chris, citing Jim Rohn and GaryVee)
- Recurring metaphor: **you rise or sink to whoever you spend the most time with** — golf with
  someone terrible and your game drops; golf with Tiger Woods and it rises. So read, take courses,
  do group coaching — then run it through your own filter. (Chris)
- **Differentiation comes from leaning into the parts of yourself that feel weird, shameful, or
  guilty** — the things you're afraid to show the world. As soon as you show them, you stand out
  immediately. (Chris)
- **Why standing out feels unnatural:** as a young child (~age 4) you were told not to point, not
  to say the weird/blunt thing, "we don't do that in polite company" — enough of that socialization
  suppressed who you are. (Chris)
- Via inner work / psychotherapy you discover an inner voice that sounds like you and that you
  *think* is you, but **it is not you — it's the voice of your most dominant parent(s).** That's
  the material you have to work through to express your real self. (Chris)
- **Origin of his signature style:** most of what he does today came from accident and willingness
  to try — e.g. wearing the hat started to **solve a shiny-head problem**, and picking bold colors
  was to stand out. (Chris, self-reported biography)
- **Anecdote (vertical integration / new businesses from a brand):** while filming a video
  conference at "the Grove" with Neil, a stranger recognized Chris from ~20 feet by his style
  alone and aggressively insisted Chris teach him to be more stylish and pay for it — illustrating
  how a distinct personal style spawns unexpected demand and adjacent business lines. (Chris)
