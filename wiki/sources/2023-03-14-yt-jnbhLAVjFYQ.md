# From Cliches to Creativity: Making Your Brand Stand Out

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do** (first person throughout — "the way I do it," "my community,"
"my industry," "if you went to design school like I did"). This is a short excerpt of
Chris teaching a live workshop room, apparently a follow-up to a previous day's
exercise on building a **two-word brand**. Persona data — all training material.

Chris coaches the room through two connected problems: (1) how to compress your identity
into a short, memorable self-label, and (2) how to escape cliché and make that label
stand out. His key reframe is that a **two-word brand is not your whole identity — it's a
hook** to start a conversation, engineered like a headline in copywriting. He then teaches
a concrete anti-cliché move: take a phrase everyone already knows and change one element
so the familiar becomes a "known unknown."

**Novelty note:** the core ideas — the two-word brand, turning a liability into an asset,
anti-cliché/stand-out — are **already well covered** in `branding.md` (Framework 10:
shadow word + transformer word, refined in the Dec-2023 masterclass and AdobeMAX keynote,
which post-date this clip). This March-2023 clip is mostly **reinforcing**, but it adds a
few genuinely-new angles worth a light promote: (a) the **"noun that's your truth"**
route to a label (vs. two adjectives), (b) the explicit **headline → subhead → body →
CTA** copywriting rationale for why the brand is only a hook, and (c) the **"change one
word of a cliché" / "known unknown"** technique with fresh examples ("mess is more,"
"mortgage Whisperer"). Flagged ★ below.

## Key claims

All claims dated **2023-03-14**, paraphrased, attributed to **Chris Do**.

- A two-word brand is genuinely hard to build, especially if you haven't spent time
  thinking about it — it only comes easily to someone who lives and breathes brand daily.
- ★ Two adjectives (e.g. "quietly fierce") are hard to make work; a better route is to
  find a **noun that is your truth** — you have to "dig deep" to find it. Example from his
  community: Abby Toddy self-labeled "**world-class hugger**" — a hugger is not normally
  something people celebrate ("everybody hugs"), but the little twist of "world-class"
  makes it unique and hers once she claims it.
- ★ **The mistake people make: thinking the two-word brand has to be everything.** It's
  not meant to be — it's just a **hook to begin a conversation**. You want a hook so
  powerful people ask "what does that mean? tell me more" (as they do with his own "loud
  introvert").
- Don't answer "what do you do?" with a draining monologue — you can watch the life drain
  out of the listener (the "Jimmy Fallon" face) as they look for the exit. A brand is a
  **dialogue**, meant to go back and forth, not a one-shot pitch.
- ★ **Copywriting rationale (borrowed structure):** a headline is designed to do exactly
  one thing — get you to read the **subhead**; the subhead's only job is to get you to
  read the **body copy**; then somewhere there's a **CTA**. So don't treat everything you
  say as a call-to-action to hire you right then; the two-word brand is the headline, not
  the close.
- **Why cliches are terrible:** we've heard them too many times, so they trigger eye-rolls.
  In design school the rule was "use the cliché, go to jail." (Design-craft craft rule.)
- ★ **Reframe on cliché (from the book *Smile in the Mind*):** "a cliché is a bankrupt
  idea — make a deposit." Cliches work *because* they're familiar; the move is to take
  something known/played-out and add a small twist so it becomes unknown — a **"known
  unknown."**
- ★ **Technique — change one component of a familiar phrase.** Example: "less is more"
  (minimalism) → Brian Collins / Collins Agency reworked it to "**mess is more**" for a
  Twitch maximalism piece by removing one letter. Also (via Sam Horn's *POP!*): take a
  popular book/movie everyone knows and reuse its concept — *The Horse Whisperer* → "I'm a
  **mortgage Whisperer**"; a "Whisperer" is someone who figures out what no one else can,
  so swap one aspect to describe what you do.
- **Recommended resources:** Sam Horn's book *POP!* (and her TED Talk) for clever ways to
  describe things — "worth its weight"; and *Smile in the Mind* (conceptual thinking).
- Workshop instruction (given as homework, not worked in the clip): take a line you think
  is such an overplayed trope that everyone uses it, and change just one part — one word,
  or the word order.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do quotes (persona voice):

> "It's just a hook to begin a conversation. You want to create such a powerful hook that
> people ask you, what does that mean, tell me more." — Chris Do

> "I'm a world-class hugger, Chris... a hugger is not something that people would
> celebrate — like, everybody hugs — why is that so special? And she just put this little
> twist on it." — Chris Do (relaying Abby Toddy's label, approvingly)

> "The headline is designed to do one thing: to get you to read the subhead. What's the
> subhead designed to do? Get you to read the body copy." — Chris Do

> "Let's not try to treat everything that we do as a call to action to hire us right then
> and there. This is a conversation, it's a dialogue, it's supposed to go back and forth."
> — Chris Do

> "A cliché is a bankrupt idea — make a deposit." — Chris Do (quoting *Smile in the Mind*)

> "You want to take something that's known, that's kind of played out, and you add just a
> little bit of a twist to it and it becomes unknown. A known unknown." — Chris Do

> "Less is more... he just took this letter away and says 'mess is more,' and then he
> completely changed the whole thing." — Chris Do (on Brian Collins / Collins Agency)

> "You just take a concept that's familiar to people and you change one aspect of it." — Chris Do
