# 3 Ways To Improve Communication Instantly

★ **L3-candidate** — supplies a concrete, reproducible **self-review protocol** for
becoming a better communicator (record → wait a day → review audio-only / video-only /
transcribed) plus the **ESL-teacher grammar tip** and the **"speech is a set of
changeable behaviors"** mindset framing. These are actionable methods not obviously
captured elsewhere in the wiki (existing coverage of listening / tone-of-voice /
feedback is about *conversation*, not *self-diagnosis of one's own delivery*). Worth
promoting into topics/mindset (or a communication/public-speaking page) and persona
`beliefs`/`voice` in a later synthesis pass.

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do** (solo monologue — first-person throughout: "my students,"
"a lot of people who follow me who are ethnic," "I went a bit New York … the Asian
tall man"; no guest, no co-host). All material here is **persona/voice data**.

Short, tightly-structured piece giving three tips to become "a more effective
communicator." The spine is a mindset claim stated at both the top and the close: the
way you speak is **just a series of behaviors**, and behaviors either serve you or they
don't — so they can be changed. Tips 1–2 are practical self-diagnosis drills; tip 3 is
the mindset that makes change possible. The recurring payoff Chris names is **authority
and credibility** — filler/non-words and grammatical errors quietly rob you of both, and
have (he says) held people back from promotions ("you seem unsure about your ideas").

## Key claims

All dated 2023-03-22. Chris Do, paraphrased unless quoted below.

**Way 1 — Self-awareness via recorded self-review.** Record yourself speaking for five
minutes ("it's going to be excruciating"). Crucially, **leave it for a day** before
watching — reviewing immediately makes you too self-critical. Then review it **three
separate ways, isolating one channel at a time**:
1. **Sound only** — turn the phone around, just listen; note the qualities of your voice,
   what you like/dislike, and what sounds you make "unnecessarily."
2. **Picture only** — mute it and just watch; note your movement, facial expressiveness,
   hand gestures, anything "non-functional."
3. **Transcript** — get the video transcribed, then take a **red highlighter** to the
   **non-words** ("the sounds we make to fill the silence") and **filler words** ("the
   words we use to fill the silence"). Chris claims this single move "will completely
   transform how you come across" — removing "ums and ahs" measurably increases students'
   clarity, authority, and credibility.

**Way 2 — Fix grammar with an ESL teacher (aimed at non-native / "ethnic" viewers).**
For people who work in Western/English-language culture, frequent grammatical errors
"robs you of authority and credibility very quickly." Fix: record yourself improvising
for **20 minutes** (e.g. talk about your favorite food or location, to tease out natural
behaviors), get it transcribed, and send **both the video and transcript to an ESL
(English-as-a-second-language) teacher**. They will zero in on your recurring
grammatical errors and send back the ~80% of stock phrases you use that are incorrect.
Cost: "a couple hundred bucks." (NB — audience-specific advice; framed by Chris as help
for his "ethnic viewers and listeners," tied to his own Asian-American background.)

**Way 3 — Mindset: speech is a set of behaviors, and behaviors can be changed.** The
way you speak is "just a series of behaviors" that either serve you or don't. Chris
cites his own range as proof he can shift register at will ("I went a bit New York …
I could do the Asian tall man — these are just behaviors"). His advice to students:
don't be "so attached to who you are in the present" that "you don't give the future
version of yourself a chance." You are "forever evolving, forever growing," and your
ability to communicate should grow with you.

**Novelty vs. existing wiki coverage:** listening, tone of voice, and giving feedback
appear elsewhere as *interpersonal/conversation* skills. This video is distinct — it's a
**self-diagnosis / rehearsal methodology** for one's own delivery (the isolate-each-
channel review, the filler-word audit, the ESL-teacher grammar fix). Treat Ways 1–2 as
**genuinely new** actionable protocols; Way 3 (behaviors are changeable / evolving self)
overlaps Chris's broader growth-mindset material but is a fresh, quotable articulation.
No contradictions with prior sources noted.

## Notable verbatim quotes

Chris Do = persona voice.

**On the core mindset (bookends the video):**
> "The way you speak is just a series of behaviors and these behaviors are either serving
> you or they're not, and behaviors can be changed."

**On the review method:**
> "Leave it for a day, don't watch it straight away. If you watch it straight away you
> are too damn self-critical."

**On non-words vs. filler words:**
> "Non-words being the sounds we make to fill the silence, filler words being the words
> we use to fill the silence."

**On the payoff of cutting filler:**
> "You'll be shocked how many of my students, just by getting rid of their 'ums' and
> their 'ahs,' increases their clarity and increases their authority and credibility
> dramatically — just from removing one damn thing."

**On the cost of poor communication:**
> "[People get] held back from promotions because people will say things like 'you seem
> unsure about your ideas.'"

**On grammar and authority:**
> "If you speak and you work with a lot of people in the Western culture and you have a
> lot of grammatical errors, this robs you of authority and credibility very quickly."

**On the changeable self (close):**
> "Don't be so attached to who you are in the present … you don't give the future version
> of yourself a chance. … You can change, you can transform, you can evolve. You're not a
> stationary being — you're forever evolving, forever growing, so should your ability to
> communicate."
