## Key claims (2023-03-26)

**Chris Do (subject — trainable; paraphrase unless quoted):**
- **Designer vs. artist.** Design is a service; art is self-expression. Best definition of
  art he's heard (Lana Wachowski, on a Cloud Atlas panel): "art is an invitation to look at
  the world from a different perspective." The moment there's any agenda outside
  self-expression (a client/commission), you're not doing pure art. Uses Herbert Simon's
  definition (via Marty Neumeier's *The Brand Gap*): a designer devises a course of action
  to move from an existing condition to an improved one — so everyone who improves a
  client's situation is a designer.
- **A client is not an inconvenience.** A common downfall of service creatives is treating
  the client (the person who enables their living) as an obstacle to self-expression. First
  find someone who values what you do and transact — that builds skill, reps, confidence,
  and a body of work. Self-actualization is hard when primary needs (food, rent, debt) aren't
  met; money doesn't solve everything but it "buys a ticket for your wife to see her dying
  parent."
- **Freelancer vs. independent business owner (his precise definitions).** A *freelancer*
  shows up to perform a service, sells **time**, cannot control how that time is spent, is
  paid for hours not output, and **cannot hire others** to do the work (and "independent
  contractor" is also a distinct legal category — he once paid back-taxes over the
  mislabeling). An *independent business owner* ("employee of one, wears many hats") does
  marketing, sales, invoicing, contracts, project management, delegation — and acts B2B.
  Tests for which you are: is the buyer upset if someone else does the work? do you sell by
  time? do you avoid sales/marketing/legal? — those lean freelancer.
- **"Words matter."** Cites Ronald J. Baker (*Implementing Value Pricing*): "all
  transformation is linguistic; to change the culture we have to change the conversation."
  Wants the community to use "freelancer" precisely rather than let it drift. Origin of the
  word (via Jonathan Stark): a "free lance" was a medieval mercenary sell-sword.
- **The buyer shouldn't care how the work gets made.** A promise to deliver to a standard is
  what matters — not who does it or how. We don't expect Phil Knight to make our shoes, Elon
  to build our cars, or Gordon Ramsay to flip our burgers; creatives wrongly think they must
  do the work themselves ("telephone designer" was Paul Rand's jab at Kyle Cooper). If quality
  silently drops after the contract is signed, that's dishonesty / lack of leadership, not a
  staffing problem.
- **Pricing = supply and demand.** To raise price, increase demand and decrease supply.
  Prerequisite (usually left unspoken): **get good at your craft first** — "charge more" is
  not "have no skill and charge more." Then **specialize** to reduce/eliminate competition;
  the goal of positioning is to become "one of one, non-fungible," so your rate is your rate
  because only you do it your way.
- **Hunter vs. farmer (outbound vs. inbound).** Outbound/direct outreach = hunting: exciting,
  feast-or-famine, you stalk a big kill then starve between. Inbound/content marketing =
  farming: unsexy, slow, seasons before fruit, dies if you stop watering. Both work — pick
  the one that fits who you are. He's an introvert who writes and "plants seeds," not a
  hunter; don't force a sales-heavy plan you're not wired for ("if you're 4'5" you can play
  basketball, but the 6'7" guys will destroy you"). Your life purpose is to find and maximize
  your unique set of skills/personality/voice/history.
- **Content-creator ≠ lead-funnel.** Cites Seth Godin (*Permission Marketing*): the internet
  was the first mass medium not built to sell ads; much "content" is just advertising disguised
  as content. Be honest about which you're doing.
- **Opportunities compound.** The more value you consistently put into the world, the cooler
  and more unforeseeable the opportunities (and, eventually, free stuff / partnerships — which
  carry their own reciprocity burden). Only partner with brands you'd genuinely use; the
  "influencer model" is renting your audience to others — better to point that marketing
  department at your own business.
- **Persona vs. shadow (Jung / brand).** Online you must do the thing you're most afraid to
  do: show the shadow. The persona is the coded, accepted public self; if that's all you show
  you become a "poor facsimile," a two-dimensional paper character no one cares about. Cites
  Russell Brunson (*DotCom Secrets*) on the "attractive character" (backstory + character
  flaws). Naming the shame you carry strips its power. "I could read between what you're
  writing and I know you're hiding — that's not going to do it."
- **Show up as *you*, drop the multiple agendas.** To the audience member paralyzed about what
  to post: the problem is multiple agendas (client-building, looking virtuous). Your only online
  agenda should be to show up as yourself — a gate that both invites people in and keeps the
  wrong ones out. His own 2014 lesson: "I don't give an f what anybody thinks." Oddly, YouTube
  is where he *is* an artist, because nobody tells him what to do.
- **Contrarian beliefs he holds.** Selling time for money is an antiquated concept to get over.
  "Feelings aren't facts" — leaning on emotion clouds judgment; a higher self requires
  non-judgmental, objective seeing. Sketching a "Paradox Principles" idea (everything you think
  is wrong; think the opposite to be successful/happy/healthier).
- **Work-life integration, not balance.** Work-life balance is a misdirected concept; aim for
  *integration* — the more your work and life overlap, the more fulfilled you are (won't suit
  everyone in your life, and that's okay). Theory: the more separate versions of yourself you
  must maintain, the less fulfilled you are (cites Gauguin leaving Paris for Tahiti to become
  his real self, via Brian Tracy). His own arc — commercials + teaching + home life pulled him
  four directions; content creation now integrates educator identity + video gear + traveling
  with his son, and he's "the most fulfilled I've ever been." "Your sole mission in life is to
  be happy," and happiness is a dynamic puzzle where each solved piece reveals a new one.

**Jamie Brindle (guest — CONTEXT ONLY, do-not-train):** art⇄commerce Venn diagram (each
subsidizes the other); adds "fishing" (job markets like Upwork) as a third mode beside
hunting/gathering; rate-raise mechanics = buy back business-development time, departing
clients subsidized by those who stay; scaling without hiring via productized services / digital
products (e.g. LUTs); "business is really just two people trying to solve a problem together —
don't overcomplicate it"; the responsibility of influence (people DMing that they quit their
job); batching 150 reels; his talking-to-himself two-character video format.

**Paul Mikhaylov (host — CONTEXT ONLY):** framed the questions (artist definition, the
freelancer "baggage," content-creator-vs-funnel, "what do you believe that most would disagree
with," "advice from your 70-year-old self"); delivered the Bloom sponsor read.

- "Hi everybody, my name is Christo. I'm a loud introvert, I'm a recovering graphic designer,
  a serial entrepreneur, a middle child, an above-average student, and a first-class
  troublemaker."
- "Art is an invitation to look at the world from a different perspective." *(Chris quoting
  Lana Wachowski, endorsing it.)*
- "The person who gives you a means to make a living… is not a person that gets in your way —
  they enable you to live your life."
- "Money doesn't solve a lot of things, but it does buy a ticket for your wife to go see her
  parent before she dies… I'm gonna talk about money when you have no money. When you have all
  the money, you won't need me. God bless you."
- "A freelancer is one who shows up at a place of work… they're selling their time, they're
  paid for the hours that they work, not for the amount of work they get done, and they're not
  allowed to hire other people."
- "All transformation is linguistic. If we want to change the culture, we have to change the
  conversation." *(Chris quoting Ronald J. Baker.)*
- "We don't expect Phil Knight to make their shoes, we don't expect Elon to build our cars, we
  don't expect Gordon Ramsay to flip our burgers. Why is it that artists and creative people
  think you've got to do the work?"
- "My rates are my rate because there's only one person who can do this work the way I do it,
  and that person's me."
- "The whole point of positioning is to ultimately reduce or eliminate competition. When you're
  one of one, non-fungible, then you have no competition."
- "The thing that you try not to do is the thing you must do… The thing you're most afraid to
  show is the thing you must show. The shadow is what we need to talk about."
- "The concept of selling time for money is an antiquated concept — I think we need to get over
  that."
- "Feelings aren't facts, and too many people lean into this emotional part that actually
  clouds judgment."
- "Work-life balance is a misdirected concept. It should be work-life integration — your life
  should be your work and your work should be your life, and the more those two things overlap,
  the more fulfilled you are."
- "Your sole mission in life is to be happy, and happiness comes from you figuring out all
  those parts and pieces… it's not a static puzzle, it's a dynamic puzzle."
- "Your only agenda online should just be to show up as yourself… a gate has multiple functions:
  it protects what's inside and it keeps what is not meant to be from coming in."

**Jamie Brindle (guest — CONTEXT / do-not-train):**
