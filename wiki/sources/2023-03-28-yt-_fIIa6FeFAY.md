# How To Make Your First $100,000 (Masterclass)

## Summary

**Speaker: Chris Do, teaching solo throughout.** This is a Futur Pro member training
(the "100 to 100 challenge" — Chris's stated goal of getting 100 Pro members past
$100,000 in revenue) that The Futur released publicly. No guest teaches; the only other
person named is "parshell/Purnima" as an example student whose hybrid business Chris
uses to illustrate the math (her situation is quoted, but the teaching and the numbers
are Chris's). All of it trains the persona.

The masterclass is a step-by-step playbook aimed at people selling professional
services and coaching (logo/identity, branding, content, web, coaching/consulting). It
moves through four blocks: (1) **goal-setting math** — reduce $100K/year to
$10K/month over 10 working months (leaving a 2-month cushion for downtime/vacation);
(2) **pricing to hit $10K/month** via hourly rate, per-deliverable, or a hybrid, plus
the labor-vs-volume quadrant and the "Minimum Level of Engagement" (MLE) policy;
(3) **marketing** — outbound (referrals, networking, cold outreach) and inbound
(content marketing, borrow-vs-build authority); and (4) **the lead-gen reality check**
— working backwards from close ratio to the number of qualified leads and top-of-funnel
leads actually required. Content is mostly a consolidation/repackaging of Chris's
standard pricing and sales teaching, but the fully-worked "$10K/month → close ratio →
×10 leads" funnel arithmetic and the MLE framing are presented as a single coherent
system here.

## Key claims

All dated 2023-03-28, paraphrased unless quoted, attributed to **Chris Do** (all
persona-trainable).

- To make $100K/year, target **$10K/month over 10 months** (10 × 10 = 100), not 12 —
  the extra two months are deliberate cushion for bad months, downtime and vacation.
- The plan scales linearly: to hit $200K double the monthly number, $300K triple it,
  $500K→$1M just swap the numbers.
- **Raising your hourly rate lets you work less for the same money:** $100/hr needs
  100 hrs/month; $200/hr needs 50; $500/hr needs only 20 hrs/month (≈5 hrs/week). A
  $50/hr bump (e.g. $100→$150) usually won't "break the client."
- Chris manages his own private-coaching demand by **raising the rate until supply and
  demand balance**, rather than taking more calls (he could do ~2/day but finds
  back-to-back daily coaching exhausting).
- **Per-deliverable pricing** to reach $10K/month: $1,000/logo → 10/month;
  $5,000/identity system → 2/month; $10,000/website → 1/month; $20,000 big website →
  1 every 2 months. A $500 logo forces ~20 logos/month (one every working day) — too
  much for quality work.
- **Hybrid model** (Purnima example): strategy/course work at $200/hr × 20 hrs = $4K,
  leaving a $6K deficit that the video-production deliverable must cover — mix
  hourly + deliverable however hits $10K.
- **Labor-vs-volume quadrant:** avoid high-labor/high-volume (the "Fiverr" cranking
  bucket, near-zero pay) and be wary of low-labor/high-volume (assembly line needs
  many clients). Aim toward low-volume work.
- **Minimum Level of Engagement (MLE):** set a threshold every client must clear —
  e.g. a $10,000 minimum speaking fee — and treat it as policy you stick to ("I have a
  minimum fee of $10,000 to speak — does it make sense for us to have a call?").
- **Two marketing types:** outbound = push (referrals, networking, cold outreach);
  inbound = pull (content marketing, lead magnets, live coaching — drawing people to
  you like a magnet).
- **Ask for referrals early** — before you finish, or even before you start the job,
  when feelings are highest. Pre-framing a referral request at signing ("if I deliver
  and you're thrilled, will you refer me to two friends?") also gives you extra
  incentive and helps close the sale.
- **Referrers act as your sales reps.** Chris paid commercial-work sales reps ~7–15%
  of new jobs and was thrilled to. But in the sub-$100K stage he advises **not paying
  cash** for referrals (money can cheapen a genuine recommendation) — send a
  high-touch personalized thank-you instead. It's easier to keep a client than win a
  new one.
- **Networking checklist:** research the event (speakers, agenda, companies), target
  3–5 companies, set appointments in advance, bring business cards + elevator pitch,
  ask "who else should I meet?" — networking is relationship-building, **no selling**
  at that stage; the goal is to "jump off platform" into a real conversation.
- **Cold outreach → warm leads:** engage genuinely with target prospects' content on
  LinkedIn, commenting in the first critical window after they post, to build name
  recognition. (Chris cites Seth Godin's *This Is Marketing*: "converting strangers
  into friends and friends into clients.")
- **Borrow vs. build authority** (credited to Mira Kaddoura / *But I'm Not an Expert*
  — "build it, borrow it, or be it"): borrow by summarizing books, resharing others'
  posts the right way (screenshot as image + your insight + tag them), and
  interviewing admired people on a podcast; then mine that into micro-content.
- **Build authority** with how-to posts, listicles ("top 10 reasons your website
  sucks"), and especially **live demonstrations** (live sales role-play, live design,
  live music) — doing it live signals real competence and separates you.
- Cites **Eric Edmeades'** "stage effect": *quality of presentation × size of
  audience = level of attraction* — bigger audience, bigger attraction.
- **The lead-gen math (the "head-hurting" part):** know your **close ratio** (100% is
  impossible; 10% needs work, 20–30% is decent, 50%+ is excellent). Units needed ÷
  close ratio = qualified appointments needed. Then **×10** for lead attrition
  (~9 of 10 interested people aren't qualified) = total leads to generate.
- Close ratio = total conversions ÷ total calls over the last ~6 months (e.g. 10
  closes of 20 calls = 50%). With no data, assume 50% provisionally.
- Real-world funnel example: an "attract your dream client" webinar had **10,000
  signups**, several hundred / ~2,500 attended at peak, only a small % booked a call
  with Carrie Green, and only a small % of those converted — illustrating why the
  top-of-funnel number must be large.

## Notable verbatim quotes

All Chris Do (voice-training data):

> "In order to make a hundred thousand dollars a year you gotta bring in ten thousand
> dollars a month because ten times ten equals a hundred... we're going to limit
> ourselves to 10 months of working with the extra cushion of the two months if we
> need it."

> "Every time you move that dollar north per hour you're gonna have to work less — and
> who wouldn't like to work 20 hours a month?"

> "In order for me to control that, what I do is I just raise the rate until the demand
> and supply of me, which is fairly limited, works out."

> "You don't have to pick one thing from the menu in case it doesn't suit your specific
> business."

> "I love your event, I would love to speak, but I have a minimum fee of ten thousand
> dollars to speak. Does it make sense for us to have a call? ... it's like your policy
> and you need to stick to this."

> "When someone gives you a referral they're acting as your sales rep, and sales reps
> get paid... [but] I would not send them money for the referral. What I would do is
> just send them a very high-touch personalized thank you."

> "No selling at this point — we're just networking. None of this was about sales.
> We're just trying to grow who we know."

> "Marketing is about the act of converting strangers into friends and friends into
> clients." *(Chris paraphrasing Seth Godin, This Is Marketing.)*

> "You don't have to be an authority, you can borrow it."

> "Take the number of appointments, multiply that by ten — that's how many leads you
> have to generate... nine out of ten times they're not qualified to work with you,
> only one out of ten."

> "You can now realize I'm not generating any leads at all, and that's where the
> problem is."
