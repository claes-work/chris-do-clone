## Key claims

_All dated 2023-04-04. Paraphrased unless quoted below; attributed per speaker._

- **[Chris Do]** A graceful exit from a client relationship is "non-violent, non-judging"
  — it works because it's framed as respect: *"I respect you enough to not stay in your
  way."* (Chris's reframe of the guest's technique.)
- **[Chris Do]** The message applies beyond traditional creatives — The Futur's audience
  is diverse, including tradespeople (electricians, plumbers), and anyone in a
  client/service relationship can use it.
- **[Chris Do]** He personally struggled with what he calls the **client-vendor
  relationship**: he'd know he needed to say something, but in his head it sounded
  combative and stressful, so — like most creatives — he'd bury it, "eat it," then leak
  it out as passive-aggression or a sarcastic comment (which he says he's not proud of).
- **[Chris Do]** His **business coach** solved this by offering "a couple ways of saying
  what you're thinking" that were far less toxic/aggressive than the versions Chris had
  imagined — i.e., the problem was phrasing, not the message.
- **[Chris Do]** *Synthesis (the promotable idea):* **language is the "key" or "shield"**
  — with the right words you don't need to be extra courageous; it's not about winning a
  fight, it's about saying things clearly so **there isn't a fight**.
- **[Guest — context]** The "say no" technique: tell the client *"I think the best way I
  can help you is if I get out of your way"*; do the professional thing, hand over
  everything they need, and let both parties part ways with no harm, no foul. It protects
  both sides.
- **[Guest — context]** He'd rather spend the time finding a better-fit provider than
  finish a bad engagement "for the sake of it" — bad-fit work means worse output he
  won't be proud of or promote anyway.
- **[Guest — context, self-reported biography]** His confidence to be this direct
  developed "in the last seven years to the last decade," from personal experience
  (losing his parents) and from the frustration of feeling "servile rather than in
  service." "The alternative is hell."
- **[Guest — context]** It's ultimately about **language**: he spent years finding
  phrasing that lets him change his circumstances so "everyone's sort of winning." Once
  he had the language, courage was no longer required — "the language is now my
  protection."

No contradictions with existing pages identified. Chris's "language as shield" framing
