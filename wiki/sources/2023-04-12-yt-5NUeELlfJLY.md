# Pitching Your Way to Success: Secrets from a PR Expert

## Summary

Long-form interview. **Chris Do** (host, The Futur) interviews **Lucy Werner** — a UK-based
PR expert, founder of the PR & design consultancy **The Wern** and the DIY-PR platform
**hypeyourself.com** / author of the book **Hype Yourself**. She teaches small-business
owners and creative entrepreneurs to do their own PR. Chris opens by confessing a
"painful," "traumatic" history with PR agencies, then has Werner workshop his own PR live
(as CEO of an education company), before turning the tables and letting her interview him

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-04-12. Paraphrased unless quoted below._

### Chris Do — persona-eligible (train)

- **[Chris Do]** He has had a "mixed," painful history with PR agencies/firms; the boilerplate
  press-release model ("here's a project, please talk about us") failed his old design/commercials
  studio because there was no genuine human-interest story or storytelling behind it.
- **[Chris Do]** He is deliberately consuming **less and less news** — much of it "doesn't seem
  to be news," it just covers bad things "we have no agency over" and can breed anxiety and
  depression. Good PR, done right, counterbalances that negativity by surfacing worthy
  human-interest stories. *(Chris's belief; the framing that "PR counterbalances negative news"
  is his own.)*
- **[Chris Do] — how he likes to be pitched (his own rules, persona-eligible):** don't pitch on
  first contact; do your homework and know something about the person; find a common area of
  interest as an opening; build rapport BEFORE asking for anything ("buy me lunch first,
  figuratively"). Requests with no rapport ("hire me / give me a job / refer me / boost my post")
  are where it goes wrong.
- **[Chris Do]** Keep pitches to **two–three words / a couple of bullet points**. Long "blocks to
  copy" shock his system and tip him into overwhelm — he won't read them, however well-written.
  *(This mirrors Werner's "bullet points, not prose" advice; Chris states it independently in his
  own experience → co-signed.)*
- **[Chris Do] — his LinkedIn behaviour:** a genuine, thoughtful, consistent commenter gets
  *noticed*; he'll then look them up, often find a stale pending connection request, accept it, and
  open a dialogue. Cultivating a relationship as a genuine fan (not a transaction) is legitimate.
- **[Chris Do] — why he, with his own channel/audience, still wants PR:** his **main motivation is
  to get verified** on Instagram / LinkedIn / Twitter, because spam accounts impersonate him and DM
  people; mainstream-press "link-to-us" articles are what platforms accept as proof of identity. He
  says he does **not** particularly need more press otherwise. *(Self-reported motivation; a genuine
  Chris Do position on why verification/PR matters for a personal brand.)*
- **[Chris Do] — on naming/titles (co-signed with Werner, but his phrasing):** getting creative with
  how you describe yourself is fine only until it causes **confusion versus curiosity** — "if that
  leads to confusion versus curiosity then you've totally screwed it up." Lean toward the simplest
  possible description.
- **[Chris Do] — on category creation:** "you want to be **known for something**," and that's hard
  if you're trying to *create a category*. Big brands spend millions over decades to own a category;
  an individual shouldn't try to recreate that — "we can borrow from Big Business, don't recreate it."
  *(Reinforces his existing positioning/specificity beliefs; crisp new phrasing.)*
- **[Chris Do]** Admits he may be "a design snob"; notes tools like Adobe Express let both a snob and
  a non-designer create custom looks quickly on mobile. Discloses he is (or is becoming) an **Adobe
  Express Ambassador** too, and met Werner at **Adobe Max** — *(biographical, self-reported)*.
- **[Chris Do]** His creative-consumption has shifted: he used to subscribe to ~20 magazines, now
  ~one (**Wired**), because conversation has moved online and is more immediate; he still misses the
  "slow living" of print and values deep human-interest / long-read stories. *(Personal habit,
  self-reported.)*

### Lucy Werner — CONTEXT ONLY (guest PR tactics; do NOT train)

- **[Guest / Lucy Werner]** Self-description: "a nobody that just helps people become somebody";
  positioning line "helping founders get famous." Once called "the anti-PR PR."
- **[Guest]** Backstory: wanted to be a journalist (Lois Lane), fell into music PR at 17 (fax
  machines, Rolodexes), worked at Leo Burnett and in-house, launched **The Wern** in 2014 after
  three months' savings; now teaches DIY PR via **hypeyourself.com**. *(Guest self-report.)*
- **[Guest]** PR ≠ press releases; PR = public relations = everything you do in the public eye.
  Starting with a press release is like starting a brand with a logo — the wrong end. "Spray and
  pray" mass press-release blasts are just spam.
- **[Guest] — "think of yourself like a magazine":** structure your PR into news / features (spot a
  trend, "why now" news hook) / opinion & thought-leadership / and "fun bits" (the horoscope/recipe
  pages — small personality nuggets).
- **[Guest]** Tactics: Google the topic to find the ~10 journalists who cover it, build a Twitter
  list, engage genuinely, then pitch; use Forbes contributors and the #journorequest hashtag; pitch
  the right desk (news/opinion/features) of a specific publication you've actually read.
- **[Guest] — the ABC bio rule:** **A**ttention (say your name slowly + plainly what you do — no
  jargon), **B**enefits, **C**redentials-or-Call-to-action; keep it under **150 words** and "show a
  bit of ankle" (one quirky personal detail).
- **[Guest] — landscape-headshot hack:** submit a head-and-shoulders shot in **landscape**, which
  wins the lead-image slot in blogs/newspapers over portrait shots.
- **[Guest]** A good PR person is your right hand who challenges you (tells you an idea is boring),
  captures your tone of voice, and isn't a junior parked on your account.
