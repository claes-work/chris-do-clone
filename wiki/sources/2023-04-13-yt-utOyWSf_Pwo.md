# Purpose Is Motivational, Impact Is Measurable with Kevin Finn (some explicit language)

## Summary

Long-form interview, and the **second/deeper installment** of a two-part **Kevin Finn**
conversation series on The Futur (the first episode is a stage-talk "dissertation" on
branding that this one repeatedly references and links back to; see
[[wiki/sources/2023-04-11-yt-Xzlv_KHIIUM]] once ingested). **Chris Do** hosts and
interviews **Kevin Finn** — an Australian brand-identity designer, writer, and author
(founder of the studio "The Sum Of"; publisher of *Open Manifesto*; author of a book on
brand principles). The bulk of the substantive frameworks in this video are **Kevin
Finn's**, delivered as his own philosophy: the brand-vs-business-vs-branding
distinction, "perception research," his six client-discovery questions, his refusal of
the word "client," his "get out of your way" exit script, and the title phrase itself —
**"purpose is motivational, impact is measurable"** — are all **Kevin, not Chris**. Chris
contributes framing, active summarizing/"playing back," probing questions (including a
long personal exchange about Kevin's parents' deaths and mortality), and a handful of his
own reflections on language, brands, and consumer power.

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-04-13. Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section. Speaker-attributed._

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- **[Chris Do]** Reframes the choice of vocabulary as strategy: agreeing with Kevin that a
  designer who positions as an "advisor" (not a supplier taking orders) and works
  "with" a business rather than "for" a client removes the power dynamic — two businesses
  electing to work together, "arriving at the same place."
- **[Chris Do]** On language as a tool: reflects on his own earlier struggle in the
  "client / vendor" relationship — he knew he needed to say something but every version in
  his head sounded combative, so like most creatives he "buried it," ate it, and leaked
  passive-aggressive/sarcastic comments. A business coach later gave him less-toxic ways
  to phrase what he was thinking. Conclusion: language is the key or "the shield" so you
  don't have to be courageous — it's not about winning a fight, it's about being clear so
  there is no fight.
- **[Chris Do]** Admiration for articulate people and writers: says he came to value words
  and writing "relatively late in the game," at 50, and didn't see himself as "a person
  who writes" until he wrote a book — echoes David C. Baker's line that "you gain clarity
  through articulation" (Chris cites *The Business of Expertise*).
- **[Chris Do]** On the shift of brand power to people: says we live in a wonderful time
  in which power has shifted from companies/corporations to people — companies used to
  own their brands and control feelings via repetition, but with the internet and social
  media the conversation now happens between people, not between corporations and us, so
  "companies or corporations don't own brands anymore — people like you and me do." Urges
  using that power responsibly, in both spending and speaking up.
- **[Chris Do]** Cites the "every dollar you spend is a vote for the world you want to
  live in" idea (attributes it to a source the captions garble as "analepe" — almost
  certainly **Anna Lappé**; `attribution: uncertain` on the exact wording, but Chris is
  quoting someone else, not coining it).
- **[Chris Do]** Draws a parallel between our relationship with brands and with ourselves:
  many people knowingly do things bad for them (binge eating, not getting out, unkind
  self-talk) because it serves "some darker part of us" we're not ready to change — so it
  makes sense we'd tolerate imperfect companies too.
- **[Chris Do]** On learning from well-read people: when someone who has "read a lot and
  thought a lot" speaks, "lean in, listen, shut up and learn something."

### Kevin Finn (GUEST — context only, does NOT train the persona)

- **[Kevin Finn]** Brand vs. business vs. branding: *branding* is the toolkit designers
  make (logos, websites, communications, frameworks, visuals, language) at a point in
  time; a *brand* evolves and deepens over a long period and is built by the business, not
  the designer. **"Brands become brands when society says so"** — not when the business
  declares itself one (analogy: cool people don't call themselves cool).
- **[Kevin Finn]** Designers should stop leaving value "on the table" by stopping at logo/
  website deliverables; they should offer to partner as long-term advisors guiding the
  business as it evolves — while being honest that building the brand is the business's
  job.
- **[Kevin Finn]** Refuses the word "client": tells prospects "you are not a client…
  you're a business I work with," positioning himself as an objective outside advisor who
  challenges their assumptions; says he's "also interviewing you" and will walk away from
  a bad fit — a deliberate choice, not a product of financial privilege.
- **[Kevin Finn]** Quotes former Saatchi & Saatchi worldwide creative director **Bobby
  Sherwood**: "only promote what you want to get more of" — so he only takes on and shows
  meaningful work with meaningful people.
- **[Kevin Finn]** "Perception research": before design, he runs confidential one-on-one
  interviews with staff and outside stakeholders (customers, ex-customers, partners,
  board), aggregates them anonymously into a report comparing the org's self-image to
  outsiders' perceptions — a baseline he says clients find therapeutic and highly
  valuable. Even when perceptions and self-image match (~50/50 on average), the value is
  concrete validation of assumptions.
- **[Kevin Finn]** His **six client-discovery questions**, asked in order: (1) What value
  do you provide? (2) Who are you talking to? (a *type* of person, not a demographic) (3)
  What are you saying to them (core message)? (4) What channels are you using (asked of
  them, not defaulted)? (5) Can you live up to what you say — and where's the proof? (6)
  Do you matter / why should anybody care / would you be missed if you shut down tomorrow?
  Q6 loops back to Q1: they care because of the value you provide.
- **[Kevin Finn]** Stages engagements (perception research as stage one, positioning/
  identity as later stages) with "stop-gaps," and deliberately "designs himself out of the
  equation" once the traditional design work is done — only ongoing *advisory* work
  continues. Self-described as "the worst business advice" but honest/efficient.
- **[Kevin Finn]** Graceful exit script: "the best way I can help you is if I get out of
  your way" — non-violent, non-judging, taking a financial hit rather than finishing a
  bad-fit job he wouldn't be proud of.
- **[Kevin Finn]** On language and mindset: "all transformation is linguistic" (quoting
  Ronald J. Baker's *Implementing Value Pricing*) — change the conversation, especially
  the language you use with yourself, and mindset → behavior → response follow "like
  dominoes." Credits building his verbal range to deliberate effort plus interviewing
  world-class thinkers (e.g., Noam Chomsky, Edward de Bono) for his publication *Open
  Manifesto*.
- **[Kevin Finn]** On mortality: losing his mother and father (both to cancer) within a
  few years — a two-year grief/depression — reshaped his values; every day is a gift, he
  now works ~4–5 hours a day yet is faster and more confident, and expands "doing" to
  include gym, family, and meaningful conversations. Chose to bury the personal story at
  the *back* of his book so as not to "put a thumb on the scale" before making his
  argument (the first chapter is "The Premise").
- **[Kevin Finn]** The title line: he uses abrasive language ("we don't have time to
  f*** around") deliberately as a jolt — contrasting it with the softer "every day is a
  gift." **"Purpose is motivational, impact is measurable"** — purpose is soft/moderate,
  impact is the sharper, measurable driver. Also: "impact equals relevance equals
  revenue" (negative impact drops relevance and revenue; positive impact raises both), and
  even toxic brands are brands — they survive only where there's no viable alternative or
  a locked-in incentive, and lose when people "vote" elsewhere. Quotes a retired US Army
  general: "if you don't like change, you're going to like irrelevance less."
