## Key claims

_All dated 2023-04-18. Paraphrased unless quoted. Speaker = **Narrator** (unconfirmed;
`attribution: uncertain`). Third-party ideas attributed to their real authors._

- **[Narrator]** Success works against change: the more money, lifestyle, and employees
  you accumulate, the slower and more predictable you become — comfort and consistency
  are the trap. Framed via Musashi: "what is big is easy to perceive… large numbers of men
  [are] easily predicted; an individual can easily change his mind."
- **[Musashi, cited]** *The Book of Five Rings* is a martial-strategy text whose lessons
  ("there is timing in everything"; "the spirit becomes like water… water adopts the shape
  of its receptacle"; make "your everyday stance your combat stance") transfer to business
  strategy.
- **[Narrator]** The lesson has three stepping stones — **timing, innovation, action** —
  and a **hidden fourth step, obsession with the chosen mission**, that binds them.
- **[Narrator, timing]** The IBM **Simon** (1992) was the first smartphone — touchscreen,
  icons, apps, email, GPS — but arrived too early: no cloud, hard drives too big, clunky
  plug-in cards; "the right idea at the wrong time." The iPhone (unveiled Jan 9, 2007)
  succeeded because the market and technology had caught up. Innovators fail by being too
  early (nobody gets it) or too late (the wave has passed).
- **[Narrator, innovation]** Apple's touchscreen paradigm was assembled from prior work:
  the multi-touch team, the acquisition of a trackpad maker for pinch-to-zoom, and the
  famous rubber-band scroll effect — all invented before the iPhone project crystallized.
  Holding too tightly to old habits makes you "stagnant, stale," unable to flow like water.
- **[Narrator, action]** Shipping the iPhone required massive follow-through: assembling
  the team and **changing the rules of the carrier relationship** — Apple told the carrier
  "you worry about the network, we'll worry about what's on the phone." Verizon refused
  (wanted its CDMA network); AT&T agreed, enabling a global GSM launch in 2007.
- **[Narrator, obsession]** Jobs's obsession extended to the internals and to saying *no*:
  sealed proprietary screws to keep users out; refusing to strip Google Maps/search from
  the iPhone even while competing with Android ("we want to make a better product than they
  do"); putting user experience and the "insanely great" mission above competitive spite.
  He kept innovating through his cancer years (diagnosis shared publicly, 2005 Stanford
  commencement), including planning the new Apple campus.
- **[Narrator, takeaway]** Instead of fearing the worst outcome of change, flip the
  question to "what is the best that could happen?" History is full of companies that
  stopped growing and lost relevance; "changing your mind is the most powerful thing that
  you can do." Closing line: study **timing, innovation, action, and obsession** with the
  mission to find your path to victory.
