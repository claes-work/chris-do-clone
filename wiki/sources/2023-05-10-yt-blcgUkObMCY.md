# How to Be a Powerful Communicator with Marshall Davis Jones

## Summary

Long-form interview. **Chris Do** hosts and interviews **Marshall Davis Jones** — a
competitive slam poet / battle rapper turned voice-and-communication coach who calls
himself a "world bridger" (founder of **mindbodyspeak.com**; the two first met in
The Futur's room on Clubhouse). The substantive material in this video is almost
entirely **Marshall's** teaching on the human voice: his thesis that **tone/timbre is
detected in a tenth of a second and is what people actually remember**; his idea of
**"voice work"** as consciously modulating tone to assert, de-escalate, or move a
conversation; the **"content is King, tone is Queen"** metaphor; storytelling as
"well-intentioned deceit"; a psychology of **embodied cognition / "affordances"**
applied to asking for money; and two hands-on exercises (an offering-the-object gesture
and **box breathing synchronized with the hands**). Chris contributes the interviewer's
framing, active playback/summarizing, and a handful of his own reflections — on the fear
of talking about money, on storytelling and self-deception, on being non-sentimental in
a "dematerialized" culture, and a closing synthesis about stripping away distraction to
focus on what you have to say.

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-05-10. Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section.
Speaker-attributed._

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- **[Chris Do]** On the fear around money (his framing, opening/recurring): people have
  an unhealthy relationship with money — afraid of it, afraid to talk about it, treating
  it as bad manners or culturally taboo; for some it reads as self-importance, for others
  it's fear of rejection ("if I say a number bigger than I think, they'll say no, and I
  can't handle the rejection").
- **[Chris Do]** Probes/reframes Marshall's "your voice betrays your mind": asks whether
  a fast talker's voice is *betraying* or simply *reflecting* their mind — people often
  speak fast because they're short on time and thinking of the next thing; people who
  aren't self-aware of their tone broadcast what they're really thinking versus the words
  they use ("if somebody says 'I love you' but not in a way reflective of how they feel,
  people paying attention pick up on it right away").
- **[Chris Do]** On manipulation: raises that the same tonal "let-you-in-on-a-secret"
  move a waiter uses can be deployed consciously to manipulate — the tool cuts both ways.
- **[Chris Do]** On Elizabeth Holmes / Theranos: her voice sounds "very fake" to him,
  seemingly a consciously applied affect; wonders why people didn't pick up on it.
- **[Chris Do]** On storytelling and self-deception (his own reflection, extending
  Marshall): we want to be lied to well beyond entertainment — not knowing is scary and
  we don't have time to understand everything, so we make up stories (lie to ourselves
  first) and accept lies from government, religion, and society because it makes us feel
  safer; therefore "those who are able to tell a better story tend to be more effective
  liars" (using "liars" loosely — storytellers, not deceivers by intent).
- **[Chris Do]** Vulnerable personal disclosure (biography): the hardest thing he's
  working on is getting on the same wavelength as his wife — he says they "secretly
  jockey for control," it's harmonious when one concedes and tense when their ideas of
  control collide, and he has to control his own reaction and tone so he doesn't drive
  her to question her own opinions.
- **[Chris Do]** On prized possessions (reflection): he's "not an overly sentimental
  person," has no heirlooms, nothing irreplaceable — even photos of his children are
  digital and recoverable; ties this to a broader trend that we live in an increasingly
  **dematerialized culture** where we pay more for fewer physical things and buy
  experiences (the iPhone as the example of one device replacing map books, cameras,
  planners, appointment books).
- **[Chris Do]** Playback of Marshall's method: paraphrases it as "the body will tell the
  mind how to think and how to speak and the words to choose" — a physical, psychological,
  feeling-based approach he says adds "ammunition to the arsenal."
- **[Chris Do]** Closing synthesis: in a world of "eyes, ears, nose, and a mouth," if you
  strip away the things you don't need to worry about (e.g., appearance), you can focus on
  what really matters — what you have to say, how you want to move about the world, and how
  you move people.
- **[Chris Do]** On his own media practice (content-strategy, light): notes he too fell
  into doing "a ton of these audio calls" (Clubhouse/LinkedIn/Twitter spaces) because
  people resonate with the story and the voice, and valued that the audio-only format
  freed him from minding appearance so he could focus on word, tone, and pitch; says he's
  moved away from Clubhouse as it filled with "marketers and drama rooms."

### Marshall Davis Jones (GUEST — context only, does NOT train the persona)

- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** Background: a competitive slam poet / battle rapper who
  learned to "read a room" and bridge ideas to strangers fast; a 2011 Grand Slam night
  where a friend won on the strength of *tone* (learned in acting school — "the voice is
  life") set him on voice work. Recites an excerpt of his 2008 poem **"Touch Screen"**
  (about social-media disconnection; reportedly performed for will.i.am's TRANSFORM
  conference and heard at Apple HQ).
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** **Voice work** = understanding how to modulate your tone to
  be assertive, de-escalate, move things along, slow down or speed up — "masterfully move
  and control the nervous systems of the people within earshot." Tone/**timbre** is "the
  quality of a sound," detected instantly: your voice makes an impression in a tenth of a
  second (analogy to how quickly a radio-dial listener decides to stay on a station).
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** People remember *how* you said it over *what* you said
  (invokes Maya Angelou, plus memory-task neuroscience); listeners subconsciously decide
  "do I want to sound like that / have the experience that would make me sound like that."
  Children register tone at ~four months, ~12 months before language — a calming tone
  counter to a child's tantrum de-escalates fast.
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** Describes Chris's voice as "surgical warmth" — precise and
  to the point but warm.
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** "Content is King, but tone is Queen" — on a chessboard the
  King is powerful but the Queen moves the board and moves people; mastery of tone is what
  makes a great storyteller. Amplification stack: silence → tone → voice → words.
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** Storytelling in business = "well-intentioned deceit":
  storytellers suspend your current reality for a believable different one ("not real yet
  … a vision of the world I believe could be"). Charisma is a "loaded gun" — protective or
  harmful; cites Elizabeth Holmes as charisma/voice leading people astray, and argues a
  consciously affected voice can be sustained only briefly (an investment call), not
  indefinitely.
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** Embodiment / **embodied cognition**: he gets the body
  involved immediately when learning a piece ("making the words dance"), then settles into
  natural grace; 25% of the brain's motor strip is devoted to the hands.
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** The "Venn diagram of imagination" whose center is the word
  **"is"** — imagery (fMRI-backed auditory/olfactory imagination) shapes perception; two
  people viewing the same thing hold different embodied points of view (vanilla vs.
  chocolate). Runs Chris through an exercise: hold a savory-meal image while telling a hard
  story, then contrast with a disgust image, to show how held imagery changes word choice
  and tone.
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** Money / **affordances** (neuroscience term for "anything you
  can do with your hands"): reframes asking for a fee as *offering something valuable from
  your possession* rather than being eager to get rid of it. Exercise: extend your hand
  offering an object the person may take or not (there's a price) vs. needing them to take
  it — the second reads as pushing/low-value. Etymology play: "sale" from reluctance; you
  trade *time* control. McDonald's-vs-fine-dining and Chick-fil-A-vs-plated-restaurant
  contrasts illustrate valuing the experience.
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** Conversations run in "layers" like Photoshop; shifting the
  image shifts context and can feel jarring; breaking a client's stuck context (e.g., "I'm
  not good enough to ask for money") "takes time to cook" through repetition, not one video.
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** On what he'd never sell: no physical heirlooms — the fragile,
  unsellable thing is the trust and stories people have confided in him; he won't trade one
  relationship for another.
- **[Marshall Davis Jones]** Two body tools: **box breathing** (inhale-hold-exhale-hold,
  four counts; used by the military) **synchronized with opening/closing the hands**; and a
  **yawn-stretch** ("appendiculation") every ~90 minutes to reset the nervous system (the
  "car battery" analogy; animals self-regulate this way).
