### Guest — hand-lettering artist (name uncertain; GUEST, context only, does NOT train the persona)

- **[Guest]** During the 2020 pandemic, with no client work coming in, she joined her
  first lettering challenge (**"36 Days of Type"**) to update her portfolio — and
  learned that **"quantity leads to quality"**: committing to a constrained challenge
  forces you to create more, which improves skills faster.
- **[Guest]** You must **start creating to find the motivation to create more** — the
  reverse of waiting for inspiration to strike before you create.
- **[Guest]** For years she was stuck doing only client work and couldn't make time for
  personal lettering; no one would ever hire her for lettering she wasn't posting about.
  Pushing through her first challenge made her **true style emerge faster** and got "more
  eyes" on her work because she posted it online.
- **[Guest]** The common objections ("I don't have time to practice," "I can't afford to
  work on personal projects instead of client work," "I'll update my portfolio when I
  get projects I like") are **just excuses** she once used herself.
- **[Guest]** Restates the familiar advice: **create your own dream projects, post them
  online, and dream clients will hire you for that kind of work.** Her own example:
  making **retro signage** during a challenge led to a paid commission to do retro
  signage published in a magazine — work she wouldn't have gotten without the personal
  project + posting it.
- **[Guest]** Posting personal work isn't only for landing clients — a challenge can also
  surface **digital products to sell.** While doing challenges in **Procreate** she made
  brushes for herself, noticed audience interest, and turned them into products; other
  possibilities include techniques, textures, or tutorials. You won't know what product
  to make until you do the work.
- **[Guest]** Challenges generate **audience feedback** (comments asking what brush,
  what colors, how she did it) that helps assess what to create and sell.
- **[Guest]** To pick a challenge: participate in an existing one (e.g., 36 Days of Type),
  join hers (a "lettering sun" challenge she ran the prior year), or invent your own with
  self-set deadlines (e.g., one piece per week for 10 weeks). What matters is committing
  and challenging yourself to make something you normally wouldn't.
- **[Guest]** Finishing a challenge gives **accountability** and **productivity**: one
  artwork per day is non-negotiable, so you produce far more than working on your own and
  avoid over-polishing a single piece for a month.
- **[Guest]** **Finding a style starts with defining your current interests** — build a
  **mood board** of things you like; an artist's style is "the sum of" their interests,
  experience, knowledge, and feelings. You may never fully find your style (it can take a
  lifetime), but the sooner you start the sooner it becomes recognizable.
- **[Guest]** **Constraints unlock creativity.** Cites a quote she attributes to the
  author of *Steal Like an Artist* (**Austin Kleon**; captioned "posting nutrient" /
  "still like an artist") — "nothing is more paralyzing than the idea of limitless
  possibilities." Give yourself restrictions on style, theme, or subject to get started;
  **"limitation equals freedom."** A tight, consistent theme also saves daily
  decision-making and builds audience anticipation for what comes next.
- **[Guest]** **Use Reels, not still images.** Short videos are a "proven and highly
  addictive formula" offering instant gratification — showing an entire process unfold in
  ~30 seconds keeps viewers engaged. Cites a "recent Facebook study" claiming people spend
  an average of **1.7 seconds** on a piece of content before deciding to scroll on
  (`attribution: uncertain` — self-reported figure, unverified).
- **[Guest]** Because you have only a couple of seconds, the **first few seconds must be a
  hook** people can't stop watching; when editing, make the opening the most interesting
  part.
- **[Guest]** **Design for human psychology, not trends.** Trends change fast and burn out;
  human behavior doesn't. Content that inspires and connects — built on the psychology of
  why people watch — is what goes viral. Practical tip: when you catch yourself watching a
  Reel, **stop and analyze why you didn't scroll past**, then re-implement that in your own
  Reels ("don't consume Reels like a normal person").
