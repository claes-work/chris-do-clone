# Secret to Running a $1 Million Solo Design Agency (with Brett Williams / DesignJoy)

## Summary

Long-form interview. **Chris Do** hosts and interviews **Brett Williams**, the solo
founder of **DesignJoy** — a one-person, productized design *subscription* service
(founded summer 2017) selling premium design (branding, product/UI, landing pages) for a
recurring fixed monthly fee (currently **$5,000/month**; started at $450/month). Brett is
the sole designer; the model is deliberately the inverse of high-headcount subscription
shops like Design Pickle ("an army of one" vs. thousands of employees). Chris opens by
admitting he has not prepped and is "discovering things" alongside the audience.

The substantive *how-to* content in this video — the subscription/productized model, the
"one request at a time" mechanic, the Trello workflow, "assumption-based design," the
zero-calls / zero-meetings policy, the double-your-rate-to-reduce-demand move, and the
$1M-revenue / 6-hour-day lifestyle — is all **Brett Williams'**, delivered as his own
playbook. Chris contributes framing, active summarizing ("saying it back to you"), the
pricing-ladder taxonomy, the psychological profiling of low- vs high-budget buyers, and
his own recurring teaching points (charge more to get better clients; you must be good
and fast; non-billable time hurts you; you can't understand a lesson until you live it).

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-05-12. Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section. Speaker-attributed._

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- **[Chris Do]** Lays out a **pricing ladder** from least to most sophisticated:
  (1) *hourly* — most "business owners/entrepreneurs" are really outsourced contractors
  billing timesheets; (2) *project-based fees* — you guesstimate the time and assume risk,
  so you can charge more; do it well and you make much more, do it poorly and you go out of
  business subsidizing the project with near-free work; (3) *retainer* — the client "buys
  in bulk," committing money in advance because they love working with you (stability for
  you, cost savings for them); (4) *subscription* — the newest form. Notes Ronald J. Baker
  frames subscription as "value-based pricing 2.0" / "the future," but Chris says he hadn't
  seen anyone do it *effectively* in bespoke design services — hence the interview.
- **[Chris Do]** **"If you want higher quality clients, charge more."** Restates this as a
  core, oft-taught principle: something about the *buyer* changes when you raise prices past
  a certain point — a psychological hurdle most creatives can't get over.
- **[Chris Do]** **You must be good — "period, full stop" — and you must be fast.** The
  subscription model only works if you are *both*; a slow-but-good person builds an
  unmanageable backlog. Speed comes from focus, repetition, and building systems/assets/
  workflow.
- **[Chris Do]** Reframes the counter-intuitive move: most creatives chase *volume* — more
  clients for less money, selling more units of time — but the better play is to raise rates
  and serve the *same* number of clients for twice the money, or cut the workload in half
  and serve the ones you keep better. Charging hourly "punishes you for being good" (finish
  faster, earn less); the internet's answer of "just take on more projects" is wrong.
- **[Chris Do]** Builds a **psychological profile contrast** of the two buyer types. The
  low-budget ($450/mo) buyer: often not a real company yet — technical founders / idea-stage
  people validating product-market fit, preserving cash, wanting cheap-dirty-quick; highly
  focused and therefore *needy and demanding*, then they "cut and run" when done, leaving
  little room for profit in the subscription's peaks-and-valleys. The high-budget ($5K/mo)
  buyer: an established business (only they can commit to $60k+/year), an *educated buyer* of
  creative services, more trusting, less needy (running a whole company), gives *actionable*
  constructive feedback and has a shared language. Invokes the management adage: "find someone
  smart, let them do their job, and get out of their effing way."
- **[Chris Do]** **Non-billable time hurts you** — meetings, client interactions, and
  project management are usually non-billable, so with a premium on your time you must
  streamline everything (why a single, familiar tool like Trello matters).
- **[Chris Do]** Cites **Blair Enns**: *"low variability in process equals low variability
  in outcomes"* — don't deviate from a documented process and you'll reliably get the
  outcomes others were happy with. Frames terms & conditions as a "natural filter" that
  removes bad-fit clients up front and gives you ground to stand on when a client tries to
  deviate.
- **[Chris Do]** On **experiential learning ("the human condition")**: you can hear a lesson
  taught for years, but you don't truly understand it until you live it — "we don't
  understand the fire will burn your hand until you put your hand in a fire."
- **[Chris Do]** Argument for **transparency / building in public**: Brett's viral break came
  because he shared his revenue openly; this is a case against creatives who refuse to share
  or do any PR/marketing/content — the "unintended benefit" is that someone amplifies you.
- **[Chris Do]** On **not over-reaching**: praises Brett for starting humbly ($450) and
  climbing to $5K rather than jumping "way past where you think you can deliver." A common
  failure: people with "zero proof" who don't know what they're doing chase things too big —
  "their eyes are bigger than their mouth and their stomach."
- **[Chris Do]** Notes personal/philosophical difference from Brett: Chris implies he *does*
  aspire to build teams/scale, whereas Brett doesn't — "there are some clear differences
  between you and I," said with admiration, not judgment.

### Brett Williams (GUEST — context only, does NOT train the persona)

- **[Brett Williams]** Founded DesignJoy (summer 2017) after an "inevitable layoff" from a
  boring corporate UX/UI job; saw Design Pickle doing subscription graphic design but nobody
  serving the *premium* end (branding, product design, landing pages), so he filled the gap.
- **[Brett Williams]** The model: recurring **fixed fee** ($5K/month, plus quarterly/yearly
  plans), design **productized** like software with a pricing-card page — "unlimited"
  requests and revisions, **2-3 day turnaround**; clients pause or cancel anytime. The
  *inclusions* are fixed; the *volume* of output is variable. ~20-25% of clients heavily
  under-utilize and pay anyway because the capacity is there when needed.
- **[Brett Williams]** Scale (self-reported): ~30-40 clients/month at peak (~a year ago),
  now ~20-25, mostly **B2B SaaS**. On the low end (20 clients × $5K) that's ~$100k gross/
  month — "north of a million dollars a year." Doesn't track MRR anymore.
- **[Brett Williams]** Burnout & the reset: DesignJoy "took off like a rocket ship" around
  COVID; he worked 12-13-hour days for ~2-2.5 years and was "M.I.A." from his wife and three
  kids. A single **Dan Rowden tweet** (~Feb, prior year) took him from **$80k→$160k/month**
  in a month; the flood caused his first-ever burnout. He responded by **canceling ~100
  booked calls, doubling his rate, removing the ability to book a call, and cutting draining
  clients** — refining his client list down to easy, high-value ones. Now works **~6 hours/
  day**.
- **[Brett Williams]** Raising prices repeatedly ($450 → $2K → $5K) always had the *opposite*
  of the intended effect: he raised rates to *slow demand*, but demand *increased* while
  clients got better. Believes $5K is still low for senior-level design; could charge more
  but doesn't want the added stress.
- **[Brett Williams]** **"One request at a time"** is the load-bearing mechanic: clients can
  put unlimited items in a *backlog* column but only one in the *current request* column
  (which lights up yellow if they drag a second). Managed via **Trello** (backlog → current
  request → approved); no onboarding calls — sign up in 30 seconds, get a Trello invite.
- **[Brett Williams]** **Zero calls / zero meetings, ever** — even for cancellations;
  communication is minimal, often silent until delivery. Defining these "weird" policies up
  front on the landing page means only clients bought into them sign up ("low variability").
  Large jobs (e.g., a 50-60-page site) are split into two-day deliverable chunks that can sit
  in "current request" for months.
- **[Brett Williams]** **"Assumption-based design"**: no wireframes, mood boards, or ideation
  — he goes straight to high fidelity (a SaaS landing page in ~an hour), lets the design
  speak, and iterates from client reaction. Explicitly says this is *not* the "right way" and
  only works because of his obsessive experience across hundreds of DesignJoy projects; tells
  people to "take what you want and throw away the rest."
- **[Brett Williams]** On readiness: it's not about years in the field but about obsession and
  volume of experience — he has few years but packed "20 years" of reps into ~4 years of the
  volume game. No objective benchmark; he keeps doing it because it works.
- **[Brett Williams]** Coaching/course: doesn't teach "make a million a year" (wrong
  expectation, and he wouldn't wish his path on anyone) — teaches **"take on five clients at
  $5K/month = $25K/month, life-changing money"** (~an hour a day of work). Launching a course,
  **productizeyourself.co** ($150; ~1,600 signups in a month).
- **[Brett Williams]** On scaling: the "million dollar question." Declines — he's a designer
  who likes designing, has no aspiration to manage/lead a team, doesn't need more money or
  time. Content as a one-man band.
- **[Brett Williams]** Origin of leads: **built in public** on Indie Hackers from day one,
  publishing revenue milestones; that's how Dan Rowden found and tweeted him. Now Twitter
  (handle "Brett from DJ") is his only marketing channel; tweets every few days.
