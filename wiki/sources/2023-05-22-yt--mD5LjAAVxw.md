# Is it Better to Charge More or Less?

## Summary

Short clip. **Chris Do** runs a two-person pricing exercise built on **"inversion
thinking"** — deliberately arguing the *opposite* of what he believes (why creatives
should charge *less*) as a way to expose how weak those arguments are, then flipping to
the case he actually holds (why creatives should charge *more*). A second speaker is
present and participates throughout; Chris explicitly hands the exercise to them
("**I'd like your help here** ... tell me why it's better for us to charge less").

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-05-22. Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section. Speaker-attributed._

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- **[Chris Do]** Introduces **"inversion thinking"** as a persuasion/reasoning tool: when
  arguing straight for a position doesn't land, take the *opposite* stance and argue it
  earnestly — the flaws surface on their own. He asks the guest to make the *charge-less*
  case sincerely so it can be dismantled.
- **[Chris Do]** States his standing goal: he tries to get creative people to **price
  projects higher so they get paid more** — which lets them **work less, do higher-quality
  work, employ people, treat those people better, and grow the economy**. Notes this
  message "sometimes doesn't work," which is why he reaches for the inversion technique.
- **[Chris Do]** The **charge-more case** he actually believes: **"a high tide, all boats
  rise"** — the value of creativity in society goes *up* if everyone charges more. Charging
  more means you don't have to "kill yourself" working: you can **take on less work,
  increase profits, and hire people**.
- **[Chris Do]** **Charge high, then you can afford to give:** if you charge **high rates to
  big corporate clients**, you can afford to charge **lower rates — or even do pro bono
  work — for those who can't afford you (e.g. non-profits)**. Charging more lets you serve
  *more* people, not fewer.
- **[Chris Do]** (During the inversion, voicing the *charge-less* arguments to knock them
  down — **these are positions he explicitly disowns**, "the arguments are fake on my part
  because I don't really believe any of them"): people who defend charging less claim
  hourly billing "punishes efficiency, innovation and experience"; that charging less means
  **less friction in the sales process, no new skill set to learn, being liked, being
  affordable to everyone** (so no need to pick a niche/lane or make hard business
  decisions), and letting you **stay a "maker" not a "manager"** by doing the work yourself.
  His point: **go just "one layer deeper" and every one of these collapses** — "it doesn't
  take much more than that."
- **[Chris Do]** Rebuts the hourly-billing defense inline: if you charge a set amount and
  get *faster*, hourly billing means you get **paid less** for the same result — the
  internet's answer ("then take on more work") doesn't actually make sense.

### Unnamed guest/colleague (context only — does NOT train the persona)

- **[Guest]** Argues, at Chris's request, the earnest *charge-less* case and observes that
  **many people argue for charging less without realizing that's what they're doing** —
  then agrees it falls apart "one layer deep."
- **[Guest]** Adds a charge-more point Chris credits as new to him: **if everyone charges a
  little more, it lifts the entire profession** — it gives people "at our level or starting
  out" something to aspire to and grow into. Laments that **senior, experienced creatives
  moving their prices in the wrong direction leaves no room for people just out of school**.
- **[Guest]** Adds: charging more lets you **afford to hire the *best* person** (not just
  "hire people"), which compounds your own transformation.
- **[Guest]** Adds the **self-worth dimension**: charging more affects your **self-esteem,
  self-worth, and self-confidence** — the pride of knowing you put years into your craft,
  proved the doubters wrong, and now earn on par with "a dentist or a high-profile lawyer,"
  a signal that society values art, design, and divergent creative thinking.
