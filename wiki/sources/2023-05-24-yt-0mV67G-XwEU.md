# Why Creatives Need to Charge More — Chris Do interviews Paco de Leon

## Summary

This is a **long guest interview** (~11.5k words) on The Futur channel. **Chris Do**
hosts and interviews **Paco de Leon**, a financial planner / bookkeeper who runs a
bookkeeping firm for creative businesses and wrote the book *Finance for the People*.
Unlike a pure guest talk, Chris is an **active, substantive participant** here: he drives
the framing, contributes his own pricing philosophy (value-/project-based pricing over
hourly, "charge more" arguments, the pricing-a-logo video, an inversion-thinking
exercise), and shares autobiographical detail. The topic is the money psychology of
creatives and why they under-charge.

## Key claims (_All dated 2023-05-24. Paraphrased unless quoted. Speaker-attributed._)

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- **[Chris Do]** Opens with a confession: he loves to make money but hates accounting;
  his "trick in life" to avoid dealing with bookkeeping is to "make so much money and have
  so much profit margin it won't even matter." (Paco later names this the "firehose
  strategy.")
- **[Chris Do]** Autobiography: a first-generation immigrant with no direction in high
  school; an **aptitude test told him to become an accountant or a stockbroker**, and he
  assumed that was his destiny (introvert, likes logic and machines). He was then
  introduced to "a professional working artist called a graphic designer," which he calls
  his "salvation" and the "ray of light" that redirected him — otherwise he'd likely have
  ended up in finance.
- **[Chris Do]** On hourly billing (his signature position): "no matter what you charge
  for your hourly rate… the issue there is that you're having a conversation where you're
  trading time for money." Even a high hourly rate keeps the focus on selling **time**
  rather than on impact / deliverables / value to the client. He frames it as "a
  conceptual, philosophical problem" and "a habit… many of us just need to get out of."
- **[Chris Do]** Rebuts the common counter-argument (effective hourly rate): people say
  value-based pricing is pointless because "you just take the amount you were paid and
  whatever hours you worked and that's your effective hourly rate." He agrees the math
  works out but insists the framing matters — staying focused on time vs. impact is the
  real problem.
- **[Chris Do]** His most-watched video is about **pricing a logo**; it draws polarized
  comments, with pushback (from other creatives, not clients) that charging a lot for a
  logo is "total BS" because clients will just hire someone cheaper. He reads this as
  creatives **arguing against their own self-interest**.
- **[Chris Do]** Ran a **Twitter experiment**: asked the most anyone ever charged for
  "just a logo" (no strategy/marketing). Answers spanned from **$0 to $750,000** for a
  single logo — used to show people how "limited thinking will actually limit the kind of
  success you're going to have."
- **[Chris Do]** "**High tide, all boats rise**" — if everyone charges more, the value of
  creativity in society goes up and the whole profession lifts. He is disheartened by
  senior, experienced people "moving the prices in the wrong direction," leaving no room
  for people just out of school to aspire to.
- **[Chris Do]** The case for charging more (his list): work less / take on less work,
  increase profits, hire people (including people **more talented than you** — assuming
  no one is better is "arrogant"), afford insurance / taxes / a retirement account / a
  living wage for staff, **subsidize** lower rates or pro bono work for nonprofits by
  charging big corporate clients high rates, and boost your own **self-esteem /
  self-worth / self-confidence** (earning at the level of "a dentist or high-profile
  lawyer").
- **[Chris Do]** Uses **inversion thinking**: asks Paco to argue earnestly for charging
  *less*, then shows every pro-cheap argument (less sales friction, no new skills, being
  liked, being affordable to everyone, staying a "maker" not a "manager") collapses if
  you "just go one layer deeper."
- **[Chris Do]** Mindset: "if you always snap back to look at the bigger picture of life,
  very few things will actually really bother you." Tells a story about wrongly accusing a
  Converse support agent over a duplicate charge (he'd bought two pairs to try one and
  return the other) as a reason he stays out of day-to-day finances; his time is "worth
  more than the $45" he was trying to recover.
- **[Chris Do]** On managing money: he leans on his **wife**, a creative person who does
  the company's finances, and only wants to be told "faster or slower" (spend more / slow
  down) — analogizing to Gene Hackman only wanting a director to say "faster or slower."
- **[Chris Do]** Positions his own work (resources, courses, tools) and Paco's
  (bookkeeping, financial services) as complementary halves of the same mission: help
  creatives earn enough to afford help and build a financial cushion so a small economic
  shift doesn't ruin them.

### Paco de Leon (GUEST — context only, does NOT train the persona)

_All below are Paco's, dated 2023-05-24, paraphrased. Financial-planning / behavioral
advice included for source completeness only — NOT persona training data._

- **[Paco de Leon]** Self-intro: helps creative professionals "feel less weird about
  their finances"; runs a service company (bookkeeping for production companies /
  marketing agencies) plus a content side (podcast, ideas online) aimed at making people
  more comfortable talking about money.
- **[Paco de Leon]** Names Chris's approach the "**firehose strategy**" and says she uses
  it too — having money removes a lot of friction.
- **[Paco de Leon]** Evolutionary-biology framing: humans are wired to fixate on
  **scarcity** (attention to dwindling food/water aided survival), which stresses us into
  action; modern sales tactics ("only three left," Black Friday time-scarcity) exploit
  that wiring, making a "lack mindset" natural. (Guest theory.)
- **[Paco de Leon]** Creative people have a "**double whammy**": their work is a direct
  extension of the self, so a low price feels like a verdict on their worth — commingling
  identity, value, and price. Sometimes you must take "a very cold and calculated look" at
  skills and recognize a low offer is "just tragically market forces," not a reflection of
  self.
- **[Paco de Leon]** Money worldview is programmed by upbringing/environment (extreme
  example: a client who unconsciously linked "payday" to abuse via an alcoholic father).
  Mindset is "one of the few things you can alter and control." Ways to surface unhealthy
  money narratives: watch your self-talk ("people like me don't invest"), observe others'
  money talk, meditate ("you can think a thought and not be that thought").
- **[Paco de Leon]** Why creatives resist the "charge more" message: change is painful;
  people prefer "the devil they know" and would rather keep a smaller mindset than confront
  that they may have wasted years under-charging. Transformation only happens when the pain
  of staying the same exceeds the pain of changing.
- **[Paco de Leon]** On pain-based marketing: agitating messaging that amplifies a
  prospect's pain "works," but she declines to use it — she doesn't want to exploit people
  already in pain. (Chris jokingly frames her as having "indoctrinated" him in
  manipulative marketing; it is banter.)
- **[Paco de Leon]** Actionable advice: schedule **weekly finance time** (≥20 min, ~1 hr
  for the self-employed) — start by just gathering logins into a password manager, then
  gently review spending; "be gentle with yourself," it's like easing into the gym.
- **[Paco de Leon]** The "**window of tolerance**" tool (she attributes it to psychiatrist
  **Daniel J. Siegel**): a 1–8 scale; check your emotional state before financial decisions
  and self-regulate back to center (cold water / walk if numb; deep breaths / pet the dog
  if hyper), because "the state you're in determines the quality of the decisions."
- **[Paco de Leon]** Her own argument for project-based over hourly (which Chris praises as
  a fresh framing): what a client pays for isn't the hours but the accumulated wins,
  **losses, failures, and mistakes** over 5–25 years of experience — best reflected in a
  project rate, "a true reflection of the value you're providing."
- **[Paco de Leon] (self-reported)** Confesses her **bookkeeping agency billed hourly
  until "the beginning of this year,"** then switched to flat/value-based pricing; she says
  it "changed the business," gave her more confidence, and let her finally see "the light
  at the end of the tunnel" on financial goals. Benefits she cites: predictable monthly
  recurring revenue, better cash-flow and investment decisions, and the ability to retain
  talent and pay staff a living wage. Prior resistance: standardizing a bid process across
  bookkeepers without losing money, and fear of losing clients when moving from ~$100/hr to
  ~$1,500/month. (All self-reported, not independently verified.)
- **[Paco de Leon]** Closing pitch: read *Finance for the People* (written for creatives
  who feel "unseen and underserved… by the personal finance industry"); visit
  thehellagroup.com; subscribe to the Nerd Letter.
