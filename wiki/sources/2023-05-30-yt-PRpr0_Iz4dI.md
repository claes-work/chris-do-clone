# From Boring to Iconic: Designing With Personality

## Summary

Short two-speaker dialogue on The Futur about the wave of luxury/automotive rebrands
("Helveticizing" — brands sanding their identities down to simple sans-serif wordmarks and
blocks) and the tension between **legibility/simplification** and **personality/expression**
in design.

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-05-30. Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section. Speaker-attributed._

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- **[Chris Do]** On the backlash to luxury/auto rebrands: critics say in one breath they want
  funkiness, individualism, heritage and intricate marks — then reject the new simplified
  marks. Chris calls this **hypocrisy**: "we want personality, but when the personality doesn't
  agree with the way we think, then we want it to be boring." His counsel: "everybody just
  relax."
- **[Chris Do]** **Shape-recognition over legibility.** People don't read individual letters,
  they **recognize shapes** — cites tests where scrambled letters/words remain readable. So the
  hand-wringing over legibility is overblown; "anybody with half a brain can figure this out."
- **[Chris Do]** Skilled designers usually know what they're doing: people who "spend a lot of
  money working on these things, developing their craft… they know what they're doing. Not
  always, but they know what they're doing." (Paired with a jab that people online *seem* smart
  and talented "but they're really not" — dismissive of armchair critics.)
- **[Chris Do]** **The business-model frame — a logo doesn't sell the product.** "If the car is
  basic crap, I don't care how good your logo is; and if the car is great, conversely, I don't
  care how bad your logo is." You're not "buying the car to drive the logo." Car companies
  "notoriously" have bad logos (and ugly cars) — so a logo is far down the list of what
  actually matters. Look at the larger picture / the business model.
- **[Chris Do]** **Iconography over letterforms is a bold, smart move.** Over time you associate
  a unique symbol/mark/wordmark with an idea and a *feeling* of a consistent product or service.
  Most people can't even read or correctly draw the marks they love — the **BMW roundel** (few
  know what the symbol comes from), the **Mercedes three-pointed star** (people asked to draw it
  often draw the peace symbol instead) — "and nobody's complaining about it." So Kia (and
  companies like it) moving from letterforms into iconography is "big and bold and smart."
- **[Chris Do]** **Don't hire an expressive designer and then sand off the personality.** When a
  client nitpicks a hand-lettered form ("the G isn't quite logical") they're asking the designer
  to "move away from why they hired you in the first place." What such a designer brings is
  personality — "expressive and, most importantly, human." "You could draw them Helvetica if
  they wanted, but what's the point?"
- **[Chris Do]** Endorses (and reframes as his own view) the idea that **legibility is not always
  the job** — agreeing with James that a book cover's or poster's job can be to provoke curiosity
  rather than to explain.

### "James" (GUEST — context only, does NOT train the persona)

- **[James]** Anti-typography-snobbery anecdote: as a design student, hated peers who'd pick up a
  restaurant menu and criticize its typography/kerning/leading — "is the burger a good burger?"
  There's always a bigger picture than the type.
- **[James]** Has designed **every cover** for one publisher for ~10 years (their 10th
  anniversary); many covers are close to illegible, but they're shown 5–10 at a time or exhibited
  at the **Tate Modern** / **Museum of Modern Art**. A cover's job is not to summarize the plot
  ("a death that occurred in a lake") but to say **"be curious, pick me up."**
- **[James]** A cover/poster uses the same **hierarchy**: something big to stop you, then a
  tagline/three words that create curiosity (going with or against the image), then the logo as an
  "uppercut" that lands. ("Something big to stop them… then the hierarchy… then the uppercut.")
- **[James]** His NYC **bus-shelter and subway posters** were deliberately *not* clear: the
  audience passes them repeatedly (twice a day, several times a week), and by the third/fifth/
  seventh viewing they get the "oh, I get it — that's awesome." **"Built-in timing."**
