## Key claims (_All dated 2023-06-04. Paraphrased unless quoted. Speaker-attributed._)

### James Victore (GUEST — context only, NOT persona training data)
- His standard answer to portfolio-review requests: **his opinion of your portfolio
  doesn't matter** — what matters is how the work makes *you* feel, how consistent you
  are, how much practice you put in, and how good you are at getting the work "out the
  door." (paraphrase)
- On when to quit a creative pursuit as "just a hobby": **never** — but persevering
  *alone*, without sharing, talking to people, or asking the right questions, leads
  only to going "to the grave pissed off." Perseverance must be paired with seeking
  mentors and asking for what you want. (paraphrase)
- **"Even if you think your work is bad, keep going, because eventually it's going to
  get good."** People he encouraged (e.g., a woman who paints flowers) visibly improved
  over time because they kept doing the work. (near-verbatim; guest)
- Creators are **poor judges of their own work** — he calls himself "a terrible judge"
  of his own beauty, strength, and the power of his words — and are often poor judges of
  others' work too. So the remedy is external: talk to people, seek direction. (paraphrase)
- He concedes he cannot fully **reconcile the two internal voices** — one saying the work
  is terrible, one saying keep going — and is honest that he has no clean answer.
  (paraphrase)
- Only **once he has "a foothold"** / something to say does he start adding real
  "quality criticism." He admits some work he personally thinks is terrible is
  nonetheless commercially successful — "so who am I?" (paraphrase)
- The harder, more useful (and highly individual) question for most makers is not
  technique but **what to do after making the work**: "most people know how to put ink
  on paper, but they don't know what to do after that... how do I commercialize this?"
  (paraphrase; guest)
