# How to Unleash YOUR Creative: W/ James Victore

> Format: The Futur long-form interview (~14k words). **Chris Do hosts and interviews
> guest James Victore** — artist/designer/author, best known for bold, expressive,
> word-driven poster and book-cover work (pieces in the Museum of Modern Art) and the
> book *Feck Perfuction*. Chris asks the questions and offers his own framings; James
> tells the stories. **Only Chris Do's lines train the persona.** James Victore is
> **GUEST CONTEXT** — a subject in his own right, not this clone's subject. His
> biographical self-reports and design philosophy do **not** feed `beliefs.md`/`voice.md`.

## Summary

A wide-ranging conversation on creativity, legibility, teaching, and giving your work
away. James Victore, the guest, argues design should be "about what it says, not what it
looks like," that illegibility is often fine (audiences are intelligent and reward
effort), and that a designer's real job is giving people **permission** to be
themselves. He tells several moving stories from his book *Feck Perfuction* — the
"bricklayer" parable, a poet's uncle who called poetry unprofitable, and a Russian
assistant "destroyed" for drawing on a wall — all illustrating how family-planted
"pre-recorded voices" kill creativity. He walks through his career philosophy (the four
"lives of the artist"), his shift to becoming his own client, and his new venture
*Your Work as a Gift* (site, newsletter, and the *Born Creative* masterclass built on 20+
years teaching at the School of Visual Arts).

Chris Do plays interviewer and periodic counterpoint. His own persona-eligible
contributions cluster around: a defense of the redesigned Kia logo and iconography over
legibility (recognizing shapes, not letters); the primacy of the product over the mark
("I'm buying the car to drive the logo"); appropriateness as the real design variable;
The Futur's mission ("help a billion people make a living doing what they love") tied to
Maslow's self-actualization; his "you are the gift" stage framing; and his "bricklayer"
metaphor for un-thinking production work. The episode is largely James's showcase; Chris's
material here restates positioning he holds elsewhere.

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-06-11. Paraphrased unless quoted. Speaker-attributed._

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- On the redesigned **Kia logo** controversy (internet designers mocked it as reading
  "KN"): Chris backs it as a "big, bold and smart move" to move **away from letterforms
  into iconography**. Over time you associate a unique symbol/word-mark with the *idea and
  feeling* of a consistent product — BMW's roundel, Mercedes's three-pointed star (people
  asked to draw it often draw a "P"), Lamborghini's bull, Ferrari's lettering — and nobody
  complains. He says he never saw a "KN." (paraphrase)
- "Everybody seems so smart and so talented on the internet, but they're really not that
  talented." He argues the internet critics contradict themselves — demanding funkiness and
  individualism, then wanting boring legibility once the personality disagrees with them.
  (paraphrase / part quote)
- **We recognize shapes, not individual letters** — scramble the letters in a word and you
  can still read it; the people who spent money developing the mark "know what they're
  doing." (paraphrase)
- **The product outranks the mark**: "if the car is a piece of crap, I don't care how good
  your logo is; if the car is great, I don't care how bad your logo is." You buy the car to
  drive it — a piece of moving sculpture, a visceral connection — not the logo. Car
  companies "notoriously" have bad logos and ugly cars. (paraphrase / part quote)
- **Appropriateness is the real variable**: there is a time and place for highly legible
  design (he "loves Helvetica" as much as anyone) and a time for expressive handwriting —
  you would not set a manual for operating life-saving equipment in bold expressive
  handwritten type. (paraphrase)
- Restates James's "four lives of the artist" back to him and endorses the takeaway: don't
  "slip into the night quietly" — share your knowledge and "make a ruckus" (crediting Seth
  Godin), help move the ball forward. (paraphrase)
- **Why he teaches**: he and James (and people like Carlos Segura) are "the exception versus
  the norm," and *because of that* he teaches now. Chris identifies as a teacher and says
  he has taught for 15+ years. (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- His **"bricklayer" stage framing** (which set up James's parable): "We're all bricklayers.
  If we're doing production, if we're not thinking, we're not consulting, we're not
  advising, we're bricklayers. And there's nothing wrong with that." (near-quote)
- Characterizes James as a **Russian nesting doll**: designer → artist → writer →
  philosopher → therapist / loving human. (paraphrase — a description *of the guest*, not a
  self-claim)
- **The Futur's mission**: "to help a billion people make a living doing what they love."
  His causal chain: if people do what they love they'll be happier; happy people have
  stronger relationships, make better parents, and make more money — "less fighting, less
  wars, less hate crimes, less murder" — a more beautiful world because people live to their
  highest potential. He maps this to **Maslow's hierarchy of needs**, with
  self-actualization at the top. (paraphrase / part quote)
- His **"you are the gift"** stage framing (parallel to James's "your work as a gift"): for
  gifts to work you can't keep them — you have to give them to people. (paraphrase)
- His **LinkedIn bio**: "I help you get paid to do what you love." (quote of his own bio)
- Reads James's *Born Creative* assignment ("big nothing, little nothing"; "always the
  other") as **genius teaching**: a brief you can't solve with tropes or easy patterns,
  forcing you to pull something out of yourself, then a second commercial phase that
  activates "right brain creative, left brain let's go make some money." (paraphrase)
- On hosting: consistent with his stated later-career shift, he lets the guest carry the
  conversation and keeps himself in the interviewer seat. (paraphrase)

### James Victore (GUEST — context only, does NOT train the persona)

_Biographical items are HIS self-reports unless independently verified._

- **Bio (self-reported)**: moved to New York at 19; "knew what I freaking wanted," so after
  ~2 years he was **asked to leave design school** — and was happy about it; started working
  professionally 3–4 months after dropping out at ~21 and "has not stopped." (paraphrase)
  [self-reported]
- **Bio (self-reported)**: started as a **book-jacket designer**, working out of a veteran
  book-designer teacher's studio (covers for big authors "from Fitzgerald all the way
  through"); taught ~20 years at the **School of Visual Arts**; work shown at MoMA (his
  first show), the Tate Modern; wife Shannon, two young kids; long-distance runner "since
  before high school." (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- **Bio (self-reported)**: reared in a military family; his father flew (a pilot), was away
  most of his childhood, and brought back posters, manga, and comics from Spain and
  Thailand; his mother worked in the **reference department of a university library** and,
  from ages ~10–12, gave him *Graphis*, *Print*, and European design annuals to look at
  while he waited for her — an unconscious graphic-design education. (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- **Core design credo**: his work "has always been about **what it says, not what it looks
  like**." He "paints in words" — ideas and typography over decoration; visuals are just
  "the teaspoon of sugar" to carry larger ideas. (paraphrase)
- **On legibility**: he doesn't mind when people have to think; he gives his audience "the
  benefit of the doubt" that they're intelligent and will make the effort — and if they
  don't, they're not his audience. His book covers are near-illegible on purpose: they say
  "be curious, pick me up," not "this is a book about a death in a lake." His NYC subway/bus
  posters used **built-in timing** — he never wanted immediate legibility, because commuters
  would decode them over repeated viewings. (paraphrase)
- **Advice to young designers**: "do yourself a favor and don't become a designer," because
  designers read the same books, dress the same, and teach themselves there's an external
  "answer" they don't have — when the answer is their own opinion and voice. (paraphrase)
- **On the artist/designer split**: he doesn't see the delineation; cites Paul Rand and
  Armin Hofmann as commercial artists who were artists. Influences: Franz Kline, Cy Twombly,
  Robert Motherwell, Jasper Johns, Picasso, Toulouse-Lautrec. (paraphrase)
- **Kia logo take**: he initially liked the redesign until "somebody peed in the pool" by
  telling him about the "KN" controversy — after which he couldn't unsee it. His verdict:
  if Kia "does a damn good job," like UPS or FedEx after their changes, nobody will
  complain; "we can get used to things." (paraphrase)
- **"Four lives of the artist"** (career arc): (1) *Who is James Victore?* — anonymity;
  (2) *Get me James Victore* — in demand; (3) *Get me someone like James Victore* — the
  cheaper/younger knockoff; (4) *Who is James Victore?* — fading out. To stay vital he
  switched from making commercial work to **becoming his own client** and getting paid for
  **wisdom** (books, speaking, teaching). (paraphrase)
- **On *Feck Perfuction***: the title is about doing your best work "but not letting
  perfection stop you" — perfection, procrastination, self-doubt, over-thinking. (paraphrase)
- **The "bricklayer" parable** (*Feck Perfuction*, ch. 10, "Your Lot in Life," told as a
  true story): a protégé wanted to be a screenwriter, but his father insisted he be a
  bricklayer — "your lot in life." After 10 years laying bricks, at the funeral of his
  father's best friend the father read a eulogy *he had written*, and the son wept —
  realizing his father was a writer. Moral: "we deny ourselves our dreams so hard." The
  protégé is now a screenwriter. (paraphrase)
- **The poet parable**: a coaching client wanted to be a poet but an uncle had told him
  "no money in poetry." Victore told him to go ask the uncle how many poems he'd actually
  written and submitted — "it's a lie." Victore says he personally knows three people
  making "a damn good living as poets." (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- **"Keep going"**: even if you think your work is bad, keep going, because eventually it
  gets good; "we're terrible judges" of our own work — and of others'. His standard answer
  to portfolio requests: "my opinion of your portfolio doesn't matter — what matters is how
  your work makes you feel," how consistent you are, and how good you are at "getting it out
  the door." (paraphrase)
- **The Russian assistant story**: an assistant, always wanting to be an artist, drew a
  meticulous picture of her parents holding hands — on her bedroom **wall** — and they
  "flipped"; that moment "destroyed" her chance of becoming an artist (she'll always work
  *near* artists, never leap). It prompted his piece "**It's Only Paint**": we have to let
  kids create, even though letting "the genie out of the bottle" is messy and dangerous, so
  give it a safe space. (paraphrase)
- **Permission**: he sees a big part of his role as **giving people permission** — telling
  his friend Norman (a marketer who'd started drawing), in his "most fatherly tone,"
  "I think you're already an artist. Keep going." He can't give "fairy dust" or magic, only
  encouragement, inspiration, and directions ("here's a vein" = a path). We're "all born
  wildly creative"; the "pre-recorded voices" that say no come originally from family.
  (paraphrase)
- **Your Work as a Gift**: his website changed from jamesvictore.com to
  yourworkasagift.com (under wife Shannon's influence) — "we want to be about other people."
  When you understand your work is a gift it changes what you make and who you work for; your
  purpose is no longer a paycheck or pleasing a boss/client but **making yourself happy
  first**, and that excitement spreads. The gift "is to the giver"; cites *Romeo and Juliet*
  ("the more I give, the more I have, for both are infinite") and Napoleon Hill. (paraphrase)
- **Offers**: *Born Creative*, a ~$199 self-study masterclass distilled from 20 years at
  SVA (abstract assignments like "big nothing, little nothing" and "always the other," then a
  commercial-brief second phase); a *Love Letters* newsletter; and a forthcoming
  three-tier subscription, "**The Right Question**." (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- **Identity/brand**: "I just want to get paid to be James Victore… I want everybody to get
  paid to be themselves." He hopes people describe him as authentic, vulnerable, bold, and
  funny. (paraphrase)
