## Summary

Classroom/critique-style session, evidently filmed in a teaching setting (an art school where Chris Do served as faculty), led by **Chris Do** as the primary instructor/critic. The framing question is "what separates good designers from great designers?" Chris fields answers from students (named Patrulla/"patrulla," Mimi/"Mimi," and Ming — Ming appears to be a fellow faculty member, not a student). Rather than answer directly, Chris teaches a method for making fuzzy quality judgments concrete: instead of debating the near-impossible gradient between "good" and "great," define traits on a slider (e.g. contrast: more = better, less = worse), build a checklist of such parameters, and use it to critique work objectively.

Along the way Chris argues against the cultural pressure to "do more," advocating mastery of one craft first ("do less but do better"), defends people who master mundane crafts, warns that online success narratives are often scams and feed anxiety/depression in young people, and shares client-brief advice: stay in the discomfort of asking clarifying questions until you have real clarity. He recounts a formative lesson from Dr. Samuel Holtzman about what teaching actually means (students being able to make similar decisions without you present), and an anecdote about a student, Daniel, asking him to define "elegant" — exposing that Chris had been using undefined words.

Chris Do is the clear teacher here; his statements are persona-eligible. Ming, Mimi, and Patrulla are other speakers and are NOT persona voice.

## Key claims

All dated 2023-06-14. Paraphrased unless in the quotes section.

- **[Chris Do]** The good-vs-great distinction is almost impossible to pin down objectively — even ten people in a room won't agree on what "great" design is; but bad-vs-great has a much bigger contrast and is easier to judge. So he reframes the exercise to bad-vs-good.
- **[Chris Do]** Critical-thinking practice: when he has an idea, he tries to disprove it as fast as possible. He demonstrates by disproving the "great designers do more than design" claim — asking whether great designers exist who ONLY do design (yes), which falsifies the claim.
- **[Chris Do]** He is often misquoted as looking down on people who "just master a craft" — he rejects this. He believes the world works better when people put in their 10,000–20,000 hours to master something.
- **[Chris Do]** There is nobility in doing humble things well (he references a Latin quote from the book *The Obstacle Is the Way*). He saw this philosophy manifested in Japan — pride taken in cleaning a table, taking out trash, or preparing a piece of fish.
- **[Chris Do]** The route to excellence is to do less but do it better; you should master something first before moving on (while acknowledging those with capacity for more can do more).
- **[Chris Do]** Online examples of very young, materially successful people create an unreal narrative about what's achievable in a given time; he estimates (unscientifically) ~90% are scams and will eventually be caught. This false narrative seeps into the subconscious and contributes to high depression and anxiety among young people.
- **[Chris Do]** It's genuinely hard just to be "good" — figuring out good design (relative to great) is very difficult, which is why he advocates focusing on being good.
- **[Chris Do]** Method for concrete critique: pick one parameter that is clearly good or bad (e.g. contrast), place it on a gradient slider (more contrast = better, less = worse), build a long list of such parameters; the list gives a sense of whether a design is good.
- **[Chris Do]** Teaching lesson from Dr. Samuel Holtzman: teaching means your students can look at work and make similar decisions without you being there. Holtzman's method — write down the five things you look for when evaluating work, put them on the board, and have students run their own work down the checklist ("the gauntlet of five").
- **[Chris Do]** Anecdote: a smart former student named Daniel asked him to define "elegant" after Chris repeatedly used the word in critique — Chris realized he'd been throwing around words he hadn't defined and that had no shared definition.
- **[Chris Do]** Client-brief tip #1: don't walk away from a client afraid to ask what you need to know; stay in the "pocket of discomfort" long enough to get clarity. His late business coach advised telling clients up front that the process will be grueling and full of questions that may annoy them — but promising you won't run off and build something without clear understanding of the problem and what they want.
- **[Chris Do]** Vague client words ("make it epic," "clean and modern," "organic") feel meaningful but are dangerous to act on by assumption. Practicing articulating the fundamentals to others further cements and clarifies the client's own thinking and speeds up decisions.
- **[Ming — other speaker, not persona]** Suggested "great" may involve the capacity/willingness to push yourself to failure to achieve your defined success; faculty discuss how stepping outside the creative-strategy realm into other interests can channel indirect insights back in.
- **[Patrulla — other speaker, not persona]** Proposed that a great designer "does more than design" in addition to being a good designer (a claim Chris then disproved).
- **[Mimi — other speaker, not persona]** Offered that a trait of bad design is not using the design principles, and named "contrast" as an example principle.
