# Why Copying Works—Copy, Combine, Transform

> Live workshop excerpt (audience present) delivered solo by **Chris Do** (@thefutur,
> 2023-06-29). Chris teaches throughout; audience members "Hal" and "Julia" and a few
> unnamed attendees only offer short answers inside his Socratic prompts. Single trainer —
> only Chris Do trains the persona. Clip cuts off mid-sentence on the copy→transform point.

## Summary

Chris runs a workshop on **reframing** and how it connects to why people fail to learn what
they came to learn. He opens by defining reframing through a value-perception analogy: the
same piece of art looks cheap in an ugly frame and valuable in a beautiful frame with good
lighting and no surrounding clutter — so **the packaging and presentation of ideas matters a
lot**, and reframing lets you look at a problem from a different point of view. He
demonstrates with the punctuation sentence "A woman without her man is nothing," read once by
a man and once by a woman, showing the same words carry opposite meaning depending on where
you "put the punctuation." He then cites the book *What's Your Problem?* (reframing is the
most in-demand thing they're asked to teach) and its **elevator example**: reframing "how do
we make the elevator go faster" into "how do we lessen the frustration of people waiting"
unlocks cheap solutions — mirrors, music, a TV, snacks — instead of buying a new elevator.

Chris then reframes his own struggle as a teacher/coach: he gives people tools, techniques,
and frameworks, yet they don't follow through. Through Socratic back-and-forth with the
audience he surfaces candidate causes — people don't like being told what to do, the need for
instant gratification and avoiding hard work, and a **disconnect between the solution and the
problem the student can actually see** (Matrix "don't worry about the vase" analogy: give
solutions directed at problems students recognize right away). He offers a **three-learner-type
model**: (1) high-level learners who get a concept from a table of contents — tend to be
leaders, managers, innovators; (2) story-format learners for whom parables work; (3) the
"pesky" kinesthetic / "believe it when I see it, I'll do it myself" type — who can become
almost uncoachable.

The remedy is a mastery mindset drawn from the Japanese concept **Shu-Ha-Ri** (shared with
him by a friend): *Shu* = protect/preserve the master's method faithfully (~10 years, echoing
Gladwell's 10,000 hours); *Ha* = break, shatter, question everything; *Ri* = leave/depart —
reduce it down and combine it into your own art. He illustrates with **Kill Bill / Pai Mei**:
the stubborn student (Uma Thurman) must have her spirit broken and come to respect the master
before she can leave and break. His point: for a period of time, do it the way you're
instructed until you've mastered it — you don't have to be obedient forever, but don't try to
break or leave the whole time. He closes on **Kirby Ferguson's "Everything Is a Remix"** and
its formula **copy, transform, combine**: you learn anything by copying, the more faithfully
the better; ability to copy is a sign of intellect (forgers have high IQ). The clip cuts off
as Chris debates Kirby on sequence — arguing you *combine* before you *transform*.

## Key claims

All dated 2023-06-29, paraphrased unless quoted, attributed to **Chris Do** (audience answers
noted separately; they do not train the persona).

- **Reframing is universal and highly applicable** — it lets you look at a problem from a
  different point of view; understanding it changed how he sees many things, including the
  world. (Chris Do)
- **Packaging/presentation of ideas drives perceived value** — the identical artwork looks
  cheap in an ugly frame and valuable in a beautiful frame with good lighting and no clutter;
  perception of value goes up with the framing, not the object. (Chris Do)
- **Meaning changes with framing even when words are identical** — "A woman without her man is
  nothing" flips meaning depending on punctuation/reading; presentation changes interpretation.
  (Chris Do)
- **Reframing is the most in-demand thing taught** to large groups, per the book *What's Your
  Problem?*. (Chris Do, citing the book)
- **Elevator reframe** — reframing "make the elevator faster" (buy a faster elevator) into
  "reduce the frustration of waiting" unlocks cheaper solutions: mirrors, music, TV, snacks.
  Mirrors near elevators exist because a self-check distracts from the wait. (Chris Do)
- **The teacher's core problem:** he gives tools, techniques, and frameworks, but people don't
  do what they came to learn — they don't get in the best shape, make the most money, or crush
  their classes despite having the instructions. (Chris Do)
- **Candidate causes of not-following-through** (surfaced with the audience): people don't
  like being told what to do; craving instant gratification while avoiding hard work; and the
  belief that paying someone substitutes for doing the failing/repetition yourself. Things
  worth achieving require time, effort, and sacrifice. (Chris Do, exploratory)
- **Disconnect between solution and perceived problem** — students don't act because the
  solution doesn't map to the problem *they* see at the time ("it will make sense later, but
  it doesn't now"). Teachers should give solutions directed at problems students recognize
  immediately (Matrix "don't worry about the vase" analogy). (Chris Do)
- **Three learner types** (an observation he "likes so it's true for now"): (1) high-level —
  get a concept from a table of contents; tend to be leaders/managers/innovators; (2) need it
  told in a story/parable format; (3) kinesthetic "believe it when I see it / I'll do it
  myself" — can become almost **uncoachable**. He once polled his class on which type they
  self-identify as. (Chris Do)
- **Shu-Ha-Ri (mastery progression):** *Shu* — protect/preserve the master's method in its
  purest form (~10 years ≈ Gladwell's 10,000 hours); *Ha* — break/shatter/question everything,
  look for better ways; *Ri* — leave/depart, reduce it down and combine it to make your own
  martial art. (Chris Do, relaying a Japanese concept a friend shared)
- **Kill Bill / Pai Mei analogy** — the headstrong student who thinks she already knows must be
  broken, come to respect and love the master, and only then leave. Many learners are stuck
  trying to *break* or *leave* the whole time instead of first mastering. (Chris Do)
- **Prescription:** you don't have to be obedient for 10 years, but for a period of time do it
  the way you're instructed until you've mastered it. (Chris Do)
- **Copy, transform, combine (Kirby Ferguson, "Everything Is a Remix")** — everything in art
  and culture relies on this formula; you learn anything by first copying, and the more
  faithful the copy the better. (Chris Do, citing/interviewing Kirby Ferguson)
- **Ability to copy is a sign of intellect** — forgers have very high IQ; you have to be able
  to copy. (Chris Do)
- **Sequence debate:** Chris argues you *combine* before you *transform* — you copy this and
  copy that, combine them, and then transform. (Chris Do; clip cuts off here)

_Audience answers (not persona training):_ guesses that people lack focus/clarity, that
there's too much information to know what to follow (unnamed attendees); "Julia" offers that
the solution "doesn't make sense to them at the time."
