# How to Generate Leads & Get Your 1st Client

> (Mo Ismail), a Futur team member.** The structure alternates: each man tells his own
> lead-generation origin story while the other "unpacks" it for actionable strategy.

> lines train the persona.** Mo Ismail is **GUEST/CO-HOST CONTEXT** — his own biography

## Summary

A practical clinic on getting leads and landing a first client. Mo opens with his own
origin: his first paid creative gig (photography for a boutique fashion store) came from
**shadowing** a more established photographer/videographer, Christopher Franklin, and
picking up his **overflow** work. Chris generalizes this into a repeatable strategy —
go to a credible rival/mentor doing what you do at a higher level, build a genuine
relationship over time, and offer to service their overflow (willing to pay for the
leads). His actionable prescription: list five companies operating at the level you
aspire to, follow/engage them everywhere until they become curious about you, then, when
it feels natural, position yourself as "the young, up-and-coming version of you."

Chris then tells his own story of how work has always come to him: a high-school
reputation for drawing spread by word of mouth (newspaper illustrations → a wrestling
coach → a silk-screening job); a college reputation as an "exceptional designer" pulled
in photographers and advertising students; job tickets at Arts Center; an Adobe After
Effects CD-ROM as marketing material; and working with title-design legend Kyle Cooper
via a friend. His hard truth: most people have to ask for work because their work is
objectively not good yet — the fix is to copy admired work to learn its decision-making,
close the gap, and let quality generate demand ("the better your work, the less you have
to market and sell"). Mo distills both stories into **five C's**: Choose, Commit, Craft,
Claim, Capital.

The final third covers a **hard pivot**: repositioning The Futur's early shop from
agency-intermediary video work to direct-client brand strategy. Chris ran a **free
45-minute "strategic business objectives" session** (framed as normally a $10,000
engagement) as the wedge, pitched it for a full year hearing "no" after "no" while
keeping the legacy business alive, then got a first yes — after which brand-strategy fees
scaled from $10k → $30k → over $100k. He closes on the mindset layer: supreme confidence
comes from a self-story of always getting what you commit to, from putting your back
against the wall (once someone pays, your reputation is on the line), and from how you
spend your free time — reading with **depth over volume**, radical curiosity, constant
self-improvement. Final note (citing Jim Rohn): the greatest investment is in yourself.

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-07-13. Paraphrased unless quoted. Speaker-attributed._

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- **The overflow strategy (generalized from Mo's story):** the repeatable move is to go
  to a credible rival or mentor doing work adjacent to (or above) yours, build a
  relationship, and pick up their **overflow** clients when they exceed capacity. That
  is "a real strategy," not a hypothetical. (paraphrase)
- **The five-companies drill:** make a list of five companies doing what you do at the
  level you'd like to be at; follow/"stalk" them everywhere on social, listen to their
  older podcasts, engage meaningfully over time so they get to know you and become
  naturally curious — **not** on the first interaction. When rapport is natural, position
  yourself: "I'm the young and up-and-coming version of you; if you ever have overflow,
  I'd love to service your clients and I'm willing to pay for those leads." (paraphrase)
- **Intention → expression → orbit:** first form the intention ("I want to work with
  people"), then express it "out into the universe" so people who can help will, then
  organically bring them into your circle and build the relationship. (paraphrase)
- **Reach beyond your grasp, not to the stars:** most people aim too high — a new grad
  wants Nike or Xbox, but "Xbox doesn't know you from a flying horse poop." Instead reach
  "a little bit beyond where your hands can grab," build a relationship, develop work,
  then keep reaching higher. (paraphrase / part quote)
- **The most natural way to sell is to tell people what you're thinking** — if you love a
  brand, tell them so and express your creativity. Example: a friend does one fictional
  project a year; he redesigned all of Southwest Airlines' touchpoints for fun, expecting
  nothing, and ended up working with Southwest Airlines. (paraphrase)
- **His own current version of the strategy:** since he doesn't have traditional clients,
  he'd get in front of the members of other people's Mastermind groups (who teach
  something different than he does) and invite those who find his work valuable into his
  own Mastermind — "put it out on the universe" so everyone knows what he does.
  (paraphrase)
- **Quantity over quality (when quality isn't there yet):** just do the work and get it
  into the real world; people in your orbit — friends, family, friends of friends —
  become unofficial salespeople who look out for you. "If quality never happens, I'm just
  doing work" and people find out about it. (paraphrase / part quote)
- **Blunt truth — "your work sucks":** the reason many people have to ask for work is
  that their work is, objectively, terrible / not yet at a recommendable level. Taking
  offense and quitting guarantees no money. (paraphrase / part quote)
- **How to close the gap:** pick someone whose work you admire, identify objectively the
  gaps between you and them, and literally **copy all of their work** to learn how they
  make decisions — then apply that thinking to your next assignment. Do it consistently
  and people will seek you out. (paraphrase)
- **The better your work, the less you have to market and sell** — the ultimate goal is
  work so good you don't have to market or sell at all. Not achievable on day one, but
  it's the target. (paraphrase — see caption garble note; the transcript drops the "won't")
- **Work is abundant if you're good and willing** — he pulled job tickets at Arts Center,
  applied, showed his portfolio, got to work. There is "no magic"; wishing for a six-pack
  without discipline doesn't produce one. You have to work for what you want. (paraphrase)
- **How the Kyle Cooper opportunity came:** a good friend from school was dating the
  title-design legend Kyle Cooper and knew the two would get along — a recurring theme
  that opportunities come through relationships and reputation. (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- **The hard-pivot playbook (advertising → direct brand strategy):** to reposition from
  agency-intermediary video work to direct-client brand strategy, do a **soft pivot** —
  keep the existing business running "to keep the money flowing" while leading with the
  new offer. (paraphrase)
- **The free-session wedge (verbatim script, below):** when a client came in for a video,
  he'd offer a free 45-minute session on their "overall strategic business objectives,"
  framed as normally a **$10,000** engagement, addressing all their concerns up front
  (why, what, value, "are you trying to sell me something"). (paraphrase — see quotes)
- **It takes a year, then it compounds:** he pitched the $10k brand-strategy session for a
  full year hearing "no" after "no" (and refused every low counter-offer) before the
  first yes. The first yes is the hardest; the next came in weeks, then days, and fees
  scaled from **$10k → $30k → over $100k**. (paraphrase)
- **Give it a year, really working:** don't just imagine it happening — read books, watch
  videos, attend workshops, seek mentors, and keep pushing until you're the person a
  client would say yes to. A year is only ~1/20th of a business career. (paraphrase)
- **Meta-lesson — back against the wall:** people are scared to commit because "the
  second someone paid you, you're on the line"; your money and professional reputation
  are at stake. It's easier to live in pondering and hoping. (paraphrase)
- **Why people don't commit for a year:** their **self-story** isn't one where committing
  reliably yields results. His own self-story: "every time I commit to something, I always
  get what I want" — and when it doesn't work, he changes the plan a little until it does.
  When you open the door to doubt, doubt comes in. (paraphrase / part quote)
- **Supreme confidence as a bargain:** he doesn't try to convince people to work with him;
  he has supreme confidence that what he delivers for the price is a bargain, and that he
  will over-deliver on value. (paraphrase / part quote)
- **Confidence is sourced in your free time:** take inventory of what you do when you're
  not working or handling responsibilities. Most people relax into comfortable routines
  (couch, magazine, crossword); he spends his on becoming a better person — books, talking
  to smart people, radical curiosity. House projects stay undone because they're low
  priority. (paraphrase)
- **The podcast is a means, not the goal:** rankings are "a result," not the point; the
  point is conversations with really smart people that help him solve a problem, with the
  audience brought along. (paraphrase)
- **Read better, not more — depth over volume:** the goal isn't reading volume; it's
  depth — understanding something well enough to explain it to others "without notes,
  without rehearsal, without preparation." (paraphrase / part quote)
- **Closing — commitment isn't enough without work:** getting the first client is very
  hard; being committed and uncompromising and putting in the work is necessary, but not
  everyone committed to a goal gets it — many (per his DMs) expect results without the
  work, and their work isn't good. The greatest investment is in yourself, not the market,
  gold, or crypto. (paraphrase) — cites **Jim Rohn**: "work hard on your job and you'll
  make a living; work hard on yourself and you'll make a fortune" (paraphrased by Chris as
  "work hard on your job and you have a career… work on your personal development and
  you'll have a life"). (attributed quote — to Rohn, not Chris)

### Mo Ismail (GUEST/CO-HOST — context only, does NOT train the persona)

_Biographical items are HIS self-reports unless independently verified._

- **Bio (self-reported):** started his creative journey after teaching college (a
  master's, focus on public speaking) left him unfulfilled by academic structure; began
  vlogging on Snapchat, which built a relationship with his former business partner
  **Christopher Franklin**. His first paid gig (~five years before this recording) was
  photography for a boutique fashion store, obtained by asking to shadow Franklin (a solo
  wedding photographer/videographer) and taking his **overflow**. (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- **His replication idea:** today he'd go to a credible agency adjacent to his work,
  pitch a project he could help their clients with, or do it free at first to prove value
  and nurture the relationship. (paraphrase)
- **The "five C's" summary:** **Choose** (decide your focus) → **Commit** (a year-long
  commitment without immediate ROI, sustained by "delusion in the vision") → **Craft** (be
  a practitioner; copy, combine into your own style, then create) → **Claim** (claim it to
  yourself first — confidence — then to the world — marketing, inbound or outbound) →
  **Capital** (do the first four well and it turns into money). (paraphrase)
