## Summary

Chris Do recaps three lessons from a business coach who worked with him for ~13 years,
framed as the most valuable advice he ever received. **(1) "Say what you think."**
Deceptively simple: out of fear of finding the right words, mismatching tone, or
provoking the client's reaction, we routinely fail to say what we actually think — we
want to know the budget but don't ask, think the terms are unfair but stay silent, hear
abstract language and guess rather than ask, all to avoid looking stupid. Chris says he
used to believe a director must never ask a client what they want (it would signal being
"out of touch" and not leading); the coach was "shocked and amused" that Chris never
asked. The fix is simple, specific questions — "what inspired you," "when you say
cinematic, give me reference points so I can get into your mind" — which garner
surprisingly rich responses. **(2) Give people who work for you permission to say no.**
Owners underestimate their power; staff/freelancers nod and agree even when a task can't
be done, then miss deadlines. Chris learned to say, explicitly, "it's okay to say no,
and I need to hear that now so I'm not disappointed later," and to check in ("do we
understand each other?"). When he tried it, a designer immediately revealed two
conflicting priorities Chris hadn't known about. The mirror image: as a business owner
you still have a "boss" — your clients — so raise scope/feasibility problems *early*
("the longer it takes to raise the problem, the more expensive it becomes to solve") and
separate the "nice-to-haves" from the "have-to-haves." **(3) Don't underestimate the
power of being an owner.** Chris was uncomfortable with the word "power" and hid in his
office, afraid to talk to clients — "robbing them of an experience only you can bring."
The coach's restaurant analogy (the executive chef/owner personally checking in on your
meal makes you feel important and builds an emotional connection to the establishment)
reframed it. His executive team staged introductions — bring Chris in, let him do a warm
check-in, then "steal him away" for another call — until Chris built it into a genuine
life skill. The larger lesson: anyone can do the work, but very few people care enough
to communicate to a client that their business matters.

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-07-16. Paraphrased unless quoted. Speaker-attributed. Chris narrates all
substantive content; the interviewer only prompts._

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

- **Best advice received: "say what you think."** Sounds basic, but "turns out we
  don't" — fear of the right words, tonality, matching the client's intention, and the
  client's reaction stop us from stating what we think, which creates a lot of stress.
  Concrete failures: wanting to know the budget but not asking; thinking terms are unfair
  but not saying so; hearing abstract language and assuming meaning rather than asking,
  "because we don't want to seem stupid or dumb." (paraphrase / part quote)
- On a **sales call**, the coach asked Chris to walk through one beginning to end and was
  "shocked and amused" that Chris never asked clients what they want — Chris had believed
  that asking would mean being "out of touch with what's going on" when he was "supposed
  to be leading the conversation." (paraphrase)
- Context [self-reported]: Chris was **working as a director talking to advertising
  agencies about commercial campaigns**, and assumed agencies came to him for a
  perspective — "a way to shoot, the way to edit" — the way he'd seen directors he looked
  up to operate. (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- The coach "gave me permission to say what I was thinking"; Chris feared it was "too
  simple, too basic, maybe too crass," as if he were "some uneducated cretin / production
  monkey" — but asking specific questions ("what inspired you," "is this campaign like
  every other you've worked on or totally different," "when you say cinematic, give me
  reference points so I can get into your mind") "garners a very interesting response."
  (paraphrase / part quote)
- **Tip 2 — managing the people who work for you.** As owner / director / creative
  director "you do not realize the power you have," so people "want to please you, they
  don't want to tell you the truth, they just want to see you happy." Chris was frustrated
  by missed deadlines and work that wasn't what he expected from freelancers and staff.
  (paraphrase)
- The coach's fix: **give people permission to say no to you** — "by default they would
  not say no; they'll nod as if they agree, as if it can be done, when they already know
  it cannot." Script it: state the ask ("I want three ideas done by Tuesday night"), then
  "is this something you can do — and it's okay to say no; I need to hear that now so I'm
  not disappointed later"; if part is confusing or too much, "let's have a conversation
  and figure out what we can do." (paraphrase / part quote)
- He tried it expecting nothing, but a designer said: "Chris, everything makes sense, I
  can do this — my problem is I've been given two other priorities; which is the greater
  priority?" Chris hadn't known about the other tasks and assumed his was most important
  "because I'm the boss," leaving the person stressed. **Lesson: give permission to say no
  in a way they can hear, and check in — "do we understand each other?" — otherwise people
  keep agreeing and you'll be disappointed time and time again.** (paraphrase / part quote)
- **Set expectations up front** — with clients and employees alike; doing so "is gonna
  save you a lot of trouble." (paraphrase; this line is voiced partly by the interviewer
  and affirmed by Chris)
- **The mirror image — you are also someone's subordinate.** "Even though you own your own
  business you still have a client; everybody has a boss." So when a client demands
  something by a date you can't hit, "raise your hand": "I heard what you want and I'm
  having a hard time reconciling this — this goes way out of scope / is way more work than
  can be done; I need to figure out with you right now what the nice-to-haves and the
  have-to-haves are, because we need to prioritize." (paraphrase / part quote)
- **Raise problems early**: "raise the problem up front as early as possible, because the
  longer it takes to raise the problem, the more expensive it becomes to solve."
  (near-quote)
- **Tip 3 — the power of being an owner.** The coach's words ("the power of the owner")
  made Chris "really uncomfortable" — "I don't want to have power, I'm not trying to be
  powerful, beat my chest… me owner, you employee, it just seems wrong." The lesson: **do
  not underestimate the power of being an owner.** The coach: "I see you hiding in your
  office afraid to talk to clients, and what you're doing is robbing them of an experience
  that only you can bring." (paraphrase / part quote) [self-reported: hiding in office]
- **The restaurant / executive-chef analogy** (the coach's, retold by Chris): a nice meal
  with your wife, then the owner/executive chef comes out — "I'm Jean-Philippe, I'm always
  wondering how your meal was; you ordered the lamb… was it cooked to your liking?" You
  feel important and form "an emotional connection to that establishment." Map that onto
  your business: when your team is with clients, "where are you in this picture?"
  (paraphrase / part quote)
- Chris confessed he didn't know how to begin the conversation and didn't want to. So his
  **executive team staged it**: introduce Chris to the client, let him do a warm check-in
  ("hi everybody, I'll be taking good care of you"), then a producer says "I hate to do
  this but I have to steal him away — he has to prep for another call," and pulls him out.
  Clients felt "that same thing" Chris had only felt once — "the owner cares." (paraphrase)
- **Over time it became a real skill**: Chris can now walk in, introduce himself, say
  "everything's great, I'm probably disturbing the workflow so I'll get out of your hair —
  let me know if you need anything," and leave. A "life skill" *and* a "big life lesson."
  (paraphrase) [self-reported]
- **Closing life lesson**: "at the end of the day, [clients] can get anybody to do the
  work, but very few people actually care enough to communicate that their business is
  important — and we don't take it for granted." (near-quote)
- Ends with a plug for **The Futur Pro Group**, a membership "specifically created for
  experienced business owners ready to scale." (paraphrase)
