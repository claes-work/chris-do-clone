## Summary

An hour-long Futur masterclass in which **Chris Do interviews Chris Voss** about the myths
of negotiation. Voss (guest) delivers the bulk of the teaching: why chasing "yes" is a
trap ("Thou shalt not covet yes"; the three kinds of yes — commitment, confirmation,
counterfeit), how to detect when you're the "favorite of the fool" (a competing bid / free
due-diligence) using a calibrated "what" question ("on a scale of 1–10, what's the chance
we get the deal?") plus dynamic silence, labeling ("it seems like…") as a diagnostic, the
three tones of voice, the accusation audit, and the neuroscience of labeling negatives.

**Chris Do's role is host/interviewer.** His own persona-eligible contribution is his
framing of *his* audience — creatives who sell — and their sales psychology (listening
with "happy ears," desperation for the sale, allergic reactions to yes-momentum tactics),
plus a few personal anecdotes (his wife, his son's piano teacher, his own introversion).
He mostly reflects back and restates Voss's points; those restatements are Do paraphrasing
Voss and are **not** independent Chris Do beliefs.

## Key claims (2023-07-20)

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

_Paraphrased unless quoted; attributed to Chris Do._

- His community is **mostly people in the creative space**, and creatives repeatedly get
  burned by "happy ears": they thought they had a deal, then the client ghosted — because
  they wanted the deal so badly they engineered the conversation to force a "yes," out of
  fear of rejection.
- Creatives are so desperate for the sale *right now* that they'll do anything to close in
  the moment, when it is **much better to build a long-term client relationship** — exit a
  bad fit on a high note and keep the probability that the prospect returns on more
  favorable terms.
- The Futur uses a version of the qualifying move itself: when a prospect only needs a
  third bid / estimate and already has a vendor they want, Do is happy to provide a bid but
  won't "go through the exercise of earnestly going through this" — he'd rather ask "tell me
  what number you need to hit." (Do describing his own practice.)
- There's "one way to tell the truth… many ways to lie," so hesitation before a number
  should give you pause. (Do restating an idea he attributes to having heard before.)
- On labeling one's own emotions: as a self-described **introvert**, when something comes
  up he tends to feel more tense/anxious and *not* say it — but naming the feeling brings
  it down and creates a healthier relationship; otherwise he starts to resent people for
  things he never communicated. It begins with the self, then applies to prospects.
- Personal anecdote: his **son's piano teacher** told the boy to practice just one part of
  a song until it felt good rather than the whole song; that changed how he played, let him
  play longer, and the son "to this day loves playing piano." (Do using it to affirm Voss's
  "perfect practice / slow reps" point.)
- Self-aware aside: "I think I'm a pretty good negotiator, and then when it comes to my
  wife it's… a whole different dynamic." (Do, on negotiating with family.)

### Chris Voss (GUEST — context only, does NOT train the persona)

_Paraphrased; attributed to Chris Voss. Recorded for corpus context, NOT for `beliefs.md`/`voice.md`._

- **"Thou shalt not covet yes"** (first of Black Swan's "12 Commandments"): yes is not
  agreement. Three kinds of yes — **commitment, confirmation, counterfeit**. "Yes momentum"
  / micro-agreements / tie-downs are "nonsense"; chasing yes creates tension, hurts the
  relationship, and makes people withhold information.
- People stuck on yes-momentum only see the distance they've *come* (F-student → C-student)
  and can't see how far they could go (the "10-win table vs. the 80-win table" Vegas
  analogy).
- **"Favorite of the fool" / "proof of life":** ≥20% of the time (Voss thinks far higher,
  maybe 80%) you're being used for a competing bid, due diligence, or free consulting.
  Asking "are we the vendor of choice?" is a useless yes-oriented question — a liar won't
  admit to lying.
- Better: a **calibrated "what" question** — "on a scale of 1–10, what's the percentage
  chance we get the deal?" It's not the number that matters but how *quickly* they answer;
  hesitation means "Houston, we have a problem."
- **Dynamic silence:** after asking, shut up and gather data with your eyes — the face
  reflects the brain. Gather visual data from the very beginning instead of rehearsing your
  own next point. He reframes "emotional intelligence" as largely *gathering data with your
  eyes* (usable even by neurodivergent people).
- **Labeling** ("it seems like you're still shopping around," "it sounds like…") as a
  diagnostic that triggers a verbal and visual response. Exit gracefully to become the
  fallback: the OPA-type rule that "the last impression is a lasting impression."
- **Predator / drama triangle:** a prospect who turns angry or vindictive when you refuse to
  keep giving free value is confirming they were playing you (persecutor ↔ victim). Cites
  the Steve Shaw co-authored book *The Full Fee Agent* and advising real-estate agents to
  refuse commodity listing presentations.
- **Three tones of voice**, mapped to fight/flight/make-friends (world splits in thirds):
  the **assertive** ("give me a call in 60 seconds or she dies") is always
  counterproductive on the other side; the **accommodator** (smiling, relationship voice)
  is almost always helpful; the **analyst / late-night FM DJ voice** (downward-inflecting,
  "drop your chin") for drawing a firm line without attack. Example: "we don't do work for
  hire" said flatly (not asked, to avoid triggering reciprocity — cites Cialdini), then
  dead silence.
- **"Small stakes practice for high stakes results"** — practice tones daily with the Lyft
  driver / Starbucks barista; "there's no good or bad, there's only trained and untrained"
  (Man on Fire / Denzel). Cites *The Talent Code* (Daniel Coyle — captioned "Court"),
  myelin, and Andrew Huberman on mental rehearsal.
- **Straight shooter vs. blunt:** a straight shooter tells the truth *gently, with emotional
  intelligence*; blunt is "blunt force trauma." Family negotiation involves inadvertent past
  wounding and whether you "walk the talk." (Illustrated by the Santa Claus story with his
  son Brandon/Braden.)
- **Accusation audit:** proactively voice every negative the other side might think ("you're
  probably going to think I'm greedy / that this is unfair / that you're wasting your time"),
  then go silent. You defuse the "elephant in the room" by naming it; humans are up to ~75%
  negative. Neuroscience: fMRI studies show labeling an emotion diminishes amygdala-type
  activity. Plugs Black Swan's newsletter "The Edge" and a Fireside session with Mark Cuban.
