## Summary

A short interview segment on the theme "how to get clients without a portfolio." **Chris
Do** interviews an **unidentified designer** who, after the magazine she worked at folded,
spent about three years building toward her own studio, which she now runs with her sister
and business partner **Amy**. Do supplies the teaching frameworks; the guest supplies the
lived story.

Do's persona-eligible contributions: (1) a **gaps diagnostic** — when work dries up, look
for which gap is real: a *confidence* gap, a *skill* gap, or an *opportunity / network*
gap; the guest wasn't short of confidence or opportunity, so the work was to close the
skill gap. (2) A **portfolio-building doctrine**: make work for yourself to practice a
skill, and "do more than 10" — repeated, constrained daily/weekly prompt exercises become
great portfolio pieces that double as marketing to get clients or even turn into a product.
(3) A **belief/manifestation** teaching: picture a future state where you've achieved some
of your goals, "magnetize yourself" to it, and the means and methods will appear — you need
"a little bit of that" self-belief. (4) A **complementary-co-founders** observation: one
partner is the rebel/risk-taker who "jumps first," the other is the pragmatist who watches
the parachute, then follows.

The guest's contribution (GUEST CONTEXT) is her story — being a "yes man" on ad work, then
doing passion projects on the side to build a style — and a networking/community point she
attributes to a woman she once met: don't over-plan *how*; decide *what* you want and talk
about it with as many people as you can, because the people around you become the
connectors who get you to the next step. She also stresses community and accountability
(friends who "call you on your stuff").

## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

_Paraphrased unless quoted; attributed to Chris Do (2023-07-28)._

- When the work stops (e.g., the magazine folds), diagnose which **gap** you actually face:
  a **confidence gap**, a **skill gap**, or an **opportunity / network gap**. In the
  guest's case she was not short of confidence and had a network of friends pushing her
  toward doing her own thing, so the real work was closing the **skill gap** over those
  years. (2023-07-28)
- A portfolio of only ads "generally are not that sexy" — having the skills isn't the same
  as having the **proof** of them in the work you can show. (2023-07-28)
- **Portfolio-building doctrine:** when you make something *for yourself* to practice a
  skill and "you do more than 10," people grab onto it. **Daily or weekly prompt
  exercises** with lots of constraints (so you don't have to invent the assignment) are
  great portfolio-building exercises that can lead to marketing materials to get clients —
  or turn into a product that pays for itself. (2023-07-28)
- **Belief / manifestation:** you need "a little bit" of the audacious self-belief that you
  will be celebrated — picture a future state where you've achieved *some* of your goals,
  "magnetize yourself to that goal," and "the means and the methods will appear."
  (2023-07-28)
- **Complementary co-founders:** it's rare and special to have a partner with complementary
  traits — the rebel/risk-taker who "jumps first" and the pragmatist who checks the
  parachute, then follows once it's proven safe. (Do describing the guest and her sister
  Amy.) (2023-07-28)

### Guest (unidentified designer — GUEST CONTEXT, does NOT train the persona)

_Paraphrased; attributed to the unidentified guest. Recorded for corpus context only._

- Her first year she took **any project** — not picky — while doing **passion work on the
  side**, making things she thought were cool to "build a style." Earlier she had "mostly
  just been kind of a yes man for clients to get their ad done" and hadn't yet learned the
  programs for the more creative work. (guest, 2023-07-28)
- A woman she once met told her the important thing isn't figuring out **how** you'll do it
  — it's figuring out **what** you want to do and talking about it with as many people as
  possible, because the people around you become the **connectors** who make better
  introductions than you could plan alone. Plan too, but also just talk it out. (guest
  relaying a third party, 2023-07-28)
- **Community and accountability** matter: her sister Amy was more confident than she was;
  left to her own devices she'd "probably just go to work for someone else." Friends who
  hold you accountable — who ask "why haven't you finished that?" — push you to the next
  level if that's what you want. (guest, 2023-07-28)
