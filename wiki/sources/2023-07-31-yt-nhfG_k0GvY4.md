## Summary

A Futur masterclass in which **Chris Do interviews Chris Voss** on the myths of
negotiation and, per the title, how to handle rejection — i.e. what to do when you realize
you are *not* the vendor of choice. Voss (guest) delivers essentially all of the teaching:
why "yes momentum" / micro-agreements / tie-downs are a trap ("Thou shalt not covet yes";
yes is not agreement; three kinds — commitment, confirmation, counterfeit), how chasing yes
only lifts an F-student to a C-student while blinding them to how far they could go, the
"favorite of the fool" / "proof of life" (≥20% of the time you're being used for a
competing bid, free due diligence, or free consulting), why "are we the vendor of choice?"
is a useless yes-oriented question, the better calibrated "what" question ("on a scale of
1–10, what's the percentage chance we get the deal?") where *how quickly* they answer
matters more than the number, dynamic silence + gathering data with your eyes, labeling
("it seems like you're still shopping around") as a diagnostic, the "Oprah rule" (last
impression = lasting impression → exit gracefully to become the fallback partner), and the
drama triangle / predatory-vindictive behavior as confirmation you were being played (cites
co-author Steve Shull and *The Full Fee Agent*).

**Chris Do's role is host/interviewer.** His persona-eligible contribution is his framing
of *his* audience — creatives who sell — and their sales psychology (an allergic reaction
to yes-momentum tactics, "happy ears," desperation to close *now*, engineering the
conversation toward a yes out of fear of rejection), plus the case for exiting a bad fit on
a high note to preserve a long-term relationship, and a personal anecdote (his wife in
Venice). He mostly reflects back and restates Voss's points; those restatements are Do
paraphrasing Voss and are **not** independent Chris Do beliefs.

## Key claims (2023-07-31)

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

_Paraphrased unless quoted; attributed to Chris Do._

- His audience is **mostly people in the creative space**, and they (like salespeople
  generally) have "grown an allergic reaction" to yes-momentum / micro-agreement tactics
  because those techniques have been "perpetrated on" them so often.
- Creatives get burned by "happy ears": they thought they had a deal, then the client
  ghosted — because they **want the deal so badly they engineer/construct the conversation
  to force a "yes," out of fear of rejection.**
- He likes Voss's approach because it lets you **embrace a "tactical no," which is "better
  than five yeses."** (Do crediting Voss's framing, not original doctrine.)
- People in his community and in sales generally are **so desperate for the sale right now
  that they'll do anything to close in the moment**, when it is **much better to have a
  client long-term** — if it doesn't work out this time, end on a very high note so there's
  a probability the prospect returns on more favorable terms next time.
- On telling truth vs. lying: "there's one way to tell the truth… many ways to lie," so
  hesitation before giving a number should give you pause. (Do restating an idea he
  attributes to having heard Voss say before.)
- His restatement of the exit playbook (Do paraphrasing Voss, for the audience): when there
  are multiple bids, ask the calibrated "what" question instead of "are we the vendor of
  choice?"; if there's hesitation, demonstrate emotional intelligence, empathy, and
  self-awareness, label it with a series of "it sounds like…" statements, then **exit with
  grace and dignity** and establish potential for a relationship down the line.
- Personal anecdote: years ago in **Venice**, a stranger on the street tried to pick up his
  wife while she was in Do's own car and asked "what is your name?" (a question you can't
  say no to) — and instead of giving her name she just said "no." "My wife is brutal." (Do,
  illustrating the reflexive resistance people have to being maneuvered.)

### Chris Voss (GUEST — context only, does NOT train the persona)

_Paraphrased; attributed to Chris Voss. Recorded for corpus context, NOT for `beliefs.md`/`voice.md`._

- **"Thou shalt not covet yes."** "Yes momentum" / momentum selling — stacking
  micro-agreements and tie-downs so the prospect says yes to the big thing — is "really
  bad." Yes is not agreement; yes is "almost always counterfeit." Three kinds of yes:
  **commitment, confirmation, counterfeit.**
- Yes-momentum only lifts a failing "F-student" to a "C-student" — enough to "not get
  fired" (make grade-point minimum) — while blinding them to the fact they're nowhere near
  a B or A student. So many people have been "hustled" with it that any attempt to get them
  to say yes now triggers concern.
- Keynote demo: ask any audience how they react to "have you got a few minutes to talk?" —
  everyone instantly says "concerned" (or "no"). Trying to get a yes **creates tension and
  concern, hurts the relationship, and makes people hold back information.** Black Swan
  teaches getting *out* of yes and into implementable agreement.
- **"Favorite of the fool" / "proof of life":** at least 20% of the time you're being used
  for a competing bid, due diligence, or free consulting. "If you don't know who the fool
  in the game is, it's probably you." Asking "are we the vendor of choice?" is a
  yes-oriented dead end — you get a yes either way; asking a liar "are you lying to me?"
  never works.
- Better: a **calibrated "what" question** — "on a scale of 1–10, what's the percentage
  chance we get the deal?" It stops people in their tracks; what matters is **how quickly
  they answer**, not the number. Instant high number = you're the favorite; hesitation =
  "Houston, we have a problem."
- **Dynamic silence:** after asking, shut up and **gather data with your eyes** — the face
  is a direct reflection of the brain. A deliberately blank face means they're filtering;
  label it ("seems like there's some stuff going through your mind right now").
- **Labeling** ("it seems like you're still shopping around," "it seems like you've got
  somebody else you really want to do business with," "it sounds like…") as a diagnostic
  that triggers a verbal and visual response.
- **The "Oprah rule":** "the last impression is the lasting impression." When you're not
  the choice now, your new landing spot is to position yourself as the long-term partner —
  state clearly what a great deal with you looks like, don't spin your wheels, and end the
  interaction so they come looking for you when their chosen vendor fails them.
- **Predator / drama triangle:** a prospect who becomes angry/vindictive when you refuse to
  keep giving free value (e.g. refusing a commodity listing presentation) is confirming they
  were playing you — the persecutor moves between victim and persecutor to keep you off
  balance. Cites co-author **Steve Shull** and their book *The Full Fee Agent*; teaches
  real-estate agents to refuse listing presentations (a due-diligence process).
