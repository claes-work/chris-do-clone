## Summary

A dense, single-speaker Chris Do session answering Pro Group questions on the business of a
creative studio. Threads: (1) **referring bad-fit leads** to your network rather than
hoarding them out of scarcity, and monetizing that via a **finder's fee / sales commission
(6–15%)**; (2) the "**take a competitor out to lunch**" networking play (cites *Never Eat
Alone*) — approach agencies bigger than you and offer to take overflow work off their
plate; (3) how to pick a **communications/business coach** (use your friends-and-family
network, don't over-index on industry-specific experience, screen for communication style);
(4) an extended **origin story** — floundering 1995–~1999, striking out on Fortune 500
bids, cold-calling competitors who laughed at him, being taught to bid by producers (Karen
Rainey, ex-E!), then hiring the business coach who transformed him. Woven through are his
signature doctrines: "**say what you think**," "**symmetry of logic**" (what's fair for a
boss is fair for an employee), "**give people permission to say no**," **choose the phone
over email for conflict**, and "**don't underestimate the power of being an owner**" (the
executive-chef / owner-presence lesson). Closes with a macro riff on the **attention
economy** (Alex Hormozi's ad-vs-content math), the **three 21st-century skills** (critical
thinking, learning how to learn, articulate persuasion/rhetoric), Photoshop+AI service
opportunities, and his headline career lesson: **switch verticals, don't add services** (a
typography course grossed >$1M vs. 15 years of $55/hr teaching).

## Key claims (2023-08-03)

_All attributed to Chris Do. Paraphrased unless quoted._

### Referrals, scarcity, and finder's fees (pricing / sales-clients)
- Bad-fit projects (budget, schedule, wrong product/marketing mix) will always come; people
  refuse to refer them out because they live in a **scarcity mindset**. Better to refer the
  prospect to someone in your network — you help two parties and they remember you.
- **In a sales state of mind, the highest priority is to serve the person in front of you,
  not yourself.** Find out what they actually need, then refer them — but **never refer
  anyone you don't like, trust, or believe in**, or you build "negative Goodwill" and do a
  disservice to all three parties.
- If you refer a lot, formalize it: ask referral partners "how much do you pay for new
  leads?" and set up a **finder's fee / sales commission** on **pre-vetted, ready-to-buy
  clients**. Fair range is **6–15%**; beyond 15% margins get untenable, though the desperate
  will pay 30–50%.
- Saying **"no" today creates demand for a "yes" tomorrow** — declining because your
  schedule is full signals integrity and standards. The worst option is over-committing and
  serving nobody well.
- **Build a referral roster proactively**, before you need it — think of yourself as a
  "**fluid agency**" (thin management layer, few in-house production resources) with vetted
  designers, developers, content marketers, and writers you can subcontract or refer to.
  "Don't wait for the [work] to come and then scramble."

### Take a competitor to lunch (sales-clients / business)
- Overlooked, counterintuitive strategy: **take a competitor out to lunch** (cites the book
  *Never Eat Alone*). Lunch is low-commitment and inexpensive; everyone has to eat.
- Approach studios **bigger than you** (4-person → 20-person; 20 → 50): "I've been an
  admirer, we work in the same space, I don't see us as competitors, I'd love to take you to
  lunch and pick your brain."
- Build rapport first (it's a social call), then offer to take overflow / conflict-of-
  interest work off their plate for a finder's fee — a **permission-based** approach where
  they invite you to share, not spam. A competitor can become a mentor.
- Reports receiving a message from a follower: "I didn't believe you... I resisted... finally
  I took a competitor out to lunch and now I'm getting work from them."

### Choosing a coach (business / mindset)
- **Perfect is the enemy of good**; the alternative to an imperfect coach is doing nothing
  and sitting in your pain for another 3–6–9 months.
- Source coaches through your **friends-and-family network**, not from stage — be wary of
  coaches whose on-stage self-promotion is part of their sales funnel.
- **Don't over-index on industry-specific experience** — a classic mistake. Screen for
  **communication style and coaching style**; schedule a call, tell them how you like to be
  coached, and trust your gut on fit.
- He tells advisors up front he wants **direct, clear communication** — "give it to me
  straight, doctor" — crediting his therapist (John Lightfoot) and business coach for that
  style, while openly unsure whether they shaped him or he selected for them (chicken-and-egg).

### The business coach and "say what you think" (business / mindset / sales-clients)
- Hired his first CPA, attorney, financial advisor, and **business coach** after two strong
  years (~$2.2M revenue, ~80% gross margins, low overhead, working out of his Venice home).
  "**It takes money to make money.**" The coach — captioned variously, spelling uncertain,
  see garbles — was with him **13+ years**, looked "like Santa Claus" (overweight, jeans,
  t-shirt, rode a motorcycle), and had **no design-industry clients** but gave immediate
  clarity in the first (free) meeting. Lesson: **don't judge a book by its cover.**
- Coach's framing: creatives think they're unique, but **"you're in the business of sales
  and marketing and customer service — it just so happens that you do design."**
- The coach's **best advice was "say what you think"** — sounds trivial but people are too
  fearful of wording, tone, and the client's reaction to actually say what they think, which
  creates chronic stress.
- Applied to sales: **ask clients what they want** (the coach was shocked Chris never did).
  Ask "what inspired you?", "can you give reference points for 'cinematic'?", "you said any
  budget — so if I came back at a million dollars, approved?" These questions make clients
  uncomfortable but yield far better solutions.
- Result: began telling clients "do you have 45 minutes to an hour? I'm going to ask a lot
  of questions that might get uncomfortable." **Win rate went from 1-in-5 to ~75%** on jobs
  of **$250K+** — to the point it felt "too easy," like "cheat codes / God mode." That's
  where his confidence comes from — "nailing half-a-million-dollar jobs left and right."
  Confidence is a **communication skill: listening, asking, being curious.**

### Symmetry of logic & permission to say no (mindset / business)
- **Choose discomfort over resentment** (attributes to Brené Brown): being clear and briefly
  uncomfortable beats going along and resenting the person afterward. "**The stress you feel
  doesn't come from the things you say — it comes from the things you don't say.**"
- **Give employees permission to say no.** By default staff nod and agree even when a task
  can't be done. Add: "Is this something you can do? It's okay to say no, and I need to hear
  that now so I'm not disappointed later." Surfaces hidden competing priorities early.
- Flip it: when a **client** overloads you, raise your hand and separate the "nice-to-haves"
  from the "have-to-haves." **Raise problems as early as possible — "the longer it takes to
  raise the problem, the more expensive it becomes to solve."** A team member who does this
  "rises in esteem."
- "**Symmetry of logic**" (credits a friend): if a rule works for a boss it should work for
  an employee, and vice versa — because you're usually both. It keeps you consistent in both
  directions.

### Conflict → pick up the phone (sales-clients)
- **Sending an email/text to handle conflict is "the equivalent of breaking up with someone
  via text" — a failure of courage.** Email can't carry tone; the reader supplies their own
  based on their mood ("what are you thinking" reads three different ways).
- For conflict resolution or hard conversations, **get on the phone / in front of the client**
  — you hear feeling and read face, body language, and micro-expressions ("the words don't
  match the body"). A five-minute call yields far more information and a stronger relationship.

### Power of being an owner (business / mindset)
- The coach: "**Do not underestimate the power of being an owner.** I see you hiding in your
  office, afraid to talk to clients — you're robbing them of an experience only you can bring."
- The **executive-chef analogy**: when the owner/chef personally checks on your meal, you
  feel important and form an emotional bond with the establishment (Chris, at 51, says it's
  happened to him once). Applied to a studio: the owner must appear before clients.
- The coach staged it — had staff introduce Chris to clients, then pull him out after a
  couple minutes on a pretext — until he learned to do it himself. Lesson: **anyone can do
  the work, but few care enough to communicate that the client's business matters.**
- Reinforced by a young-Chris memory: as a messenger picking up a tape at **Rock Paper
  Scissor** (run by editor **Angus Wall**, a longtime David Fincher collaborator), the
  executive producer treated him — a "young dumb kid" she took for a courier — with care.

### Origin story (business / mindset)
- **Started his business in 1995** (pre-Facebook, pre-Google; had to ask his brother "what
  is the internet"), age **23–24**, fresh out of school, bidding on Fortune 100/500 work
  (Nissan, Sun Microsystems, Janus funds) and **striking out every time** — "heartbreaking...
  devastating." Believed the myth that "if the work is good enough you never have to sell."
- Got **terrible advice** from director friends ("just take control of the call"); cold-
  called competitors for help and **they laughed or hung up** — a catch-22 (no work without
  experience, no experience without work).
- Rescued by producers who taught him to bid — notably **Karen Rainey** (former executive
  producer at **E! Entertainment Television**), who built him a professional five-page Excel
  bid and **gave him the file**; he refined that "wave bidding" for years.
- Found his business mentor ~4–5 years in; a **cousin who'd known him since age five** later
  told him "you've changed... the confidence, how you carry yourself" — offered as proof of
  "the power of coaching."

### Why the Pro Group exists (business / content-strategy / mindset)
- Built the **Pro Group** as the resource he desperately wanted when starting: a place to be
  coached and to be around **peers going through the same battles** — because it's lonely and
  isolating being an entrepreneur.
- **Jim Rohn**: "you are the average of the five people you spend the most time with" — so
  being around other business owners raises your "business IQ."
- You learn best from someone **slightly ahead** of you (relevant, doable); advice from
  someone far ahead feels inapplicable ("I don't have those accolades").
- **Teaching is learning**: "the best student is also the best teacher" (credits **Jim
  Kwik**); "you gain clarity through articulation" (credits **David C. Baker**). Fifteen
  years of teaching made him a clearer thinker. Ask new members to bring "the learner's
  mindset and the teacher's spirit of generosity."
- On what he wants in return: "**you winning is the biggest thing you can do for me**" —
  members' wins are the "fuel for my fire" / proof his teaching works.

### Three 21st-century skills (mindset / content-strategy)
- **(1) Critical thinking** — consume, compare/contrast, do topical reading, form your own
  informed truth amid an abundance of conflicting expert opinion.
- **(2) Learning how to learn (and unlearn, relearn)** — especially with AI, where **asking
  the right question / prompt** matters more than ever. Learning is infinite: "you don't hit
  a ceiling, you just hit a plateau — that's the base of a new mountain."
- **(3) Articulating your thinking persuasively** — writing, storytelling, visuals, speaking.
  **Rhetoric** was once taught like math (Socrates, Plato, the Senate); we've lost it. "The
  most effective communicators of the 21st century will be the most influential."

### Attention economy & AI service opportunities (content-strategy / business / pricing)
- We're in the **information → attention economy**. Universities are no longer information
  centers but **curators** ("they sell you your time back"); the same is true of influencers.
- **Look at where people spend money** to find what the market wants. They're spending on
  social media, podcasts, YouTube, Instagram, LinkedIn — so point design skills there.
- **Alex Hormozi** math: reportedly spends $40K → $120K/month on social (with his wife,
  "~a quarter million a month") because self-created content at equivalent CPM (~$20) is
  ~5× cheaper than buying ads, and he controls it — "those who capture, direct, and monetize
  attention will be the future billionaires." Chris situates himself far below Hormozi but
  spends **>$20K/month** across three editors, a social-media person, and a content writer.
- **Photoshop + AI opportunity**: help creators optimize YouTube titles/thumbnails (use
  ChatGPT to generate high-CTR title ideas, human curates) and produce scroll-stopping
  social content. "Three clients... a hundred thousand dollars a year easily," as retainers.

### Switch verticals, don't add services (business / content-strategy / pricing)
- When your industry is dying (print media, newspapers), **don't add new services** — that
  makes you an inexperienced newbie "chasing the tail." Instead, **take your exact same skill
  set to an industry one degree different that needs it now.**
- His own proof: taught **15 years** at ArtCenter and Otis at ~**$55/hour** (less than he
  paid freelancers at ~$100/hr) — "nobody gets rich teaching." He took the **same lessons,
  same personality, same style** and recorded them on **YouTube**, moving earning potential
  "from zero to thousands of dollars an hour" via brand deals, ad revenue, and speaking.
- **His first typography course** — recorded once in ~6 weeks — has **grossed over $1M**,
  versus not even $100K from 15 years of classroom teaching. "**Don't add more services —
  switch the vertical.**"
