## Summary

A ~90-minute Futur interview in which **Chris Do interviews the Hood twins (Hoodzpah)**
about how they built a small, well-known design studio without a formal design education.
The guests deliver the bulk of the narrative — their scrappy origin story, why they
deliberately stayed a ~4-person studio and positioned as "an expert, not an agency," how
they landed big brands (Red Bull via Instagram DM, Asics via a friend, subcontracting
through agencies to earn logo-carousel credibility), and their three-tier logo pricing
($1,000 for mom-and-pops up to $50–60k for established brands, SeaGeek their biggest).

**Chris Do's role is host/interviewer, but he actively delivers his own pricing doctrine**
rather than just reflecting. His persona-eligible contribution is a compact value-based
pricing masterclass: value is what the *client* perceives, not what the work costs you;
the "labor theory of value" is why people can't stomach a logo priced like a BMW;
perception is reality (Seth Godin, "All Marketers Are Liars"); you can *lose* a job by
quoting too low because big brands buy assurance; raise your number until the client
pushes back so you leave nothing on the table; and set big-hairy-audacious goals meant to
be missed. He adds personal anecdotes (his face on a NASDAQ Times Square billboard via an
Instagram contest; describing himself as a "loud introvert"; LA's standoffishness) and a
diagnostic gap framework for would-be founders (confidence gap / skill gap / opportunity-
or-network gap).

## Key claims (2023-08-06)

### Chris Do (persona-eligible)

_Paraphrased unless quoted; attributed to Chris Do._

- **Value is what the client sees it is, not what you see it is.** "The value isn't in what
  you see it is, it's what they see it is, because they're prepared to spend a different
  amount of money — so don't use your own value system to decide this is the way it's going
  to be." Creatives must do "recon" to read the client's value system.
- **Raise the number until they push back.** He doesn't tell himself he's "worth" a number;
  he asks "I wonder how much they'd be willing to pay," is unoffended by "no," and is
  "pleasantly shocked" when they keep saying yes. Repeated yeses are a signal you're worth
  more than you think → try a new number. You *want* some resistance to confirm you met
  their ceiling and left no money on the table — "there's nothing unethical about this."
- **You can lose a job by being too cheap.** When a big brand (Red Bull, Nike, Asics)
  launches something worth a quarter-billion in revenue and you quote a "ridiculously low"
  price, they read you as a new, small, fly-by-night operation. They want to buy
  **assurance** — a team, a process, proper attention — not a bargain.
- **Labor theory of value explains the resistance.** People have a "false attachment to
  labor and value" (invokes Karl Marx's labor theory of value): historically value is
  measured through labor, so they can't process a logo being worth as much as a 5-Series
  BMW.
- **Perception is reality.** The food tastes better because of the ambiance, tablecloths,
  and how you're spoken to; cites Seth Godin's *All Marketers Are Liars* — "we're complicit
  in the lie." We are emotional buyers who lie to ourselves; building your brand means
  engineering that experience in the buyer's mind (Louis Vuitton vs. Kirkland).
- **Set goals you're meant to fail.** Challenges the guests to set "big hairy audacious
  goals" you can't hit and deliberately fall short — "it's not as important that you achieve
  those goals but the person you become in the pursuit" (attributes the idea to Jim Rohn or
  Jack Canfield, unsure which). Use a "safe goal" plus a "dream big goal."
- **A founder-readiness diagnostic:** when someone wants to go out on their own he looks for
  three gaps — a **confidence gap**, a **skill gap**, or an **opportunity/network gap** —
  and reads which one is actually blocking them.
- **Manifestation framing:** once you "magnetize yourself" to a future goal you've partly
  achieved, "the means and the methods will appear"; the guest (Amy) is "living proof."
- **On himself:** self-describes as a **"loud introvert"** who learned to read the room and
  get out of his comfort zone. On teaching/mentoring, jokes he's "investing in the future"
  like a grandparent "training the next generation" so they throw him work when he's
  "coughing out dust." Says being around **learners** — regardless of chronological age —
  keeps him honest and grounded and out of his own bubble.
- **On LA:** having grown up mostly in California, he grants the stereotype that LA people
  can be standoffish — "everybody is famous, everybody's beautiful, everybody is talented…
  who the heck are you and what can you do for me?"
- **Personal anecdote — Times Square:** his face briefly appeared on a NASDAQ billboard in
  Times Square. A design internet friend (captioned "Chris Green") told him a company was
  featuring people; only five people had commented (comment was the entry requirement), so
  "my odds of one out of five" were good. He submitted a photo, got picked, and scrambled
  friends to shoot it — framed as a "put yourself out there / play the odds" parable.
- **On market saturation:** popularity is cyclical — "familiarity breeds contempt"; there's
  a real advantage to being the fresh new voice. First movers get first-mover advantage,
  but "the market settles and the cream rises to the top," so there's always room for people
  who do it better or position it differently (Stance socks, adult cartoons after The
  Simpsons).

### Amy Hood & Jennifer Hood — Hoodzpah (GUESTS — context only, do NOT train the persona)

_Paraphrased; attributed to the Hood twins. Recorded for corpus context, NOT for
`beliefs.md`/`voice.md`._

- **Origin / education:** born in California, formative years in Kentucky, moved back at 16.
  No design degree — two graphic-design classes at community college plus ~3 years of
  on-the-job training at a "coupon-clipper" local magazine (rapid-fire ad work, "the 10,000
  hours"). Founded Hoodzpah ~age 25 after the magazine folded, forced into it because they
  were "not hireable on paper."
- **Deliberately small:** chose to stay a ~4-person studio and "just use our name," growing
  via bigger projects and pay rather than headcount — positioning as "an expert, not an
  agency." Diverges from Chris Do here, who argues real growth beyond a boutique means
  hiring and delegating.
- **Landing big brands:** Red Bull came via an Instagram DM (they hashtagged work in the
  surf/moto niche); Asics via a friend met at their own creative meetup; early credibility
  came from subcontracting through agencies to earn logo-carousel logos (History Channel,
  Tribeca, 72andSunny). Big-brand capsule/event budgets are often smaller than expected;
  some brands "throw their notoriety around" to demand crazy-low prices. "It's a numbers
  game."
- **Pricing:** three tiers keyed to client size/budget — small/mom-and-pop (as low as
  ~$1,000, often friends), mid-sized, and big established brands (up to **$50–60k** for a
  logo; ~2–3 of the top tier per year). SeaGeek was their biggest logo. Practice a sliding
  scale of value (My Fair Lady anecdote). Set an annual revenue goal, always higher than
  last year, and have hit it every year — plus a "dream big" goal.
- **Money mindset:** Kentucky/farming work ethic and a saving grandmother instilled thrift
  and guilt around getting paid; had to learn to "talk money" confidently, using a perfected
  proposal deck (take notes on the call, do "recon," send the number next day) before they
  could quote live. Cite Blair Enns as a proponent of talking money on the phone.
- **Advice to would-be founders:** ideally work 2–3 years for someone else on their dollar
  first and build 3 months' savings; but nothing forces outreach like no fallback. Start
  something on the side to prove the model with 2–3 clients (low risk, high reward).

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION (host vs. guest, noted not hidden): On scaling, **Chris Do** argues that
> to grow beyond a boutique creative service you "actually have to hire people and delegate,"
> whereas the **Hood twins** deliberately stayed small and reject scale-for-scale's-sake.
> This is a guest position, not a shift in Chris Do's view.
