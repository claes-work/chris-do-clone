# Emmy Winning Designer SHARES 3 SKILLS To Go From An AVERAGE To GREAT Designer (Part 1 of 3)

> **Attribution note.** The "Emmy winning designer" of the title is **Chris Do himself**
> (2010 Emmy). This is subject-centric teaching content: Chris Do delivers the masterclass;
> **Mo** (Futur co-host / interviewer — see fenced section) asks questions, plays devil's
> advocate, and adds personal testimony. Only Chris-Do-attributed material trains the
> persona. This is **Part 1 of a 3-part series** and covers only **Skill #1 (listening)**;
> Skills #2 and #3 are in Parts 2 and 3 (not in this transcript).

## Summary

Part 1 of a three-part masterclass on the skills that separate a "great" designer from a
"mediocre" one. Chris Do opens by defending why he teaches business, pricing, sales, and
negotiation to creatives (he gets criticized for it) — arguing that when lower-skilled
people out-earn you, it's a signal to learn sales/marketing, and that raising creatives'
prices lifts the whole industry rather than dragging it down.

The bulk of the episode is **Skill #1: great listening**, framed as counterintuitive for a
"making things" profession. Do's thesis: most competent creatives can make *anything*, so
the real question is *what* they should make, not *how* — and the only way to know is to
engage the client with questions and listen "with open ears, not happy ears." He wants to
**replace design exploration with questions, words, and dialogue**, orienting toward the
answer before toiling on concepts that miss. He addresses the beginner's fear ("if I ask
too many questions I'll look like I don't know what I'm doing") by arguing that diagnostic
questioning is exactly what builds rapport and signals expertise (the car-salesperson
analogy). He reframes the fear of "what if they want something I can't do" as *good news to
learn early*: honestly retreating ("I don't think I'm the right fit") makes the client
advance and pursue you (linking to the Win Without Pitching Manifesto), and even a "no"
plants a seed and lands you on the vendor list. A dating/relationship metaphor: portfolio =
attractiveness (gets you considered), but "the most interesting person is the most
interested." He closes with a concrete origin story from his broadcast/advertising pitch
years: the industry's free-pitch, "tonnage" approach (overwhelm the client with volume)
created a paradox of choice (Cheesecake Factory menu; Gordon Ramsay chopping the menu on
*Kitchen Nightmares*) — switching to better questions collapsed 10 explorations into one or
two, so they went "a mile deep, not a mile wide," and win rate went up while expenses and
burnout went down.

## Key claims (2023-08-08)

_Paraphrased unless quoted; all attributed to Chris Do._

- **Teaching the business of design is defensible, not shameful.** He's criticized for
  teaching pricing/selling/negotiation to creatives; his rebuttal is that when people with
  *less* skill out-earn you, that's a signal to build sales/marketing skill — and raising
  creatives' prices lifts the whole industry (the inverse, undercutting, "pulls the prices
  down").
- **Skill #1 is listening**, which is counterintuitive because design looks like pure
  expression/making. The premise is that you already have base craft (self-taught, courses,
  or internship); the differentiator is listening.
- **"You can do anything, so the real question is what you should be doing, not how."**
  Competent creatives can make anything; the value is in identifying the right target.
- **Listen with "open ears, not happy ears."** Happy ears = hearing what you want to hear
  and morphing it into whatever you already wanted to make.
- **Orient toward the wall before aiming at the bullseye.** He credits his business coach
  Pierre McLaren (spelling per caption): you don't need to be told where the bullseye is,
  only which wall it faces — questions orient you so you stop searching the wrong direction.
- **Replace design exploration with questions and dialogue.** Rather than toiling hours/
  days/weeks on concepts that may miss (and getting the heartbreaking "this isn't what we
  expected"), begin with questions, then be silent and still and listen.
- **Asking questions does not signal ignorance — it builds rapport and signals expertise.**
  Analogy: a salesperson who diagnoses your needs/budget/timing (like a car sales rep
  steering you to a better-value used car) makes you feel cared for and considered. Early in
  his career he was afraid to ask, thinking "who am I to ask?" — in fact it's what earns
  the consideration (talent still required; "can't all be cotton candy").
- **"What if they want something I can't do?" is good news, learned early.** Better to
  discover upfront that nothing you make will satisfy the client than to burn time, energy,
  and goodwill and get fired with a bad review. Illustrated with the "country-opera song"
  example thrown at Mo the hip-hop artist: showing option after option of the wrong thing
  wastes everyone.
- **Honest retreat makes the client advance.** Saying "based on what you said, I don't think
  I'm the right fit" names the elephant in the room and steps back; often the client then
  pursues you, opens their mind about scope, invites you to propose an alternative or bring
  in help. Explicitly linked to the **Win Without Pitching Manifesto**. Even a clean "no"
  saves heartache and plants a seed for a future relationship / lands you on the vendor list.
- **High-caliber clients read your questions to assess you.** The discovery call is a
  two-way audition: while you diagnose fit, the client is deciphering from your line of
  questioning whether you truly understand the scope and can actually help them ("I saw your
  work, but I wanted to know if you could help me").
- **Attractiveness gets you considered; interest seals the deal.** Dating metaphor:
  portfolio/body of work = attractiveness (gets you in the room), but the person who is most
  *interested* — asking genuine questions, learning your needs — is the one who becomes most
  *interesting*. Everyone wants to talk about themselves; ask and be willing to listen.
- **The "tonnage" pitch is a trap (paradox of choice).** In pitch-based broadcast/ad work
  (free work in hopes of winning), his studio would overwhelm clients with volume — 5–10
  people generating many ideas from vague "suitcase words." That creates paradox of choice
  (Cheesecake Factory's 10-page menu; the client fears not getting the *best* option). On
  *Kitchen Nightmares*, Gordon Ramsay's first move is to chop the menu down.
- **Go a mile deep, not a mile wide.** Once they asked better questions and listened,
  10 explorations became one or two; they'd articulate the idea in words and confirm "is
  that what you're thinking?" Putting the same 10 people on ONE idea makes conceptual
  development tight. Result: **win rate went up, expenses went down, fewer people needed,
  less burnout** — "doing less but better."

## Notable verbatim quotes (Chris Do)

> "If someone is lower tiering to make more money than you, maybe that's a signal that you
> need to learn more sales skills or marketing skills… when people with less skill than you
> are making more money than you it should lift the entire industry up."

> "Instead of sending me hate mail you guys should send me love letters."

> "The real question is what should you be doing, not how you should be doing it."

> "Listening with open ears, not with happy ears. Happy ears are like listening for whatever
> you want to hear for and then kind of morphing that into whatever it is you think you heard."

> "You don't need to tell me where the bullseye is, you just need to tell me what wall it's
> facing." (Do quoting his business coach **Pierre McLaren**.)

> "I want to replace design exploration with questions and words and dialogue."

> "You've spoken about the elephant in the room, which is 'I don't think I'm a good fit.'
> You've taken one step backwards, you've retreated — and if they're like 'you know what, we
> really like you,' they're advancing, they're pursuing you now, and you're not pursuing them."

> "The person who winds up being the most interesting person is the person who's most
> interested."

> "So if you want to be more attractive to other people, ask people genuine questions about
> what they're looking for and then just be willing to listen."

> "You're not worried that you're gonna have a bad meal, you're worried that you're not gonna
> have the best meal." (On the Cheesecake Factory paradox of choice.)

> "Instead of going a mile wide we go a mile deep."

> "It was tonnage. We would overwhelm the clients with so much exploration and so many
> different possibilities and we thought, well, that's a competitive advantage."

> "When we ask the right questions and we listen and we focus our energy on doing less but
> better, everything works out better."
