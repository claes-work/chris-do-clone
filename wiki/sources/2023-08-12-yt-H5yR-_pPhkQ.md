## Summary

A live teaching session (The Process show) in which Chris Do works a room of designers
through his core answer to "how do I charge more?" His thesis: the money is not in the
craft, the portfolio, the awards, or years of experience — those are "excuses." What lets
one person charge 10x for the identical deliverable is **confidence, trust, self-worth,
and above all the quality of the questions you ask.** He builds the case through a live
example (Aaron the cinematographer charges $5,000 for a job Chris would charge $50,000
for — then Chris just hires Aaron to do the work and pockets the difference), a role-play
with a recent-grad named Christian who "closes" too fast at $5,000 and leaves $45,000 on
the table, and two personal stories: the near-bankruptcy Pictionary story (a year and a
half into Blind, three months of runway, staff refusing to leave) and the **$48,000 Buick
optical-titles broker story** (age 22, he brokered a shoot he'd never done for a price he
made up "very scientifically" — i.e. arbitrarily). The teaching arc: stop prescribing,
**stay in the diagnostic phase** like a good doctor; be **"passionately detached"** from
the outcome; lead with **empathy for the value the client receives**; and understand that
clients don't hire the best designer — they hire the person who makes them feel it will
get done.

## Key claims (paraphrase; dated 2023-08-12 — doctrine circa 2016)

_Attributed to Chris Do unless a fenced block says otherwise. Paraphrase unless quoted._

- **Two "regular guys" can charge wildly different prices for identical work.** In the
  live example, Aaron would charge $5,000 for a job Chris would price at $50,000 — same
  client, same deliverable. Chris's move: charge the $50,000, then **hire Aaron to do the
  actual work** for his $5,000, and keep ~$45,000. `(2023-08-12)`
- **The differentiator is not tangible.** The room offers boldness, experience, overhead,
  studio, kids, "peace of mind," trust, history, communication, confidence, personality.
  Chris strips each away (no company, no portfolio — "I sold it, signed a non-compete")
  to show none of the measurable things are the real lever. `(2023-08-12)`
- **"The way you demonstrate your value is by the quality of the questions you ask."**
  This is the central claim of the session. Anyone can be taught better questions; good
  questions signal what you know and how you think, and they make the client feel
  uniquely understood. `(2023-08-12)`
- **Diagnose as long as possible before prescribing.** Model every professional you trust
  (doctor, mechanic, dermatologist, hair stylist): the one who spends the most time
  learning about your needs is the one you trust most. Christian's failure in the
  role-play was **closing with no discovery, no negotiation, no questions.** `(2023-08-12)`
- **"Passionately detached."** Stay neutral and detached from the result while remaining
  genuinely passionate about helping — like advising your best friend or mother. If you're
  attached to closing, you can't do the diagnostic work. `(2023-08-12)`
- **Empathy over self-interest.** Christian priced at $5,000 because that's what *he*
  wanted; he wasn't thinking about the value the *client* would receive. Pricing should
  follow the value delivered, surfaced through questions. `(2023-08-12)`
- **Self-worth precedes price.** The name, awards, studio, and portfolio are "just
  excuses"; you have to look inside and believe in yourself ("Mastery mindset"). People
  wait for external validation before believing they're worth something — that waiting is
  the trap. `(2023-08-12)`
- **Clients don't hire the best designers — they hire assurance.** "People do not hire the
  best designers; they hire the people that can assure them, make them feel like it's
  going to get done." Trust is more psychological than design. Do a "stinker" job with
  enthusiasm and you earn the big trusted project later. `(2023-08-12)`
- **You don't have to choose between meaningful work and money.** Rejects the "designer's
  dilemma" (sell out and eat vs. stay pure and starve): "you should be able to do the work
  you love and make a ton of money — but you do need to acquire different skills."
  `(2023-08-12)`
- **Good work = solving the client's problem** — "sometimes it looks good, sometimes it
  doesn't, I don't really care." Solve the problem, make them feel great about the
  process, and higher-level clients keep trusting you with more. `(2023-08-12)`
- **The $48,000 Buick story (age ~22).** He brokered optical film titles (shooter "Dan,"
  captioned "Cony") for a Buick commercial job two relationships removed — digitized Dan's
  reel on his Avid, cut a sample, and named the price by a "very scientific approach"
  (i.e. he made it up), reasoning only "I want enough runway that I don't have to work for
  6 months." The client said yes. `(2023-08-12)`
- **"Sometimes dumb is good."** He was "too stupid, too young, too cocky to know what the
  rules were" — and that ignorance let him charge freely. "The more you learn about how
  things are supposed to work, the more you start to [sell] yourself short." `(2023-08-12)`
- **Near-bankruptcy loyalty story.** ~18 months into Blind, three months of runway, two
  biggest clients gone; he told his staff (all women, friends) to leave and paid them in
  advance, but they refused and stayed, playing Pictionary daily until a pay-it-forward
  referral (free work he'd done in school) revived the business. `(2023-08-12)`

> ⚠️ **CONTRADICTION / TENSION (flag for synthesis).** The headline live example here is a
> pure **arbitrage/markup** move — buy Aaron's labor at $5k, sell it at $50k, pocket the
> spread — justified by "confidence/trust/questions." This sits in tension with the more
> disciplined **value-based-pricing** doctrine Chris Do is known for elsewhere (price to
> the *quantified value the client receives*, surfaced via discovery), where the number is
> anchored to client outcomes rather than to "the number I want so I don't have to work
> for 6 months" (the Buick story's explicit logic). This may be reconciled as an
> *early/2016-era, deliberately provocative* framing. **Cannot be cross-checked here** — no
> other pricing source is loaded in this single-file ingest. Coordinator/synthesis should
> compare against later pricing pages before this trains `beliefs.md`.
