## Summary

The closing installment of the three-part masterclass, covering **Skill #3: conceptual
design / semiotics** — the "god-tier" layer beyond good (Skill #1, listening) and great
(Skill #2, typography). Chris Do defines conceptual design as the deliberate combination of
two elements (image + image, image + word, word + word) to create a "hybrid third meaning" —
citing the book *A Smile in the Mind* as the canonical reference for this idea, and warning
that claiming "my concept is a face and a banana" is *not* a concept, just a combination.
He walks through canonical examples: the FedEx logo's negative-space arrow (Landor
Associates), a *Dark Knight Rises* poster's negative-space Batman symbol, meme structure
("if you strip away the image the meaning is lost; if you pull away the words the meaning is
lost — that's semiotics"), Doc Martens' "the mainstream is polluted" ad copy, and *The
Economist*'s recurring copywriting brilliance (the Trump "Midas touch" headline-inversion
ad, "lose the ability to slip out of a meeting unnoticed," "the pregnant pause… don't be the
father"). He extends the idea into three-dimensional/ambient media (the twin-toilet
drink-responsibly ad, the TV-Guide fold-out stair-climb fitness ad, the crunches-in-the-
gutter magazine spread) and into sequential/motion design via a personal case study: a title
sequence he designed for a friend's short film *Crank Calls*, where a spinning rotary-phone
dial visually doubles as a revolver's cylinder — a title sequence that won a British D&AD
Pencil. He closes on how to *develop* this skill (awareness first, then pattern-recognition
across "seven or eight formulas") and a personal disclosure about being a private-art-school
teacher for 15+ years and facing "who's this wanker" YouTube skepticism.

## Key claims (2023-08-22)

_Paraphrased unless quoted; attributed to Chris Do._

- **Conceptual design = semiotics: combining two things to create a hybrid "third meaning."**
  Putting a tomato over someone's face isn't "just a tomato and a face" — it's a statement
  (critique, "they should be removed from the office," etc.). Students who say "my concept is
  a face and a banana" are naming a combination, not a concept — "that don't mean Jack to me."
- **The canonical reference is the book *A Smile in the Mind*** (wit and humor in design). A
  witty conceptual piece works like a joke or puzzle: too complicated and it frustrates ("I
  don't get it"); too easy and it requires no mental effort. **There's a sweet spot** — no
  scientific formula, but you know it when you find it and "you smile in the mind."
- **FedEx logo (Landor Associates): a negative-space arrow hidden between the E and the X.**
  Chris imagines the room where the creative director declared it the winning direction
  because it would "last 10, 20, 100 years" — a **simple, timeless expression, not
  gimmicky or overlabored.** Personal anecdote: his son spotted the arrow in a FedEx truck
  logo unprompted, proof "even children can understand" a well-made conceptual mark.
- **Christopher Nolan's *Oppenheimer* reminded him of *The Dark Knight Rises*** — a poster/
  trailer shot of a city crumbling that, in the negative space between buildings, also reads
  as the Batman symbol: "one moment they're able to create an image that has two reads."
- **Memes are semiotics, distilled**: "if you strip away the image, the meaning is lost; if
  you pull away the words, the meaning is lost." A familiar image + a new phrase changes the
  image's meaning — "it gives the image even more power."
- **Copywriting is a conceptual medium too.** Doc Martens' "the mainstream is polluted" ad
  (gas-mask imagery, dirty/sewer visuals) targeted an anti-establishment audience knowingly.
  *The Economist* is his standing example of brilliant, audience-aware copy (world-leader/
  C-suite readership; simple red-and-serif layout): "lose the ability to slip out of a meeting
  unnoticed"; "the pregnant pause… don't be the father"; and a pre-2016 ad that inverted
  "Donald Trump" to "Trump Donald" to signal "beat him? read us" (explicitly framed as **not
  a political statement**, predating Trump's national political prominence).
- **Ambient/3D conceptual media examples**: a responsible-drinking campaign using two extra
  (fake) toilets next to a real one ("if you can't tell which is real, you've had enough to
  drink"); a TV Guide print ad that becomes a fold-out image of someone climbing 100 flights
  of stairs (gym/anti-couch-potato message); a magazine spread where the gutter fold becomes
  a woman's midsection so lifting the page animates a crunch.
- **Sequential/motion conceptual design — the *Crank Calls* title-sequence case study.**
  Working on a title sequence for a friend's short film about a bullied man exacting revenge
  on a tyrant neighbor via a crank call, Chris landed on the visual metaphor of an **old
  rotary phone dial that doubles as a revolver's cylinder** — spinning through the credits,
  then locking, spinning back "empty," landing on one bullet. Pitched it as "the phone as a
  weapon, an undercurrent of violence"; his friend Terry's reaction: "That's it, Chris." The
  3D artist (Lawrence) built and animated it; **it won a D&AD (British Design and Art
  Direction) Pencil** — "what I have not been able to win again."
- **How to develop this skill**: it starts with **awareness** — actually looking at your own
  work and asking whether it does this at all, rather than aspiring to be "conceptual" while
  producing work that isn't. Being a "super analytical person," Chris broke the pattern down
  into what he estimates are **"seven or eight formulas"** for building hybrid-meaning
  concepts, which is how he's able to teach and recall them systematically.
- **Personal disclosure**: he taught at a private art school for **over 15 years**, breaking
  down these concepts repeatedly with students. He's aware some viewers see him as "this
  30-something kid" (he was over 40 at filming) perpetuating "creatives overpricing
  themselves," and frames sharing this material as a way to earn continued engagement/comments
  that "encourage us to make more content like this."
- **Recommended resources** (paraphrased, self-reported reading list): *A Smile in the Mind*
  (now back in print, cheaper than the ~$600 out-of-print copies); *The Ad Concept Book*;
  and an international advertising magazine he calls "Luze Archive" (`attribution: uncertain`
  spelling — almost certainly ***Lürzer's Archive***), which he says he's subscribed to
  consistently for ~20 years despite print being "dead."

## Notable verbatim quotes (Chris Do)

> "When you put things together it forces a relationship… it's not just a tomato, it's not
> just a person's face, but it's saying something."

> "My concept is to use a face and a banana and like — yeah, that don't mean Jack to me."

> "When you create a conceptual piece that's witty, it's like a joke or a puzzle that the
> audience gets to figure out. If you make the puzzle too complicated it's frustrating… if
> you make it too easy… that required no mental effort at all. So there's a sweet spot."

> "They probably explored 100 different configurations… this is what we're gonna sell them
> on because this is genius. It's gonna last 10, 20, 100 years."

> "If you strip away the image, the meaning is lost. If you pull away the words, the meaning
> is lost."

> "Once you get it, you feel smarter, you feel like you're in on the joke, and you feel like
> connected to this thing."

> "Nobody taught me this when I learned — I'm like, 'oh, that's what that is.'… I start to
> break down… what are the patterns I see, are there only so many ways of doing this — in
> fact, like seven or eight formulas."

> "So he crank-calls him… and it occurred to me what the visual metaphor is: he's using a
> phone to exact his vengeance."

> "What many of you don't know is I taught at a private art school for over 15 years — these
> are concepts I've broken down time and time again."
