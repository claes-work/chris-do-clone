## Summary

Chris Do interviews a former student, "Sho," who built an AI-cloning company and pitched
creating an AI-trained version of Chris ("dobot") to scale his teaching. The episode traces
the project from v1 (trained on Chris's book, the $10,000 Business Bootcamp course material,
and Q&A session transcripts) through v2 (more Socratic/diagnostic, asking clarifying
questions before answering) to v2.1 (refined after a live Twitter Spaces demo where a GPT
platform update temporarily made it verbose and "not Chris's style"). Chris recounts being
moved by how well the tool captured his diagnostic, question-first communication style, and
frames the project against his standing "teach a billion people" mission — a way to extend
his reach beyond the 1:1 time he can personally give, at a fraction of his $5,000/hour
consulting rate. The back half is a long, first-person **Chris Do position on AI ethics and
copyright** in creative work (diffusion models, "looking" vs. copying, two categories of
infringement), plus a personal disclosure that one of his first ad-industry jobs (~2010) was
3D graphics work for the first *Avengers* film's Iron Man visuals. The episode closes with
Chris relaying an approving anecdote from **Daniel Priestley** about AI's wealth-generation
potential.

## Key claims (2023-09-03)

_Paraphrased unless quoted; attributed to Chris Do._

- **The "teach a billion people" mission is explicitly restated** as the frame for why he's
  building an AI clone: "we have this really big hairy audacious goal, which is to teach a
  billion people how to make a living doing what they love… it might take multiple
  lifetimes." He says he isn't worried about the scale of the goal because setting it clearly
  "helps you make better decisions" and "you magnetize yourself and draw the people and
  resources you need."
- **What fed the first version of "dobot"** (self-reported, in his own words): his book
  (*Pocket Full of Do*, "those are my thoughts collected, it's clear"), the **Business
  Bootcamp** — described as a **$10,000 program** with **7 or 8 modules** covering
  "bidding, negotiations, mindset, communication — all the things I think people need to
  learn to run a proper business" — plus Q&A session recordings from "the last couple of
  years."
- **A properly framed question is 50% of the answer** — his stated reason for wanting the
  clone to ask diagnostic questions rather than answer immediately: "if somebody were to ask
  me a question today I would not just tell them the answer because I need to know more about
  what their intentions are."
- **His own consulting rate: $5,000/hour** (self-reported) — "if you wanted to book with me
  for an hour, it would cost you five thousand dollars an hour. I don't have many takers for
  that price." He frames the AI clone as a way to reach the people priced out of that rate:
  "I want dobot to do most of the work" so it can "handle 90% of your questions… at a
  fraction of the cost." *(Note: distinct from the previously-fenced "$10k/day" figure
  elsewhere in the corpus, which belongs to David C. Baker, not Chris — this $5,000/hour
  figure is Chris's own self-reported rate.)*
- **He is "not just my own content" — he is explicit about citing sources.** "I am the
  product of multiple influences, from the books I've read, from the videos I've watched…
  when it mentions a concept I normally try my best to cite and quote people… I think it's
  honorable to cite your sources, both for you and for the creator of the original thought…
  I'm always a little leery when people say things and they say it as if they came up with it
  by themselves." *(Reinforces the citation ethos already documented elsewhere in the
  corpus.)*
- **AI ethics/copyright — his considered, first-person position** (a long unscripted answer,
  explicitly flagged by Chris himself as "too meaty to fully cover"):
  - He recounts a design-school-department-chair professor's framing that "AI is not doing
    anything different than what humans do — it's just called looking, and then you make it
    your own," and explains diffusion models technically (in his own words) as processing a
    *blurred/diffused* version of training images, not lifting pixels directly — "like a
    child seeing the world for the first time."
  - His own extension of the argument: humans looking at other people's work for inspiration
    are also "borrowing elements, assets, that spark" but "give yourself a pass that it's not
    copying — but you are."
  - **Two kinds of copyright infringement that actually bother him**: (1) "blatant lifting of
    assets and images that don't belong to you" without payment or credit — "unforgivable…
    should come at a heavy cost"; (2) style/technique influence (e.g., admiring and adopting
    Saul Bass's graphic simplicity, or a color palette) — "I don't believe you're turning
    yourself into the authorities… nobody is."
  - He acknowledges the threat non-designers using AI tools pose to trained professionals'
    livelihoods ("a very threatening gesture, and I can see why people are really concerned")
    but says the ethics/legal question is "not for me to decide… for the courts to figure
    out."
  - **His practical advice**: "don't wait for the courts to figure this out — you need to be
    playing with the tools, integrating some of these things into your workflow, and you draw
    the line for yourself. But don't artificially handicap yourself" — comparing refusal to
    use AI tools to refusing to use Photoshop/Illustrator for photo retouching out of
    principle: "you're putting yourself at a distinct disadvantage."
- **Biography (self-reported, new to the corpus): early 3D/motion work on the first
  Marvel *Avengers* film.** "One of the first movie campaigns I worked on was for the
  Avengers film, the first Marvel Avengers, back in — my — 2010 or something like that, and I
  had to create a lot of the graphics in 3D for Iron Man." He reflects that even without
  today's neural-network tools, "the code from back then" hinted at what would become
  possible.
- **Relayed (not original) anecdote — Daniel Priestley**, from a conference in Newcastle:
  Priestley told Chris "very confidently" that AI "is going to be the source of generating
  billions of new money for people who embrace it… the only problem is this money is not
  going to be distributed evenly." Chris uses this to close the episode with an
  encouragement-not-fear framing: "are you going to count yourself amongst [the winners]…
  stay curious."
- Lighter asides (self-reported, lower-stakes): he used to go to therapy with a therapist he
  names "Joan," and describes the emotional value of undivided attention in that context;
  references the film *Her* (Spike Jonze) as an analogy for emotionally real human–AI
  relationships.
