# Don't Justify Your Prices. Do This Instead.

Solo monologue by **Chris Do** (@thefutur, 2023-10-19) on how to handle the client price
objection ("Why does it cost this much?" / "I can't afford this") without capitulating or
justifying. Single speaker throughout; a "friend" and generic "client" appear only inside
Chris's illustrative examples.

★ **L3-candidate** — contains two distinct, reusable methods for `topics/pricing` and
`topics/sales-clients`: (1) the **have-to-have vs. nice-to-have re-design** negotiation
move, and (2) the **"never justify your price" / symmetry-of-logic** reframe.

## Summary

Chris opens by naming the visceral anxiety that hits when a mutually-interested prospect
asks why the price is so high or says they can't afford it — the cold sweat, the urge to
immediately capitulate and drop the price, or the opposite urge to lash out defensively.
He then gives two strategies.

**Strategy 1 — Reframe the price objection as a creative re-design problem (when there is
mutual interest).** All business is a negotiation; take the emotion out — it's a price, not
your self-worth. When both parties want to work together, "anything can happen." Rather than
discounting, Chris scripts a line that keeps the deal alive by separating the client's
**have-to-haves** (the MVP — e.g. for a videographer: the shoot, the finished color-graded
edit, delivery by a date) from the **nice-to-haves** (title design, 3D animation product
demo, extravagant locations). You deliver the MVP at the price, then let the client choose
which nice-to-have(s) matter most, advising them where they will and won't get good ROI.
Outcomes: either they find more budget and take all of it (great), or they stick to budget
and you lean into your expertise as a trusted advisor to recommend where to spend and where
to save.

**Strategy 2 — Never justify your price (the psychological reframe).** Justifying concedes
the "higher ground" to the prospect. Chris applies his **"symmetry of logic"** test: if a
principle is true it should hold across most areas of life, not just one. Dating analogy: if
a person you're interested in opens with "why should I date you?" and you start listing your
job, credit, and car, you've let them make you prove your worth — and you'd never open that
way yourself. Enter relationships (and deals) as equals. In most industries — car
dealership, supermarket, car wash — nobody justifies prices; it even feels uncomfortable to
*ask* why a can of beans costs 78 cents, so why is it good to *answer* it in creative
services? Your price is the output of your own calculations (overhead, margin, cost of
living, experience, quality). At that point you simply present it: this is the price, I'm
not really negotiable, and if it's not for you I understand — "for every customer there's a
vendor." A prospect who immediately challenges price and demands justification signals a
hard client ("a long day"). The root cause is people-pleasing and conflict-avoidance: we
treat every question as legitimate, and "justify your price" is not, in his opinion, a
legitimate question. He closes by framing his blunt stance as coming from support and love,
not arrogance — clients respect you more for holding your ground.

## Key claims

All dated 2023-10-19, paraphrased, attributed to **Chris Do** unless noted.

- **All business is a negotiation** — remove the emotion; a price objection is just how two
  business people talk about a thing. The price is not you, not your self-worth, and not the
  quality of your work. (Chris Do)
- **Mutual interest is the foundational layer of a deal.** When both parties want to work
  together, anything can be negotiated; when one party doesn't want it, there's no point.
  (Chris Do)
- **Method — re-design instead of discount:** respond to "I can't afford this" with an
  invitation to "get creative and design this in such a way that you get what you need,"
  separating **have-to-haves** (the MVP) from **nice-to-haves**. Deliver the MVP at the
  price, then price/prioritize the extras. (Chris Do)
- **Let the client rank the nice-to-haves** and advise on ROI — position yourself as trusted
  advisor: as much as you'd like it to look a certain way, some spend is "unnecessary money"
  and you can save them a couple bucks where they won't feel it. (Chris Do)
- **Two good outcomes** from the re-design move: the client finds more budget and takes all
  the extras, OR they hold budget and ask which one you recommend — either way the deal and
  the relationship stay intact. (Chris Do)
- **Never justify your price** — justification is a sign of conceding the higher ground to
  your prospect. (Chris Do; he references an earlier workshop where he first said this.)
- **"Symmetry of logic"** — if a principle works one way it should work in most other ways;
  if it only works in one instance and fails everywhere else in your life, question whether
  it's actually true or just something you want to be true. (Chris Do)
- **Sales is human dynamics / a social construct** — enter the relationship as an equal, not
  as a "simp" begging to prove worth. (Chris Do)
- **Cross-industry proof:** in most industries other than creative services, people don't
  justify prices — car dealerships, supermarkets, car washes. It's even uncomfortable to ask
  why a can of beans is 78 cents; if it's uncomfortable to ask, it shouldn't be something you
  feel obligated to answer. (Chris Do)
- **Your price is already the output of your own calculations** — overhead, profit margin,
  cost of living, level of experience, level of quality. At that point, just present it.
  (Chris Do)
- **"For every customer there's a vendor"** — if the price doesn't work for them, you're
  probably not the right person, and that's fine. (Chris Do)
- **Prospects who immediately challenge price and demand justification are a red flag** —
  from experience, taking on clients like that leads to an unenjoyable engagement ("a long
  day"). (Chris Do; self-reported / experience-based)
- **Root cause is people-pleasing and conflict-aversion** — we're conditioned to answer
  every question and treat each as legitimate; "why is it this much / justify your price" is
  not a legitimate question. (Chris Do, opinion)
- **Holding your ground earns respect** — clients will respect you more for not throwing
  yourself at a project and losing self-respect; his blunt short clips come from support and
  love, not arrogance. (Chris Do)
