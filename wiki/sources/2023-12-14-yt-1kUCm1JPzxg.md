# How To Build A Successful Personal Brand (Full Masterclass)

★ L3-candidate: personal branding for topics/branding + persona — this is a substantial, first-person Chris Do teaching session that lays out his full "Unbland Yourself"–adjacent personal-branding method (shadow words + transformer word → two/three-word brand → story lens). High-value for both `wiki/topics/branding/` and `persona/beliefs.md` + `persona/voice.md`.

## Summary

**Chris Do delivers this masterclass himself** (in-person workshop/retreat setting, taking live questions from named attendees — Ellen/"Alan," Mo, Devon, Egor, "Trigo"/Drigo, Scott). The material is therefore **persona data** for Chris Do. Attendees appear only as questioners/foils; their words are context, not persona.

The session frames personal branding not as a marketing/sales tactic but as a process of self-recovery: remembering who you are and having the courage to show it. Chris walks through the history of branding (differentiation → preference), the Jungian persona/shadow model, and his refined workshop exercise for landing a "two-word brand" starting from shadow words plus a "transformer word." He weaves in a long tangent (his wife's "rememberer" conspiracy videos) to set up his core thesis: personal branding is memory, not invention. Second half is Q&A covering personal vs. business/studio brand, avoiding performative vulnerability ("trauma dumping"), how to tell whether vulnerability is genuine, story as differentiator, and a closing riff (money is neutral; alignment, not money, is "getting the job done").

## Key claims
_All dated 2023-12-14. Paraphrased unless quoted. Speaker = Chris Do unless noted._

- **There may be no such thing as a "business brand."** What people call a corporate brand is the collective output of a team of marketers/writers/designers creating an experience; its goal is to get you to buy. Chris is "allergic" to that sell-oriented framing being imported into personal branding.
- **Personal branding, done his way, is self-inquiry, not selling** — "I want to know who the hell I'm supposed to be" and to "show up 100% as I am." Doing so paradoxically attracts more people.
- **Branding = differentiation + preference.** History: specialization → competition → identity design → over-abundance of choice (internet/social multiply it). Modern branding is only ~30 years old. A brand is an "irrational emotional connection" to a thing; in an age of abundance we shop for meaning, not survival.
- **Every product has a story; a product without a story is a commodity** (easily replaced). He illustrates with water: Arrowhead (~35¢, filtered tap) vs. Fiji (~$3, "artisanal," exotic bottle) vs. Voss (~$5) — same water, different stories, different prices. Applied to people: "What's your story? How manufactured is it? What's the integrity/authenticity of your voice?"
- **Jung's persona vs. shadow.** The ego regulates who you are in public. The persona is "who you pretend to be to be accepted in society"; the shadow is the shameful stuff you hide. The persona is installed by parents in childhood. Pretend long enough and "you forget who the hell you are."
- **Personal branding is memory, not invention** — "It's not invention, it's memory." His job is to get people to *remember* who they are (tied to the "rememberer" conspiracy tangent).
- **True strength comes from weakness/vulnerability.** The historically captivating figures people admire (Gandhi, MLK, Socrates, Mandela, Dalai Lama, Mother Teresa) were physically unimposing but radiated conviction — not the muscular "alpha" (The Rock) social-media archetype.
- **Contrarian positioning ("zig/zag/zug").** Marketing rule he finds true every time: when everyone does one thing, do the opposite; you'll stand out. Since social feeds are saturated with images of perfection/strength/glamour, showing vulnerability reads as "refreshing."
- **Intention is detectable.** "We can smell intention from a mile away." Vulnerability that's actually attention-seeking / trauma-dumping is sensed and repels people. Genuineness must feel uncomfortable to reveal.
- **The two-word brand exercise (refined method):**
  1. Start from *shadow words* — write words you'd use when not liking yourself, plus what critics/trolls/a critical parent would say.
  2. Keep only the words that emotionally *trigger* you (courage requires discomfort to reveal).
  3. Compress to one or two words (use ChatGPT if you can't).
  4. Add a **transformer word** that turns the negative into a positive — "this is where the magic happens."
  - Example: Tom Ross (founder of Design Cuts) → "people pleaser" (shadow) → "**proud people pleaser**" (transformed; useful because he runs communities).
  - The two/three-word brand is "a title to the movie/book" — intriguing enough that people open chapter one; it becomes a **lens** to search your life for infinite on-theme stories. Turns a liability into an asset.
- **Don't manufacture trauma.** A woman with a normal childhood asked if she should invent trauma — "No, please don't." You don't need trauma; people-pleasing isn't traumatic. Brains wrongly normalize whatever others reveal.
- **Don't just invert his brand.** People hear his "loud introvert" and reflexively offer "shy extrovert" — "That required zero soul searching." 55 workshop attendees produced the same lazy flip. Real work is uncomfortable.
- **His own brand admission:** "loud introvert" wasn't the product of shadow work — he just did what worked and formalized it ("I'm a formula guy — if it works, I do it again"). His LinkedIn posts that touch painful/introvert moments **outperform ~3:1** on engagement (e.g., ~3,000 vs ~1,000).
- **Finding your core identity (tree metaphor).** He was "desperately seeking Chris" until college, where graphic design and specifically **typography** became a trunk he could build on ("I can suck at everything else… but I'm going to be good at type"). Rings of the tree from core outward: designer → teacher → entrepreneur → motion designer/director. "When you find your core, everything's pretty easy."
- **Personal vs. studio brand (to a solo studio owner):** for a studio of one there's no real conflict — your personal brand *is* figuring out your POV, values, origin/transformation story. Challenges the assumption "a team gives more clout" via critical thinking: define clout (access to bigger clients + higher fees), then find counterexamples (Seth Godin, Aaron Draplin, James Martin, Dan) — individuals with more clout than teams. Lesson: collaborate because you *want* to, not because you think you must present as a team. "Words shape thinking."
- **On "should" questions (attributed to Mo, paraphrasing Chris from an earlier call):** people usually seek *permission*, not answers; Chris can't answer a "should" question because he can't hold your accountability — he can only guide you to the answer you already want.
- **On pandering / trendiness of vulnerability:** "Don't do something just because it's popular or unpopular. Do it because it's true." Only ~1% of the world actually does vulnerability; we over-magnify a few examples (Brené Brown, Jo Koy, Hasan Minhaj).
- **Test for performative vulnerability (attributed partly to a co-teacher, then Chris's version):** ask "am I doing this for validation, praise, or approval?" — if yes, it's not true vulnerability. Chris's cleaner test: **journal test** — write as if no one will read it. If you'd shade the story so your "mom reads it and treats you nicer," it's performance.
- **Story rules:** don't make yourself the hero — "someone else is the hero, not me. I'm the idiot usually." Cites Hormozi's stage tactic (name three bad things about yourself, then one good — the good one lands harder) and Dan's line "sage on the stage vs. guide on the side." Be suspicious of "I struggled then got totally cured" stories — we relate to the *struggle*, not the resolution. Pixar's rule: we admire characters for their struggles more than their success.
- **Audiences eventually co-own your brand.** Build a strong enough community and you lose sole control; when you act inconsistently with the brand they turn on you (Nike; **Elon Musk / "love the car, hate Elon"** stickers as the live example).
- **Complicity in the story (Seth Godin, *All Marketers Are Liars*):** buyers and sellers agree on a shared "lie" (wine, tennis racket) — real differences are smaller than we claim. If you refuse to tell your story for fear it's "too me-me-me," you become the commodity. Fame gets mistaken for skill (Draplin on young designers' top-10 lists).
- **Responsibility of teaching (gunpowder/Oppenheimer analogy):** "I'm going to teach you what I know. How you use it… is your responsibility." Same video yields opposite outcomes ("con artist" vs. "$40K / six-/seven-figure" results) depending on the viewer's lens. "I'm just here for the action takers."
- **Money is neutral (closing debate with Mo/Drigo):** money doesn't make you jaded — *misalignment with who you're supposed to be* does. "Getting the job done" = being in alignment and going for it regardless of consequences, not chasing money you hate (e.g., don't become a realtor you'd hate — people who *love* real estate will "eat your lunch"). Jesse Itzler story: "a billion times zero is zero" — be a billionaire in the heart. Alignment tends to produce both happiness and, he suspects, wealth (in varying dimensions — Dalai Lama/Mother Teresa rich spiritually).
