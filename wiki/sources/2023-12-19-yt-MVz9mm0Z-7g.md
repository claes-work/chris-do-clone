# Personal Branding — Most Important Thing You Do (AdobeMAX 2023)

Chris Do keynote at Adobe MAX 2023 (full video, ~60–90 min compressed from an 8–10 hour
workshop). Solo, first-person delivery of his complete personal-branding framework — the
"unbland yourself" thesis. Prime persona/voice/beliefs material. All content is
Chris-attributed (solo keynote); no MC intro survives in the transcript.

## Summary

Chris Do argues that in a social-media world where "corporations are trying to be more
human and humans are trying to be more like corporations," the most valuable thing a
person can build is a distinctive **personal brand** — and that building one is
counterintuitive: it is an act of **self-discovery, not creation**, that requires
**vulnerability** rather than polish. He opens with a friend's quote — "people don't
fall in love with corporations, they fall in love with personalities" — and dissects why
figures like designer Aaron Draplin, or the "Ugly Company" upcycled-fruit brand, draw
people in: they are refreshingly transparent, self-aware, and vulnerable, and they lean
INTO their weaknesses rather than sanitizing them.

He walks through his four-part comic-book-mythology framework for a personal brand
(origin story / community / defining attributes / the enemy), and layers on several
tools: the **three Fs** of storytelling (facts, feelings, fun), the **new brand ROI /
karmic-equity** model (give without asking; every ask resets the account), the **infinite
game** over short-term performance marketing, the Jungian **Persona vs. Shadow**, a
**Shadow-work exercise** to surface a hidden truth about yourself, and the **two-word
brand** — a shadow word plus a "transformer" word (e.g. "attention broker," "loud
introvert") that you own and build stories around. He closes on **style** (silhouette,
color, signature, monogram, packaging), **making enemies** to galvanize fans, and a naming
game around his own "-do" identity. Final summary slide: *weak is strong, perfect is
boring, real is relatable, stop selling start serving, complexity is an enemy, adversity
is an ally.*

## Key claims

_All dated 2023-12-19 (keynote delivery). Paraphrased unless quoted in the next section._

**On the core thesis**
- Corporations are trying to be more human while humans try to be more like corporations
  — sanitizing their content, running it by a legal team, so as not to offend anyone and
  ending up saying nothing.
- The goal of personal branding is to "unmask your superpower," and it is
  counterintuitive — the opposite of what you think it is.
- Personal branding is NOT social-media hacks or gaming the algorithm; promising that was
  "a trick to get you here." It is meaningful, soulful self-work.
- Personal branding is not an act of creation but an act of **self-discovery** — your
  self-story, self-concept: who you are and what makes you you.
- It doesn't take much to stand out and be different; when everyone tries to blend in,
  simply going the opposite direction makes you stand out.

**On what makes people/brands magnetic**
- Aaron Draplin drew people because he was "refreshingly transparent," bringing the full
  weight of his personality and not hiding from it (opens his TEDx by sharing his middle
  name, age, hometown, music taste, political affiliation, his dog).
- The Ugly Company turned a bland dried-fruit product into a charismatic brand by (1)
  turning a weakness into a strength — the discarded/ugly becomes more valued than the
  discarded thing — and (2) trading on guilt over food waste, and (3) telling a story full
  of personality that customers then repeat for them.
- Universally admired figures (people name Mother Teresa, Gandhi, the Dalai Lama) almost
  always share three traits: **self-confident, self-aware, and vulnerable** — the same
  three traits as the Ugly Company.
- We must be willing to be vulnerable to build meaningful connection (attributed to Brené
  Brown). Staying vulnerable is a risk you must take to make a meaningful connection.

**Frameworks and tools**
- A personal brand has four components drawn from comic-book mythology: **origin story**
  (birth, parents, cultural capital), **community** (city, allies/sidekicks), **defining
  attributes** (catchphrases, quirks, idiosyncrasies), and **the enemy** (what you stand
  against — the part most people ignore).
- **Three Fs** (credited to Brendan Kane): humans connect to facts, feelings, and fun; a
  good story includes all three or you lose up to a third of your audience each.
- **Brand definition** (credited to Marty Neumeier): a brand is a person's gut feeling
  about a product, service, or organization — "it's not what you say it is, it's what they
  say it is." Products are made in factories; brands are built in the hearts and minds of
  people.
- Your brand is formed by every interaction; every person forms a separate opinion, so
  there are potentially "infinite versions of ourselves" (tied to a Marvel Multiverse
  metaphor — the aim is to unify the disjointed self into a cohesive whole).
- **New brand ROI / karmic equity**: doing good while expecting nothing back compounds
  exponentially; every time you ask for something (sell a course, ask for a job/portfolio
  review) the account resets to zero. Karma is the energy used to reset the balance.
- **Old vs. new rules of brand**: old = make something for everyone, tell OUR story,
  attract customers, build awareness; new = understand the CUSTOMER's story, make what
  THEY want, give them a story to tell, create brand affinity.
- **Revenue vs. value / infinite game** (references a Harvard Business Review article):
  performance marketing is good for short-term revenue but comes at the cost of long-term
  brand-building value; play the infinite game instead.
- **Persona vs. Shadow** (credited to Carl Jung, contrasted with Freud): the Persona is
  the front-facing self we use to get along; the Shadow is the private real self. Spend
  too long in the Persona and you forget who you really are ("you pretend to be somebody
  long enough and you forget who you are").
- **The two-word brand**: reduce how you describe yourself (not what you DO) to two words,
  because "complexity is the enemy of comprehension, retention and execution." A good
  brand story can be told by others when you're not in the room. Combine three words into
  two, or two into one hybrid; bonus for alliteration/rhyme; don't make it merely cute.
- **Shadow word + transformer word**: start from a shadow word (a hidden, uncomfortable
  truth) and add a "transformer" word that changes its meaning while keeping its power
  (e.g. "proud people pleaser," "attention broker," "gold digger"→"gold digger"). Warning:
  don't wash out the original painful meaning.
- **EO 5% rule** (attributed to Entrepreneurs' Organization): people share the middle 90%
  and hide the top 5% (big wins that trigger jealousy) and bottom 5% (abuse, divorce, hard
  times). Share your 5% high-highs and low-lows — that's what connects.
- **Naysayer exercise**: list what your worst critics, cynics, and trolls would say about
  you (excluding family) and reclaim those words (Jenna Kutcher = "gold digger"; a late-
  career strategist = "grandmother"; Rick Morris = "noble assassin"; Ash Thorp = "art
  prostitute").
- **Conflict/tragedy in story** (references Robert McKee's *Story*, and Stan Lee's
  Spider-Man origin): "if there's no conflict there's no story." Adversity is your ally —
  tap into trauma to become relatable. The hero overcomes and grows stronger from trauma;
  the villain stays victimized and seeks revenge.
- **Superpower → name → problems** (credited to Stan Lee on creating Spider-Man): the
  hardest part is figuring out the superpower, then you name it, then invent personal
  problems. Peter Parker became Spider-Man not when bitten but when he chose NOT to act and
  it cost his uncle's life.
- **The mentor**: name your mentor and we make assumptions about your values; add the
  conflict — how you initially resisted and what you learned. (Notes Tony Robbins' mentor
  was Jim Rohn; Gary Vaynerchuk now cites his mother more than his father as mentor.)
- **The enemy / make enemies** (credited to Freddy O of an agency, "make enemies and gain
  fans" — order matters): true fans need true critics; pick a fight, stand against
  something. "Your complaint can become your calling." Name the enemy to own it. Examples:
  Dr. Squatch vs. "big soap," Dove vs. beauty standards, Oatly, Patagonia ("we're in
  business to save our home planet"), Apple's "I'm a Mac / I'm a PC." Ask "we're in
  business to end WHAT?"
- **Naming to own** ("to name something is for you to own it" — cf. Moore's Law, Murphy's
  Law): build your identity outward from your name until a snowflake becomes an avalanche.
- **Style**: be intentional about packaging because "you are the package." Style
  (credited to John Prine/"Prime") is "if you keep making the same mistake long enough it
  becomes your style." Silhouette first (from character animation), then color (red-sock
  demo — one red element makes someone stand out), then signature/monogram. Turn your
  perceived flaws into deliberate style ("what were you blessed with").

**Complaint rule**
- You're allowed to complain about something once; complain twice and you're a whiner —
  otherwise do something about it and invent the solution.
