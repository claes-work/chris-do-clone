★ L3-candidate — This is a distinct, fully-articulated selling philosophy ("Natural Born Seller" / serve-don't-sell) that belongs in `wiki/topics/sales-clients/`. It contains Chris Do's signature SALES acronym (Serve, Ask, Listen, Empathize, Summarize), the current-state/desired-future-state/obstacle framework, and multiple named techniques worth their own topic pages.

## Summary

A recorded keynote by **Chris Do** titled "Natural Born Seller: To tap into your natural gift to influence and persuade people," delivered at the RGD Design Thinkers conference in Toronto and re-published to the Futur's YouTube channel as "Selling Without Being Salesy: The Kind Way to Sell."

Chris addresses an audience of mostly self-identified introverted designers and argues that selling does not require the loud, manipulative "Wolf of Wall Street" archetype. He reframes sales as **change management** — helping someone who wants something get from their current state to a desired future state by identifying what stands in the way. He walks through foundational premises (people already know they must spend money to solve a problem; spend is proportionate to problem size relative to the buyer; an inbound prospect has already qualified you; clients always have at least three options — hire you, hire someone else, do nothing), then presents his central reframe of the acronym **SALES = Serve, Ask, Listen, Empathize, Summarize**.

He grounds the philosophy in his own origin story: a 2002 turning point when his (now late) business coach **Kier McLaren** told him his sub-major-league sales approach was capping him at a ~1-in-5 win rate above $200K, and three coaching sessions helped him roughly double revenue ($2.2M → $3.9M) and lift his close rate from 20% to 75%. He then tells five stories (a fishing-lodge cold conversation with "Ernie" at Oyes/OES; a Clubhouse roleplay "selling" to Blair Enns of Win Without Pitching; a fitness coach who sold Chris via scaled "why isn't the score lower" questions; a client "Carrie" who won by raising the price objection first and throwing shade on cheaper competitors; and the "most perfect sales pitch" built entirely on naming the buyer's top pain points). He closes with question-based closing mechanics (full-value listening, playing back exact language, the hypothetical "if-then" close waiting for an emphatic yes), book recommendations, and a Q&A where he says his current strategy is simply to make free content so good it sells for him.

Speakers: **Chris Do** (keynote presenter — persona). A **conference host / Q&A moderator** appears only in the final Q&A (asking Chris questions) — context, not persona. All substantive teaching is Chris.

## Key claims

All dated 2023-12-23 (publication). All paraphrased unless in the verbatim section. All attributed to **Chris Do** unless noted.

- **Selling is not reserved for loud extroverts.** Most designers/creatives are introverts, and the gregarious "gift of gab" archetype is not a prerequisite for selling. — Chris Do
- **Reject the manipulative sales archetype; adopt a permission-based alternative.** Instead of the aggressive "Wolf of Wall Street / used-car salesman" model, sell in a way that is permission-based, creates value for others, is led by curiosity, and given in the spirit of generosity. — Chris Do
- **Sales is change management.** Someone wants something and can't get it; selling is helping them move from a current state to a desired future state by identifying the obstacle in the way. Two core questions: "What do they want?" and "What's getting in their way?" — Chris Do
- **People already know they must spend money to make a problem go away**, so you are not forcing anything gross on them. — Chris Do
- **Spend is proportionate to the size of the problem relative to the buyer** (not relative to you). Small problems = small budgets; big problems = big budgets — so hunt for big problems to solve. — Chris Do
- **An inbound prospect has already qualified you.** If they email, call, or DM you, they've seen enough; you don't need to sell them. You begin the conversation with goodwill, and everything after either builds or erodes trust. — Chris Do
- **Clients always have at least three options:** hire you, hire someone else, or do nothing. Realizing this removes the "you must hire me" pressure that produces nervous, poor-listening energy on a call. — Chris Do
- **Your job on the call is to help them get clarity and make the best decision for themselves**, even if that means telling them they don't need to do anything, or that it's not a good fit. — Chris Do
- **Shift from self-centered to client-centered.** Stop talking about your process, equipment, and how beautiful your work is; adopt a mindset of serving others (putting their needs ahead of yours). — Chris Do
- **Shift from giving advice to asking open-ended questions.** "Whoever asks more questions wins / is in control of the relationship." — Chris Do
- **Talk less, listen more; empathize** with the client's past bad experiences and their broader set of problems — this builds meaningful connection. — Chris Do
- **The SALES reframe: Serve, Ask, Listen, Empathize, Summarize.** Follow this framework and you never have to sell in a way that feels salesy. — Chris Do
- **We are all "natural born sellers"** — citing Zoe Chance's book *Influence Is Your Superpower*, we influenced people pre-language as babies; we must tap back into that natural ability and model a non-icky version of selling. — Chris Do (attributing concept to Zoe Chance)
- **Self-disclosure:** Chris is not a trained sales expert and used to hate selling; he wants to dispel the design-school myth that "if your work is good enough you won't need to sell" — believing that myth means you never acquire the business-conversation skills to flourish. — Chris Do
- **Origin of his sales transformation (2002):** coach **Kier McLaren** told him that above ~$200K, clients can hire anyone in the world, so competition changes — "you're using a minor-league strategy to play in the Major League." "What got you here won't get you there." — Chris Do (attributing the lesson to Kier McLaren)
- **Results claim (self-reported):** three coaching sessions helped him nearly double gross revenue from ~$2.2M to ~$3.9M in one year and raise close rate from 20% to 75%. — Chris Do (self-reported)
- **Story 1 (fishing lodge / Ernie):** courage to simply have a conversation surfaces the pain ("our clients are literally dying, we can't attract young people"); then ask "does this warrant an additional conversation?" — Chris Do
- **Practice technique:** practice asking learning-oriented questions in low-stakes settings before a real client call, or old habits (pitching/selling) take over. People love attention — give it and you become the most interesting person in the room. — Chris Do
- **Story 2 (Blair Enns / Clubhouse):** recommends the book *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto*; demonstrates "manufacturing problems" via neutral, observational, open-ended questions (noticing a gap between what he loves and what the client is doing) rather than insulting the client's work. "Selling is conversational." "Sales sounds like a conversation between two friends." — Chris Do
- **Story 3 (fitness coach):** clients sell themselves. Ask what's important and its importance on a 1–10 scale, then ask "why isn't the score lower?" to make them justify it (people have a need to stay consistent with themselves — attributed to Tony Robbins). People buy on emotion, not facts. — Chris Do
- **Story 4 (Carrie / price objection):** raise the objection first — "I'm most likely going to be the highest bid; is your decision based on price?"; flip the conversation and take the power back; tell them to talk to others; educate them on warning signs of someone who doesn't know what they're doing ("throwing shade on your competition"); close with "call me only if you don't feel fully confident in your options." Result (self-reported by Carrie): she doubled her revenue. — Chris Do (relaying Carrie's account)
- **Story 5 (the "perfect pitch" / pain points):** the best pitch named Chris's top three pain points before proposing anything ("it felt like he was looking into my soul"). Technique: "Whenever I speak to clients just like you, they usually tell me their top three biggest problems are ___," then ask "is that right?" — listening for "that's right." Only do this with real data, never guess. — Chris Do
- **Pre-call prep questions:** What functional/financial/emotional pain does the client have? What negative outcomes do they fear? What objections might they raise (usually one of five: experience, vertical expertise, price, timeline, team size)? — Chris Do
- **Definition:** "Sales is not convincing, persuading, or manipulation. Selling is helping. It's about being curious. Selling is learning. Selling is an act of generosity." — Chris Do
- **Two skills matter most: asking and listening.** — Chris Do
- **Question framework** from Michael Bungay Stanier's *The Coaching Habit*: be slower to give advice, stay curious longer; "What's on your mind?" → "And what else?" (repeat) → "What's the real challenge for you?" You can literally read the script and it works. — Chris Do (attributing to Michael Bungay Stanier)
- **Full-value listening** (attributed to Kevin Dy): treat whatever the other person says as the most important thing you've ever heard; every next question should follow up on their last answer — that proves you're listening. Most podcasters fail this by marching through a fixed list of questions. — Chris Do (attributing to Kevin Dy)
- **Listening stack:** current state → desired future state → obstacle → how they measure success / what's valuable to them; then play back and summarize using their exact words ("fatigued" not "tired"); confirm with "did I miss anything?" — Chris Do
- **The hypothetical close:** "So if you saw a solution that did X, Y, Z in this time for this amount of money, then you'd be willing to move forward?" — wait for an emphatic yes (not a strained yes or pregnant pause), then commit to sending a proposal. Power comes from them committing to their own words. — Chris Do
- **Quote (Jeffrey Gitomer):** "Don't tell your customers something they don't know about you; tell them something they don't know about them." Great questions are an unfair, legal competitive advantage. — Chris Do (attributing to Jeffrey Gitomer)
- **Trust scorecard:** you lose a point every time you advise, talk, assume, are ambiguous, or too assertive in tone; you gain a point for big/beautiful/specific questions, intent listening + playback, curiosity focused on the client's needs, and a calm ("late-night FM DJ") voice. — Chris Do
- **Biggest personal mistake (Q&A):** before learning to sell, he thought his job was to tell clients what he thought they should have — immediately pitching ideas, cameras, lenses, sequences — which was making assumptions instead of being curious, and clients wouldn't call back. — Chris Do
- **On telling clients what to do (Q&A):** eventually yes, but make it "as painful as possible" first — make them do all the talking; a 30-minute call becomes a 90-minute exhausting call, but then you propose only ONE tailored solution instead of 17 guesses. — Chris Do
- **Current selling strategy (Q&A):** "I don't think I'm selling anymore." Make content so good and give it all away for free — no funnels, no email traps — and it sells for you. If you genuinely try to help whoever is in front of you, the selling has already begun (example: a no-intention-to-sell Miami workshop that generated inbound buying interest). — Chris Do
