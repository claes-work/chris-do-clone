# Branding for Non Creatives: Crash Course Full Keynote (2024)

## Summary

A full keynote by Chris Do delivered to a live audience of **accountants** (with a realtor and a graphic designer in the room) at a business conference organized by "Hector and Carlos" (event hosts; also references "Ron"). The talk is a crash-course primer on branding fundamentals aimed at "non-creatives" / "left-brainers." Chris walks the room through a connected chain: **brand → branding → positioning → premium pricing → marketing → the customer/value-prop framework → applied brand deconstruction.**

Core throughline: a brand is a *gut feeling* others hold about you (not what you claim); branding is *impression management*; positioning is the space you occupy relative to competitors, won through *reframing*; price *communicates* position; and marketing (via Seth Godin) is the generous act of helping people get what they want. He teaches by deconstructing real brand case studies (Listerine vs. Scope, Coke vs. Pepsi, 7-Up "the Uncola," Apple "I'm a Mac / I'm a PC," Harley-Davidson, and at length "The Ugly Company" upcycled-fruit packaging). A large interactive middle section pivots into **mindset/confidence** — his "who is *they*?" riff and the argument that people don't lack confidence, they lack *permission*, illustrated by his own pricing-escalation story ($250 → $500 → $750 → $1,500 → $5,000 an hour).

Attribution is clean: the body is Chris speaking solo in the first person. The very first ~13 lines are an MC/preview montage echoing Chris's own lines before "let's give an incredible welcome to Mr. Chris Do"; the final ~50 lines are Chris's to-camera post-keynote reflection.

## Key claims

_All dated 2024-01-10. Paraphrased unless quoted. Biographical items marked `[self-reported]`._

**Brand & branding**
- A brand is a person's *gut feeling* about a product, service, or organization — it is not what you say it is, it's what they say it is (paraphrase; Chris credits **Marty Neumeier**, "the guy who wrote the book on brand").
- Products are made in factories; brands are made in the hearts and minds of the people you serve, at every touchpoint of contact (paraphrase, crediting Neumeier).
- A logo is not the brand — it's a "container for meaning." A logo has no inherent meaning; a beautiful logo on a company whose products people don't love is empty. The brand runs deeper: voice, behavior, beliefs, values, and customer experience.
- Branding is **impression management** — how you influence people's perceptions of you (Chris credits his friend **Dr. Christine Loo-cer / Christine** for this framing). You imprint on everyone from your children to your boss; manage that impression via dress, speech, bearing, content, consistency, and energy.
- You can *influence* feelings but not *control* them — you can show up, present, be consistent, and behave a certain way, but the other person decides.
- Quotes he leans on: Jeff Bezos — "brand is what people say about you when you're not in the room"; and "if enough people have a similar gut feeling about you, then you have a brand" (Neumeier).
- Warning: managing impression can drift into becoming a *Persona* that moves away from the real person; audiences are "very good at sniffing out inauthentic brands." Aim to be *true, not inauthentic*.

**Positioning & reframing**
- Positioning = the space you occupy in the hearts and minds of your customers **relative to your competitors** (paraphrase; credits **Al Ries & Jack Trout**, *Positioning*). The competitor relationship is the essential part.
- Positioning is the "art of reframing" — change the frame, change the meaning and perceived value (his metaphor: spilled paint on canvas vs. the same canvas framed with a red velvet rope in a gallery).
- To position, "pick a fight": identify competitors, then declare how you're different — often via near-"guerrilla warfare" that reframes a competitor's *strength* into a *weakness*.
- Practical repositioning recipe: (1) identify who the customer is *really* choosing between (don't be too aspirational — it must resonate), (2) name their top three traits (be observant, or "just write it into ChatGPT"), (3) position yourself as the *opposite* / anti-them, forcing a choice.
- Case studies used to teach reframing: **Scope vs. Listerine** ("the taste people hate twice a day" → Scope's "no more medicine breath," reportedly taking ~33% of the market from zero); **Coke vs. Pepsi** (Coke "leaned into who they are" — "it's the real thing" — while Pepsi "chased trends"/celebrities as "the choice of a new generation," now #3 behind Coke and Diet Coke); **7-Up "the Uncola"**; **Apple "I'm a Mac / I'm a PC"** repositioning ~97%-share Microsoft/PC as old and uncool ("you know the advertising is working when the CEO is upset").
- Applied to the room: to compete with a big accounting firm like PwC ("a big faceless organization"), position as the small accountant who cares — steal the Mac/PC frame.

**Pricing / premium as positioning**
- The purpose of positioning is to *reduce or eliminate competition*. "When you're a company of one, you don't compete on price anymore, you command a premium."
- Counter-intuitive: in professional services, don't make it *easy* to work with you — make it *harder*. Create scarcity and signal "I'm worth it; there's a premium."
- **Price communicates position.** Be bold enough to say you're worth a premium; if you're not yet, "close the gap" by upskilling / improving service.
- The "Friday 6 PM phone-call" test (credited to **"Blen"** — a mentor figure): charge enough that when a client calls on your personal time, you're *happy* to pick up. Charge so much you stop counting hours, don't want timesheets, and care only about the outcome.
- Charging more attracts a different, higher-belief buyer: when the fee is high, the buyer "just jumps" because it cost them so much to reach you; price is the barrier that filters out non-believers.

**Marketing (via Seth Godin)**
- Advertising is only *one tactic* of marketing — "putting out a message on repeat, invading your space." It used to work; "it doesn't work anymore."
- Marketing is "the generous act of helping people get what they want" — not what *you* want — with no expectations (credits **Seth Godin**). "Do something that matters for people who care" — find a passionate niche (hot sauce, coffee — not ketchup).
- Sell to people who *want* to hear from you rather than interrupting people who don't; "people are looking for you right now."
- **Permission marketing** = people volunteer to participate in a long-term marketing campaign of increasing value; build a "marketing curriculum" that gives broad value up front and deeper, higher-value material as the relationship progresses. Three tests: is it *anticipated*, *personal*, and *relevant*?
- Customer-journey model **AIDCA** (a variant of AIDA): Awareness → Interest → Desire → Conversion → **Advocacy**. Most people stop at conversion; getting a client is hard, keeping one is easier — deliver *delight* so customers tell others (word-of-mouth referral).
- "The purpose of a business is to create and keep a customer" (credits **Peter Drucker**).

**The value-prop / brand-build framework**
- Always start with the customer; sometimes you must *create* a new customer. Focus less on demographics (age, income, title, location) and more on **psychographics** (beliefs, values, opinions) — those shape behavior more.
- You can generate a serviceable customer/buyer persona with ChatGPT (give it what you make + city + a few constraints; "don't let it go wild"); it already knows demographics + psychographics.
- Framework: understand the customer → their current state vs. desired future state (with a "wall"/blocker between) → your **value proposition** is the bridge/solution → then add **tone of voice** and **look & feel**.
- Sell **benefits (emotional), not features**: "access to someone 24 hours a day" is a feature; "sleep better at night," "a clear vision for the future," "get your spouse off your back" are benefits.
- Building brand language: brainstorm many words without judgment, then *cross off the first ~10 generic words* (e.g., "innovative") and push past them; finally pick the two words that most resonate for tone of voice and two for look & feel. Build a **mood board** (Pinterest/Behance/Instagram) by curating images to those words.
- **Harley-Davidson** worked example: value prop is emotional — self-expression on two wheels, adventure, freedom, independence, outlaw culture ("stick it to the Man") — *not* horsepower or 0-60. Tone of voice: rebellious, passionate. Look & feel: distressed, counterculture. Includes the agency "Wolves" #RollYourOwn campaign ("Raise Hell before lunch," "morning donut," "lap dance," "the black market — $99, any less and you could be arrested") as reframing / secret-language examples.

**Language, cults, and IP**
- Brands build cult-like tribes with a *secret language*. He cites (with caveats) the Netflix series *How to Become a Cult Leader* and the Heaven's Gate cult redefining words (body → "vessel") so members signal they're "in."
- Two benefits of proprietary language: you make people use *your* words, and you can trademark/patent it, creating IP (e.g., Adidas "adiprene," Nike fabric/tech names). Advice: name your own framework/system so it becomes a brand asset.

**The Ugly Company (extended applied case study)**
- Upcycled-fruit snack brand ("The Ugly Company") that turns a *weakness into a strength*: names itself "ugly," embraces imperfect produce, and sells a *feeling* (doing good, anti-waste, "beautiful on the inside") rather than the (visually unremarkable) dried fruit itself.
- Uses copy reframing a common expression ("don't judge a fruit by its cover"), self-deprecating humor ("pit happens"), and hard facts ("more peaches are thrown away in California than the state of Georgia produces annually").
- **The three F's** (credited to **Brandon Kane**, author of *Hook Point*, who "grew a million followers in one month"): people process via **facts, feelings, and fun** — include all three or you lose up to two-thirds of the audience.
- **Upcycled vs. recycled**: recycled items enter the trash and are chemically broken down/renewed; upcycled items *never enter the trash* — a new life is found for them. Chris calls upcycling "a powerful concept."
- "People don't fall in love with corporations; they fall in love with personalities" (credits brander **friend "Yo Santos"**). Humanize the brand; put a face behind it.

**Mindset / confidence (interactive middle section)**
- People who say they need "confidence" usually need something specific: experience, or **validation** — which you only get by *doing it* (a chicken-and-egg risk).
- His thesis: what people actually seek is **permission** — "we're all just looking for permission from somebody." Give yourself the permission slip.
- "**Who is *they*?**" riff: whenever people say "it can't be done" / "we were told this," no one can ever name the "they." "You see that person every single day… it's you in the mirror." He jokes he'll "make a shirt: WHO THEY."
- "Creative folks… live in the world of their imagination, and then the real world aligns to that." Everyone (including marketers) is creative.
- "Decide and you will be" — stop letting someone else tell you what you are; "decide it and be it," "fake it till you make it."

**Applied takeaway / the "hack"**
- You don't need to be a designer: go to an upscale/curated grocery store (Whole Foods, Erewhon), find a package/product that *speaks to you*, buy it, and **deconstruct** it (colors, typography, texture, language, tone of voice), then use it as a template/foundation for your own brand.
- Typefaces carry personality; "playing to type" vs. "playing against type" (e.g., a pretty cursive script on the word "ugly" is playing against type) — "this is why you need to hire designers… there's a secret language we all understand."
- Closing summary sequence: (1) get clear on who you serve; build a strong buyer persona; (2) align your value prop to their desired future state; (3) speak in a resonant tone of voice; (4) match the look and feel; (5) the hardest part — **behave consistently across the entire customer journey** so customers become advocates.
- Proof of brand power: "The Ugly Company paid me exactly \$0 to tell you their story… and here I am on stages all over the world telling people their story."
