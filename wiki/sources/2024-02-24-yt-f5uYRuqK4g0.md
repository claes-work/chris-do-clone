## Summary

Long-form autobiographical interview: Chris Do as the guest on the **Travis Makes Friends Podcast** (host: Travis; co-host Rich/Richard is referenced), republished on The Futur's YouTube channel under the title "Who Is Chris Do & The Futur? Origin story." Recorded in Las Vegas.

**Attribution:** Nearly the entire episode is Chris Do speaking in the first person about his own life; Travis asks the questions and offers commentary (context, not subject). All biographical/opinion content below is Chris-attributed unless marked otherwise. Most claims are `[self-reported]` life history that cannot be registry-verified from this source alone.

The arc: born in Vietnam and fled the fall of Saigon at age 3; raised in San Jose as a first-generation immigrant in a striving middle-class family; the artistic-vs-engineering split in his extended family that he sees himself sitting between; a bored, high-GPA high-school career (including a report-card forgery anecdote); rejection from three universities' art programs; community college and a mentor who pointed him to Art Center College of Design; a fast early career in advertising (Cole & Weber, Seattle) that he walked away from; the founding of his studio **Blind** (Dec 1995) on a $5,000 check from his uncle's business partner; ~25 years running Blind (grossed $80M+); freelancing for main-title-design legends (Kyle Cooper, Garson Yu / Imaginary Forces) which shaped his eye and his pricing nerve; the formation of his core pricing philosophy through repeatedly raising rates and never getting a "no"; a long parable about a particle-effects specialist ("Rift Dagger") told from the buyer's side; the wind-down of Blind (~2019) to go all-in on education; and the founding of **The Futur** after an earlier education partnership ("The School" with Jose Caraballo) split. Also: extended riffs on the broken economics of higher education and how he is advising his own two sons.

## Key claims

_All dated 2024-02-24 (interview publication). Paraphrased unless quoted below. `[self-reported]` = Chris's own account of his life; not independently verified in this source._

**Origin & childhood**
- Chris was born in Vietnam and **fled Saigon at age 3** during the fall of Saigon / escape from communism (~1975). He has no memory of his home country. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- The family was resettled via a **church-based (Catholic) sponsorship program**, landing first in **Kansas City**; their sponsor was an older widowed white woman (referred to affectionately as "Aunt Barney"). Other relatives were sponsored to Arkansas and San Jose. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- The extended family eventually regrouped in **San Jose, Northern California** (more of his father's siblings were there); Chris says the San Jose choice was largely logistical/"random," not a grand plan. `[self-reported]`
- Grew up in a **suburban, middle-class** San Jose neighborhood; family moved every few years to progressively better neighborhoods chasing the "American dream." As a first-generation immigrant kid he always **identified the family as poor**, and was surprised (via school paperwork) to learn their income didn't match that self-image. `[self-reported]`
- Later moved to **Los Angeles for school and has lived in LA ever since**; at time of recording he had recently moved to **Pasadena** (in the new house ~1 year). `[self-reported]`

**Family structure (no family names recorded per repo policy)**
- **Middle child of three brothers.** Older brother is 4 years older; younger brother is 1 year younger. `[self-reported]`
- Older brother: highly logical, **master's degree from Stanford**, computer-science / software engineer in Silicon Valley. Younger brother: free-form artist who **never went to college**. Chris positions himself literally in the middle — a **Bachelor's degree in art and design**. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- His **father is an engineer**; his father's siblings are technical, stable, in Silicon Valley. His **mother's side of the family are artists** (photographers, painters) — emotional, creative, financially unstable, many divorces. Chris frames his own identity as the fusion of these two poles. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- His **mother is a self-taught artist** (oils and pastels) who worked at **IBM as a designer/drafter on chip / chipboard design**, drawing by hand at high magnification. Chris says his own path did **not** come directly from her, but from an early unexplained "obsession with emblems and logos." `[self-reported]`

**School, rebellion, forgery**
- First-generation-immigrant household with **extreme academic expectations** — straight A's expected, C's unacceptable ("if I got C's I'm packing my bags"). Education was seen as the only clear path up after losing country and culture. `[self-reported]`
- Got A's easily but was **bored by school**; gravitated to electives (woods/metal shop). Graduated high school with a **3.8 GPA**. `[self-reported]`
- Told a **report-card forgery story**: once received a C (science), intercepted the mailed carbon-copy report card, and used his mother's professional drafting eraser and a dulled pencil to redraw the grade up to a B (deciding an A would look too suspicious), then smudged carbon over it — "that's how I learned how to do forgery pre-Photoshop." Frames it as a real (not fabricated) story; parents supposedly never found out. `[self-reported]` (mischief anecdote — strong voice/character detail)

**Rejection → Art Center**
- Says he only chose art as a major because he thought it would be **easier to get into the program**; applied to **UCLA, UC San Diego, and Cal Poly San Luis Obispo** and was **rejected from all three** art programs. UCLA's letter said he "demonstrates potential but you're not ready" — which he now agrees was true. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- Went to **community college**, moving to San Diego to live with his older brother (who was prepping for grad school). At **San Diego City College**, an instructor named **Candace Lopez** strongly encouraged him after a journaling exercise in which he wrote that he wanted to build a portfolio to get into Art Center. `[self-reported]`
- Was pointed to **Art Center College of Design (Los Angeles)** by his boss at a screen-printing shop, **Brad Shabuya** ("there's all these cocky bastards just like you, you'll fit right in"). Art Center's tuition was **the same as Stanford's**, to his father's dismay. Chris spent two semesters building a portfolio and got in; calls it one of the best schools in the country and credits the whole trajectory to that single recommendation. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`

**Early advertising career (Seattle)**
- **December 1994:** a copywriter friend, **Colleen Matthews**, got a job at **Cole & Weber** (ad agency, Seattle) and recommended Chris as her art-director partner. He sent a deliberately minimal **4-piece portfolio in a FedEx box**, was hired (by "Dolly") at **$40,000/year**, and did not try to negotiate. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- **January 1995:** started at Cole & Weber; his boss **Kevin Jones** — whom he repeatedly calls "the world's best boss" — flew him **back and forth to LA** so he could finish his last Art Center semester, and offered to raise him to **$85,000/year** (a 21-year-old with an expense account, pitching big accounts). `[self-reported]`
- Worked on the winning pitch for **Silicon Graphics (SGI)** ("supercomputers that render frames for James Cameron's *The Abyss*"). Felt slighted when the agency celebrated the win without him and when a new, disrespectful boss reassigned him to mere production/layout work under another art-director team. `[self-reported]`
- **Walked away from the agency:** told the disrespectful boss "I respectfully decline" and left his office — an act of insubordination. Kevin Jones, instead of firing him, offered him a different account; Chris chose to leave, finish school, and concluded he was **"unemployable"** because he can't work under people he doesn't respect. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`

**Between jobs → founding Blind**
- After graduating Art Center (having skipped portfolio orientation / on-campus interviews because he intended to stay in advertising), he briefly worked at **Epitaph Records** (LA punk label) during the mid-90s punk resurgence (The Offspring era; met **Brett Gurewitz / "Mr. Brett" of Bad Religion**, who said the company grew 1,000%). Left after ~1.5 months when his boss was fired. `[self-reported]`
- Turned down a **$55,000/year** job from **Ian Dawson**, executive producer at **Novocom** — "if there's not an eight in front of that number I'm not even interested." `[self-reported]`
- **The founding call:** his **uncle** (name withheld per repo policy) phoned, knowing Chris had always wanted to start a business; the uncle and his business partner (**Bob**), who ran **boutique hotels**, wanted to start a design company and offered to finance it. Over dinner at the **Westin Bonaventure** hotel, Bob wrote a **$5,000 check on the spot** as a "good faith deposit" against a requested ~$100,000 — without even reading Chris's 14-page business plan (which had "phantom projections"). `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- Founded a studio called **Blind** (Dec 1995). The financing partners held **51%**. When their overseas boutique-hotel developments hit government/financial trouble, they defaulted; Chris **kept the $5,000 and regained full ownership**, then relaunched under essentially the same name (two words). `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- **Blind ran from December 1995 to ~2018/2019 (~25 years)** and **grossed over $80 million**. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`

**Mentors, main-title design & the pricing philosophy**
- Freelanced for **Kyle Cooper** and **Garson (Yu)** — first at **R/GA**, later **Imaginary Forces**. He credits Kyle Cooper as "the preeminent main title designer in the world," credited with the **renaissance of main-title design**. Both mentors had studied at **Yale under Paul Rand**, with lectures from **Armin Hofmann** — a design lineage Chris found more knowledgeable about type/design history than anyone he'd met. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- Says he'll gladly follow authority he **respects for its competence**, but not authority "for the sake of being authority" — a through-line he ties to both his school disillusionment and his advertising exit. `[self-reported]`
- **"Ghost-riding" story:** his school friends (Michelle, Jesse, Vanessa) did much of the actual design work while Chris presented to clients; he steadily raised his day-rate to Imaginary Forces (500 → 600 → 700 → 800/day), told the producer ("Saffron") to "check with Kyle" if she objected, and they always kept paying. He eventually revealed to Kyle Cooper that a team of three was doing the work; Kyle had never wondered how one person produced so much in varied styles. `[self-reported]`
- **~2 years into Blind (~1997):** Kyle Cooper offered to **buy the company** ("big part of a small thing or a small part of a big thing"). Chris asked **$1 million**; Kyle balked and later said he should have bought it because it was cheap. From this Chris derived his signature line: **today's price is always the cheapest — tomorrow's bill will be higher.** `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- Early on, low overhead was his source of pricing power: lived in a **$1,200/month loft**, minimal expenses, so he could freely say **no** to bad-fit work — "that gives me a lot of power to say no." `[self-reported]`
- **Pricing thesis:** raising prices and standing firm kept working, so he never had reason to charge less; charging less removes friction but starts a "vicious cycle" (can't afford to hire, over-worked, under-paid, self-belief erodes). He names the buyer-side failure mode **projection** — projecting your own poor relationship with money onto the client. `[self-reported]`
- **"Rift Dagger" parable (buyer's side):** on a six-figure job for a national baked-goods company, a team of 7–8 people on rented supercomputers/render farms couldn't deliver a particle-simulation test. A specialist named **"Rift Dagger"** ran a successful test for free within hours — remotely, from his phone at an auto shop, using his own optimized code — then charged **$1,800/day** (vs the team's $4,500/day for nothing). Chris fired the whole team, hired him, and uses it as proof that great talent should charge confidently and that buyers are happy to pay for solved problems. `[self-reported]`

**The Futur & the shift to education**
- Chris had **taught at private art schools for ~15 years** before ever making a YouTube video — his whiteboard/teaching fluency comes from those reps. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- First education venture: **"The School,"** a partnership with **Jose Caraballo** (name garbled in transcript as "Jose caber" — `attribution: uncertain` on spelling), an Art Center connection who already ran an education company and got Chris into **YouTube, information products, and a one-to-many model**. They ran it ~2.5 years, then split; Chris then started **The Futur**. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`
- **The Futur** is framed not as a design school but as an education platform that **finds and fills gaps in the market** — began with design courses (typography, UX), then pivoted to business skills (negotiation, sales, pricing), which is what it's now best known for. `[self-reported]`
- Ran Blind and the education company **in parallel** for a period before winding Blind down (~2019). The wind-down decision was driven by the team asking "does this still excite you?" — it no longer did. `[self-reported]`
- After announcing they'd take no more client work, they were **tested by Xbox** (a favorite client, both Chris and his creative director "Matthew" are gamers): a **$400,000** commercial offer — turned down; a bigger follow-up offer — turned down even more easily; then an offer to **buy the company** that ultimately didn't close. Frames this as "words are cheap until you have to sacrifice something." `[self-reported]`
- At recording, Chris states he is **51 years old**, that Blind grossed **$80M+**, and that **The Futur is currently a "$4–5 million company"** on its way toward similar scale. `[self-reported]` `biography-relevant`

**On his own children (context — no names given)**
- Has **two sons**: the **oldest is in college at Columbia** (a "brand-name guy" who told Chris "I need structure, I want the system"); the **youngest (17)** wants to go to art school, and Chris is steering him toward a **gap year** to live/travel first ("live some life before you go there... you have nothing to reflect"). `[self-reported]`
- The oldest son has **run his own business since age ~15.5**, files taxes, and "made more money before he turned 18 than [Chris] did by the time [he] was 22." `[self-reported]`

**On higher education (opinion, dated 2024-02-24)**
- Believes "1,000%" in the **power of education** but distrusts the **institutions** built around it. Argues student debt is uniquely dangerous (government-backed loans for people with no credit; non-dischargeable in bankruptcy; loans later sold to private banks) and that administrations can behave like "front of house for banks." `[self-reported opinion]`
- Explains institutional inertia via **board dynamics** (he has served on boards): boards are legally/financially liable for risky decisions, so they **default to killing new ideas** — "if you do nothing new [and] it dies, that ain't your fault." `[self-reported opinion]`
- Thinks the **degree is a poor proxy** for knowledge/skill/character and that demonstrable-work fields (design, video, podcasting) are already moving past the paper credential; expects real change to require capital-heavy outsiders ("Bezos or Musk") rather than governments. `[self-reported opinion]`

**Reputation / relationships**
- His **first clients after founding Blind were all former employers/colleagues** (Kevin Jones, Novocom, Imaginary Forces) — his stated first lesson: do your best work regardless of pay and leave gracefully, because reputation brings the work back. `[self-reported]`
- Closing answer to "who you know vs. what you know": **"clearly who you know"** — knowledge is easy to acquire; the right people are the hard part, and he believes The Futur would be a "$100 million company if we knew the right people." `[self-reported]`

## Notable verbatim quotes

_All spoken by Chris Do unless marked. Transcribed from auto-captions; minor garbles cleaned only where obvious._

**On being a refugee child:**
> "I don't have any memory of my home country... I was three years old when we fled Saigon — fall of Saigon, communism, escape, land in Kansas City."

> "I always identified as us as being poor and was shocked one day to find out — because we had to fill out some paperwork for school — how much our parents made. I'm like, 'Mom, Dad, we're not poor, not based on these numbers. Why do we still live this way?'"

**On the family's academic pressure:**
> "If I got C's, I'm packing my bags and leaving home. Enough B's and we're having problems. The expectation is you must get straight A's, and that's it."

**On the report-card forgery:**
> "That's how I learned how to do forgery, pre-Photoshop."

> "A is too much, I'm going to give myself a B... and then you take the carbon, you rub it so you smudge a little bit, you give a little war damage, and then you put it right back in there."

**On sitting between two family worlds:**
> "My dad's an engineer... on my mom's side of the family they're all super emotional, beautiful, artistic people — photographer, painter, artists, all broke, all trying to figure out life, many divorces. And then I am, quite literally, the middle of my two parents."

**On the obsession that became a career:**
> "I didn't know it at the time, but I had an unhealthy obsession with emblems, logos. I didn't even know that was a thing."

> "I skateboarded, and the companies I found the greatest connection with had the best graphics."

**On the college rejections:**
> "[UCLA said] you demonstrate potential but you're not ready. I'm like, 'I'll show you one day.'... It was true. I wasn't ready."

> "I went to community college, and that's, like, for Asian kids, like purgatory. You're not alive, you're not dead, you're just somewhere in between."

**On how he found Art Center:**
> "He could have told me 'go to RISD, go to School of Visual Arts,' and that's where I would have gone. I know nothing about Art Center except that it's in LA and one person had told me that's where you need to go."

**On leaving the ad agency ("I respectfully decline"):**
> "I looked him right in the eye, in his office in the corner office, and said, 'Well— I respectfully decline,' and I walked out of his office."

> "I am not appreciated, and I would never feel like I belong. So I'm going to go back to school and finish."

**On being unemployable:**
> "Somebody told me, 'Chris, you're unemployable.' And it's true — I'm unemployable. I cannot work for people... because I'm too stubborn. I have ideas and I don't like the way people do things."

**On respecting competence over authority:**
> "If you're just authority for the sake of being authority, then we're probably not going to get along very well. But... then I'm totally fine to be under you, to report to you, to take orders from you."

> "I will go into shark-infested waters with you, because you know what you're doing. I'm going to learn a lot."

**On the $5,000 founding check:**
> "This is a good faith gesture. I'm interested in doing the business with you. It looks like you're asking for 100 grand — consider that an early deposit."

> "Five grand feels like 100 grand when you're broke and you have a massive amount of credit card debt."

**On giving away 51%:**
> "I'm kicking myself in the butt thinking I only got 5,000 bucks and I gave away 51% of my company — world's dumbest business investment... Luckily they defaulted. I kept the 5,000 bucks. I was once again a free man."

**On the pricing philosophy (the core line):**
> "The price I give you today will always be the cheapest price. Wait till you see tomorrow's bill. It'll always be more than today."

> "The narrative is this: I try to ask for more, I stood my ground, it keeps working. So why would I ever in my life now ask for less?"

**On charging what you're worth:**
> "Because it is. Because I'm a bastard." _(on why his day-rate kept rising 500 → 600 → 700 → 800)_

> "As long as I can pay off my student loans, the minimum payment, pay rent, I'm good. So that gives me a lot of power to say no."

**On projection (the buyer's psychology):**
> "There's a word for it. It's called projection. You take your life values, your sense of self-worth, and you project it onto other people — when we know that no two people think the same thing."

**On the "Rift Dagger" specialist:**
> "I'm not telling you just as the seller of goods — I'm also telling you, take it from me as the buyer... he was that good, and he knew he was that good, which is why he did the work for free first."

> "The candle that burns twice as bright burns twice as fast." _(quoting Blade Runner, on the specialist's burnout)_

**On reputation as the first source of clients:**
> "Wherever you go, do the best job you can regardless of how much you're being paid — because what's more important is the reputation you leave behind... and when you leave gracefully, they will give you work."

**On teaching before YouTube:**
> "I taught at the private art schools for 15 years before I even made a freaking video on YouTube... If you're working with eight students in a classroom for five hours, you either get good or you go home."

**On winding down Blind for The Futur:**
> "Let's leave the golden goose and let's jump on the pirate ship and see what happens."

> "It was a difficult decision for about 24 hours, and then it wasn't really."

**On turning down Xbox:**
> "Words are cheap... until you have to actually lose something and sacrifice something. It's just words. It's easy [to say] we're not going to take on client work — but when one of our favorite clients calls..."

**On advising his son (art school / living first):**
> "Just do something with your life first before you go to an art school, because you've lived nothing. You have nothing to reflect into the world — and that's what you're going to do, you're going to regurgitate all that garbage that people are doing online right now."

**On luck and self-belief:**
> "I think in life, when you're set off in the right direction, everything becomes much easier. So what I'm about to tell you came from just being very blessed and fortunate to be exactly where I'm supposed to be."

> "I just happened to be pointed the right direction, with the right amount of cockiness and self-belief and the right opportunities."

**On who-you-know vs. what-you-know (closing):**
> "It's clearly who you know... It's easy to learn; it's not so easy to find the right people to help fill in the gaps. I think we'd be a hundred-million-dollar company if we knew the right people — but we're not, we're like a four or five million company right now."
