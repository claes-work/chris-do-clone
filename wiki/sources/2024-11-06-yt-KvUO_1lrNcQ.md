# From $13 to $100K Brand Deals on Instagram w/ @Adrian Per

## Summary

Interview episode on The Futur. **Chris Do hosts**; the guest is **Adrian Per**
(a.k.a. "OMG Adrian"), a Filipino-American filmmaker and Instagram content creator
whom Chris first met in the green room at Adobe MAX (introduced by "Drego"/"Drigo"
and "Mo"). The conversation traces Adrian's creative arc — from breaking apart Dr.
Dre beats as a 10-year-old in the Bay Area, to a commercial/music-video directing
career, to hitting $13 in his bank account at age 32 (October 2022), to pivoting into
Instagram content and short-form brand-deal work. Chris's role is interviewer: he asks
questions, reframes Adrian's story into broader lessons for creatives, adds his own
producer's-eye commentary on the economics of branded content, forgiving immigrant
parents, and the craft of efficient filmmaking.

> only** — they are NOT Chris Do persona data and must not enter Chris's biography.
> The persona-relevant material is limited to Chris's questions, framings, opinions,
> and his own disclosed anecdotes (e.g. his attorney, his own filming struggles).

## Key claims

### Chris Do (persona-relevant)

- **[2024-11-06] (paraphrase)** Chris argues that admired creators with large
  followings did NOT have a divinely ordained, clear path — success is far messier than
  outsiders assume; as recently as ~18 months ago Adrian still doubted whether he was
  meant to do this. Chris uses the guest's story to debunk the "it was always destined"
  myth.
- **[2024-11-06] (paraphrase)** On taxes for creatives/freelancers: when you get a check
  for, say, $10,000 you are NOT $10,000 richer — at minimum ~35% is owed in taxes, and
  nobody tells new earners this. Chris presents it as a common, non-crazy trap.
- **[2024-11-06] (paraphrase)** All parents, well-intentioned or not, are imperfect; we
  should remove "perfection" as the bar they're measured against. Parents run an
  "operating system" that's outdated relative to the current world — and you'll be
  outdated too when you have kids. Acknowledging they did their best with limited
  resources lets you redirect energy away from resentment. (Chris notes he is a parent.)
- **[2024-11-06] (paraphrase)** Most parents fundamentally want their kids not to suffer
  and to have a better life; when they see a child struggling they can't articulate it,
  so it comes across as disappointment and judgment.
- **[2024-11-06] (paraphrase)** On the independent-vs-commercial-artist transition: doing
  work for yourself is fine and ego-driven, but the moment you involve collaborators it
  becomes bigger than what you want. Maturing from independent artist to successful
  commercial artist requires accepting this. You can write crazy songs for yourself, but
  if nobody wants to play or buy them it's hard to sustain a living.
- **[2024-11-06] (paraphrase)** Business isn't won by imitating something you saw on TV;
  it comes from genuine appreciation — knowing enough about someone's work to offer, when
  invited, a couple of observations, without attacking what they already do.
- **[2024-11-06] (paraphrase — key economic insight)** Brands buying creator content get
  a bargain: they pay not just for production services but for **distribution**. The
  larger the creator's following and engagement, the more they're really buying. So brands
  get a more effective, trusted, authentic delivery vehicle, and more brands should support
  artists this way. Chris frames the production-budget vs. media-buy distinction from his
  own commercial-production background.
- **[2024-11-06] (self-reported anecdote — context about Chris)** Chris got paid
  **$140,000** to work with a tech company on a video shot on his own iPhone and edited
  himself; his attorney of ~30 years (whom he met after being sued early in his career for
  a copyright issue that Chris says was not his fault) couldn't wrap his head around the
  amount, contrasting it with the old days of ~$100K for an entire music video where the
  director keeps only ~10%.
- **[2024-11-06] (paraphrase)** Chris admires the "economy of filmmaking" — shooting for
  the edit, not overshooting for safety — and compares it to Clint Eastwood (who refuses
  extra takes) and Christopher Nolan.
- **[2024-11-06] (self-reported anecdote)** Chris describes his own struggle with a
  "walk into the camera then speak" hook: walking in makes him forget his line, and after
  seven takes he gives up and just speaks to camera. He voices genuine admiration for
  solo creators who push through that friction.
- **[2024-11-06] (paraphrase)** Chris references the **Dogme 95** filmmaking rules
  (available light only, no scene manipulation, handheld) as a kindred stripped-down
  philosophy to Adrian's solo run-and-gun method.
- **[2024-11-06] (paraphrase)** Chris on personal style: he says friends tell him "I don't
  know what to do with this" (what to wear) and end up looking like J.Crew; he treats dress
  as a form of self-expression and part of one's brand, though not make-or-break.

### Adrian Per (context only — NOT persona data)

- **[2024-11-06] (context)** At 32 (Oct 2022) Adrian had ~$13 in his account despite a
  Grammy-nominated / award-winning commercial and music-video career; he'd applied to
  ~60 creative and non-creative jobs and landed zero interviews (over/under-qualified).
- **[2024-11-06] (context)** He overspent early six-figure income (Rolexes, iced-out chain,
  cars) without understanding taxes, then liquidated savings for taxes and family funeral
  costs — which pushed him into making content.
- **[2024-11-06] (context)** Brand-deal economics that stunned him: creators making vertical
  selfie-style product videos can be paid what a brand would spend on a legacy commercial.
- **[2024-11-06] (context)** His current time/revenue mix: ~80% self-funded personal passion
  content (money-losing), ~10% production-company work-for-hire, ~10% paid brand partnerships.
- **[2024-11-06] (context — craft method)** His "paper edit" process: ideate from music,
  pick an instrumental, write the script to the beat, build a shot list before filming so
  the edit is essentially done before shooting; talks locked to a song's tempo (~92 BPM);
  uses vertical center-line framing with the lead eye down the middle; minimal gear,
  autofocus, available light, live lavalier audio, solo shooting.
- **[2024-11-06] (context)** The head/CEO of Instagram highlighted his profile; Adrian
  treats accolades and flops with the same emotional distance to keep going.
- **[2024-11-06] (context — attribution: uncertain on exact intro names)** Introduced to
  Chris at Adobe MAX by people transcribed as "Drego"/"Drigo" and "Mo"; his handle is
  **@OMGAdrian** across platforms.
