# 10 Habits I Quit To Go From Broke To Millionaire — Stop Wasting Your Life

Solo monologue by Chris Do. He frames the video as the 10 things he quit "to go from broke to millionaire" — the same things he says he quit to build "two eight-figure businesses" `[self-reported]`. Almost entirely first-person mindset/wealth material; strong feed for `beliefs.md` and `voice.md`.

★ **L3-candidate** — a dense, quotable spine of Chris's discipline/mindset framework (time > money, accountability, delayed gratification, risk, skills-over-money, saying no). Good candidate for promotion into `persona/beliefs.md`.

## Summary

The 10 habits (plus a bonus) Chris says he quit:

1. Quit **spending time to save money** — time is the most precious resource; trade money for time.
2. Quit **spending time with energy vampires** — negative people drag you to their average.
3. Quit **shiny object syndrome** — constant task/goal switching means you move laterally, not forward.
4. Quit **taking advice from the wrong people** — only take advice from people who've done what you want to do.
5. Quit **playing it safe** — all profit comes from risk; safe returns are the "sad part."
6. Quit **blaming others** — radical accountability; own even what isn't your fault to learn from it.
7. Quit **acting on impulse** — think long-term, in decades not days; delayed gratification.
8. Quit **saying yes to everything** — every yes is a no to something else; "hell yeah or hell no."
9. Quit **looking for shortcuts** — life is difficult and rewards difficult work; pursue skills, not money/things.
10. Quit **being an information hoarder** — love applying more than learning; test skills in the marketplace.
11. **(Bonus)** Quit **comparing yourself to others** — compare only against your old self.

## Key claims

All attributed to Chris Do, dated 2025-03-11, paraphrased unless quoted below.

1. People underestimate the value of their time, treating it as infinite and renewable when it is the most precious, non-renewable resource; successful entrepreneurs trade money for time. Example: driving across town and waiting in line 30 minutes to save on toilet paper is a bad trade — and in business, interviewing "the 50th person for a $10 job" is the same mistake. He cites Peter Drucker's *The Effective Executive* on protecting and consolidating discretionary time, and names meetings as the biggest corporate time-suck. (Attribution note: he says "Peter duer" / "Peter Ducker" — garble for **Peter Drucker**.)
2. You become the average of the five people you spend the most time with (attributed to Jim Rohn); time spent with pessimists / people with bad money habits pulls you toward the same behavior, so cut off "energy vampires." Successful people live in possibility ("I can" vs "I can't").
3. Shiny object syndrome — like a dog distracted by a squirrel — means constantly switching task, focus, goal or mission and second-guessing, so that over 1/5/10 years you've moved laterally and done nothing with your life.
4. Use criteria for whose advice you take: as a rule of thumb (not always true), take advice only from people who are where you want to be and have done what you want to do — not anonymous social-media voices telling you what's "dumb" or "good."
5. Playing it safe yields safe (small) returns — a mutual fund or bank keeps money safe but it "doesn't go anywhere," which is the sad part. Millionaires take big risks (he cites tech stocks and cryptocurrencies as examples of high risk / high reward), willing to make nine bad bets for one that pays off 1,000x. Cites Drucker: in business all profit comes from risk; being first carries all the risk but paves the way.
6. Chris is a "big believer in radical accountability" — take responsibility for everything that happens to, for, and with you; it's the only way to grow and learn. Blaming others makes you a fatalist. Even if life were 1% changeable, he'd rather believe he has influence than accept nothing can change. "Success has many fathers but failure has one mother" — no one owns failure, but understanding failure gives a better blueprint for success, so he'll take the blame even when it isn't his fault, because he wants to learn.
7. Quit acting on impulse; play the long game, think in decades not days — delayed gratification. He relays advice from his dad `[self-reported]`: you have a short window to "make it," so rather than having fun and working a little (which prolongs the time to success), make all the sacrifices and work extremely hard, achieve success, and then decide from that platform whether to coast or keep compounding.
8. Every yes is a no to something else. Chris says as "a middle child and a people pleaser" `[self-reported]` he always said yes to avoid others' discomfort, then failed to get his work done, honor his relationships, and created more stress. Cites Derek Sivers' *Hell Yeah or Hell No*: if it doesn't fill you with joy, say no. Saying no more often gives power to your yes. (Attribution note: "Derek sver" = **Derek Sivers**.)
9. Quit looking for shortcuts — anything promising results with no effort, money or time can't work; his mom taught him "if it's too good to be true, it's probably not true" `[self-reported]`. Life is difficult and rewards difficult work. Cites a recent conversation with Mark Manson (*The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F*), paraphrasing: "the avoidance of suffering is a form of suffering itself" — a Buddhist-flavored idea. Chris built his life to embrace difficult things few will do; scarce things are valuable. Shortcuts try to get the result without acquiring the skill — instead, treat life as a pursuit of skills, not money or things, because skills compound in you and can't be taken away; the more valuable skills you acquire, the more valuable you become.
10. Quit being an information hoarder — many stay "forever students" (master's, PhD) to feel safe and delay testing their skills in the real marketplace. Love learning but love *applying* what you learn even more; learning for its own sake is "some form of mental masturbation." His hack: before reading a book, ask why am I reading this, what do I want from it, and how will I apply it — then read searching for immediately implementable pieces.
11. (Bonus) Quit comparing yourself to others — "comparison is the thief of joy." The only worthwhile comparison is against your old self: what you did, how your mind expanded, the risks and ventures you took, the relationships you built, how much you grew in the last year.
