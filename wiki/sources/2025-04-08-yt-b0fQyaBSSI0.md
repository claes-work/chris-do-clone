# How I Went From Charging $500 to $100,000 For A Logo (PRICING DEEP DIVE)

Solo Chris Do monologue (no co-host detected — the entire video is delivered first person by Chris Do). Attribution is Chris Do throughout unless noted.

## Summary

A signature, single-topic deep dive by Chris Do on **value-based pricing** — his "price the client, not the job" philosophy. The video was made in direct response to audience demand: a prior video ("40 brutal business truths that I wish I knew when I was 20") contained the line "price the client and not the job," and viewers near-unanimously asked for a deep dive. Chris frames the same creative work (e.g., a logo/identity system) as commanding vastly different prices depending on the client's context, stakes, and the perceived expertise of the designer.

He structures the video as the **what**, the **why**, the **how**, and the **common mistakes**. The core argument: pricing by time/labor ignores expertise and, more importantly, ignores the *outcome* the client is buying — and it caps income, attracts budget/price-shopper clients, and leads to burnout and resentment. The better path is to flip the usual sequence: start with the client's desired outcome, define success metrics, assign a value to that outcome, then propose a price that is a modest fraction (he cites ~20%) of the value, "discounted for uncertainty" (Blair Enns's language).

The video closes with an extended **real, recent case study** (from "the last couple of weeks"): Chris closed a ~$69,000–$70,000 branding/design engagement for a friend's large-scale annual event — largely over text message — after years of relationship-building and a deliberate price-anchoring remark ("I'd love to help you, but I'm not cheap"). This story doubles as biography, illustrating his multi-decade positioning and his transition from small logo fees to five- and six-figure creative engagements.

Recommended reading given: Blair Enns, *Pricing Creativity* and *The Four Conversations*; Ron Baker, *Implementing Value Pricing*. Ends with a pitch for his coaching community, **The Futur Pro Group** (bi-weekly calls, 300+ creative entrepreneurs).

Note on the title figure: the thumbnail/title claims "$500 to $100,000 for a logo," but the transcript itself does not narrate a specific $500 starting logo fee or a specific $100,000 logo sale — the concrete case study lands at ~$69,000 for an event branding package. The "$500 → $100,000" framing appears to be a title-level illustration of the range rather than a transcript-cited transaction. ## Key claims

All claims dated 2025-04-08, paraphrased, attributed to Chris Do unless noted.

- **The trap:** Most creative entrepreneurs are stuck in a pricing trap without realizing it — pricing based on time/labor. [Chris Do]
- **Two value drivers:** The two largest contributing factors to value are (1) time, effort, and energy, and (2) expertise. Most people focus only on time. [Chris Do]
- **Historical reason:** We equate value with time because of a post-industrial-revolution habit; he references Marx's labor theory of value (charging for the labor required to make something) as the mindset to move away from. [Chris Do]
- **Experience paradox of hourly billing:** If you measure only time, a 20-year veteran can end up earning *less* than an inexperienced person who takes four times as long — hourly billing rewards taking longer rather than the result achieved. [Chris Do]
- **"Just charge more per hour" is a band-aid:** Raising your hourly/day rate is a temporary fix, not a real solution ("a band-aid on a wound that requires surgery"); there is always a ceiling on what people will pay per unit of time. [Chris Do]
- **No one buys your time:** Clients buy an outcome, a result, or a benefit — they rarely buy the thing you actually do. [Chris Do]
- **Value is subjective:** Like beauty and fairness, value has no objective measure; you must understand the specific person's needs, wants, dreams, goals, urgency, and business outcomes through conversation. [Chris Do]
- **Same work, different value (logo example):** The identical logo/identity work has vastly different value to a local mom-and-pop (a slick logo can even *hurt* a local business's charm) versus a multi-location business expanding across state lines, where branding does the hard work of communicating quality/story to strangers. [Chris Do]
- **Cost-of-getting-it-wrong scales with the client:** A design mistake costs a local business a couple thousand dollars in reprints; for a national franchise it can destroy tens of thousands of dollars of printing plus recalls and retraining — which is why big companies invest far more to hire the right people. [Chris Do; figures self-reported/illustrative]
- **Why big brands pay millions for a "simple" logo (Pepsi/Jaguar):** You could replicate a finished refresh, but you don't see the invisible work — trademark/positioning research, thousand-touchpoint application, 3–10 year plans — and to a multinational a single freelancer represents (1) execution risk, (2) flight risk (they cite giving someone half a million dollars who could "disappear to Puerto Rico"), and (3) they'd never hire you because you're not on their radar. [Chris Do]
- **Consequences of time-pricing:** You attract budget/price-shopper clients, become an order-taker instead of a strategic partner, hit an income ceiling, and slide into burnout and resentment. [Chris Do]
- **"There are no victims, only volunteers":** Before complaining pricing isn't fair, recognize you volunteered for the system you built. He attributes the line to his business coach. [Chris Do, quoting his business coach]
- **The method — flip the sequence (from Blair Enns's *Pricing Creativity*):** Instead of scope → time → price, go (1) ask the desired outcome/result, (2) ask how they'll measure success and what impact success has on the business, (3) assign a value to that outcome, (4) propose a price that feels fair relative to the value, "discounted for uncertainty." [Chris Do, crediting Blair Enns]
- **The 20% anchor:** A fair consulting price is roughly 20% of the value created; e.g., if the work would generate $100,000 in new year-one revenue, ~$20,000 is a fair price for an 80% upside. This double-anchors the client (once on the big success number, once on the smaller price). [Chris Do; figures illustrative]
- **Confirm the yes carefully:** Ask "would you be confident/joyful to move forward for $X?" and listen for false yeses (hesitation, stretched or upward-intonation yes = a problem); surface objections then with "what might be giving you pause? Is there another stakeholder not here today?" [Chris Do]
- **Build the bid backward:** Once you have verbal buy-in on the number (e.g., 20k), you write the scope/proposal to land at that number rather than pricing up from hours. Getting started = a 50% check. [Chris Do]
- **When hourly is fine:** Hourly still makes sense sometimes — especially change orders after an initial bid. He is explicit he is "not here to shame you" if time-pricing is working. [Chris Do]
- **Do NOT value-price when you're the subcontractor:** If a client hires an agency and the agency hires you as an independent contractor/freelancer, don't try to value-price — the agency takes the risk and deserves the profit; pay the sub a day/hour/weekly rate. Value-pricing requires talking to the decision-maker with stake in the outcome, not a mid-level manager. [Chris Do]
- **Common mistakes when value-pricing:** (1) Being agenda-driven / always steering to the thing you sell instead of the client's real outcome (acting selfishly, not as a fiduciary); (2) being uncomfortable talking about money/business and transmitting that skittishness; (3) not being curious long enough / rushing the discovery; (4) using self-centered language ("if I showed you") instead of neutral/objective language ("if you were presented with a solution"); (5) sending bloated proposals — keep it to a simple 1–2 page proposal, no bios/awards. [Chris Do]
- **Be willing to walk away:** If you can't deliver the outcome, recuse yourself, admit it honestly, and offer a referral — act as a fiduciary for the client. [Chris Do]
- **"Those who don't talk about money don't make it":** Practice money conversations on a low-stakes job you don't care about, not on your dream client. [Chris Do, quoting Blair Enns]
- **Charge more to get better clients:** Raising prices attracts better clients; the friend who paid ~$69k loved the process and opened new doors (asked Chris to teach him/his community). [Chris Do]
- **Relationships over transactions:** Cultivate multi-year relationships, not job-to-job. Years of established credibility/trust made the deal permission-based; he planted a gentle, permission-based seed rather than an aggressive ask. [Chris Do]
