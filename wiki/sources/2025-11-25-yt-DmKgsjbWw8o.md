# The Scene Every Salesman Needs to See

## Summary

Chris Do breaks down a sequence from **Better Call Saul (Season 4)** — the arc where
the disbarred Jimmy McGill (Saul Goodman) takes a job selling cell phones at "CC
Mobile," finds the store dead with no customers, and reinvents the product from the
inside out. Instead of selling phone technology (data plans, roaming, hands-free), he
repositions the exact same phones as **privacy** ("burner phones") and sells them to a
completely different set of customers — a cash-business owner hiding from the IRS,
street kids, and finally a biker gang. Chris uses each beat of the scene to teach
positioning, problem-awareness, market creation, and going to where the customers are.
He closes by tying it to his own pivot from making commercials to client-direct brand
strategy/design. This is a film-analysis teaching format like his other TV-scene sales
breakdowns; a Mad Men breakdown is teased at the end.

## Key claims

All dated 2025-11-25, paraphrased, attributed to Chris Do unless noted.

- ★ **Positioning defined:** positioning is the space you occupy in the heart and mind
  of the customer relative to your competitor. To win it, you find a customer whose
  problem your product is the perfect solution for. (L3-candidate for topics/sales-clients)
- ★ **Create the customer, don't change the product:** you can take the same product,
  leave it unchanged, and move it to an entirely different category of people to sell
  to — inventing a new market rather than fighting for the old one. Chris cites Peter
  Drucker: the purpose of a business is to make a profit and to create the customer.
  (L3-candidate)
- **Sell the deeper want, not features/functions:** instead of pitching technology and
  convenience, Jimmy sold privacy — a fundamentally different, more emotionally charged
  need attached to the same object.
- **Make people problem-aware to get rich:** "if you want to get rich, teach people
  about the problem and make them problem aware." You agitate a problem the customer
  already feels, then present your product as the clean solution.
- **Agitate the problem, then the sale sells itself:** once you agitate the problem
  properly, use the language the customer understands, and frame a problem they
  identify with, the sale closes on its own.
- **Speak the customer's language / meet their existing pain:** with the biker gang,
  Jimmy leads with "private conversations are few and far between" inside the
  correctional system — a pain point they already feel — rather than pitching a phone.
- **Manufacture demand and scarcity:** Jimmy fakes a phone call with an "existing
  customer," refuses a fake lowball offer ("everyone wants these things"), writes "On
  hold, do not sell" on a stack of phones, and destroys a brand-new phone on demand —
  all to create artificial demand and curiosity.
- **Name the solution:** coining a clever, memorable term for your solution ("information
  hygiene") is a strong way to package an idea and make it stick.
- **Perception and packaging — look like your customers:** when Jimmy shows up looking
  like a lawyer, kids call him a narc and won't buy. He changes into a tracksuit
  (his future signature look), looks like the people, and the phones fly out of the
  trunk. If you don't look like your customers, your customers have a problem with you.
- **Go to where the customers are:** don't wait for low, occasional store traffic —
  bring the product/service/offer to where the customers already are to reduce
  friction. Modern parallel: if your customers are on TikTok, make TikTok content; if
  business-oriented, LinkedIn; in between, Instagram.
- ★ **Blue Ocean strategy:** if you're not selling an abundant amount, you may need to
  change lanes. A crowded, competitive space is a "red ocean" where everyone fights
  for the same business; move your boat to a space with less competition (blue ocean)
  and you can have the whole ocean to yourself. (L3-candidate)
- **Personal application (self-reported):** Chris used to make commercials for a living,
  but saw fewer people watching commercials and the market shrinking. Being impatient,
  he pivoted to client-direct work — brand strategy and brand design — reusing the same
  skill sets but finding a different customer. That, he says, is how you apply
  positioning in your business.
