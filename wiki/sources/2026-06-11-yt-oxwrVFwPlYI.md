# The Blue Ocean Strategy: How to Make the Competition Irrelevant

## Summary

Short solo clip from Chris Do's **personal channel** (@ChrisDo, not @thefutur). Chris
explains the "Blue Ocean" idea — moving into uncontested market space rather than
fighting rivals head-on — using a boat/fishing metaphor and then a business case
study. He credits **Kim & Mauborgne** implicitly via the "blue ocean" label (the
concept originates in their book *Blue Ocean Strategy*; Chris is applying/teaching it,
not originating it) and explicitly cites the late **Dr. Clayton Christensen** and his
book *The Innovator's Dilemma*.

The bulk of the clip is the **Honda motorcycle in America** story: Honda's small,
light, underpowered bikes couldn't compete with the dominant Harley-Davidson (loud,
powerful, heavy, expensive). By all sales measures the U.S. launch was a failure. But
when the Honda team rode the bikes off-road on trails and hills to blow off steam,
onlookers took notice — and a new category, dirt bikes / off-roading, was born. Honda
found a new use case no competitor was serving, moved into a lane with no competition,
and gained a U.S. footprint. This connects to Chris's broader "create the customer /
positioning" theme (cf. [[wiki/sources/2025-11-25-yt-DmKgsjbWw8o]]).

Attribution: solo Chris narration throughout; the `>>` markers in the transcript are
music cues, not a second speaker. Attribution is clear (Chris).

## Key claims

All dated 2026-06-11, paraphrased, attributed to Chris Do unless noted.

- The Blue Ocean strategy — moving your "boat" to uncontested waters where there's
  little or no competition — is a valid strategy. *(Framework credited to Kim &
  Mauborgne, Blue Ocean Strategy; Chris is applying it.)*
- Clayton Christensen's *The Innovator's Dilemma* addresses this same dynamic of new
  entrants finding unserved lanes. *(Chris attributes the idea to Christensen.)*
- Competing head-on against a dominant incumbent on the incumbent's own terms is a
  losing game: Honda's smaller, lighter, cheaper bikes lost to Harley-Davidson on the
  freeway/power dimension the market then valued.
- A product's apparent weakness in one arena can be a decisive strength in another —
  Honda's small, agile bikes were ideally suited to off-road trails.
- New markets can be discovered almost by accident, by observing how people actually
  use (or want to use) the product — the dirt-bike category emerged from bystanders
  noticing the Honda team riding off-road.
- The strategic move is to find (or create) a new use case / new lane with no
  competition rather than fight for share in a crowded one — this is how you make the
  competition irrelevant.
