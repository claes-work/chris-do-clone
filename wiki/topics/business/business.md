# Business — hub

Chris Do's frameworks for the **business of design** — how to scale a creative studio,
what work in a service business is actually essential, how to position and focus a
practice, how he thinks about money, the books that shaped him, and the mission that
drives it all. Grounded in his lived history running [[wiki/entities/blind|Blind]]
(founded Dec 1995, wound down ~2018–2019) and [[wiki/entities/the-futur|The Futur]]
(est. 2014 as The Skool / 2016 rebrand).

Pricing gets its own hub — see [[wiki/topics/pricing/pricing]]. This page covers the
surrounding business architecture; where money and pricing overlap they are cross-linked.

> Most large figures on this page are **[self-reported]** — Chris's own account of his
> business, not registry-verified. See [[wiki/entities/blind]] and
> [[wiki/entities/the-futur]] for the registry records and the caveats.

## 1. Scaling a creative studio — "I do / We do / You do"

Chris's central model for getting the founder out of the delivery seat. His framing:
**entrepreneurship is really teaching** — your success is bounded by how well you can
transfer your values, decision-making, and creativity to others
([[wiki/sources/2022-12-01-yt-Qn5PPMhHkXM]], 2022-12-01).

> "Entrepreneurship is really about being a teacher more than anything else. How you're
> able to teach your values, your principles, your decision making process, your
> creativity to others will determine how successful you are." (2022-12-01)

The failure mode he warns against: the founder as **bottleneck**, running on an
instinctive "secret sauce" they never learn to transfer — so if they were "hit by a
bus," the company collapses. He cites Apple/Steve Jobs as the counter-example of
knowledge successfully transferred (2022-12-01).

The three-phase delegation/mentorship model, borrowed from teaching and applied to
management ([[wiki/sources/2022-12-01-yt-Qn5PPMhHkXM]], 2022-12-01):

1. **I do** — the trainee shadows, observes, takes notes, says nothing (even sitting
   silently on client calls). Instructing them to stay silent actually *lowers* their
   anxiety. Debrief afterward: "what was said versus what was meant."
2. **We do** — a hybrid stage: introduce the trainee to the client as the lead (e.g.
   art director), let them field questions, and reassure a nervous client they are not
   getting a "B team." Do this 2–3 times across a few projects before moving on.
3. **You do** — the trainee is the full point person with autonomy; the founder
   deliberately stays out (staying involved undermines their authority and makes clients
   route around them), offering only optional weekly/bi-weekly office hours.

> "The way that I'm able to train people and help them to level up and simultaneously
> free myself from working in the agency is to use this model: I do, we do, you do. It's
> something that teachers use, but it's also applicable in business management."
> (2022-12-01)

**Case study — Tom & *Dogtown and Z-Boys*.** Chris illustrates the model with "Tom," an
Art Center grad hired as a junior designer and progressed to client-facing art director
on the main-title design for the documentary *Dogtown and Z-Boys* (dir. Stacy Peralta).
By the "You do" stage Tom and Peralta dealt directly as friends. (Tom is a third-party
context figure, not the subject.) The film and director are verifiable; the studio credit
and Tom's role are as Chris tells it ([[wiki/sources/2022-12-01-yt-Qn5PPMhHkXM]],
2022-12-01, `[self-reported]`).

**The payoff / "cloning yourself."** The best case: clients *forget* Chris was ever
involved, or praise the trainee directly. Done right, hires come to share your core
values — otherwise "you'll always be stuck at being company of one" (2022-12-01). This is
the classic reframe of working **on** the business instead of **in** it.

> "In order for you to be a good entrepreneur, you have to be a great teacher. You have
> to be able to replicate — basically clone yourself." (2022-12-01)

The delegation discipline is echoed in his coaching of Melinda Livsey
([[wiki/sources/2017-06-21-yt-D8BN2YSyYkg]], 2017-06-21): delegation always costs more
time up front (explaining, building process, accepting the other person won't work at
your level), but every entrepreneur must accept and optimize it anyway. Practical tips
there: build a stable of 2–3 go-to freelancers and give them steady work so they stay
available; record a video brief (voice + bullets) waiting in the collaborator's inbox.

## 2. The essential-work insight — own the relationship, not the craft

Chris's most counter-intuitive business claim: **the craft you were hired for is usually
not the essential work.** To find your first hire, list your essential vs. non-essential
work across every role you play — you'll discover work you thought essential is not
([[wiki/sources/2022-12-27-yt-o8IdDEJym1U]], 2022-12-27).

He applies it to himself: he was hired for *design*, but design turned out to be
non-essential — he could hire lower-skilled people, generate the ideas, and elevate their
work through art direction until it "would pass as my own." **The essential role is owning
the client relationship** ([[wiki/sources/2022-12-27-yt-o8IdDEJym1U]], 2022-12-27).

> "Clients hire people that they trust and that they believe can get the work done.
> They're not as concerned as you might originally believe as to who does the work
> themselves." (2022-12-27)

This is validated biographically by the "ghost-riding" story at Imaginary Forces: a team
of three (Michelle, Jesse, Vanessa) did much of the actual design while Chris presented to
clients and steadily raised his day rate — Kyle Cooper never wondered how "one person"
produced so much in so many styles ([[wiki/sources/2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]],
2024-02-24, `[self-reported]`). The lesson that seeded the model: from the start his
"whole modus operandi was to get other people to do what it is that I was doing"
([[wiki/sources/2022-12-01-yt-Qn5PPMhHkXM]], 2022-12-01).

## 3. Focus & positioning — pressure = force / area

Chris frames focus with physics: **pressure equals force divided by area.** Reduce the
area (the number of things you do) and the same force produces a breakthrough
([[wiki/sources/2022-12-27-yt-o8IdDEJym1U]], 2022-12-27).

> "If you want to have a breakthrough you have to have a powerful force to literally break
> through, and in physics we know that pressure equals force divided by area." (2022-12-27)

Creatives are taught to show breadth ("soup to nuts"), but breadth makes you hard to
market and blocks expertise/authority. Chris's own origin: he did logo, brochure,
animation, and editing — saying yes to everything — and never felt confident because he
was always doing each thing for the first time (2022-12-27).

**The "holy F" focus moment.** On his first film-title job — main titles for *Eraser*
(Schwarzenegger), for Kyle Cooper / R/Greenberg — the supervising animator showed him
basics he should have known but didn't, because he was spread too thin. That night he told
his girlfriend (now wife) that motion design was the field to focus on; Blind dropped all
other work and focused only on motion design in year one
([[wiki/sources/2022-12-27-yt-o8IdDEJym1U]], 2022-12-27, `[self-reported]`; company/film
names flagged for verification in the source). He attributes "making the difficult
business decision" (the discipline of narrowing) to **Blair Enns**.

**Positioning: "raise the gate."** Position yourself as an expert/equal, not a chaser.
Your website is a filter — show the caliber of clients and glossy, well-cased work (or,
alternatively, publish process/pricing) so under-budget prospects self-select out; "like
attracts like." He uses the Barneys department-store analogy: the brand experience "told
me everything" without a word, and can be designed to filter clients
([[wiki/sources/2017-06-21-yt-D8BN2YSyYkg]], 2017-06-21).

> "If you show a site with a hundred logos... it looks like they're worth $20 to me... I
> would show less work, I would spend more time developing case studies and showing each
> project in its best possible light." (2017-06-21)

This connects to the sales discipline he draws from **Blair Enns** ("retreat and follow" /
"win without pitching"): replace pitching with conversations; pursuing clients reads as
desperate, and "desperate people rarely win business or are treated fairly"
([[wiki/sources/2022-12-15-yt-6Thuc_W_dDU]], 2022-12-15). See also §5 reading list and
[[wiki/topics/pricing/pricing]].

## 4. Money philosophy — the 5 money rules

From "5 Money Rules To Make More Money In 2023"
([[wiki/sources/2022-12-27-yt-o8IdDEJym1U]], 2022-12-27), lessons Chris says he learned in
his early 20s:

1. **Delegate.** Creatives trade finite time for money; the only way to scale beyond a
   solo operator is to give away non-essential work (ties to §1 and §2). Burnout from
   working weekends is what forced him to learn this.
   > "The only way that you can scale beyond being a solo operator is to delegate — to
   > give away some of the work that you're doing." (2022-12-27)
2. **Shift from hourly to project/value-based pricing.** Selling units of time invites the
   client to manage your time and signals your time (not the outcome) is the valuable
   thing. Clients buy outcomes/results. (Full treatment: [[wiki/topics/pricing/pricing]].)
   > "When you sell units of time you're inviting the client to manage your time."
   > (2022-12-27)
3. **Focus** — see §3 (pressure = force / area).
4. **Learn to have the money conversation.** "Those who don't talk about money don't make
   it." Being good at money talk is "a sign of good business acumen, not a sign of poor
   manners." Delay creates anxiety, and "anxious people don't buy." Techniques (price
   bracketing, reverse anchoring, three-tier good/better/best, order-of-magnitude bids)
   live in [[wiki/topics/pricing/pricing]]. He credits qualifying-before-bidding to
   **Blair Enns**: "I'm not in the bid building business" ([[wiki/sources/2017-06-21-yt-D8BN2YSyYkg]], 2017-06-21).
   > "Those who don't talk about money don't make it, so learning how to have the money
   > conversation can be transformative." (2022-12-27)
5. **Invest — especially in compoundable skills.** To make money you must spend/risk
   money (he cites Sean Cannell's "scared money don't make money" and Peter Drucker's "in
   business all profit comes from risk"). Invest profit in people, training, and the best
   tools/equipment for your team — running a 20–30 person agency on janky hardware
   "artificially caps human potential." But the biggest lever is investing in **yourself**:
   acquiring marketable, compounding skills "raises the basement of what your value is."
   (He cites Alex Hormozi here.)
   > "Invest as much money in your self-development as possible until you make so much
   > money it's no longer possible... you're raising the basement of what your value is in
   > the world." (2022-12-27)
   > "They can take away your house, they can take away your cars... but they can't take
   > away what you keep up here." (2022-12-27)

**Money mindset — "no power over me."** Rooted in a refugee/immigrant backstory: escaping
communism, the family "didn't have much." Chris says he gives money no power over him,
which is the source of his freedom to say no to bad-fit work
([[wiki/sources/2017-06-21-yt-D8BN2YSyYkg]], 2017-06-21):

> "I come from nothing, I will go back to nothing. I'm comfortable in nothingness... the
> power that money has over me, it's whatever it is I give it. So I give it no power over
> me." (2017-06-21)

He reframes his own risk-taking as *not* courage but a **secret backup plan** — he can
always go work for someone else at his market rate (stated ~$400–600k/year), which lets
him "bet the farm" ([[wiki/sources/2017-06-21-yt-D8BN2YSyYkg]], 2017-06-21;
[[wiki/sources/2022-12-27-yt-o8IdDEJym1U]], 2022-12-27). Low overhead early on (a
$1,200/month loft) was his source of pricing power — "that gives me a lot of power to say
no" ([[wiki/sources/2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]], 2024-02-24).

## 5. Reading list & influences

Chris repeatedly traces his ideas to their sources ("genealogy of ideas," a concept he
attributes to Austin Kleon). His core recommendations
([[wiki/sources/2022-12-15-yt-6Thuc_W_dDU]], 2022-12-15, unless noted):

- **Blair Enns** — *The Win Without Pitching Manifesto*, *Pricing Creativity*, *The Four
  Conversations*. The most-cited influence across the corpus: positioning over pitching,
  "retreat and follow," qualifying before building a bid, "price the client not the job,"
  "discounted for uncertainty," and "those who don't talk about money don't make it"
  ([[wiki/sources/2022-12-15-yt-6Thuc_W_dDU]], 2022-12-15;
  [[wiki/sources/2017-06-21-yt-D8BN2YSyYkg]], 2017-06-21;
  [[wiki/sources/2025-04-08-yt-b0fQyaBSSI0]], 2025-04-08). Chris did a 12-part series with
  Enns on the Futur channel.
- **Ron Baker** — *Implementing Value Pricing*. Foundational to the value-pricing method
  ([[wiki/sources/2025-04-08-yt-b0fQyaBSSI0]], 2025-04-08).
- **Marty Neumeier** — *The Brand Flip*. "Customers create the brand" — you can no longer
  control your brand, only influence it; buying is joining a tribe (2022-12-15).
- **Jim Rohn** — *Seven Strategies for Wealth & Happiness* (Chris misremembers the title
  as "seven principles..."; `attribution: uncertain` on the exact title). A "framework for
  life"; Rohn as the source upstream of Tony Robbins, Darren Hardy, Brian Tracy
  (2022-12-15).
- **Michael Bungay Stanier** — *The Coaching Habit*. Leaders should be slower to give
  answers ("answers steal people of thinking"); stay curious longer; open with "What's on
  your mind?" and the "AWE" question — "And what else?" (2022-12-15).
- **Kevin Daley** — *Socratic Selling*. Chris's most useful selling book: open with a
  question, not a presentation; "relive the past before you rush the future"; the two real
  levers for more money are being better at **marketing** and **selling** (cutting costs
  doesn't grow a business) (2022-12-15).
- **Seth Godin** — permission marketing. The theoretical backbone of Chris's
  attraction-marketing playbook: "strangers to friends, and friends to customers"
  ([[wiki/sources/2023-03-09-yt-CiyNGOIbwcE]], 2023-03-09).
- Bonus: **Tony Hsieh** *Delivering Happiness*, **Gary Keller** *The One Thing*, **Mark
  Manson** *The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F*ck* (2022-12-15).

**Marketing as a business system — attraction marketing.** Chris positions attraction
marketing as a **hybrid** between transactional direct outreach (fast money, targeted) and
transformational content/personal-brand building (slow, compounding goodwill). Go outbound
when you can't cover basic needs; go inbound once you have a runway to think 3–5–10 years
out. Playbook: create genuinely valuable content → extract nuggets → free resource that
makes the audience self-identify → escalating micro-commitments → soft offer. The Futur
has made 1,800+ videos "mostly asking for nothing" to build that goodwill
([[wiki/sources/2023-03-09-yt-CiyNGOIbwcE]], 2023-03-09).

> "One model is more transactional... My model is more transformational, it's not
> transactional." (2023-03-09)

## 6. The mission — "teach 1 billion people"

Chris's stated big-hairy-audacious goal: **teach and impact 1 billion lives** / "teach 1
billion people how to make a living doing what they love"
([[wiki/sources/2019-08-12-yt-f7T1Zs28Deo]], 2019-08-12;
[[wiki/entities/the-futur]]). The through-line of the whole business is that
entrepreneurship *is* teaching (§1), and his identity shift over ~2.5 years from
"designer" to "teacher" is the personal engine behind it
([[wiki/sources/2023-03-09-yt-CiyNGOIbwcE]], 2023-03-09):

> "When people ask me what do you do, I don't pause so much anymore. I just say I'm a
> teacher. That's connected to who I am." (2023-03-09)

The Futur is framed not as a design school but as an education platform that **finds and
fills gaps in the market** — starting with design courses (typography, UX), then pivoting
to the business skills (negotiation, sales, pricing) it's now best known for
([[wiki/sources/2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]], 2024-02-24). His teaching fluency comes from
~15 years teaching at private art schools before he made a single YouTube video
([[wiki/sources/2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]], 2024-02-24;
[[wiki/sources/2023-03-09-yt-CiyNGOIbwcE]], 2023-03-09):

> "I taught at the private art schools for 15 years before I even made a freaking video on
> YouTube... If you're working with eight students in a classroom for five hours, you
> either get good or you go home." (2024-02-24)

The mission has an explicit anti-institutional edge: Chris believes "1,000%" in the power
of **education** but distrusts the **institutions** — student debt is uniquely dangerous
(non-dischargeable, government-backed loans sold to private banks), and boards default to
killing new ideas out of liability. He positions The Futur, in spirit, as a **private art
school without the crippling debt** ([[wiki/sources/2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]],
2024-02-24, `[self-reported opinion]`).

## 7. Lived history — the numbers behind the frameworks

The business advice is grounded in [[wiki/entities/blind|Blind]] and
[[wiki/entities/the-futur|The Futur]]. Key self-reported figures (see entity pages for the
registry records and caveats):

- **Founded Blind on a $5,000 check** [self-reported] — over dinner at the Westin
  Bonaventure, his uncle's business partner "Bob" wrote a $5,000 check on the spot against
  a requested ~$100,000, without reading the 14-page business plan. Financing partners held
  51%; when they defaulted, Chris kept the $5,000 and regained full ownership
  ([[wiki/sources/2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]], 2024-02-24).
  > "Five grand feels like 100 grand when you're broke and you have a massive amount of
  > credit card debt." (2024-02-24)
- **Blind grossed "$80 million" lifetime** over ~23–25 years (1995–2018/19) [self-reported].
  Repeated across sources but never independently audited — treat strictly as self-reported
  ([[wiki/sources/2022-12-01-yt-Qn5PPMhHkXM]], 2022-12-01;
  [[wiki/sources/2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]], 2024-02-24; [[wiki/entities/blind]]).
- **The Futur is "a $4–5 million company"** [self-reported], stated at recording (2024),
  on its way toward similar scale ([[wiki/sources/2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]], 2024-02-24).
  > "I think we'd be a hundred-million-dollar company if we knew the right people — but
  > we're not, we're like a four or five million company right now." (2024-02-24)
- **Walked away from client work (~2018–2019)** to go all-in on The Futur, turning down a
  $400,000 Xbox commercial (and a bigger follow-up, and a buy-out that didn't close)
  [self-reported] ([[wiki/sources/2024-02-24-yt-f5uYRuqK4g0]], 2024-02-24).
  > "Let's leave the golden goose and let's jump on the pirate ship and see what happens."
  > (2024-02-24)

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION: **The Futur revenue framing.** Chris calls The Futur "a $4–5 million
